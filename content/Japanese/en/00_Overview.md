# Japanese (日本語) — Overview

## About This Topic

This topic provides a comprehensive, progressive curriculum for learning Japanese from scratch to an advanced level. The 25 lessons are organized into 5 stages that build upon each other, moving from foundational concepts through practical real-world application.

Japanese is spoken by approximately 125 million people, primarily in Japan, and is one of the most studied languages in the world. It features three writing systems (hiragana, katakana, and kanji), a rich honorific system, and a grammar structure fundamentally different from English. Japanese is essential for engaging with Japan's culture, technology, business, and media.

## Prerequisites

- No prior Japanese knowledge required (the course begins from zero)
- Basic understanding of grammar concepts in your native language is helpful

## Difficulty

- **Tier**: Beginner (starts from scratch, progresses to advanced)
- **Estimated scope**: JLPT N5 through N2/N1 levels (A1 through B2/C1 CEFR equivalent)

## Learning Path

```
Stage 1: Foundations (01-05) — JLPT N5
  Build the basics: writing systems, greetings, sentence structure, numbers, basic verbs
  ↓
Stage 2: Elementary (06-10) — JLPT N5/N4
  Expand: adjectives, te-form applications, particles, daily vocabulary, location expressions
  ↓
Stage 3: Intermediate Grammar (11-15) — JLPT N4/N3
  Deepen: conditional forms, passive/causative, relative clauses, compound sentences, keigo intro
  ↓
Stage 4: Advanced (16-20) — JLPT N3/N2
  Refine: advanced keigo, expressing opinions, abstract grammar, idiomatic expressions, kanji mastery
  ↓
Stage 5: Practical Application (21-25) — JLPT N2/N1
  Apply: reading comprehension, writing, Japanese culture, business Japanese, JLPT exam preparation
```

## How to Use This Course

1. **Follow the order**: Lessons build on each other. Complete each stage before moving to the next.
2. **Do the exercises**: Every lesson includes practice exercises with answers. Work through them.
3. **Learn the writing systems first**: Lesson 01 is critical. Master hiragana before proceeding.
4. **Review regularly**: Language learning requires repetition. Review previous lessons periodically.
5. **Practice writing**: Don't just read — write out hiragana, katakana, and kanji by hand. Muscle memory matters.
6. **Use romaji as a crutch, not a goal**: Romaji is provided in early lessons to help, but transition to reading kana as soon as possible.
7. **Reference lessons**: Lessons 05 (verbs), 15 (keigo), and 20 (kanji) serve as useful references to return to.

## Key Features of Japanese

Japanese has several distinctive features that differ from English and other languages:

- **Three writing systems**: Hiragana (ひらがな) for native words, katakana (カタカナ) for foreign words, kanji (漢字) for meaning-dense characters
- **SOV word order**: Subject-Object-Verb, the opposite of English SVO
- **Particles**: Postpositional markers (は, が, を, に, で, etc.) indicate grammatical roles
- **Verb-final**: The verb always comes at the end of the sentence
- **No articles or plurals**: Japanese lacks "a/the" and generally does not mark plural
- **Honorific system (敬語 / keigo)**: Three levels of politeness deeply embedded in the language
- **Topic-prominent**: Sentences are organized around a topic (marked by は), not just a subject
- **Omission of the obvious**: Subjects and objects are freely dropped when contextually clear
