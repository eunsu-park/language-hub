## 1. Standard Italian and Its Tuscan Base

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|        STANDARD ITALIAN — L'ITALIANO STANDARD             |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  Based on: 14th-century literary Tuscan (fiorentino)     |
|  Key authors: Dante, Petrarca, Boccaccio (le tre corone) |
|                                                          |
|  Codified by:                                            |
|    Pietro Bembo — "Prose della volgar lingua" (1525)     |
|    Accademia della Crusca — Founded 1583                 |
|    (First Italian dictionary, "Vocabolario" — 1612)      |
|                                                          |
|  At unification (1861):                                  |
|    Only ~2.5% of Italians spoke standard Italian         |
|    The rest spoke local dialects as their first language  |
|                                                          |
|  Today:                                                  |
|    ~95% of Italians speak standard Italian               |
|    ~50% still use dialect regularly (ISTAT surveys)      |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### Why Tuscan?

The prestige of the Tuscan literary tradition, especially the works of:

| Author | Major Work | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Dante Alighieri** (1265-1321) | *La Divina Commedia* | Demonstrated the literary power of the vernacular over Latin |
| **Francesco Petrarca** (1304-1374) | *Il Canzoniere* | Refined the lyric poetry tradition in Tuscan |
| **Giovanni Boccaccio** (1313-1375) | *Il Decamerone* | Established Tuscan prose as a literary standard |

> **Key Point**: Standard Italian is not simply modern Florentine. It is a literary standard based on 14th-century Florentine, later modified and enriched by contributions from other regions, especially Rome and Milan.

### Isoglosses: The Lines That Divide

| Isogloss | Location | Separates |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| **La Spezia-Rimini Line** | From La Spezia (Liguria) to Rimini (Romagna) | Northern from Central dialects |
| **Roma-Ancona Line** | From Rome (Lazio) to Ancona (Marche) | Central from Southern dialects |

Features that differ across these lines:

| Feature | North | Center | South |
|---------|-------|--------|-------|
| Voicing of intervocalic stops | Common (*amiga* for *amica*) | Rare | Rare |
| Double consonants | Often simplified | Preserved | Strengthened |
| Final vowels | Often dropped | Preserved | Sometimes modified |
| Vowel system | Up to 15 vowels | 7 vowels (like standard) | 5 vowels |

## 3. Northern Dialects (Dialetti Settentrionali)

### 3.1 Piemontese

Spoken in Piedmont, centered around Turin (Torino).

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Loss of final vowels | *can* | cane (dog) |
| Nasal vowels (French influence) | *pan* [pã] | pane (bread) |
| *u* pronounced [y] (like French *u*) | *lun-a* [ˈlyːna] | luna (moon) |
| Subject pronoun clitics | *Mi i son* | Io sono (I am) |

**Sample Piemontese sentence:**
- *A l'é na bela giornà.* = E una bella giornata. (It's a beautiful day.)

### 3.2 Lombardo

Spoken in Lombardy, centered around Milan (Milano).

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Vowel nasalization | *pà* [pã] | pane (bread) |
| *u* → [y] | *lüna* | luna |
| Dropped final vowels | *lavorà* | lavorare (to work) |
| Negation with *minga* | *El vegn minga.* | Non viene. (He's not coming.) |

**Sample Milanese sentence:**
- *Sem andaa a cà.* = Siamo andati a casa. (We went home.)

### 3.3 Veneto

Spoken in the Veneto region, centered around Venice (Venezia).

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Interdental [θ] or [ð] | *sì* [θi] | sì |
| Loss of double consonants | *belo* | bello (beautiful) |
| *-tion* → *-sion* | *nasion* | nazione (nation) |
| Interrogative particle *-lo* | *Vien-lo?* | Viene? (Is he coming?) |

**Sample Venetian sentence:**
- *No go mai visto niente.* = Non ho mai visto niente. (I never saw anything.)

### 3.4 Emiliano-Romagnolo

Spoken in Emilia-Romagna.

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Extensive vowel reduction | *ragàs* | ragazzi (boys) |
| Strong nasal vowels | *pan* [pã] | pane |
| Final vowel loss | *tèmp* | tempo (time) |

## 4. Central Dialects (Dialetti Centrali)

### 4.1 Toscano (Tuscan)

The dialect closest to Standard Italian, but with distinctive features:

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Gorgia toscana** | Aspiration of voiceless stops between vowels: /k/ → [h], /t/ → [θ], /p/ → [ɸ] | *la casa* → [la ˈhaːsa] |
| **Spirantization** | /k/ → [h] in "la coca-cola" → [la ˌhoha ˈhɔːla] | Common in everyday speech |
| **Fono-syntactic doubling** | Strengthening of initial consonant after certain words | *a casa* → [akˈkaːsa] |

**Gorgia toscana examples:**

| Standard pronunciation | Tuscan pronunciation | Word |
|-----------------------|---------------------|------|
| [la ˈkaːsa] | [la ˈhaːsa] | la casa (the house) |
| [la ˈtorta] | [la ˈθorta] | la torta (the cake) |
| [il ˈpane] | [il ˈɸane] | il pane (the bread) |

> **Note**: Gorgia toscana is the most recognizable feature of Tuscan speech. Florentines often joke that they "drink their C's" (*bere le C*).

### 4.2 Romano (Roman)

The dialect of Rome, which has strongly influenced modern Standard Italian, especially informally:

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Rhotacism: *l* → *r* before consonant | *er sole* | il sole (the sun) |
| Gemination after stressed vowels | *annamo* | andiamo (let's go) |
| *-nd-* → *-nn-* | *quanno* | quando (when) |
| *noi/voi* → *noantri/voantri* | *noantri annamo* | noi andiamo (we go) |
| Diphthong simplification | *bono* | buono (good) |

**Common Roman expressions:**

| Roman | Standard Italian | English |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| *Daje!* | Dai! / Forza! | Come on! / Let's go! |
| *Aò!* | Ehi! | Hey! |
| *'Sto / 'Sta* | Questo / Questa | This |
| *Che te frega?* | Che te ne importa? | What do you care? |
| *Mortacci tua!* | (Strong expletive) | (Strong Roman exclamation) |

## 5. Southern Dialects (Dialetti Meridionali)

### 5.1 Napoletano (Neapolitan)

One of the most widely spoken and culturally rich dialects, with a strong literary and musical tradition.

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Schwa [ə] for unstressed final vowels | *bell* [bɛllə] | bello (beautiful) |
| Initial vowel change: *u-* → *o-* | *overò* | vero (true) |
| *mb-* → *mm-* | *'a mamma* | la mamma (the mother) |
| Definite articles: *'o, 'a, 'e* | *'o guaglione* | il ragazzo (the boy) |
| Past tense with *tenere* | *Tengo fatto* | Ho fatto (I have done) |

**Sample Neapolitan sentences:**

| Neapolitan | Standard Italian | English |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| *Comm stai?* | Come stai? | How are you? |
| *Nun saccio niente.* | Non so niente. | I don't know anything. |
| *Uè, guagliò!* | Ehi, ragazzo! | Hey, boy! |

### 5.2 Siciliano (Sicilian)

Recognized by UNESCO as a distinct language. Rich literary tradition dating to the Scuola Siciliana (13th century).

| Feature | Example | Standard Italian |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| 5-vowel system (no /e/ vs /ɛ/, /o/ vs /ɔ/) | *nivi* | neve (snow) |
| *-nd-* → *-nn-* | *quannu* | quando (when) |
| *-mb-* → *-mm-* | *amma* | gamba (leg) |
| *ll* → *dd* (cacuminal) | *beddu* | bello (beautiful) |
| Definite articles: *u, a, i* | *u picciriddu* | il bambino (the child) |
| Verb ending differences | *parrari* | parlare (to speak) |

**Sample Sicilian sentences:**

| Sicilian | Standard Italian | English |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| *Comu stai?* | Come stai? | How are you? |
| *Nun sacciu nenti.* | Non so niente. | I don't know anything. |
| *Chi bedda!* | Che bella! | How beautiful! |

### 5.3 Other Southern Varieties

| Dialect | Region | Notable Feature |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| **Abruzzese** | Abruzzo | Schwa replaces most unstressed vowels |
| **Pugliese** | Puglia | Strong vowel reduction, distinctive intonation |
| **Calabrese** | Calabria | Mix of Southern and Sicilian features |
| **Lucano** | Basilicata | Archaic vocabulary, transitional between Neapolitan and Calabrese |
| **Salentino** | Southern Puglia | Closer to Sicilian than to Pugliese |

## 6. Insular Dialects (Dialetti Insulari)

### 6.1 Sardo (Sardinian)

Sardinian is the most conservative Romance language, preserving many Latin features:

| Feature | Sardinian | Latin | Italian |
|---------|-----------|-------|---------|
| Preservation of Latin *k* before *e/i* | *kentu* | centum | cento (hundred) |
| Latin *-ll-* retained | *stella* | stella | stella (star) |
| Definite article from *ipse* | *su/sa* | ipse/ipsa | il/la |
| Preservation of Latin *-t* | *amat* | amat | ama (he/she loves) |

**Sardinian varieties:**
- **Logudorese** (central-northern) -- most conservative
- **Campidanese** (southern) -- more influenced by Italian
- **Gallurese** and **Sassarese** (northern) -- closer to Corsican

> **Important**: Sardinian is officially recognized as a minority language (*lingua minoritaria*) by Italian law (Law 482/1999).

## 7. Pronunciation Differences by Region

### 7.1 Key Phonetic Phenomena

| Phenomenon | Description | Where |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Gorgia toscana** | /k, t, p/ → [h, θ, ɸ] between vowels | Tuscany |
| **Raddoppiamento fonosintattico** | Doubling of initial consonant after certain words | Central/Southern Italy |
| **Lenition** | Weakening of consonants between vowels | Northern Italy |
| **Schwa insertion** | Unstressed vowels → [ə] | Southern Italy |
| **s voicing** | /s/ → [z] before voiced consonants | Northern Italy |
| **Affricate variation** | [ts] vs [dz] in words like "zero" | Varies by region |

### 7.2 Raddoppiamento Fonosintattico (RF)

This is the doubling of a consonant at the beginning of a word after certain trigger words:

| Trigger | Example | Pronunciation |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| *a* (preposition) | *a casa* | [akˈkaːsa] |
| *e* (conjunction) | *e bene* | [ebˈbɛːne] |
| *come* | *come mai* | [komemˈmai] |
| *qualche* | *qualche cosa* | [kwalkekˈkɔːsa] |
| *città* (oxytone words) | *città bella* | [tʃittabˈbɛlla] |

> **Regional note**: RF is natural in Central/Southern Italian speech but absent in Northern Italian pronunciation. This is one of the clearest markers of a Northern vs. Southern accent.

### 7.3 Regional Pronunciation of Standard Italian

| Word | Northern | Central (Rome) | Southern | Standard |
|------|----------|----------------|----------|----------|
| *casa* | [ˈkaːza] | [ˈkaːsa] | [ˈkaːsa] | [ˈkaːsa] |
| *zero* | [ˈdzɛːro] | [ˈdzɛːro] | [ˈtsɛːro] | [ˈdzɛːro] |
| *la gente* | [laˈdʒɛnte] | [laˈdʒɛnne] | [laˈdʒɛndə] | [laˈdʒɛnte] |
| *il sole* | [il ˈsoːle] | [er ˈsoːle] | [il ˈsoːlə] | [il ˈsoːle] |
| *buono* | [ˈbwɔːno] | [ˈbɔːno] | [ˈbwɔːnə] | [ˈbwɔːno] |

## 10. Code-Switching Between Dialect and Standard

### 10.1 When Italians Switch

Modern Italians frequently switch between standard Italian and their local dialect:

| Context | Typical Choice |
|---------|---------------|
| Work, formal meetings | Standard Italian |
| Family gatherings | Dialect or mixed |
| With friends (informal) | Regional Italian or dialect |
| Social media | Mixed (standard + dialect expressions) |
| Expressing strong emotions | Dialect (swearing, exclamations) |
| Discussing local food, traditions | Dialect terms preferred |

### 10.2 Italian Regional (Italiano Regionale)

Between pure dialect and Standard Italian, there exists a middle layer called **italiano regionale** -- standard Italian with regional pronunciation, vocabulary, and syntax:

| Region | Regional Italian Feature | Standard |
|--------|------------------------|----------|
| Milan | *Piuttosto che* (meaning "oppure") | *Piuttosto che* (rather than) |
| Rome | *Er* for *il*, *daje* for encouragement | Standard articles, *dai* |
| Naples | *Tengo* for *ho* (I have) | *Ho* |
| Sicily | *Minchia!* as general exclamation | (No equivalent) |
| Sardinia | *Eja* for *sì* | *Sì* |

### 10.3 Generational Differences

| Generation | Dialect Use |
|-----------|------------|
| Born before 1940 | Dialect as first language; Italian learned at school |
| Born 1940-1970 | Bilingual; dialect at home, Italian in public |
| Born 1970-1990 | Italian dominant; passive knowledge of dialect |
| Born after 1990 | Italian native; dialect limited to fixed expressions |

## 11. Historical Context: Unification and Standardization

### 11.1 Timeline

| Year | Event | Language Impact |
|------|-------|----------------|
| 1321 | Death of Dante | *Divina Commedia* establishes Tuscan literary prestige |
| 1525 | Bembo's *Prose della volgar lingua* | Formally proposes Tuscan as the literary standard |
| 1583 | Accademia della Crusca founded | Begins codifying Italian vocabulary |
| 1612 | First *Vocabolario degli Accademici della Crusca* | First authoritative Italian dictionary |
| 1827 | Manzoni's *I Promessi Sposi* (revised) | Promoted modern Florentine as the standard |
| **1861** | **Unification of Italy** | Only ~2.5% speak standard Italian |
| 1870 | Rome becomes capital | Roman influence on the national language grows |
| 1877 | Compulsory elementary education (Coppino Law) | Spread of Italian through schools |
| 1923 | Gentile Reform | Strengthened Italian in all schools |
| 1954 | RAI television begins broadcasting | TV becomes the greatest standardizer |
| 1960s-70s | Mass internal migration (South → North) | Dialect mixing, pressure toward standard |
| 1999 | Law 482 | Legal protection for 12 linguistic minorities |

### 11.2 The Questione della Lingua

The centuries-long debate about what form of Italian should become the national standard:

| Position | Advocate | Proposal |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Archaic Tuscan** | Pietro Bembo | Use 14th-century Tuscan literary language |
| **Courtly language** | Baldassare Castiglione | Use the language of the educated courts |
| **Living Florentine** | Niccolò Machiavelli | Use contemporary Florentine speech |
| **Modern Florentine** | Alessandro Manzoni (19th c.) | Modernize Italian based on educated Florentine |

### 11.3 Role of Television

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|     THE ROLE OF RAI TV IN LANGUAGE UNIFICATION            |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  1954: RAI begins broadcasting                           |
|  1960: "Non è mai troppo tardi" (Alberto Manzi)          |
|        — TV literacy program that taught ~1.5 million    |
|          illiterate adults to read and write Italian      |
|                                                          |
|  Effect:                                                 |
|    - First time most Italians heard standard Italian     |
|      spoken daily in their homes                         |
|    - News anchors used a neutral "broadcast Italian"     |
|    - Entertainment programs spread a common vocabulary   |
|    - Advertising created shared cultural references      |
|                                                          |
|  Result: TV did more for Italian standardization in      |
|  30 years than schools had done in 90 years.             |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

<details>

### Exercise 4: True or False

1. At the time of Italian unification in 1861, the majority of Italians already spoke standard Italian.
2. Sardinian is considered the most conservative Romance language.
3. Gorgia toscana refers to the doubling of consonants in Southern speech.
4. The TV show "Non è mai troppo tardi" helped teach millions of Italians to read.
5. Swiss Italian is based on Tuscan, just like Standard Italian in Italy.
6. Raddoppiamento fonosintattico is common in Northern Italian speech.
7. Sicilian was recognized by UNESCO as a distinct language.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **False** -- Only about 2.5% spoke standard Italian at unification.
2. **True** -- Sardinian preserves many Latin features (e.g., *kentu* for *centum*).
3. **False** -- Gorgia toscana is the aspiration of voiceless stops in Tuscany, not consonant doubling.
4. **True** -- Alberto Manzi's TV program taught approximately 1.5 million adults to read and write.
5. **False** -- Swiss Italian is based on Lombard dialects, not Tuscan.
6. **False** -- Raddoppiamento fonosintattico is characteristic of Central and Southern speech; it is absent in Northern Italian.
7. **True** -- UNESCO has recognized Sicilian as a distinct language.

</details>

### Exercise 5: Code-Switching Scenarios

For each situation, indicate whether a modern Italian would more likely use (a) Standard Italian, (b) Regional Italian, or (c) Pure dialect.

1. Giving a presentation at work
2. Chatting with grandparents in a small southern village
3. Ordering food at a restaurant in their hometown
4. Posting on social media
5. Expressing anger after stubbing their toe

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **(a) Standard Italian** -- Formal professional context
2. **(c) Pure dialect** (or at least strong dialect) -- Elderly relatives in informal setting
3. **(b) Regional Italian** -- Semi-informal, local context
4. **(b) Regional Italian** or **(a) Standard** with dialect expressions mixed in
5. **(c) Dialect** -- Strong emotions often trigger dialect use, especially for exclamations

</details>
