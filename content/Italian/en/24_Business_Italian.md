## 1. Professional Greetings and Titles

### 1.1 The Lei Form (Formal "You")

In Italian business contexts, **Lei** (formal "you") is always used unless explicitly invited to switch to **tu** (informal):

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|          FORMAL vs. INFORMAL ADDRESS                      |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  FORMAL (Lei) — Used in business:                        |
|    Lei parla italiano? (Do you speak Italian?)           |
|    Come sta, Dottore? (How are you, Doctor?)             |
|    Le presento il mio collega. (Let me introduce...)     |
|                                                          |
|  INFORMAL (tu) — Used among close colleagues:            |
|    Tu parli italiano? (Do you speak Italian?)            |
|    Come stai? (How are you?)                             |
|    Ti presento il mio collega. (Let me introduce...)     |
|                                                          |
|  SWITCHING: "Possiamo darci del tu?"                     |
|  (Can we switch to informal?)                            |
|  — Only the senior/higher-ranking person should suggest  |
|    this switch.                                          |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### 1.2 Professional Titles

In Italy, titles are used extensively in business. The most common:

| Title | Abbreviation | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Signore / Signora** | Sig. / Sig.ra | Anyone (default) |
| **Dottore / Dottoressa** | Dott. / Dott.ssa | Anyone with a university degree (laurea) |
| **Ingegnere** | Ing. | Engineers |
| **Avvocato** | Avv. | Lawyers |
| **Architetto** | Arch. | Architects |
| **Ragioniere** | Rag. | Accountants |
| **Professore / Professoressa** | Prof. / Prof.ssa | University professors, high school teachers |
| **Direttore / Direttrice** | Dir. | Directors, managers |
| **Presidente** | Pres. | Presidents, chairpersons |
| **Onorevole** | On. | Members of Parliament |

> **Important**: In Italy, *Dottore/Dottoressa* is used for ANYONE with a university degree (*laurea*), not just medical doctors. Using this title is expected in formal contexts. When in doubt, use *Dottore/Dottoressa*.

### 1.3 Professional Greetings

| Situation | Italian | English |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Morning** (until ~13:00) | Buongiorno, Dottore/Dottoressa. | Good morning, Doctor. |
| **Afternoon** (after ~13:00) | Buonasera, Ingegnere. | Good afternoon/evening, Engineer. |
| **Meeting someone** | Piacere di conoscerLa. | Pleased to meet you. |
| **Introducing yourself** | Mi presento: sono [Nome Cognome]. | Let me introduce myself: I am [Name]. |
| **Introducing someone** | Le presento il Dott. Rossi, il nostro direttore. | Let me introduce Dr. Rossi, our director. |
| **Leaving** | ArrivederLa. (formal) / Arrivederci. (standard) | Goodbye. |

## 2. Business Email Writing

### 2.1 Email Structure

| Section | Italian | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **To** | A: | A: dott.rossi@azienda.it |
| **CC** | CC: | CC: segreteria@azienda.it |

| **Greeting** | Gentile Dott. Rossi, | Dear Dr. Rossi, |

| **Body** | [Content] | [Content] |
| **Closing** | In attesa di un Suo riscontro... | Awaiting your response... |
| **Sign-off** | Cordiali saluti, | Kind regards, |

### 2.2 Formal Email Opening Phrases

| Mi permetto di contattarLa per... | I take the liberty of contacting you to... | First contact |
| Come da accordi... | As agreed... | Confirming something |
| Con la presente, La informo che... | With this letter, I inform you that... | Official notification |

### 2.4 Formal Email Closing Phrases

| Italian | English | Formality |
|---------|---------|-----------|

| | Buongiorno a tutti e grazie per la presenza. | Good morning everyone, and thank you for attending. |

## 6. Telephone Calls

### 6.1 Standard Telephone Formulas

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|          ITALIAN TELEPHONE ETIQUETTE                      |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  ANSWERING:                                              |
|    "Pronto?" (Hello?)                                    |
|    "[Company name], buongiorno." (formal)                |
|                                                          |
|  IDENTIFYING:                                            |
|    "Sono [Name] della [Company]."                        |
|    (I am [Name] from [Company].)                         |
|                                                          |
|  ASKING TO SPEAK TO SOMEONE:                             |
|    "Vorrei parlare con il Dott. Rossi, per cortesia."   |
|    (I would like to speak with Dr. Rossi, please.)      |
|                                                          |
|  ASKING WHO'S CALLING:                                   |
|    "Chi parla?" (Who is speaking?)                       |
|    "Da parte di chi?" (On behalf of whom?)               |
|                                                          |
|  TRANSFERRING:                                           |
|    "Glielo/Gliela passo." (I'll put you through.)       |
|    "Attenda in linea, per favore." (Please hold.)        |
|                                                          |
|  PERSON UNAVAILABLE:                                     |
|    "Mi dispiace, il Dott. Rossi è in riunione."         |
|    "Vuole lasciare un messaggio?"                        |
|    (Would you like to leave a message?)                  |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### 6.2 Telephone Phrase Table

| Situation | Italian | English |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Answering | Pronto? / Pronto, chi parla? | Hello? / Hello, who's speaking? |
| Identifying yourself | Buongiorno, sono Marco Bianchi della ABC. | Good morning, I'm Marco Bianchi from ABC. |
| Asking to speak to someone | Potrei parlare con la Dott.ssa Rossi? | Could I speak with Dr. Rossi? |
| Putting on hold | Un attimo, prego. Resto in linea. | One moment, please. I'll stay on the line. |
| Transferring | Le passo il Dott. Bianchi. | I'll transfer you to Dr. Bianchi. |
| Person unavailable | È momentaneamente assente. | He/she is momentarily absent. |
| Leaving a message | Potrebbe riferirgli/le di richiamarmi? | Could you ask him/her to call me back? |
| Confirming | Le confermo l'appuntamento per giovedì. | I confirm the appointment for Thursday. |
| Ending | La ringrazio. ArrivederLa. | Thank you. Goodbye. |
| Bad connection | Non La sento bene. Può ripetere? | I can't hear you well. Can you repeat? |

## 8. Business Culture and Etiquette

### 8.1 Italian Business Culture

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|          ITALIAN BUSINESS CULTURE KEY POINTS              |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  RELATIONSHIPS:                                          |
|    - Personal relationships are CRUCIAL in business      |
|    - Trust is built over time (meals, events, rapport)   |
|    - "Fare affari" (doing business) starts with          |
|      "fare amicizia" (making friends)                    |
|                                                          |
|  HIERARCHY:                                              |
|    - Italian companies tend to be hierarchical           |
|    - Decisions often come from the top                   |
|    - Titles and respect for seniority matter             |
|    - Always address seniors by title                     |
|                                                          |
|  COMMUNICATION STYLE:                                    |
|    - Expressive and animated (gestures are normal)       |
|    - Indirect disagreement is preferred                  |
|    - Eloquence is valued                                 |
|    - Interrupting can be normal (sign of engagement)     |
|                                                          |
|  TIME:                                                   |
|    - Meetings may start 10-15 min late (especially       |
|      in Central/Southern Italy)                          |
|    - Northern Italy (Milan, Turin) tends to be           |
|      more punctual                                       |
|    - However, being late yourself is NOT recommended     |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### 8.2 Business Meal Etiquette

| Rule | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Business lunches are common** | Major decisions and deals often happen over a meal |
| **Lunch is the main meal** | Business lunches can last 1.5-2 hours |
| **The host pays** | The person who invites typically pays (*offrire*) |
| **Don't rush** | Taking time is seen as a sign of respect |
| **Wine is normal** | A glass of wine at a business lunch is perfectly acceptable |
| **Coffee after the meal** | Espresso, never cappuccino (after lunch) |
| **No phones** | Checking your phone during a meal is considered rude |
| **Don't discuss business immediately** | Start with small talk (weather, sports, food, family) |

### 8.3 Gift-Giving

| Situation | Appropriate Gift | Avoid |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Visiting a client | Quality wine, artisan chocolates | Cheap gifts, cash |
| Holiday gift | Panettone/pandoro at Christmas, local specialties | Personal items (too intimate) |
| Thank-you gift | A bottle of good wine, flowers | Chrysanthemums (funeral flowers!) |

### Exercise 7: Business Culture True or False

1. In Italian business, you should switch to "tu" immediately to appear friendly.
2. Business lunches in Italy can last up to two hours.
3. Northern Italian businesses tend to be more punctual than Southern ones.
4. It is appropriate to discuss business right away at a business meal.
5. Chrysanthemums are an excellent gift for a business contact.
6. The title "Dottore" is only used for medical doctors in Italy.
7. The person who invites typically pays for the business meal.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **False** -- Always use *Lei* (formal) until the senior/higher-ranking person suggests switching to *tu*.
2. **True** -- Business lunches are important social events and can last 1.5-2 hours.
3. **True** -- Northern Italy (Milan, Turin) tends to be more time-conscious.
4. **False** -- Start with small talk (weather, food, sports, family) before transitioning to business.
5. **False** -- Chrysanthemums (*crisantemi*) are funeral flowers in Italy and should be AVOIDED as gifts.
6. **False** -- *Dottore/Dottoressa* is used for ANYONE with a university degree (*laurea*), not just medical doctors.
7. **True** -- The host (*chi invita*) is expected to pay.

</details>
