## 1. Body-Related Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| In bocca al lupo! | In the mouth of the wolf! | Good luck! |
| Costare un occhio della testa | To cost an eye from the head | To cost a fortune |
| Non avere peli sulla lingua | To have no hairs on the tongue | To speak bluntly, be frank |
| Avere le mani in pasta | To have hands in the dough | To be involved in / have a finger in every pie |
| Avere le mani bucate | To have holed hands | To be a spendthrift |
| Stare con le mani in mano | To stand with hands in hand | To do nothing, be idle |
| Dare una mano | To give a hand | To help out |
| Non vedere l'ora | To not see the hour | To look forward to eagerly |
| Avere il sangue freddo | To have cold blood | To be calm under pressure |
| Perdere la testa | To lose the head | To go crazy / fall madly in love |
| Rompere le scatole | To break the boxes | To annoy, bother someone |
| Essere in gamba | To be in leg | To be smart, capable |
| Avere fegato | To have liver | To have guts, courage |
| Fare orecchie da mercante | To make merchant's ears | To pretend not to hear |
| Restare a bocca aperta | To remain with mouth open | To be amazed |

### Example Sentences

- **In bocca al lupo** per l'esame di domani! -- **Crepi il lupo!** (Good luck on tomorrow's exam! -- Thanks! [lit. May the wolf die!])
- Quel ristorante **costa un occhio della testa**. (That restaurant costs a fortune.)
- Marco **non ha peli sulla lingua**: dice sempre quello che pensa. (Marco speaks bluntly: he always says what he thinks.)
- Lei **ha le mani in pasta** in tutti i progetti dell'ufficio. (She has a finger in every project at the office.)
- **Non vedo l'ora** di andare in vacanza! (I can't wait to go on vacation!)
- Smettila di **rompermi le scatole**! (Stop bothering me!)

> **Cultural Note**: When someone says *In bocca al lupo!*, the traditional response is *Crepi il lupo!* (May the wolf die!) or simply *Crepi!*. Responding with *Grazie* is considered bad luck by superstitious Italians.

## 2. Food-Related Idioms

Italian culture is deeply connected to food, and this is reflected in the language:

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| Rendere pan per focaccia | To give bread for focaccia | To give tit for tat, retaliate |
| Essere al verde | To be at the green | To be broke |
| Avere il prosciutto sugli occhi | To have prosciutto over the eyes | To be blind to the obvious |
| Non è farina del tuo sacco | It's not flour from your sack | It's not your own work/idea |
| Essere come il prezzemolo | To be like parsley | To be everywhere, always showing up |
| Buono come il pane | Good as bread | Very kind, good-natured |
| Mangiare la foglia | To eat the leaf | To see through a trick |
| Avere le mani in pasta | To have hands in the dough | To be deeply involved |
| Fare polpette di qualcuno | To make meatballs of someone | To beat someone badly |
| Se non è zuppa è pan bagnato | If it's not soup, it's wet bread | It's six of one, half dozen of the other |
| La ciliegina sulla torta | The cherry on the cake | The icing on the cake |
| Dire pane al pane e vino al vino | To call bread bread and wine wine | To call a spade a spade |

### Example Sentences

- Sono **al verde** questo mese; non posso uscire. (I'm broke this month; I can't go out.)
- Hai **il prosciutto sugli occhi**! Non vedi che ti sta mentendo? (You have prosciutto over your eyes! Don't you see he's lying to you?)
- Quella ragazza è **come il prezzemolo**: la trovi dappertutto! (That girl is like parsley: you find her everywhere!)
- Il tuo vicino è **buono come il pane**. (Your neighbor is good as bread -- very kind.)

## 5. Money and Work Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| Fare il portoghese | To play the Portuguese | To sneak in without paying |
| Non avere il becco di un quattrino | To not have the beak of a penny | To be completely broke |
| Costare un patrimonio | To cost a fortune | To be very expensive |
| Guadagnarsi il pane | To earn one's bread | To make a living |
| Battere la fiacca | To beat laziness | To slack off, be lazy |
| Fare un buco nell'acqua | To make a hole in water | To fail at something |
| Tirare la cinghia | To pull the belt | To tighten one's belt, economize |
| Avere l'acqua alla gola | To have water at the throat | To be in serious trouble (esp. financial) |

### Example Sentences

- Hanno provato ad entrare al cinema **facendo i portoghesi**. (They tried to sneak into the cinema without paying.)
- Dopo il licenziamento, avevano **l'acqua alla gola**. (After the layoff, they were in serious financial trouble.)
- Dobbiamo **tirare la cinghia** fino alla fine del mese. (We need to tighten our belts until the end of the month.)

> **Cultural Note**: *Fare il portoghese* supposedly originated from an 18th-century Roman story where the Portuguese ambassador invited all Portuguese residents in Rome to a free event, and many non-Portuguese pretended to be Portuguese to get in free.

## 9. Colloquial Expressions and Filler Words

### 9.1 Essential Filler Words

|-----------|----------------|---------|
| **Boh!** | I don't know / I have no idea | "Dove sono le chiavi?" -- "Boh!" |

| **Basta!** | Enough! / Stop it! | "Basta con le bugie!" |

## 10. Italian Gestures and Non-Verbal Communication

Italian communication is famously rich in gestures. These are not random; they have specific meanings understood by all Italians.

### 10.1 Essential Italian Gestures

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|          ITALIAN GESTURE GUIDE                            |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  1. "MA CHE VUOI?" (What do you want?)                  |
|     Fingers bunched together, tips pointing up,          |
|     hand moves up and down repeatedly.                   |
|     → The most iconic Italian gesture.                   |
|     → Expresses: confusion, frustration, "What?!"        |
|                                                          |
|  2. "PERFETTO" (Perfect)                                 |
|     Thumb and index finger form a circle (OK sign),      |
|     other fingers extended.                              |
|     → Means: delicious, excellent, perfect               |
|                                                          |
|  3. "NON ME NE FREGA" (I don't care)                    |
|     Flicking the chin outward with the back of           |
|     the fingers.                                         |
|     → Means: I don't care / It's not my problem          |
|                                                          |
|  4. "FURBETTO" (Sly one)                                |
|     Index finger pulling down the lower eyelid.          |
|     → Means: Be careful / Watch out / I see you          |
|                                                          |
|  5. "PAURA" (Fear)                                       |
|     Hands shaking in front of the body.                  |
|     → Means: I'm scared / It's scary / Wow               |
|                                                          |
|  6. "HO FAME" (I'm hungry)                              |
|     Hand flat, edge hitting the stomach repeatedly.      |
|     → Means: I'm hungry                                  |
|                                                          |
|  7. "ANDIAMO!" (Let's go!)                               |
|     Hand flat, palm up, waving toward oneself.           |
|     → Means: Come on / Let's go                          |
|                                                          |
|  8. "BASTA!" (Enough!)                                   |
|     Both hands palms down, pushing downward.             |
|     → Means: Stop / Enough / Calm down                   |
|                                                          |
|  9. "LE CORNA" (The horns)                               |
|     Index and pinky fingers extended, middle and         |
|     ring fingers folded (like rock horns).               |
|     → Means: Warding off bad luck (superstition)         |
|     → Also: "Your partner is cheating" (directed         |
|       at someone)                                        |
|                                                          |
|  10. "CHE BARBA!" (How boring!)                          |
|      Hand stroking an imaginary beard.                   |
|      → Means: How boring / What a drag                   |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### 10.2 Gesture Usage in Context

| Situation | Gesture | Verbal Equivalent |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Someone asks you a confusing question | Bunched fingers ("Che vuoi?") | Ma che dici? / Cosa vuoi? |
| Food is delicious | OK sign near mouth | Buonissimo! / Squisito! |
| You don't care about something | Chin flick | Non me ne frega niente. |
| Warning someone to be careful | Pull down lower eyelid | Stai attento! / Occhio! |
| Wanting someone to hurry up | Palm-up beckoning | Dai, sbrigati! / Muoviti! |
| Expressing that something is crazy | Finger rotating at temple | È matto/a! / È fuori di testa! |

### 10.3 Physical Distance and Touch

| Cultural Norm | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Greeting kisses** | Two kisses on the cheeks (right cheek first) between friends and family |
| **Personal space** | Closer than Northern European / American norms |
| **Touching while talking** | Arms, shoulders -- very common and not considered invasive |
| **Eye contact** | Direct and sustained; avoiding eye contact can seem evasive |
| **Handshakes** | Firm, with eye contact; common in professional settings |

### Exercise 5: Gesture Identification

Describe what each gesture means.

1. Someone bunches all their fingertips together pointing upward and shakes their hand.
2. A person pulls down their lower eyelid with one finger while looking at you.
3. Someone flicks their chin outward with the back of their fingers.
4. A person makes the "OK" sign (thumb and index circle) near their mouth after eating.
5. Someone extends their index and pinky fingers (like horns) behind another person's head.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **"Ma che vuoi?"** (What do you want? / What are you talking about?) -- expresses confusion or frustration
2. **"Furbetto" / "Occhio!"** -- "Watch out!" / "I see what you're doing!" / "Be careful!"
3. **"Non me ne frega niente"** -- "I don't care" / "It doesn't concern me"
4. **"Buono!" / "Perfetto!"** -- The food is delicious / excellent
5. **"Le corna"** -- Implying someone's partner is unfaithful (or warding off bad luck)

</details>

### Exercise 6: Context-Based Selection

Choose the best proverb for each situation.

1. Your friend rushes through a project and makes many errors.
   - a) Chi dorme non piglia pesci.
   - b) La gatta frettolosa fece i gattini ciechi.
   - c) L'appetito vien mangiando.

2. A colleague says they'll start a diet "tomorrow" for the hundredth time.
   - a) Tra il dire e il fare c'è di mezzo il mare.
   - b) Meglio tardi che mai.
   - c) Chi trova un amico trova un tesoro.

3. Someone receives a free but slightly damaged gift and complains.
   - a) Chi la fa l'aspetti.
   - b) A caval donato non si guarda in bocca.
   - c) Non è tutto oro quel che luccica.

4. Your alarm didn't go off and you missed an opportunity.
   - a) Piove sul bagnato.
   - b) Chi dorme non piglia pesci.
   - c) Sbagliando s'impara.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **b) La gatta frettolosa fece i gattini ciechi.** (Haste makes waste.)
2. **a) Tra il dire e il fare c'è di mezzo il mare.** (Easier said than done.)
3. **b) A caval donato non si guarda in bocca.** (Don't look a gift horse in the mouth.)
4. **b) Chi dorme non piglia pesci.** (The early bird catches the worm.)

</details>
