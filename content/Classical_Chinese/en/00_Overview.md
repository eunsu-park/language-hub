# Classical Chinese (漢文) — Overview

## About This Topic

This topic provides a comprehensive, progressive curriculum for learning Classical Chinese (漢文, 한문) — also known as Literary Sinitic (文言文, 문언문). Classical Chinese was the shared written language of educated East Asia for over two millennia. From the Analerta of Confucius to Korean royal court documents, from Japanese Buddhist texts to Vietnamese imperial decrees, Classical Chinese served as the lingua franca of written communication across China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam.

For Korean students, 한문 is not a foreign language — it is the foundation of over 60% of Korean vocabulary (한자어/漢字語). Understanding Classical Chinese unlocks the meaning behind everyday Korean words, grants access to the vast corpus of East Asian classical literature, and deepens appreciation of Korean cultural heritage.

This course covers 25 lessons organized into 5 stages, progressing from individual characters to reading original classical texts.

## Target Audience

- Korean students studying 한문 (Classical Chinese / Literary Chinese)
- Students preparing for 한자능력검정시험 (Hanja Proficiency Test)
- Anyone interested in East Asian classical literature and philosophy
- Korean language learners seeking deeper understanding of 한자어 (Sino-Korean words)

## Prerequisites

- No prior Classical Chinese knowledge required (the course begins from zero)
- Basic Korean literacy is helpful for understanding Korean readings of characters
- Familiarity with basic Korean grammar concepts aids comprehension of translation methods

## Difficulty

- **Tier**: Beginner (starts from scratch, progresses to advanced)
- **Estimated scope**: Hanja Level L5 through L1 (approximately 500 to 3,000+ characters)

## Learning Path

```
Stage 1: Character Foundations (01-05) — Hanja Level L5
  Build the basics: radicals, nature characters, people/actions, compounds, simple sentences
  ↓
Stage 2: Grammar Foundations (06-10) — Hanja Level L4
  Expand: function words, statement patterns, negation, questions, Korean reading methods
  ↓
Stage 3: Classical Texts (11-15) — Hanja Level L3
  Read originals: Analects, Mencius, historical texts, poetry, Daoist classics
  ↓
Stage 4: Advanced Reading (16-20) — Hanja Level L2
  Deepen: advanced grammar, literary techniques, Korean classical writings, Buddhist texts
  ↓
Stage 5: Mastery & Application (21-25) — Hanja Level L1
  Apply: exam preparation, classical essay writing, comparative literature, research methods
```

## Hanja Level Reference

> **Hanja Proficiency Levels (漢字能力檢定)**
>
> - **L5 (入門)**: ~500 characters, basic radicals and compounds
> - **L4 (初級)**: ~1,000 characters, grammar foundations
> - **L3 (中級)**: ~1,500 characters, classical text reading
> - **L2 (上級)**: ~2,000 characters, advanced literary analysis
> - **L1 (最上級)**: 3,000+ characters, mastery and composition
>
> Approximate mapping to Korean education:
> - **L5**: Middle school basics
> - **L4**: High school level
> - **L3**: University entrance
> - **L2**: University level
> - **L1**: Graduate/specialist level

## How to Use This Course

1. **Follow the order**: Lessons build on each other. Complete each stage before moving to the next.
2. **Do the exercises**: Every lesson includes practice exercises with answers. Work through them.
3. **Learn characters in context**: Do not memorize isolated characters. Learn them through compounds and sentences.
4. **Master radicals early**: Lesson 01 is critical. Understanding radicals accelerates all future character learning.
5. **Practice Korean reading**: 현토 (adding Korean particles to Classical Chinese) is a uniquely Korean skill. Practice it regularly.
6. **Review regularly**: Character recognition requires repetition. Use spaced repetition for vocabulary.
7. **Read aloud**: Classical Chinese has rhythm and cadence. Reading aloud helps internalization.
8. **Connect to modern Korean**: Every Classical Chinese character you learn illuminates Korean vocabulary you already know.

## Key Features of Classical Chinese

Classical Chinese has several distinctive features that differ from modern Korean and modern Chinese:

- **Monosyllabic words**: Most words are single characters — one character, one meaning
- **No inflection**: Characters do not conjugate or decline; context determines grammatical role
- **Flexible word class**: The same character can function as noun, verb, or adjective depending on position
- **Topic-prominent**: Sentences are organized around topics, not fixed subject-verb-object order
- **Economy of expression**: Classical Chinese is extremely concise — a four-character phrase can express what takes a full sentence in modern languages
- **Function words (虛詞)**: Particles like 之, 乎, 者, 也 provide grammatical structure
- **Inversion for emphasis**: Word order changes to highlight important elements
- **Parallel structure**: Balanced phrases and couplets are a hallmark of elegant classical writing
