## 1. SOV Word Order

### 1.1 The Fundamental Difference

English follows **SVO** (Subject-Verb-Object) order. Korean follows **SOV** (Subject-Object-Verb) order. The verb ALWAYS comes at the end.

> **English (SVO) vs Korean (SOV)**
>
> English: I [S] eat [V] an apple [O].
> Korean: 저는 [S] 사과를 [O] 먹어요 [V]. (I — apple — eat)
>
> English: She [S] studies [V] Korean [O].
> Korean: 그녀는 [S] 한국어를 [O] 공부해요 [V]. (She — Korean — studies)

### 1.2 Key Principles of Korean Word Order

1. **The verb is ALWAYS last.** This is the one non-negotiable rule.
2. **Other elements are flexible.** Subject, object, and adverbs can be rearranged for emphasis.
3. **Particles mark roles.** Because of particles (은/는, 이/가, 을/를, etc.), word order is flexible — the particles tell you what role each word plays, regardless of position.
4. **Subjects/objects are often omitted** when they are clear from context.

```
All of these mean "I eat an apple":

저는 사과를 먹어요.        (Standard: S-O-V)
사과를 저는 먹어요.        (Emphasis on "I": O-S-V)
사과를 먹어요.             (Subject dropped: O-V)
먹어요.                    (Both dropped: V — "eat" — context fills in)
```

### 1.3 Comparison with English Sentence Patterns

| English | Korean | Pattern |
|---------|--------|---------|
| I go. | 저는 가요. | S-V |
| I eat rice. | 저는 밥을 먹어요. | S-O-V |
| I give a friend a book. | 저는 친구에게 책을 줘요. | S-IO-O-V |
| I study Korean at school. | 저는 학교에서 한국어를 공부해요. | S-Place-O-V |

## 2. Topic Marker 은/는

### 2.1 What is a Topic?

The topic (주제, juje) is what the sentence is "about." Korean explicitly marks the topic with the particle **은/는**:

- **은** after a consonant: 학생**은** (because 생 ends in ㅇ consonant)
- **는** after a vowel: 저**는** (because 저 ends in ㅓ vowel)

> **Topic Marker 은/는**
>
> - **Rule**: Consonant ending + 은, Vowel ending + 는
>
> - **Consonant ending**: 학생 → 학생은 (student), 선생님 → 선생님은 (teacher), 한국 → 한국은 (Korea)
> - **Vowel ending**: 저 → 저는 (I), 사과 → 사과는 (apple), 커피 → 커피는 (coffee)

### 2.2 Functions of 은/는

| Function | Example | Translation |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Setting the topic | 저**는** 학생입니다. | As for me, I am a student. |
| Contrast | 커피**는** 좋아요. 차**는** 싫어요. | Coffee, I like. Tea, I dislike. |
| General statements | 한국어**는** 재미있어요. | Korean (in general) is fun. |

### 2.3 Examples

| Korean | Literal | Natural English |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| 날씨는 좋아요. | As for the weather, it is good. | The weather is nice. |
| 이것은 책입니다. | As for this thing, it is a book. | This is a book. |
| 저는 미국 사람입니다. | As for me, I am an American person. | I am American. |
| 한국 음식은 맛있어요. | As for Korean food, it is delicious. | Korean food is delicious. |

## 3. Subject Marker 이/가

### 3.1 The Subject Marker

The subject marker **이/가** identifies the subject of the sentence, often introducing **new information** or answering "who/what":

- **이** after a consonant: 학생**이** (because 생 ends in ㅇ)
- **가** after a vowel: 누구**가** (because 구 ends in ㅜ)

### 3.2 은/는 vs 이/가 — The Key Difference

> **은/는 (Topic) vs 이/가 (Subject)**
>
> 은/는 marks KNOWN information (the topic being discussed): "Speaking of X..."
>
> 이/가 marks NEW information (what's being asserted): "It is X that..."
>
> Example conversation:
> - Q: 누가 학생이에요? — "WHO is the student?"
> - A: 민수가 학생이에요. — "MINSU is the student." (new info)
>
> - Q: 민수는 뭐예요? — "What about Minsu? (What is he?)"
> - A: 민수는 학생이에요. — "Minsu is a student." (topic=Minsu)
>
> Same sentence, different particles, different nuance:
> - 민수**가** 학생이에요 = It's MINSU who is the student (focus on who)
> - 민수**는** 학생이에요 = As for Minsu, he is a student (topic already established)

### 3.3 Special Cases for 이/가

| Usage | Example | Translation |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| New information / answer to "who/what" | **누가** 왔어요? 민수**가** 왔어요. | Who came? Minsu came. |
| After 좋다/싫다 (like/dislike) | 저는 커피**가** 좋아요. | I like coffee. (Lit: Coffee is good to me.) |
| After 있다/없다 (exist/not exist) | 시간**이** 있어요. | I have time. (Lit: Time exists.) |
| After 되다 (become) | 의사**가** 되었어요. | I became a doctor. |
| Describing what just happened | 비**가** 와요. | It's raining. (Rain is coming.) |

## 4. Copula 이다/아니다

### 4.1 이다 — "to be"

The copula 이다 (ida) means "to be" and attaches directly to a noun (no space):

```
[Noun]이다  →  [Noun] is/am/are

학생이다    →  (to) be a student
한국 사람이다 → (to) be a Korean person
```

**Conjugated Forms**:

| Level | After Consonant | After Vowel | Example |
|-------|----------------|-------------|---------|
| Formal polite (합쇼체) | -입니다 | -입니다 | 학생입니다 / 의사입니다 |
| Informal polite (해요체) | -이에요 | -예요 | 학생이에요 / 의사예요 |
| Casual (해체) | -이야 | -야 | 학생이야 / 의사야 |

| Korean | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| 저는 학생입니다. | I am a student. (formal) |
| 이것은 책이에요. | This is a book. (polite) |
| 오늘은 월요일이에요. | Today is Monday. (polite) |

### 4.2 아니다 — "to not be"

아니다 (anida) is the negative copula. Unlike 이다, the noun takes the subject marker 이/가 before 아니다:

```
[Noun]이/가 아니다  →  [Noun] is NOT

학생이 아니다      →  (to) not be a student
```

**Conjugated Forms**:

| Level | Form | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| Formal polite | 아닙니다 | 학생이 아닙니다 (I am not a student) |
| Informal polite | 아니에요 | 학생이 아니에요 (I'm not a student) |
| Casual | 아니야 | 학생이 아니야 (Not a student) |

### 4.3 Summary Comparison

> **이다 vs 아니다 — Quick Reference**
>
> **Affirmative:**
> - 저는 학생입니다. — I am a student.
> - 이것은 펜이에요. — This is a pen.
>
> **Negative:**
> - 저는 학생이 아닙니다. — I am not a student.
> - 이것은 펜이 아니에요. — This is not a pen.
>
> Note the structural difference:
> - **이다**: [Noun]입니다 (noun + copula, no space)
> - **아니다**: [Noun]이/가 아닙니다 (noun + 이/가 + negative copula)

## 5. Demonstratives 이/그/저

### 5.1 The Three-Way Distinction

Korean has a three-way demonstrative system based on distance from speaker and listener:

| Demonstrative | Meaning | Distance |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| 이 (i) | This | Close to the **speaker** |
| 그 (geu) | That | Close to the **listener** / known/mentioned |
| 저 (jeo) | That (over there) | Far from **both** speaker and listener |

> **Three-Way Demonstrative System**
>
> - **이** (this) — near the speaker (near me)
> - **그** (that) — near the listener / mentioned
> - **저** (that over there) — far from both of us
>
> - 이 책 = this book (I'm holding it)
> - 그 책 = that book (you're holding / we talked about)
> - 저 책 = that book (over there on the shelf)

### 5.2 Demonstrative Series

| Prefix | + 것 (thing) | + 곳/데 (place) | + 사람 (person) | + 때 (time) |
|--------|------------|----------------|----------------|------------|
| 이 | 이것 (this thing) | 이곳/여기 (this place/here) | 이 사람 (this person) | 이때 (this time) |
| 그 | 그것 (that thing) | 그곳/거기 (that place/there) | 그 사람 (that person) | 그때 (that time) |
| 저 | 저것 (that thing over there) | 저곳/저기 (that place over there) | 저 사람 (that person over there) | — |

**Shortened forms** (very common in speech):

| Full Form | Short Form | Meaning |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| 이것 | 이거 | this (thing) |
| 그것 | 그거 | that (thing) |
| 저것 | 저거 | that (thing, over there) |
| 이것은 | 이건 | as for this |
| 그것은 | 그건 | as for that |

### 5.3 Examples

| Korean | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| 이것은 뭐예요? | What is this? |
| 그것은 가방이에요. | That is a bag. |
| 저 건물은 학교예요. | That building (over there) is a school. |
| 이 사람은 제 친구예요. | This person is my friend. |
| 그 영화는 재미있었어요. | That movie (we talked about) was fun. |

## 6. 있다/없다 — Existence and Possession

### 6.1 Two Important Verbs

**있다** (itta) and **없다** (eoptta) are among the most frequently used words in Korean. They have two main meanings:

> **있다 / 없다**
>
> **Meaning 1: EXISTENCE**
> - 있다 = to exist, there is/are
> - 없다 = to not exist, there is/are not
> - 고양이가 있어요. — There is a cat. / A cat exists.
> - 고양이가 없어요. — There is no cat.
>
> **Meaning 2: POSSESSION** (to have / not have)
> - 있다 = to have
> - 없다 = to not have
> - 저는 차가 있어요. — I have a car.
> - 저는 차가 없어요. — I don't have a car.
>
> Note: The thing that exists/is possessed takes 이/가 (not 을/를)

### 6.2 Conjugation

| Level | 있다 | 없다 |
|-------|------|------|
| Formal polite | 있습니다 | 없습니다 |
| Informal polite | 있어요 | 없어요 |
| Casual | 있어 | 없어 |

### 6.3 Location + 에 + 있다/없다

To say something is **at** a location, use the particle **에** (at/in/on):

```
[Place]에 [Thing]이/가 있어요.    There is [Thing] at [Place].
[Thing]은/는 [Place]에 있어요.    [Thing] is at [Place].
```

| Korean | Translation |
|--------|-------------|
| 책상 위에 책이 있어요. | There is a book on the desk. |
| 냉장고에 우유가 없어요. | There is no milk in the fridge. |
| 화장실은 2층에 있어요. | The bathroom is on the 2nd floor. |
| 여기에 뭐가 있어요? | What is here? |

### 6.4 Common Location Words

| Korean | English | Korean | English |
|--------|---------|--------|---------|
| 위 | above/on | 아래/밑 | below/under |
| 안 | inside | 밖 | outside |
| 앞 | front | 뒤 | behind |
| 옆 | beside/next to | 사이 | between |
| 오른쪽 | right side | 왼쪽 | left side |
| 여기 | here | 거기 | there |
| 저기 | over there | 어디 | where |

## 7. Question Words

### 7.1 Essential Question Words (의문사)

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|---------|
| 뭐 (무엇) | mwo (mueot) | what | 이것은 뭐예요? (What is this?) |
| 누구 | nugu | who | 누가 왔어요? (Who came?) |
| 어디 | eodi | where | 어디에 가요? (Where are you going?) |
| 언제 | eonje | when | 언제 와요? (When are you coming?) |
| 왜 | wae | why | 왜 안 가요? (Why aren't you going?) |
| 어떻게 | eotteoke | how | 어떻게 해요? (How do you do it?) |
| 몇 | myeot | how many / what number | 몇 시예요? (What time is it?) |
| 얼마 | eolma | how much (price) | 이것은 얼마예요? (How much is this?) |
| 어떤 | eotteon | what kind of | 어떤 음악을 좋아해요? (What kind of music do you like?) |
| 어느 | eoneu | which | 어느 나라에서 왔어요? (Which country are you from?) |

### 7.2 Special Note on 누구

When 누구 (who) is used as a subject, it becomes **누가** (not 누구가):

```
누구 + 가 → 누가    (irregular contraction)

누가 왔어요?       Who came?
누가 했어요?       Who did it?
이것은 누구의 것이에요?   Whose is this? (누구 + 의 = whose)
```

### 7.3 뭐 vs 무엇

뭐 is the casual/spoken form of 무엇:

| Context | Form | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| Spoken/casual | 뭐 | 뭐 먹어요? (What do you eat?) |
| Written/formal | 무엇 | 무엇을 하십니까? (What do you do?) |
| With particles | 무엇 / 뭐 | 뭐가 있어요? / 무엇이 있어요? (What is there?) |

## 8. Basic Question Formation

### 8.1 Making Questions in Korean

Korean question formation is remarkably simple — much simpler than English (no do/does/did, no subject-verb inversion).

> **Korean Question Formation**
>
> **Method 1**: Rising intonation (informal polite — 해요체)
> - Statement: 학생이에요.↘ (is a student)
> - Question: 학생이에요?↗ (is [someone] a student?)
> → Just raise the intonation at the end!
>
> **Method 2**: Formal question ending (합쇼체)
> - Statement: 학생입니다. (is a student)
> - Question: 학생입니까? (is [someone] a student?)
> → Change -ㅂ니다 to -ㅂ니까
>
> **Method 3**: Question word + sentence
> - 뭐예요? — What is it?
> - 어디에 가요? — Where are you going?
> - 언제 와요? — When are you coming?
> → No word order change needed — just add the question word

### 8.2 Yes/No Questions

| Statement | Question | Translation |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| 학생입니다. | 학생입니까? | Are you a student? (formal) |
| 학생이에요. | 학생이에요? | Are you a student? (polite) |
| 한국 사람이에요. | 한국 사람이에요? | Are you Korean? |
| 커피 있어요. | 커피 있어요? | Is there coffee? / Do you have coffee? |

### 8.3 Information Questions (WH-Questions)

| Korean | Translation | Pattern |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| 이것은 뭐예요? | What is this? | [Topic] + 뭐 + 이다 |
| 누가 학생이에요? | Who is the student? | 누가 + [noun] + 이다 |
| 어디에 가요? | Where are you going? | 어디 + 에 + verb |
| 언제 와요? | When are you coming? | 언제 + verb |
| 왜 안 먹어요? | Why don't you eat? | 왜 + 안 + verb |
| 어떻게 가요? | How do you go? | 어떻게 + verb |
| 몇 살이에요? | How old are you? | 몇 + counter + 이다 |
| 이거 얼마예요? | How much is this? | [thing] + 얼마 + 이다 |
