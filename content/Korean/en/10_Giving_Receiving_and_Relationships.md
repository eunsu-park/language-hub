## 1. 주다 (To Give) and 받다 (To Receive)

### 1.1 주다 — To Give

**Structure**: [Giver]가/이 [Receiver]에게/한테 [Thing]을/를 주다

| Korean | Translation |
|--------|------------|
| 친구에게 선물을 줬어요. | I gave a present to my friend. |
| 엄마가 저한테 용돈을 줬어요. | Mom gave me allowance. |
| 선생님께 꽃을 드렸어요. | I gave flowers to the teacher. (honorific) |

### 1.2 받다 — To Receive

**Structure**: [Receiver]가/이 [Giver]에게서/한테서 [Thing]을/를 받다

| Korean | Translation |
|--------|------------|
| 선물을 받았어요. | I received a present. |
| 친구한테서 편지를 받았어요. | I received a letter from my friend. |
| 부모님께 용돈을 받았어요. | I received allowance from my parents. |

### 1.3 Honorific Giving: 드리다

When giving to someone of higher status, use **드리다** instead of 주다:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              Giving: 주다 vs. 드리다                            │
│                                                               │
│  주다 — Standard "to give"                                     │
│  Used when giving to: peers, younger people, animals          │
│  친구에게 선물을 줬어요.    (I gave a friend a present.)         │
│                                                               │
│  드리다 — Humble/Honorific "to give"                           │
│  Used when giving to: elders, teachers, bosses, parents       │
│  선생님께 선물을 드렸어요.  (I gave the teacher a present.)     │
│                                                               │
│  Key: Use 드리다 when you want to show respect to the          │
│  recipient. It humbles the giver (you).                       │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Neutral | Honorific | Meaning |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 줬어요 | 드렸어요 | gave (to someone respected) |
| 주세요 | — | please give (to me) |

### 1.4 Conjugation of 주다 and 받다

| Tense | 주다 (give) | 받다 (receive) | 드리다 (give, humble) |
|-------|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| Dictionary | 주다 | 받다 | 드리다 |
| Present (해요체) | 줘요 | 받아요 | 드려요 |
| Past (해요체) | 줬어요 | 받았어요 | 드렸어요 |
| Future | 줄 거예요 | 받을 거예요 | 드릴 거예요 |
| Imperative | 주세요 | — | — |

## 2. Doing Something for Someone: -아/어 주다

The pattern **-아/어 주다** expresses doing an action **for someone's benefit**. It is one of the most useful and frequently used patterns in Korean.

### 2.1 Formation

```
Verb stem + 아/어 주다  →  "do [verb] for someone"

Apply the same vowel harmony rules:
  Last vowel ㅏ/ㅗ → 아 주다
  Other vowels → 어 주다
  하 → 해 주다
```

### 2.2 Common -아/어 주다 Expressions

| Base Verb | -아/어 주다 | Polite Request | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| 돕다 (help) | 도와주다 | 도와주세요 | Please help (me) |
| 가르치다 (teach) | 가르쳐 주다 | 가르쳐 주세요 | Please teach (me) |
| 보다 (look) | 봐 주다 | 봐 주세요 | Please look at / watch (for me) |
| 사다 (buy) | 사 주다 | 사 주세요 | Please buy (for me) |
| 만들다 (make) | 만들어 주다 | 만들어 주세요 | Please make (for me) |
| 읽다 (read) | 읽어 주다 | 읽어 주세요 | Please read (for me) |
| 열다 (open) | 열어 주다 | 열어 주세요 | Please open (for me) |
| 닫다 (close) | 닫아 주다 | 닫아 주세요 | Please close (for me) |
| 기다리다 (wait) | 기다려 주다 | 기다려 주세요 | Please wait (for me) |
| 설명하다 (explain) | 설명해 주다 | 설명해 주세요 | Please explain (for me) |

### 2.3 Examples in Context

```
사진 좀 찍어 주세요.
sajin jom jjigeo juseyo.
Could you take a photo (for me), please?

이름을 써 주세요.
ireum-eul sseo juseyo.
Please write your name (for me).

천천히 말해 주세요.
cheoncheonhi malhae juseyo.
Please speak slowly (for me).

엄마가 밥을 만들어 줬어요.
eomma-ga bab-eul mandeureo jwosseoyo.
Mom made food for me.

형이 숙제를 도와줬어요.
hyeong-i sukje-reul dowajwosseoyo.
My older brother helped me with homework.
```

### 2.4 -아/어 주다 vs. Plain Verb

| Plain | With -아/어 주다 | Difference |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| 문을 열었어요. | 문을 열어 줬어요. | Opened the door (neutral) vs. Opened the door *for someone* |
| 책을 읽었어요. | 책을 읽어 줬어요. | Read a book (neutral) vs. Read a book *to/for someone* |
| 가르쳤어요. | 가르쳐 줬어요. | Taught (neutral) vs. Taught *someone (as a favor)* |

## 3. Social Hierarchy and Relationship Terms

Korean society places great importance on social hierarchy, and the language reflects this through specialized vocabulary.

### 3.1 선배 / 후배 (Senior / Junior)

| Korean | Romanization | Meaning | Context |
|--------|-------------|---------|---------|
| 선배 | seonbae | senior | School or workplace — someone who entered before you |
| 후배 | hubae | junior | School or workplace — someone who entered after you |

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              선배 / 후배 System                                 │
│                                                               │
│  This is NOT about age — it's about when you entered          │
│  an organization (school, company, club).                     │
│                                                               │
│  Example at university:                                       │
│  2022 입학 (enrolled 2022) → 선배 to 2023/2024 students       │
│  2024 입학 (enrolled 2024) → 후배 to 2022/2023 students       │
│                                                               │
│  A 25-year-old who enrolled in 2024 would call                │
│  a 22-year-old who enrolled in 2023 "선배."                    │
│                                                               │
│  Usage: 선배(님), used as a title or term of address           │
│  "김 선배" = Senior Kim                                        │
│  후배 is used to refer to juniors, not to address them.       │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 3.2 Sibling Terms Used Socially

The sibling terms (형, 오빠, 누나, 언니) extend far beyond family:

| Term | Literal | Social Usage |
|------|---------|-------------|
| 형 (hyeong) | older brother (male speaker) | Any slightly older male friend/acquaintance (used by males) |
| 오빠 (oppa) | older brother (female speaker) | Any slightly older male friend/acquaintance (used by females) |
| 누나 (nuna) | older sister (male speaker) | Any slightly older female friend/acquaintance (used by males) |
| 언니 (eonni) | older sister (female speaker) | Any slightly older female friend/acquaintance (used by females) |
| 동생 (dongsaeng) | younger sibling | Any slightly younger friend/acquaintance (both genders) |

| Example | Context |
|---------|---------|
| 준호 형, 같이 밥 먹자. | (male to older male) Junho hyeong, let's eat together. |
| 민지 언니, 내일 시간 있어요? | (female to older female) Minji eonni, are you free tomorrow? |
| 이 사람은 제 후배 동생이에요. | This person is my junior/younger acquaintance. |

> **Note**: Using these terms implies closeness. You would not use them with strangers unless invited to. Between new acquaintances, getting permission to use 형/오빠/누나/언니 is a social milestone.

### 3.3 Other Social Terms

| Korean | Meaning | Usage |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 친구 | friend | Specifically means someone **the same age** as you |
| 아는 사람 / 지인 | acquaintance | Someone you know but aren't close to |
| 동기 | same cohort/class | Entered the same year (school, military, company) |
| 동갑 | same age | 우리 동갑이에요! (We're the same age!) |

> **Important**: In Korean, 친구 strictly means someone born in the same year. Someone a year older is not your 친구 — they are 형/오빠/누나/언니.

## 4. Age and the Korean Age System

### 4.1 Korean Age vs. International Age

Korea traditionally used a unique age system, though it officially adopted the international system (만 나이) in June 2023.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              Korean Age Systems                                │
│                                                               │
│  만 나이 (International age) — NOW OFFICIAL                    │
│  Your age based on your birthday                              │
│  Born: 2000-06-15 → In March 2026 = 25 years old             │
│                                                               │
│  한국 나이 (Korean age) — TRADITIONAL (still used casually)    │
│  You are 1 at birth, +1 every January 1st                     │
│  Born: 2000-06-15 → In March 2026 = 27 years old             │
│                                                               │
│  Formula: 한국 나이 = Current year - Birth year + 1            │
│                                                               │
│  Since June 2023, 만 나이 is used for all legal/official      │
│  purposes. But many Koreans still reference 한국 나이 in       │
│  casual conversation.                                         │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 4.2 Asking About Age

Age is extremely relevant in Korean social interactions because it determines the speech level and terms of address.

| Korean | Translation | Politeness |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| 나이가 어떻게 되세요? | How old are you? | Polite (standard) |
| 몇 살이에요? | How old are you? | Casual polite |
| 몇 년생이에요? | What year were you born? | Common among peers |
| 연세가 어떻게 되세요? | How old are you? | Very respectful (to elders) |

| Response | Translation |
|----------|------------|
| 스물다섯 살이에요. | I'm 25 years old. (native Korean number) |
| 95년생이에요. | I was born in '95. |

> **Social reality**: Koreans frequently ask age early in a relationship — it is not rude. It establishes how you address each other and which speech level to use.

### 4.3 Birth Year as Social Currency

In Korea, your **birth year (년생, nyeonsaeng)** is as important as your name when meeting new people. Two people born in the same year (동갑) can immediately use 반말 with each other after mutual agreement.

## 5. Titles and Forms of Address

### 5.1 Common Titles

| Korean | Romanization | Usage |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| -씨 (ssi) | ssi | Attached to a name. Polite but not very formal. 김민수 씨 |
| -님 (nim) | nim | Honorific suffix. Very respectful. 고객님 (customer), 사장님 (boss) |
| 선생님 | seonsaengnim | Teacher. Also a respectful address for any professional |
| [직함]님 | [title] + nim | Job title + 님: 부장님, 과장님, 교수님 |

### 5.2 How to Address People

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              Korean Forms of Address                           │
│                                                               │
│  FORMAL / WORKPLACE:                                          │
│  [Last name] + [Title]님                                      │
│  김 부장님 (Department Head Kim)                               │
│  박 교수님 (Professor Park)                                    │
│                                                               │
│  POLITE / GENERAL:                                            │
│  [Full name] + 씨                                              │
│  김민수 씨 (Mr./Ms. Kim Minsu)                                │
│  ※ Using 씨 with LAST NAME ONLY is rude.                      │
│  ✗ 김 씨 (rude)  ✓ 김민수 씨 (polite)                         │
│                                                               │
│  CASUAL / CLOSE:                                              │
│  [First name] + 아/야                                          │
│  Vowel ending: 민수야!  Consonant ending: 민정아!              │
│  Used among close same-age friends (반말 relationship)         │
│                                                               │
│  STRANGERS / SERVICE:                                         │
│  아저씨 (middle-aged man)                                      │
│  아줌마 / 이모 (middle-aged woman)                              │
│  학생 (student — used by adults to address young people)       │
│  사장님 (shop owner, regardless of actual position)            │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 5.3 -씨 Rules

| Correct | Incorrect | Why |
|---------|----------|-----|
| 김민수 씨 | 김 씨 | Using 씨 with last name only sounds dismissive |
| 민수 씨 | — | First name + 씨 is fine when close enough |

## 6. 존댓말 and 반말: Speech Level Basics

Korean has a fundamental division between formal/polite speech (존댓말) and casual speech (반말). This is not just grammar — it is a social contract.

### 6.1 Overview

| 존댓말 (jondaenmal) | 반말 (banmal) |
|--------------------|--------------|
| Polite/formal speech | Casual/informal speech |
| Used with: strangers, elders, superiors | Used with: close friends of same age, children |
| Verb ending: -아/어요, -습니다 | Verb ending: -아/어, -다 |
| Default mode for adults | Must be mutually agreed upon |

### 6.2 Quick Comparison

| 존댓말 (해요체) | 반말 (해체) | Meaning |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| 어디 가요? | 어디 가? | Where are you going? |
| 밥 먹었어요? | 밥 먹었어? | Have you eaten? |
| 이거 뭐예요? | 이거 뭐야? | What is this? |
| 좋아요. | 좋아. | It's good. |
| 고마워요. | 고마워. | Thank you. |
| 알았어요. | 알았어. | I understand. |
| 안녕하세요. | 안녕. | Hello. |

### 6.3 When to Use Which

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              When to Use 존댓말 vs. 반말                        │
│                                                               │
│  ALWAYS USE 존댓말:                                            │
│  ✓ Meeting someone for the first time                         │
│  ✓ Speaking to anyone older than you                          │
│  ✓ Speaking to teachers, bosses, customers                    │
│  ✓ Public/formal situations                                   │
│  ✓ When in doubt                                              │
│                                                               │
│  YOU MAY USE 반말:                                             │
│  ✓ With close friends born the same year (동갑)               │
│  ✓ With people younger than you (after establishing rapport)  │
│  ✓ With children                                              │
│  ✓ After the older person says "말 놓으세요" (feel free to     │
│    speak casually) or "반말해도 돼요" (you can use 반말)       │
│                                                               │
│  SWITCHING TO 반말:                                            │
│  Common phrase: "우리 말 놓을까요?"                              │
│  (Shall we speak casually to each other?)                     │
│  This is a social agreement — both parties should consent.    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 6.4 Social Consequences

- Using 반말 to an elder or stranger is **extremely rude** — comparable to a serious insult.
- Using 존댓말 to a close same-age friend feels **cold and distant**.
- When in doubt, **always default to 존댓말**. Nobody is offended by being spoken to politely.

## 7. Cultural Notes: Hierarchy in Daily Life

### 7.1 Age Determines Everything

In Korean culture, even a **one-year** age difference creates a hierarchical relationship. The older person is 형/오빠/누나/언니, and the younger person is 동생. This affects:

- **Speech level**: The younger person uses 존댓말; the older may use 반말
- **Behavior**: The younger person pours drinks, defers in decisions
- **Titles**: The younger person uses a title; the older can use the name

### 7.2 Pouring Drinks

| Rule | Details |
|------|---------|
| Pour for elders | Always pour with two hands or with one hand supporting the other arm |
| Receiving from elders | Hold your glass with two hands |
| Drinking in front of elders | Turn your head to the side — do not face them directly |
| Who pours first | The youngest person at the table pours first for the eldest |

### 7.3 The 형/동생 Dynamic

The 형 (or 오빠/누나/언니) relationship comes with responsibilities:

| Role | Expectation |
|------|-------------|
| 형/오빠/누나/언니 | Often pays for meals, gives advice, takes care of 동생 |
| 동생 | Shows respect, follows along, uses polite language |

This is not a rigid rule, but a general social expectation that shapes daily interactions.

### 7.4 Using 우리 (Our/We)

Koreans use **우리** (our) where English uses "my":

| Korean | Literal | Natural English |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| 우리 엄마 | our mom | my mom |
| 우리 나라 | our country | my country |
| 우리 학교 | our school | my school |
| 우리 집 | our house | my house |

This reflects the Korean emphasis on group identity and belonging.
