## 1. Why Learn Hanja?

Approximately **60% of Korean vocabulary** is Sino-Korean (한자어) — words derived from Chinese characters. This makes Hanja knowledge one of the most powerful tools for expanding your Korean vocabulary at the advanced level.

**Benefits of learning Hanja**:

| Benefit | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Vocabulary expansion** | Knowing 100 Hanja lets you decode thousands of compound words |
| **Reading comprehension** | Academic texts, news, and formal documents rely heavily on Sino-Korean vocabulary |
| **Word precision** | Many Korean homophones are distinguished by their Hanja origin |
| **Name reading** | Korean personal names are almost always based on Hanja |
| **TOPIK preparation** | TOPIK II tests Sino-Korean vocabulary extensively |
| **Cultural literacy** | Understanding Korean proverbs, idioms, and historical references |

**How common is Sino-Korean vocabulary?**

| Domain | Approximate % Sino-Korean |
|---|---|
| Everyday conversation | ~40% |
| Newspaper articles | ~65-70% |
| Academic writing | ~70-80% |
| Legal/government documents | ~80%+ |
| Scientific terminology | ~75-85% |

## 2. Hanja vs. Hangul: Historical Context

### Before Hangul (Before 1443)

Before King Sejong the Great invented Hangul in 1443, **all Korean writing used Classical Chinese** (한문). This meant:
- Only the educated elite (양반) could read and write
- Official documents, literature, and records were all in Chinese characters
- The spoken Korean language had no dedicated writing system

### The Invention of Hangul (1443)

King Sejong created Hangul (originally called 훈민정음, "Correct Sounds for Instructing the People") specifically so that:
- Common people could read and write
- Korean sounds could be accurately represented
- Literacy would no longer be limited to the aristocracy

> 나랏 말미 중국과 달라...
> "The language of our nation is different from that of China..."
> — Opening of 훈민정음 (Hunminjeongeum) preface

### Modern Use of Hanja

Today, Korean is written almost entirely in Hangul, but Hanja still appears in specific contexts:

| Context | How Hanja Appears | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Newspaper headlines** | Occasionally in parentheses | 경제(經濟) 성장 |
| **Academic papers** | Clarifying ambiguous terms | 사기(詐欺) vs. 사기(士氣) |
| **Legal documents** | Formal terminology | 계약서(契約書) |
| **Personal names** | Birth certificates, official records | 김민수(金敏秀) |
| **Company names** | Traditional businesses | 삼성(三星), 현대(現代) |
| **Temple/historical sites** | Signage and plaques | 불국사(佛國寺) |

## 3. How Sino-Korean Words Work

### One Hanja = One Syllable = One Meaning

Each Hanja character has:
1. A **Korean reading** (음, eum): how it is pronounced in Korean (one syllable)
2. A **meaning** (훈, hun): what the character means
3. One or more **compound words** it participates in

**Example**:
- 學 → Korean reading: **학** (hak) → Meaning: **study/learning**
- 校 → Korean reading: **교** (gyo) → Meaning: **school**
- Combined: **학교** (hakgyo) = school (study + school)

### Compound Formation Patterns

Most Sino-Korean words are **two-character compounds**:

| Pattern | Structure | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Modifier + Noun** | Adjective-like + Noun-like | 大學 → 대학 (big + study = university) |
| **Noun + Noun** | Two nouns combined | 山水 → 산수 (mountain + water = landscape/arithmetic) |
| **Verb + Object** | Action + Target | 讀書 → 독서 (read + book = reading) |
| **Noun + Verb** | Subject + Action | 地震 → 지진 (earth + shake = earthquake) |
| **Prefix + Root** | Modifier prefix | 不可 → 불가 (not + possible = impossible) |

### The Power of Hanja: One Root, Many Words

Knowing a single Hanja root unlocks many compound words:

**學 (학, study/learning)**:
| Word | Hanja | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 학교 | 學校 | school |
| 학생 | 學生 | student |
| 학문 | 學問 | scholarship/academics |
| 대학 | 大學 | university |
| 과학 | 科學 | science |
| 수학 | 數學 | mathematics |
| 문학 | 文學 | literature |
| 의학 | 醫學 | medicine (study) |
| 독학 | 獨學 | self-study |
| 유학 | 留學 | studying abroad |

## 6. Hanja-Hangul Synonym Pairs

Korean often has two words for the same concept: a native Korean (순우리말) word and a Sino-Korean (한자어) word. The Sino-Korean version is usually more formal.

| Sino-Korean (Formal) | Native Korean (Casual) | English |
|---|---|---|
| 식사(食事) | 밥 | meal |
| 부모(父母) | 어버이 | parents |
| 자녀(子女) | 아이 | children |
| 주택(住宅) | 집 | house |
| 서적(書籍) | 책 | book |
| 의복(衣服) | 옷 | clothing |
| 안면(顔面) | 얼굴 | face |
| 치아(齒牙) | 이 | tooth/teeth |
| 수면(睡眠) | 잠 | sleep |
| 음식(飮食) | 먹을 것 | food |
| 언어(言語) | 말 | language/speech |
| 세계(世界) | 온 누리 | world |
| 부인(夫人) | 아내 | wife |
| 자동차(自動車) | (none — Hanja only) | car |
| 전화(電話) | (none — Hanja only) | telephone |

> **Usage note**: In formal writing, speeches, and news, Sino-Korean words are preferred. In casual conversation, native Korean words are more natural. Many modern concepts (전화, 컴퓨터, 자동차) only have Sino-Korean or loanword forms.

### Register Differences in Practice

| Context | Sentence |
|---|---|
| Casual | 아이가 아파서 **병원**에 갔어요. (My kid was sick so we went to the hospital.) |
| Formal | **자녀**가 **질환**이 있어 **의료** 기관을 방문하였습니다. (The child had an illness and visited a medical institution.) |
| Casual | **밥** 먹었어? (Did you eat?) |
| Formal | **식사**하셨습니까? (Have you had your meal?) |

## 7. Korean Personal Names and Hanja

Almost all Korean personal names are based on Hanja. Understanding this system helps you appreciate the meaning behind names and read formal documents.

### Structure of Korean Names

Korean names follow the pattern: **성(姓) + 이름(名)**

- **성 (Family name)**: Usually 1 syllable (1 Hanja character)
- **이름 (Given name)**: Usually 2 syllables (2 Hanja characters)

### Most Common Korean Family Names

| Hangul | Hanja | Romanization | Approximate % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 김 | 金 | Kim | ~21% |
| 이 | 李 | Lee/Yi | ~15% |
| 박 | 朴 | Park | ~8% |
| 최 | 崔 | Choi | ~5% |
| 정 | 鄭 | Jeong/Jung | ~4% |
| 강 | 姜 | Kang | ~2% |
| 조 | 趙 | Jo/Cho | ~2% |
| 윤 | 尹 | Yoon | ~2% |
| 장 | 張 | Jang/Chang | ~2% |
| 임 | 林 | Im/Lim | ~2% |

> About **63% of Koreans** share just the top 5 surnames.

| 민 | 敏/民 | clever/people | 민수, 민지, 민호 |
| 수 | 秀/洙 | excellent/water | 민수, 수진, 영수 |
| 지 | 智/志 | wisdom/will | 민지, 지연, 지훈 |

| 준 | 俊/準 | handsome/standard | 준호, 준서, 준영 |
| 성 | 成/聖 | achieve/holy | 성민, 성호, 성진 |
| 은 | 恩/銀 | grace/silver | 은지, 은서, 은혜 |
| 서 | 瑞/書 | auspicious/book | 서연, 서준, 서영 |
| 하 | 夏/河 | summer/river | 하늘, 하영, 하준 |

### Generational Names (돌림자)

Traditionally, Korean families assign one Hanja character that is shared among all members of the same generation:

```
Grandfather's generation: 鍾 (종) — 김종수, 김종호, 김종민
Father's generation:      英 (영) — 김영수, 김영호, 김영민
My generation:            民 (민) — 김민수, 김민호, 김민지
```

> **Modern trend**: This tradition is becoming less common. Many modern Korean parents choose names for meaning and sound without following generational naming conventions.

### Reading Names on Official Documents

On Korean official documents (주민등록증, certificates), names often appear with Hanja in parentheses:

```
성명: 김민수(金敏秀)
Name: Kim Minsu (金 = Gold, 敏 = Clever, 秀 = Excellent)
```

2. 人: **인**간(human), **인**구(population), 한국**인**(Korean person), 외국**인**(foreigner), 개**인**(individual)
3. 國: 한**국**(Korea), 외**국**(foreign country), **국**민(citizen), **국**내(domestic), **국**제(international)
4. 大: **대**학(university), **대**통령(president), **대**기업(conglomerate), **대**회(competition), 최**대**(maximum)

</details>

Break each word into its Hanja components and explain the meaning.

| Word | Hanja 1 | Hanja 2 | Combined Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. 도서관 | 圖(도, picture) | 書(서, book) + 館(관, building) | ? |
| 2. 입학 | ? | ? | ? |

| 5. 자동차 | ? | ? + ? | ? |

<summary>Answers</summary>

### Exercise 5: Context-Based Hanja Decoding

Use your knowledge of Hanja to guess the meaning of these words you may not have seen before.

1. 무인(無人) — 無 means "without/none"
2. 복학(復學) — 復 means "return/restore"
3. 화산(火山) — You know both characters
4. 해외(海外) — 海 means "sea/ocean"
5. 야간(夜間) — 夜 means "night"

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. 무인 = without + person = unmanned (무인 자동차 = unmanned/self-driving car)
2. 복학 = return + study = returning to school (after leave of absence)
3. 화산 = fire + mountain = volcano
4. 해외 = sea + outside = overseas/abroad

### Exercise 6: Homophone Disambiguation

Korean has many homophones. Use Hanja to distinguish these pairs.

1. 사기(詐欺) vs. 사기(士氣)
2. 전기(電氣) vs. 전기(傳記)
3. 기사(記事) vs. 기사(騎士) vs. 기사(技師)
4. 수도(首都) vs. 수도(水道)

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. 사기(詐欺) = fraud/scam (deceive + cheat) vs. 사기(士氣) = morale (soldier + spirit)
2. 전기(電氣) = electricity (electric + energy) vs. 전기(傳記) = biography (transmit + record)
3. 기사(記事) = news article (record + matter) vs. 기사(騎士) = knight (ride + warrior) vs. 기사(技師) = engineer/technician (skill + master)
4. 수도(首都) = capital city (head + city) vs. 수도(水道) = waterworks/plumbing (water + way)

</details>

Given one word, write 3 more words that share the same Hanja root.

3. 생활(生活) — 生(생, life): _____, _____, _____

<details>

## Key Takeaways

1. **~60% of Korean vocabulary is Sino-Korean** — Hanja knowledge dramatically accelerates vocabulary building
2. **One Hanja = one syllable = one meaning** — compound words combine these building blocks logically
3. **100 core Hanja characters** can unlock thousands of Korean words through compound formation
4. **Hanja-Hangul pairs** exist: formal Sino-Korean vs. casual native Korean (식사 vs. 밥)
5. **Korean names** are almost always Hanja-based — understanding Hanja lets you read the meaning behind names
6. **Homophones** are disambiguated by Hanja — this is why Hanja sometimes appears in parentheses in texts
