# Korean (한국어) — Overview

## About This Topic

This topic provides a comprehensive, progressive curriculum for learning Korean from scratch to an advanced level. The 25 lessons are organized into 5 stages that build upon each other, moving from foundational concepts through practical real-world application.

Korean is spoken by approximately 80 million people across South Korea, North Korea, and diaspora communities worldwide. It features Hangul, a systematic alphabet invented in 1443 by King Sejong the Great, widely praised as one of the most logical writing systems ever created. Korean has an agglutinative grammar with a rich system of speech levels, particles (postpositions), and Sino-Korean vocabulary derived from Chinese characters. Korean is essential for engaging with Korea's culture, technology, entertainment (K-drama, K-pop), and rapidly growing global influence.

## Prerequisites

- No prior Korean knowledge required (the course begins from zero)
- Basic understanding of grammar concepts in your native language is helpful

## Difficulty

- **Tier**: Beginner (starts from scratch, progresses to advanced)
- **Estimated scope**: TOPIK I-1 through TOPIK II-5/6 levels (A1 through C1/C2 CEFR equivalent)

## Learning Path

```
Stage 1: Foundations (01-05) — TOPIK I-1
  Build the basics: Hangul, greetings, sentence structure, numbers, basic verbs
  ↓
Stage 2: Elementary (06-10) — TOPIK I-2
  Expand: adjectives, particles, verb endings, daily vocabulary, social expressions
  ↓
Stage 3: Intermediate (11-15) — TOPIK II-3
  Deepen: irregular verbs, connective endings, indirect speech, honorifics, complex sentences
  ↓
Stage 4: Advanced (16-20) — TOPIK II-4
  Refine: advanced grammar, reading comprehension, Hanja, idiomatic expressions, thematic vocabulary
  ↓
Stage 5: Practical Application (21-25) — TOPIK II-5/6
  Apply: business Korean, writing, Korean culture, media language, TOPIK exam preparation
```

## How to Use This Course

1. **Follow the order**: Lessons build on each other. Complete each stage before moving to the next.
2. **Do the exercises**: Every lesson includes practice exercises with answers. Work through them.
3. **Learn Hangul first**: Lesson 01 is critical. Master Hangul before proceeding — it can be learned in a few hours.
4. **Review regularly**: Language learning requires repetition. Review previous lessons periodically.
5. **Practice writing**: Don't just read — write out Hangul by hand. Muscle memory matters for stroke order.
6. **Avoid romanization as a crutch**: Romanization is provided in early lessons to help, but transition to reading Hangul as soon as possible.
7. **Reference lessons**: Lessons 05 (verbs), 14 (honorifics), and 18 (Hanja) serve as useful references to return to.

## Key Features of Korean

Korean has several distinctive features that differ from English and other languages:

- **Hangul (한글)**: A systematic, scientifically designed alphabet invented in 1443 by King Sejong the Great — 14 consonants and 10 vowels organized into syllable blocks
- **SOV word order**: Subject-Object-Verb, the opposite of English SVO
- **Agglutinative morphology**: Verb stems stack multiple suffixes to express tense, mood, politeness, and more
- **Particles (postpositions)**: Markers (은/는, 이/가, 을/를, 에, 에서, etc.) indicate grammatical roles, appearing after the word they modify
- **Verb-final**: The verb always comes at the end of the sentence
- **No articles or grammatical gender**: Korean lacks "a/the" and does not mark gender
- **Speech levels (존댓말 / 반말)**: 7 levels of politeness deeply embedded in verb endings and vocabulary
- **Topic-prominent**: Sentences are organized around a topic (marked by 은/는), not just a subject
- **Omission of the obvious**: Subjects and objects are freely dropped when contextually clear
- **Sino-Korean vocabulary**: Approximately 60% of Korean vocabulary derives from Chinese characters (Hanja), enabling systematic word-building
