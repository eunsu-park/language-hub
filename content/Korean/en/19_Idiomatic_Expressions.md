## 1. Body-Part Idioms (신체 관용어)

Korean is extraordinarily rich in idioms based on body parts. These expressions are used constantly in everyday conversation and appear frequently on TOPIK II.

### 손 (Hand) Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **손이 크다** | Hands are big | To be generous (especially with food) | 우리 어머니는 **손이 커서** 음식을 항상 많이 만들어요. (My mother is generous — she always makes a lot of food.) |
| **손이 빠르다** | Hands are fast | To be quick/efficient at work | 그 사람은 **손이 빨라서** 일을 금방 끝내요. (That person is quick with their hands, so they finish work fast.) |
| **손을 놓다** | To let go of hands | To stop doing something / neglect | 공부에서 **손을 놓으면** 따라가기 어려워요. (If you stop studying, it's hard to catch up.) |
| **손을 대다** | To put hands on | To start working on / to touch | 이 일에 **손을 대지** 마세요. (Don't touch/get involved in this matter.) |
| **손을 쓰다** | To use hands | To take measures / pull strings | 미리 **손을 써서** 표를 구했어요. (I pulled strings in advance and got tickets.) |

### 발 (Foot) Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **발이 넓다** | Feet are wide | To know many people / well-connected | 그 사람은 **발이 넓어서** 어디에서든 아는 사람이 있어요. (He knows people everywhere — he's well-connected.) |
| **발이 묶이다** | Feet are tied | To be stuck / unable to go | 폭설 때문에 **발이 묶였어요**. (I was stuck because of the heavy snow.) |
| **발을 빼다** | To pull feet out | To withdraw / back out | 문제가 생기자 **발을 뺐어요**. (When problems arose, he backed out.) |
| **발을 끊다** | To cut off feet | To stop visiting / sever ties | 그 식당에 **발을 끊었어요**. (I stopped going to that restaurant.) |

### 눈 (Eye) Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **눈이 높다** | Eyes are high | To have high standards | **눈이 높아서** 마음에 드는 사람이 없대요. (Her standards are so high that she can't find anyone she likes.) |
| **눈이 낮다** | Eyes are low | To have low standards | (Opposite of 눈이 높다 — less common but understood) |
| **눈에 띄다** | To catch the eye | To stand out / be noticeable | 빨간색 옷이 **눈에 띄었어요**. (The red outfit stood out.) |
| **눈 밖에 나다** | To go outside the eyes | To fall out of favor | 상사의 **눈 밖에 나면** 힘들어요. (If you fall out of your boss's favor, it's tough.) |
| **눈을 감다** | To close eyes | To pass away (euphemism) / to overlook | 이번만 **눈을 감아** 주세요. (Please overlook it just this once.) |
| **눈치가 빠르다** | 눈치 (sense) is fast | To be perceptive / read the room well | **눈치가 빨라서** 분위기를 금방 파악해요. (She's perceptive and quickly reads the atmosphere.) |

### 귀 (Ear) Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **귀가 얇다** | Ears are thin | Easily influenced by others | **귀가 얇아서** 남의 말을 다 믿어요. (She's easily influenced — she believes everything people say.) |
| **귀를 기울이다** | To tilt ears | To listen carefully / pay attention | 전문가의 조언에 **귀를 기울여야** 해요. (You should pay careful attention to the expert's advice.) |
| **귀에 못이 박히다** | Nails driven into ears | To hear something so often you're sick of it | 어머니 잔소리가 **귀에 못이 박히도록** 들었어요. (I've heard my mother's nagging so much I'm sick of it.) |

### 입 (Mouth) Idioms

| Idiom | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **입이 무겁다** | Mouth is heavy | Can keep secrets | 그 사람은 **입이 무거워서** 비밀을 잘 지켜요. (That person can keep secrets — their mouth is heavy.) |
| **입이 가볍다** | Mouth is light | Cannot keep secrets | **입이 가벼운** 사람에게 비밀을 말하면 안 돼요. (You shouldn't tell secrets to someone who can't keep them.) |
| **입이 짧다** | Mouth is short | To be a picky eater | 아이가 **입이 짧아서** 먹는 것이 별로 없어요. (The child is a picky eater.) |
| **입을 모으다** | To gather mouths | To speak in unison / agree | 모두가 **입을 모아** 칭찬했어요. (Everyone praised in unison.) |

### Other Body-Part Idioms

| Idiom | Literal | Actual Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **코가 높다** | Nose is high | To be arrogant/proud | **코가 높은** 사람은 친구가 별로 없어요. (Arrogant people don't have many friends.) |
| **머리가 좋다** | Head is good | To be smart | 그 학생은 **머리가 좋아서** 뭐든 빨리 배워요. (That student is smart — learns everything quickly.) |
| **간이 크다** | Liver is big | To be bold/audacious | 사장님 앞에서 그런 말을 하다니, **간이 크네요**. (Saying that in front of the boss — you're bold.) |
| **얼굴이 두껍다** | Face is thick | To be shameless | 실수를 하고도 사과 안 하다니 **얼굴이 두껍다**. (Making a mistake and not apologizing — how shameless.) |
| **뼈가 빠지게** | Until bones come out | To work extremely hard | **뼈가 빠지게** 일했어요. (I worked my bones off.) |
| **배가 아프다** | Stomach hurts | To be jealous/envious | 친구가 성공하니까 **배가 아파요**. (I'm jealous because my friend succeeded.) |

## 2. Proverbs (속담)

Korean proverbs (속담) carry centuries of folk wisdom. They appear in everyday conversation, TOPIK tests, and written texts. Here are the 20 most essential proverbs.

### Top 20 Korean Proverbs

| # | Proverb | Literal Meaning | Actual Meaning | English Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **소 잃고 외양간 고친다** | Fix the barn after losing the cow | Acting too late — preventing damage after it's done | Closing the barn door after the horse has bolted |
| 2 | **가는 말이 고와야 오는 말이 곱다** | Outgoing words must be nice for incoming words to be nice | Speak kindly to be spoken to kindly | Do unto others as you would have them do unto you |
| 3 | **원숭이도 나무에서 떨어진다** | Even monkeys fall from trees | Even experts make mistakes | Even Homer nods |
| 4 | **세 살 버릇 여든까지 간다** | Habits from age 3 last to age 80 | Early habits die hard | Old habits die hard |
| 5 | **낮말은 새가 듣고 밤말은 쥐가 듣다** | Day words are heard by birds, night words by mice | Be careful what you say — someone is always listening | Walls have ears |
| 6 | **등잔 밑이 어둡다** | It's dark under the lamp | You overlook what's nearest to you | You can't see the forest for the trees |
| 7 | **하늘의 별 따기** | Picking stars from the sky | Something extremely difficult/impossible | Looking for a needle in a haystack |
| 8 | **누워서 떡 먹기** | Eating rice cakes lying down | Something very easy | A piece of cake |
| 9 | **백지장도 맞들면 낫다** | Even a sheet of paper is lighter when lifted together | Working together is better | Two heads are better than one |
| 10 | **돌다리도 두드려 보고 건너라** | Tap even a stone bridge before crossing | Be cautious — check everything first | Look before you leap |
| 11 | **호랑이도 제 말 하면 온다** | Even a tiger comes when you talk about it | Speak of the devil and he shall appear | Speak of the devil |
| 12 | **빈 수레가 요란하다** | An empty cart is noisy | People with less knowledge talk more | Empty vessels make the most noise |
| 13 | **고생 끝에 낙이 온다** | Joy comes at the end of suffering | Good things come after hardship | Every cloud has a silver lining |
| 14 | **꿩 먹고 알 먹고** | Eat the pheasant and eat its eggs too | Getting double benefit from one action | Kill two birds with one stone |
| 15 | **콩 심은 데 콩 나고 팥 심은 데 팥 난다** | Plant beans, get beans; plant red beans, get red beans | You reap what you sow | You reap what you sow |
| 16 | **미꾸라지 한 마리가 온 웅덩이를 흐린다** | One loach muddies the whole pond | One bad person ruins the whole group | One bad apple spoils the barrel |
| 17 | **가려운 곳을 긁어 주다** | Scratch where it itches | To do exactly what someone needs | Hit the nail on the head |
| 18 | **우물 안 개구리** | A frog in a well | Someone with a narrow worldview | A frog in a well (big fish in a small pond) |
| 19 | **시작이 반이다** | Starting is half | Getting started is the hardest part | Well begun is half done |
| 20 | **바늘 도둑이 소 도둑 된다** | A needle thief becomes a cow thief | Small wrongs lead to big ones | Give an inch and they'll take a mile |

### Using Proverbs in Sentences

- 일을 다 망치고 나서야 대책을 세우다니, **소 잃고 외양간 고치는** 격이에요. (Coming up with a plan only after messing everything up is like fixing the barn after losing the cow.)
- 시험 떨어졌다고 너무 슬퍼하지 마. **원숭이도 나무에서 떨어진다**잖아. (Don't be too sad about failing the exam. Even monkeys fall from trees.)
- **시작이 반이에요**. 일단 시작해 보세요! (Starting is half the battle. Just give it a try!)

## 3. Four-Character Idioms (사자성어)

Four-character idioms (사자성어, literally "four-character mature words") are borrowed from Classical Chinese and are widely used in Korean formal speech, writing, and proverbs. Learning these demonstrates advanced literacy.

### Essential 사자성어

| 사자성어 | Hanja | Korean Reading | Meaning | Usage Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **일석이조** | 一石二鳥 | 일석이조 | Kill two birds with one stone | 운동도 하고 친구도 만나고, **일석이조**네요! (Exercise and meet friends — two birds with one stone!) |
| **자화자찬** | 自畫自讚 | 자화자찬 | Self-praise (lit. paint yourself and praise yourself) | 자기가 잘했다고 계속 말하면 **자화자찬**이에요. (Constantly saying you did well is self-praise.) |
| **일취월장** | 日就月將 | 일취월장 | Rapid progress (lit. daily achievement, monthly advancement) | 한국어 실력이 **일취월장**하고 있어요. (Your Korean skills are improving rapidly.) |
| **전화위복** | 轉禍爲福 | 전화위복 | Turning misfortune into blessing | 실직한 것이 오히려 **전화위복**이 됐어요. (Losing my job actually turned into a blessing in disguise.) |
| **유비무환** | 有備無患 | 유비무환 | Preparedness prevents trouble | **유비무환**이라고 미리 준비하는 게 좋아요. (As they say, preparedness prevents trouble — it's best to prepare in advance.) |
| **시행착오** | 試行錯誤 | 시행착오 | Trial and error | 많은 **시행착오** 끝에 성공했어요. (I succeeded after much trial and error.) |
| **동고동락** | 同苦同樂 | 동고동락 | Sharing hardship and joy together | 10년 동안 **동고동락**한 친구예요. (We're friends who've shared hardship and joy for 10 years.) |
| **다다익선** | 多多益善 | 다다익선 | The more the better | 참가자가 많으면 좋아요. **다다익선**이에요. (The more participants, the better.) |
| **이심전심** | 以心傳心 | 이심전심 | Heart-to-heart communication (telepathy) | 오래된 친구라 **이심전심**으로 통해요. (We've been friends so long we communicate without words.) |
| **자업자득** | 自業自得 | 자업자득 | Reaping what you sow (self-inflicted) | 공부 안 하고 시험 떨어진 건 **자업자득**이에요. (Failing the exam without studying is your own doing.) |
| **우왕좌왕** | 右往左往 | 우왕좌왕 | Confused, not knowing what to do | 갑자기 비가 와서 사람들이 **우왕좌왕**했어요. (When it suddenly rained, people were running around in confusion.) |
| **삼삼오오** | 三三五五 | 삼삼오오 | In small groups (threes and fives) | 학생들이 **삼삼오오** 모여서 이야기하고 있어요. (Students are gathered in small groups chatting.) |
| **대기만성** | 大器晩成 | 대기만성 | Great talents mature late | 아직 젊으니까 걱정 마세요. **대기만성**이에요. (You're still young, don't worry. Great talents bloom late.) |
| **좌지우지** | 左之右之 | 좌지우지 | To control/manipulate at will | 한 사람이 회의를 **좌지우지**했어요. (One person controlled the meeting.) |
| **고진감래** | 苦盡甘來 | 고진감래 | Sweetness comes after bitterness (= 고생 끝에 낙이 온다) | 힘들었지만 결국 성공했어요. **고진감래**예요. (It was hard but I finally succeeded. Sweet comes after bitter.) |

## 4. Onomatopoeia and Mimetic Words (의성어/의태어)

Korean has one of the richest systems of sound-symbolic words in the world. These words are divided into two categories:

- **의성어 (Onomatopoeia)**: Words that imitate sounds
- **의태어 (Mimetic words / Ideophones)**: Words that represent actions, states, or appearances (not sounds)

### 4.1 Common Onomatopoeia (의성어)

| Korean | Sound | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **멍멍** | Dog barking (woof woof) | 개가 **멍멍** 짖었어요 | The dog barked woof woof. |
| **야옹** | Cat meowing | 고양이가 **야옹** 울었어요 | The cat meowed. |
| **꼬끼오** | Rooster crowing | 수탉이 **꼬끼오** 울었어요 | The rooster crowed. |
| **엉엉** | Loud crying (wailing) | 아이가 **엉엉** 울었어요 | The child cried loudly. |
| **훌쩍훌쩍** | Sniffling / sobbing | **훌쩍훌쩍** 울고 있었어요 | They were sobbing. |
| **깔깔** | Laughing loudly (hahaha) | **깔깔** 웃었어요 | They laughed loudly. |
| **킥킥** | Giggling/snickering | **킥킥** 웃었어요 | They giggled. |
| **두근두근** | Heart pounding (thump thump) | 심장이 **두근두근** 뛰었어요 | My heart was pounding. |
| **쿵쿵** | Heavy thudding | 문을 **쿵쿵** 두드렸어요 | They banged on the door. |
| **째깍째깍** | Ticking (clock) | 시계가 **째깍째깍** 소리를 내요 | The clock is ticking. |
| **보글보글** | Bubbling / boiling | 찌개가 **보글보글** 끓고 있어요 | The stew is bubbling away. |
| **쏴아** | Rushing water / pouring rain | 비가 **쏴아** 내렸어요 | Rain poured down. |
| **바스락** | Rustling | 낙엽이 **바스락** 소리를 냈어요 | The fallen leaves rustled. |

### 4.2 Common Mimetic Words (의태어)

| Korean | Appearance/Action | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **반짝반짝** | Sparkling/twinkling | 별이 **반짝반짝** 빛나요 | The stars are twinkling. |
| **빙글빙글** | Spinning round and round | 팽이가 **빙글빙글** 돌아요 | The top is spinning round and round. |
| **살살** | Gently / softly | 아기를 **살살** 안았어요 | I held the baby gently. |
| **꾸벅꾸벅** | Nodding off | 수업 중에 **꾸벅꾸벅** 졸았어요 | I nodded off during class. |
| **깜짝** | Startled / surprised | **깜짝** 놀랐어요! | I was startled! |
| **헐레벌떡** | In a hurry / panting | **헐레벌떡** 뛰어왔어요 | They came running in a hurry. |
| **느릿느릿** | Slowly / sluggishly | 거북이가 **느릿느릿** 걸어요 | The turtle walks slowly. |
| **후다닥** | Quickly / in a flash | **후다닥** 도망갔어요 | They ran away in a flash. |
| **두리번두리번** | Looking around nervously | **두리번두리번** 주위를 살폈어요 | They looked around nervously. |
| **빈둥빈둥** | Idling / being lazy | 하루 종일 **빈둥빈둥** 놀았어요 | I lazed around all day. |
| **뚝뚝** | Dripping | 눈물이 **뚝뚝** 떨어졌어요 | Tears fell drop by drop. |
| **말랑말랑** | Soft / squishy | 떡이 **말랑말랑**해요 | The rice cake is soft and squishy. |

### 4.3 Sound Symbolism Patterns

Korean onomatopoeia and mimetic words follow systematic vowel and consonant patterns that convey meaning through sound.

#### Vowel Alternation: ㅏ/ㅗ (Bright) vs. ㅓ/ㅜ (Dark)

| Bright (ㅏ/ㅗ) | Dark (ㅓ/ㅜ) | Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **반짝반짝** (sparkling — small, bright) | **번쩍번쩍** (flashing — big, intense) | ㅏ = small/light, ㅓ = big/heavy |
| **살살** (gently — light touch) | **설설** (less common variant) | ㅏ = delicate, ㅓ = heavier |
| **깡충깡충** (hopping — small animal) | **껑충껑충** (leaping — big jumps) | ㅏ = small, ㅓ = large |
| **달랑달랑** (dangling — small, light object) | **덜렁덜렁** (dangling — bigger, heavier) | ㅏ = small, ㅓ = large |
| **졸졸** (trickling — small stream) | **줄줄** (streaming — bigger flow) | ㅗ = small, ㅜ = large |

#### Consonant Tensing: Plain → Tense → Aspirated

| Plain | Tense (된소리) | Aspirated | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **반짝** (sparkle) | **빤짝** (— rare) | **판짝** (— rare) | Tense = more intense |
| **보글보글** (bubbling) | **뽀글뽀글** (cutely bubbling) | — | Tense = cuter/more intense |
| **두근두근** (thump thump) | **뚜근뚜근** (— rare) | — | Standard form most common |
| **살살** (gently) | **쌀쌀** (chilly — different meaning!) | — | Tensing can change meaning |

#### Doubling (반복)

Most Korean sound-symbolic words are doubled (reduplicated) to indicate continuation or repetition:

| Single | Doubled | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 반짝 | **반짝반짝** | continuous sparkling |
| 두근 | **두근두근** | continuous pounding |
| 깜짝 | (not doubled — single sudden event) | one-time surprise |
| 빙글 | **빙글빙글** | continuous spinning |

1. 소 잃고 **외양간** 고친다
2. **원숭이**도 나무에서 떨어진다
3. 낮말은 새가 듣고 **밤말은 쥐가 듣는다**
4. 시작이 **반이다**
5. 빈 수레가 **요란하다**

</details>

### Exercise 3: 사자성어 Application

Choose the correct 사자성어 for each situation.

Options: 일석이조, 자업자득, 시행착오, 이심전심, 전화위복

1. 회사에서 잘리고 나서 자기 사업을 시작했는데 대성공했어요. → _______
2. 공부 안 하고 시험에 떨어졌어요. → _______
3. 운동도 하고 살도 빠지고! → _______
4. 오래된 부부라서 말 안 해도 서로의 마음을 알아요. → _______
5. 많은 실패를 겪으면서 결국 좋은 방법을 찾았어요. → _______

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **전화위복** (turning misfortune into blessing — fired but then business success)
2. **자업자득** (reaping what you sow — didn't study, failed)
3. **일석이조** (two birds with one stone — exercise and weight loss)
4. **이심전심** (heart-to-heart — long-married couple understands each other)
5. **시행착오** (trial and error — many failures before finding the right method)

</details>

### Exercise 5: Sound Symbolism — Vowel Alternation

Choose the correct form based on the description.

1. A small kitten jumping lightly: **깡충깡충** or **껑충껑충**?
2. A bright, small light flickering: **반짝반짝** or **번쩍번쩍**?
3. A large waterfall streaming heavily: **졸졸** or **줄줄**?
4. A small bell dangling: **달랑달랑** or **덜렁덜렁**?

<summary>Answers</summary>

2. **반짝반짝** (ㅏ vowel = small, bright light)
3. **줄줄** (ㅜ vowel = large, heavy flow)
4. **달랑달랑** (ㅏ vowel = small, light object)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Body-part idioms** are used daily in Korean — 손(hand), 발(foot), 눈(eye), 귀(ear), 입(mouth) idioms are the most common
2. **Proverbs (속담)** carry cultural wisdom and appear on TOPIK — learn the top 20 for both comprehension and expression
3. **Four-character idioms (사자성어)** demonstrate advanced literacy — they come from Classical Chinese and are used in formal speech and writing
4. **Onomatopoeia (의성어) and mimetic words (의태어)** are uniquely rich in Korean — understanding vowel alternation (ㅏ vs. ㅓ) and doubling patterns is key
5. These expressions make your Korean sound **natural and culturally fluent** — textbook grammar alone cannot achieve this
