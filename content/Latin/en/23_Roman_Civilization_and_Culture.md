## 1. Government and Politics

### 1.1 Three Eras of Roman Government

| Era | Period | Head of State | Key Feature |
|-----|--------|---------------|-------------|
| **Regnum** (Kingdom) | 753–509 BC | *Rex* (King) | 7 legendary kings |
| **Res Publica** (Republic) | 509–27 BC | *Consules* (2 Consuls) | Elected magistrates, Senate |
| **Imperium** (Empire) | 27 BC–AD 476 | *Princeps / Imperator* | Autocratic rule behind republican forms |

### 1.2 Magistracies and the Cursus Honorum

The *cursus honorum* ("course of offices") was the sequential order of public offices held by aspiring Roman politicians:

| Office | Latin | Minimum Age | Number | Duties |
|--------|-------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Quaestor | *quaestor* | 30 (Sulla) | 20 | Financial administration, treasury |
| Aedile | *aedilis* | 36 | 4 | Public works, games, markets |
| Praetor | *praetor* | 39 | 8 | Justice, provincial governance |
| Consul | *consul* | 42 | 2 | Supreme executive, military command |
| Censor | *censor* | — | 2 | Census, public morals, Senate roll |

**Special magistracies:**

| Office | Latin | Purpose |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Tribune of the Plebs | *tribunus plebis* | Protect plebeians, veto power (*intercessio*) |
| Dictator | *dictator* | Emergency sole ruler (6-month term) |
| Master of the Horse | *magister equitum* | Dictator's deputy |

### 1.3 The Senate

The *senatus* (from *senex*, "old man") was the advisory body of elder statesmen:

- **Membership**: ~300 (expanded to 600 under Sulla, 900 under Caesar)
- **Authority**: *auctoritas* — moral authority rather than legal power
- **Decrees**: *senatus consultum* (SC) — technically advisory, practically binding
- **Presiding officer**: Consul (or praetor in consul's absence)
- **Famous phrase**: *Senatus Populusque Romanus* (SPQR) — "The Senate and the Roman People"

### 1.4 Key Political Vocabulary

| Latin | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| *comitia* | assemblies (voting bodies) |
| *suffragium* | vote |
| *lex, legis* (f.) | law |
| *ius, iuris* (n.) | right, law, justice |
| *imperium* | supreme command, empire |
| *potestas* | power, authority |
| *provincia* | province, sphere of command |
| *res publica* | the commonwealth, the state |
| *civis* | citizen |
| *peregrinus* | foreigner |

## 2. Roman Law

### 2.1 The Twelve Tables (Leges Duodecim Tabularum)

Written c. 450 BC, these were the first codification of Roman law. Posted in the Forum on bronze tablets, they covered:

| Table | Subject |
|-------|---------|
| I–III | Court procedure and debt |
| IV–V | Family law (*patria potestas*) and inheritance |
| VI–VII | Property, ownership, and boundaries |
| VIII–IX | Delicts (torts), crimes, and constitutional law |
| X | Sacred law (funerals) |
| XI–XII | Supplementary laws |

### 2.2 Categories of Roman Law

| Category | Latin | Description |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Civil law | *ius civile* | Law applying to Roman citizens |
| Law of nations | *ius gentium* | Law common to all peoples |
| Natural law | *ius naturale* | Universal principles of justice |
| Honorable law | *ius honorarium* | Praetorian edicts supplementing civil law |

### 2.3 Legal Principles Still in Use

| Latin Maxim | Meaning | Modern Application |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| *Ignorantia legis neminem excusat* | Ignorance of the law excuses no one | Universal legal principle |
| *Nulla poena sine lege* | No punishment without a law | Criminal law foundation |
| *In dubio pro reo* | When in doubt, for the defendant | Presumption of innocence |
| *Pacta sunt servanda* | Agreements must be kept | Contract law |
| *Res iudicata* | A matter judged | Case law / double jeopardy |
| *Dura lex, sed lex* | The law is hard, but it is the law | Rule of law |

### 2.4 Legacy

Roman law is the foundation of **civil law systems** used in continental Europe, Latin America, and much of Asia and Africa. The *Corpus Iuris Civilis* of Justinian (AD 529–534) codified Roman law and became the basis of medieval and modern European legal systems.

## 3. Roman Religion

### 3.1 The Pantheon

Roman gods were largely adapted from Greek counterparts:

| Roman God | Greek Equivalent | Domain | Key Epithet |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|-------------|
| Iuppiter (*Iuppiter*) | Zeus | Sky, thunder, king of gods | *Optimus Maximus* |
| Iuno (*Iuno*) | Hera | Marriage, women | *Regina* |
| Minerva (*Minerva*) | Athena | Wisdom, crafts, war | — |
| Mars (*Mars*) | Ares | War, agriculture | *Pater* |
| Venus (*Venus*) | Aphrodite | Love, beauty | *Genetrix* |
| Apollo (*Apollo*) | Apollo | Music, prophecy, sun | *Phoebus* |
| Diana (*Diana*) | Artemis | Hunting, moon | — |
| Neptunus (*Neptunus*) | Poseidon | Sea | — |
| Mercurius (*Mercurius*) | Hermes | Trade, messages | — |
| Vulcanus (*Vulcanus*) | Hephaestus | Fire, forge | — |
| Ceres (*Ceres*) | Demeter | Grain, harvest | — |
| Bacchus (*Bacchus*) | Dionysus | Wine, revelry | *Liber* |

### 3.2 Augury and Divination

| Practice | Latin | Description |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Augury | *augurium* | Interpreting bird flight and behavior |
| Haruspicy | *haruspicina* | Examining animal entrails (especially liver) |
| Auspices | *auspicium* | Observing signs before action (*avis* + *specere*) |
| Sibylline Books | *Libri Sibyllini* | Prophetic texts consulted in emergencies |

The phrase ***auspicium melioris aevi*** ("an omen of a better age") reflects this tradition.

### 3.3 Festivals

| Festival | Date | Honoring | Activities |
|----------|------|----------|------------|
| Saturnalia | Dec. 17–23 | Saturn | Gift-giving, role reversal, feasting |
| Lupercalia | Feb. 15 | Faunus | Purification, fertility rites |
| Floralia | Apr. 28–May 3 | Flora | Spring flowers, theatrical performances |
| Parentalia | Feb. 13–21 | Ancestors | Tomb visits, offerings to the dead |
| Ludi Romani | Sep. 4–19 | Jupiter | Chariot races, theatrical shows |

### 3.4 Household Religion

Every Roman home had:

- **Lares** (*Lares*) — guardian spirits of the household and crossroads
- **Penates** (*Penates*) — gods of the storeroom and family provisions
- **Genius** (*Genius*) — the protective spirit of the *paterfamilias*
- **Lararium** — a household shrine, usually near the hearth

## 4. Roman Military

### 4.1 Legion Structure (Imperial Period)

| Unit | Latin | Size | Commander |
|------|-------|------|-----------|
| **Legion** | *legio* | ~5,000 | *legatus legionis* |
| **Cohort** | *cohors* | ~480 (1st cohort: ~800) | *tribunus* |
| **Century** | *centuria* | ~80 | *centurio* |
| **Contubernium** | *contubernium* | 8 | *decanus* |

A full legion also included:
- *equites legionis* — 120 cavalry
- Engineers, physicians, and support staff
- Total with auxiliaries: ~10,000

### 4.2 Ranks

| Latin | Rank |
|-------|------|
| *miles gregarius* | Common soldier |
| *immunis* | Soldier exempt from fatigue duties |
| *optio* | Second-in-command of a century |
| *centurio* | Commander of a century |
| *primus pilus* | Senior centurion (1st cohort, 1st century) |
| *tribunus militum* | Military tribune (6 per legion) |
| *praefectus castrorum* | Camp prefect |
| *legatus legionis* | Legion commander (senatorial rank) |

### 4.3 Tactics and Formations

| Formation | Latin | Description |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Battle line | *acies triplex* | Three lines: *hastati*, *principes*, *triarii* (Republican) |
| Tortoise | *testudo* | Overlapping shields above and on sides |
| Wedge | *cuneus* | V-shaped attack formation |
| Circle | *orbis* | Defensive formation when surrounded |

### 4.4 Famous Battles

| Battle | Date | Significance |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Cannae | 216 BC | Hannibal's double envelopment — worst Roman defeat |
| Zama | 202 BC | Scipio defeats Hannibal — ends Second Punic War |
| Pharsalus | 48 BC | Caesar defeats Pompey |
| Actium | 31 BC | Octavian defeats Antony and Cleopatra |
| Teutoburg Forest | AD 9 | Varus loses 3 legions to Germanic tribes |

## 5. Architecture and Engineering

### 5.1 Key Innovations

| Innovation | Latin | Significance |
|------------|-------|-------------|
| **Concrete** | *opus caementicium* | Revolutionized construction; enabled domes and vaults |
| **Arch** | *arcus* | Load-bearing curved structure |
| **Vault** | *fornix* | Extended arches into 3D |
| **Dome** | *tholus* | Largest unsupported dome for 1,300 years (Pantheon) |
| **Roads** | *viae* | 400,000 km network; many still in use |
| **Aqueducts** | *aquaeductus* | Gravity-fed water systems spanning valleys |

### 5.2 Famous Structures

| Structure | Date | Features |
|-----------|------|----------|
| Colosseum (*Amphitheatrum Flavium*) | AD 72–80 | 50,000 seats, retractable awning (*velarium*) |
| Pantheon | AD 118–125 | 43m unreinforced concrete dome, oculus |
| Forum Romanum | 7th c. BC onward | Political, religious, commercial center |
| Appian Way (*Via Appia*) | 312 BC | First major Roman road, Rome to Brindisi |
| Pont du Gard | c. 19 BC | Three-tier aqueduct bridge in southern France |

### 5.3 Road Construction

Roman roads (*viae*) were built in layers:

```
Surface:     summa crusta (paving stones)
Bedding:     nucleus (gravel and lime cement)
Foundation:  rudus (rubble)
Base:        statumen (large stones)
Ground:      fossa (drainage ditches on either side)
```

The phrase ***Omnes viae Romam ducunt*** ("All roads lead to Rome") reflects the centrality of the road network.

## 6. Social Structure

### 6.1 Social Classes

| Class | Latin | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Patricians** | *patricii* | Hereditary aristocracy, original ruling class |
| **Plebeians** | *plebeii* | Common citizens, majority of population |
| **Equestrians** | *equites* | Wealthy non-senatorial class, business and finance |
| **Freedmen** | *liberti / libertini* | Former slaves, limited political rights |
| **Slaves** | *servi* | No legal rights, property of owners |
| **Foreigners** | *peregrini* | Non-citizens, subject peoples |

### 6.2 The Family (Familia)

The Roman *familia* was led by the *paterfamilias* (eldest male), who had *patria potestas* (paternal power) over:

- Wife (in *manus* marriage — increasingly rare in late Republic)
- Children (including adult sons, until the father died or emancipated them)
- Slaves and freedmen of the household
- All family property

### 6.3 Women in Rome

| Aspect | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Citizenship | Citizens, but no vote or public office |
| Property | Could own property (especially in late Republic/Empire) |
| Marriage | Required father's consent; could initiate divorce (late Republic) |
| Education | Upper-class women often well educated |
| Famous women | Cornelia (mother of the Gracchi), Livia (wife of Augustus), Agrippina |

### 6.4 Slavery

- Slaves came from war, piracy, debt, and birth
- Domestic slaves (*servi domestici*) could be well treated; mine slaves had brutal conditions
- Manumission (*manumissio*) was common — freed slaves became *liberti*
- Freedmen's children were full citizens
- Major slave revolts: Spartacus (73–71 BC)

## 7. Daily Life

### 7.1 Housing

| Type | Latin | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Town house | *domus* | Single-family, atrium-centered, for the wealthy |
| Apartment block | *insula* | Multi-story rental housing, 3–8 floors |
| Country villa | *villa rustica* | Farm estate with agricultural production |
| Luxury villa | *villa urbana* | Country retreat for the wealthy |

**Layout of a *domus*:**

```
Street entrance (ostium)
    ↓
Atrium (reception hall, impluvium pool)
    ↓
Tablinum (office of paterfamilias)
    ↓
Peristylium (colonnaded garden)
    → Triclinium (dining room)
    → Cubiculum (bedroom)
    → Culina (kitchen)
```

### 7.2 Bathing (Thermae)

The Roman baths were a central social institution:

| Room | Latin | Temperature |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Changing room | *apodyterium* | — |
| Cold bath | *frigidarium* | Cold |
| Warm room | *tepidarium* | Warm |
| Hot bath | *caldarium* | Hot |
| Exercise yard | *palaestra* | — |

Heated by the ***hypocaustum*** — an underfloor heating system with furnace-heated air circulating through channels beneath raised floors.

### 7.3 Education

| Stage | Latin | Age | Content |
|-------|-------|-----|---------|
| Primary | *ludus litterarius* | 7–11 | Reading, writing, arithmetic |
| Secondary | *grammaticus* | 12–15 | Literature, grammar, Greek |
| Higher | *rhetor* | 16+ | Rhetoric, oratory, philosophy |

**Writing tools:**
- *stilus* — pointed instrument for wax tablets
- *tabula cerata* — wax-covered wooden tablet
- *calamus* — reed pen for papyrus
- *volumen* — papyrus scroll

### 7.4 Entertainment

| Type | Latin | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Gladiatorial games | *munera gladiatoria* | Armed combat in amphitheaters |
| Chariot racing | *ludi circenses* | 4 factions (Blue, Green, Red, White) at Circus Maximus |
| Theater | *ludi scaenici* | Comedy (Plautus, Terence) and tragedy |
| Dinner party | *cena* | Multi-course meal, reclining on couches |

## 8. The Legacy of Rome

### 8.1 What Rome Left the World

| Domain | Legacy |
|--------|--------|
| **Language** | Latin → Romance languages; 60% of English vocabulary |
| **Law** | Civil law systems, legal concepts, terminology |
| **Government** | Republic, Senate, separation of powers, *veto* |
| **Engineering** | Roads, aqueducts, concrete, arch, dome |
| **Calendar** | Julian calendar → Gregorian calendar; month names |
| **Alphabet** | The Latin alphabet used worldwide |
| **Christianity** | Rome adopted and spread Christianity; Vatican Latin |
| **Literature** | Literary genres, rhetoric, historiography |

### 8.2 Latin Phrases in Daily Use

| Phrase | Meaning | Context |
|--------|---------|---------|
| *et cetera* (etc.) | "and the rest" | General |
| *vice versa* | "with the position turned" | General |
| *ad hoc* | "for this (purpose)" | Business, politics |
| *per capita* | "by heads" | Economics |
| *status quo* | "the state in which" | Politics |
| *bona fide* | "in good faith" | Law |
| *alma mater* | "nourishing mother" | Education |
| *carpe diem* | "seize the day" | Popular culture |
| *veni, vidi, vici* | "I came, I saw, I conquered" | Popular culture |
| *anno Domini* (AD) | "in the year of the Lord" | Calendar |
