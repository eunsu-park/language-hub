## 1. From Classical to Vulgar Latin

### 1.1 What is Vulgar Latin?

**Vulgar Latin** (*sermo vulgaris*) refers not to "crude" Latin but to the everyday spoken language of the Roman people (*vulgus* = "the common people"). It differs from the literary Classical Latin of Cicero and Virgil in:

- Pronunciation and phonology
- Simplified grammar (especially case system)
- Vocabulary innovations
- More analytic (word-order based) rather than synthetic (inflection-based) structure

### 1.2 Major Sound Changes

| Change | Classical | Vulgar | Result |
|--------|-----------|--------|--------|
| Loss of final *-m* | *rosam* | *rosa* | Case distinction blurred |
| Loss of vowel length | *mālum* ≠ *mălum* | both *malu* | Quality replaced quantity |
| AE → E | *caelum* /ae/ | *celum* /e/ | Monophthongization |
| OE → E | *poena* /oe/ | *pena* /e/ | Monophthongization |
| AU → O | *aurum* /au/ | *orum* /o/ | In some regions |
| Palatalization of C/G | *centum* /k/ | *tsentum* /ts/ | Before front vowels |
| Intervocalic B → V | *habere* /b/ | *avere* /v/ | Spirantization |
| Loss of H | *homo* /h/ | *omo* /Ø/ | H became silent |
| Vowel shift | 10 vowels (5 long, 5 short) | 7 vowels (open/closed) | Quantity → quality |

### 1.3 The Vulgar Latin Vowel System

Classical Latin had 10 vowels distinguished by length. Vulgar Latin merged these into 7 vowels distinguished by quality:

```
Classical:     ī   ĭ   ē   ĕ   ā   ă   ŏ   ō   ŭ   ū
                \  /   \ /    \ /    \ /   \  /
Vulgar:         i     e  ɛ    a    ɔ  o     u
                        (open)      (open)
```

This merger explains why *pĭra* ("pear," short *i*) → Spanish/Italian *pera* (with /e/), while *fīlum* ("thread," long *ī*) → Spanish *hilo*, Italian *filo* (with /i/).

### 1.4 Grammatical Simplification

| Feature | Classical Latin | Vulgar Latin | Modern Romance |
|---------|----------------|-------------|----------------|
| Cases | 6 cases (nom, gen, dat, acc, abl, voc) | Mainly 2 (nom vs. oblique/acc) | No case system (except pronouns) |
| Articles | None | *ille/illa* → definite article | *el/la* (Sp.), *le/la* (Fr.), *il/la* (It.) |
| Demonstratives | *hic, ille, iste, is* | *ille* dominant, *iste* → proximal | *ese* (Sp.), *ce* (Fr.) |
| Future tense | *amabo* (synthetic) | *amare habeo* ("I have to love") | *amaré* (Sp.), *aimerai* (Fr.) |
| Perfect tense | *amavi* (synthetic) | *habeo amatum* ("I have loved") | *he amado* (Sp.), *j'ai aimé* (Fr.) |
| Passive voice | *amor* (synthetic) | *sum amatus* or reflexive | *se* construction or *essere* + PPP |
| Comparison | *-ior, -issimus* | *magis/plus* + adj. | *más* (Sp.), *plus* (Fr.), *più* (It.) |

### 1.5 Vocabulary Changes

Some Classical words were replaced by different words in Vulgar Latin:

| Meaning | Classical | Vulgar | Romance Outcome |
|---------|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| to speak | *loqui* | *parabolare* (from Gr. *parabolē*) | Sp. *hablar*, Fr. *parler*, It. *parlare* |
| to eat | *edere* | *manducare* ("to chew") | Fr. *manger*, It. *mangiare* |
| horse | *equus* | *caballus* ("nag, workhorse") | Sp. *caballo*, Fr. *cheval*, It. *cavallo* |
| house | *domus* | *casa* ("hut") | Sp. *casa*, It. *casa* |
| beautiful | *pulcher* | *bellus* ("pretty") | Sp. *bello*, Fr. *beau*, It. *bello* |
| head | *caput* | *testa* ("pot") / *caput* | Fr. *tête*, It. *testa*; Sp. *cabeza* (< *caput*) |

### 2.2 Key Sound Correspondences

| Latin | Spanish | French | Italian |
|-------|---------|--------|---------|
| CL- | ll- (*clamare* → *llamar*) | — | chi- (*chiamare*) |
| F- (before vowel) | h- (*filium* → *hijo*) | f- (*fils*) | f- (*figlio*) |
| -CT- | -ch- (*noctem* → *noche*) | -it (*nuit*) | -tt- (*notte*) |
| PL- | ll- (*plenum* → *lleno*) | pl- (*plein*) | pi- (*pieno*) |
| -LI- | -j- (*filium* → *hijo*) | -l (*fils*) | -gli- (*figlio*) |
| initial J- | j- /x/ (*iam* → *ya*) | j- /ʒ/ (*ja/déjà*) | gi- /dʒ/ (*già*) |

### 2.3 When Did Latin "Die"?

Latin never died — it evolved. Key milestones:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 3rd–5th c. AD | Regional spoken varieties diverge significantly |
| AD 476 | Fall of Western Roman Empire (political, not linguistic event) |
| AD 813 | Council of Tours — sermons must be in *rustica Romana lingua* (= vernacular, not Latin) |
| AD 842 | **Strasbourg Oaths** — earliest text in French (*lingua romana*) |
| 10th c. | Earliest texts in Spanish, Italian |
| 13th–14th c. | Dante writes *Divina Commedia* in Italian; *lingua volgare* gains literary prestige |

## 3. Medieval Latin

### 3.1 Characteristics

Medieval Latin (c. AD 500–1500) differs from Classical in several ways:

| Feature | Classical | Medieval |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Spelling | *ae, oe* | *e* (monophthong) — *caelum* → *celum* |
| Vocabulary | Classical stock | New words from Germanic, Arabic, Greek |
| Word order | Flexible (SOV tendency) | More fixed (SVO tendency) |
| *quod* clauses | Rare (acc. + inf. preferred) | *quod/quia* + indicative replaces acc. + inf. |
| Subjunctive | Nuanced sequence of tenses | Simplified; often interchangeable with indicative |
| Prepositions | Cases carry meaning | Prepositions reinforce or replace case meanings |

### 3.2 University Latin

From the 12th century, Latin was the language of European universities:

| Term | Meaning | Modern Survival |
|------|---------|----------------|
| *universitas* | "the whole, community" | University |
| *magister* | "master, teacher" | Master's degree |
| *baccalaureus* | "bachelor" (uncertain etymology) | Bachelor's degree |
| *doctor* | "teacher" (*docere*) | Doctoral degree |
| *thesis* | "proposition" (Gr.) | Dissertation |
| *disputatio* | "debate, argument" | Academic disputation |
| *collegium* | "association, society" | College |
| *campus* | "field" | University grounds |
| *alumnus/alumni* | "foster child/children" | Graduate(s) |
| *alma mater* | "nourishing mother" | One's university |

### 3.3 Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

The greatest medieval Latin writer. His style is precise and analytical:

> **Respondeo dicendum quod Deum esse quinque viis probari potest.**
> (*Summa Theologiae* I, q.2, a.3)

**Translation:** "I answer that it must be said that the existence of God can be proved in five ways."

**Notable features:**
- *Respondeo dicendum quod* — formulaic scholastic structure ("I answer that it must be said that")
- *Deum esse* — acc. + inf. (indirect statement, still used but alongside *quod* clauses)
- Clear, logical prose — optimized for argumentation, not rhetoric

### 3.4 Medieval Hymns

> **Dies irae, dies illa,**
> **solvet saeclum in favilla,**
> **teste David cum Sibylla.**

**Translation:** "Day of wrath, that day / shall dissolve the world in ash, / as David and the Sibyl bear witness."

**Features:**
- Rhyming verse (unknown in Classical Latin poetry)
- Accentual rhythm (stress-based, not quantitative)
- *saeclum* for *saeculum* (syncope — loss of unstressed syllable)

## 4. Renaissance and Neo-Latin

### 4.1 The Humanist Revival

Renaissance humanists (14th–16th c.) sought to restore Classical Latin, rejecting Medieval Latin as "barbarous."

| Humanist | Dates | Contribution |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Petrarch** | 1304–1374 | Rediscovered Cicero's letters; championed Classical style |
| **Lorenzo Valla** | 1407–1457 | *Elegantiae Linguae Latinae* — grammar treatise restoring Classical usage |
| **Erasmus** | 1466–1536 | *Colloquia*, *Encomium Moriae* (*Praise of Folly*); witty Neo-Latin prose |
| **Thomas More** | 1478–1535 | *Utopia* — political philosophy in Latin |

### 4.2 Scientific Latin

Latin remained the language of science into the 18th century:

| Author | Work | Date | Significance |
|--------|------|------|-------------|
| **Copernicus** | *De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium* | 1543 | Heliocentric model |
| **Vesalius** | *De Humani Corporis Fabrica* | 1543 | Modern anatomy |
| **Newton** | *Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica* | 1687 | Laws of motion, gravity |
| **Linnaeus** | *Systema Naturae* | 1735 | Binomial nomenclature |
| **Gauss** | *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* | 1801 | Number theory |

#### Copernicus (1473–1543)

> **In medio vero omnium residet Sol. Quis enim in hoc pulcherrimo templo lampadem hanc in alio vel meliore loco poneret quam unde totum simul possit illuminare?**

**Translation:** "In the center of all resides the Sun. For who, in this most beautiful temple, would place this lamp in any other or better position than that from which it can illuminate the whole at once?"

- *in medio omnium* — "in the middle of everything"
- *quis ... poneret* — rhetorical question with potential subjunctive
- *pulcherrimo templo* — superlative: "most beautiful temple" (metaphor for the cosmos)
- *unde ... possit illuminare* — relative clause of characteristic (subjunctive)

### 4.3 Erasmus: Colloquial Neo-Latin

> **Nulla calamitas solitaria.** (*Adagia*)

**Translation:** "No misfortune comes alone." (Cf. English "It never rains but it pours.")

## 5. Ecclesiastical Latin

### 5.1 The Language of the Church

Latin has been the official language of the Roman Catholic Church for nearly two millennia:

| Domain | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| **Liturgy** | Mass was in Latin until Vatican II (1962–65); *Tridentine Mass* still celebrated |
| **Documents** | Papal encyclicals, canon law, official Vatican communications |
| **Theology** | *Summa Theologiae*, catechisms, doctrinal statements |
| **Music** | Gregorian chant, polyphonic masses, hymns |
| **Administration** | Vatican Latin is used for ATM screens, official stamps, Twitter (@Pontifex_ln) |

### 5.2 Ecclesiastical vs. Classical Pronunciation

| Letter/Combo | Classical | Ecclesiastical |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| C before e, i | /k/ (*Kikero*) | /tʃ/ (*Chichero*) |
| G before e, i | /g/ (hard) | /dʒ/ (soft, as in *gem*) |
| V | /w/ (*weni, widi, wiki*) | /v/ (*veni, vidi, vichi*) |
| AE | /ae/ (diphthong) | /e/ (monophthong) |
| TI before vowel | /ti/ | /tsi/ (*natio* = "natsio") |
| GN | /gn/ | /ɲ/ (as in Italian *gnocchi*) |
| PH | /pʰ/ | /f/ |
| SC before e, i | /sk/ | /ʃ/ (as *sh*) |

### 5.3 Key Ecclesiastical Vocabulary

|-------|---------|

### 5.4 Famous Prayers and Phrases

**The Lord's Prayer (Pater Noster):**

> **Pater noster, qui es in caelis, sanctificetur nomen tuum. Adveniat regnum tuum. Fiat voluntas tua, sicut in caelo et in terra.**

**Translation:** "Our Father, who art in heaven, hallowed be thy name. Thy kingdom come. Thy will be done, on earth as it is in heaven."

- *sanctificetur* — 3rd sg. pres. pass. subjunctive (jussive) — "let it be hallowed"
- *Adveniat* — 3rd sg. pres. act. subjunctive (jussive) — "let it come"
- *Fiat* — 3rd sg. pres. act. subjunctive of *fieri* — "let it be done"

**Common Ecclesiastical phrases:**

| Phrase | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| *Deo volente* (D.V.) | "God willing" |
| *Requiescat in pace* (R.I.P.) | "May he/she rest in peace" |

## 7. Learning to Read Medieval and Neo-Latin Texts

### 7.1 Adjustments from Classical Latin

When reading post-Classical texts, be prepared for:

| Classical | Medieval/Neo-Latin | Example |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| *ae* | *e* | *caelum* → *celum* |
| Acc. + infinitive | *quod/quia* + finite verb | *dixit quod venit* for *dixit eum venire* |
| No articles | *ille/illa* used as articles | *ille rex* ≈ "the king" |
| Deponent verbs | Sometimes used as active | *sequere* for *sequi* |
| *cum* + subjunctive | *cum* + indicative | (temporal distinction lost) |
| Strict case usage | Prepositions more common | *de illo* for genitive |

### 7.2 Recognizing the Period

| Clue | Likely Period |
|------|--------------|
| Classical grammar, no rhyme, quantitative meter | Classical (1st c. BC–1st c. AD) |
| Rhyming verse, accentual meter, theological content | Medieval (6th–14th c.) |
| Classical grammar revived, humanist themes | Renaissance Neo-Latin (14th–17th c.) |
| Scientific terminology, precise definitions | Scientific Latin (16th–18th c.) |
| Liturgical formulas, *Dominus*, *sanctus* | Ecclesiastical (any period) |

### 7.3 Sample: Comparing Periods

**Same idea — "God exists" — in different periods:**

| Period | Latin |
|--------|-------|
| Classical (Cicero) | *Esse deos certum est.* ("That gods exist is certain.") |
| Medieval (Aquinas) | *Deum esse per quinque vias probari potest.* ("That God exists can be proved in five ways.") |
| Neo-Latin (Descartes) | *Deus necessario existit.* ("God necessarily exists.") |

| Renaissance | 14th–17th c. | Humanist revival of Classical norms |
| Scientific | 16th–18th c. | Technical treatises: Newton, Linnaeus |
| Ecclesiastical | All periods | Vatican, liturgy, church pronunciation |
| Modern | 19th c.–present | Mottos, law, medicine, taxonomy, abbreviations |

| Change Type | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| Monophthongization | *ae* → *e* |
| Palatalization | *c* /k/ → /tʃ/ before *e, i* |
