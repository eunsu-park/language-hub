## 1. Stoic Philosophy in Latin

### 1.1 Overview of Roman Stoicism

Stoicism was the dominant philosophy of educated Romans from the late Republic onward. The major Latin Stoic writers:

| Author | Dates | Major Works |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Cicero** | 106–43 BC | *De Officiis*, *De Finibus*, *Tusculanae Disputationes* |
| **Seneca** | c. 4 BC–AD 65 | *Epistulae Morales*, *De Brevitate Vitae*, *De Ira* |
| **Epictetus** | c. AD 50–135 | *Discourses* (Greek, but influential on Latin tradition) |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | AD 121–180 | *Meditationes* (Greek, but a Roman emperor) |

### 1.2 Key Stoic Concepts

| Latin Term | Meaning | Philosophical Significance |
|------------|---------|---------------------------|
| *virtus* | virtue, moral excellence | The sole good; derived from *vir* ("man") |
| *ratio* | reason, calculation | The governing principle of the universe and the soul |
| *natura* | nature | Living *secundum naturam* ("according to nature") is the goal |
| *sapiens* | the wise person | One who has achieved virtue; the Stoic ideal |
| *animus* | mind, spirit | The rational soul that must govern the passions |
| *aequanimitas* | equanimity | Evenness of mind amid fortune and misfortune |
| *apatheia* (Gr.) / *tranquillitas* | freedom from passion | Not suppression but mastery of emotions |
| *fatum* | fate, destiny | The chain of cause and effect governed by divine reason |
| *officium* | duty, proper function | What reason requires in each situation |
| *indifferentia* | indifferent things | Health, wealth, etc. — preferred but not truly good |

### 1.3 Seneca: Epistulae Morales

Seneca's 124 letters to Lucilius are the most accessible Latin philosophical texts. Written in a conversational style, they address practical ethics.

#### Passage 1: On the Shortness of Life (Ep. 1.1)

> **Ita fac, mi Lucili: vindica te tibi, et tempus quod adhuc aut auferebatur aut subripiebatur aut excidebat, collige et serva.**

**Word-by-word:**

| Latin | Form | Translation |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Ita fac | imperative | "Do thus" / "Act in this way" |
| mi Lucili | vocative | "my dear Lucilius" |
| vindica te tibi | imperative + reflexive + dative | "claim yourself for yourself" |
| et | conjunction | "and" |
| tempus | acc. sg. n. | "the time" |
| quod | rel. pronoun (acc. sg. n.) | "which" |
| adhuc | adverb | "until now" |
| aut auferebatur | impf. pass. | "was either being stolen" |
| aut subripiebatur | impf. pass. | "or was being snatched away" |
| aut excidebat | impf. act. | "or was slipping away" |
| collige et serva | imperatives | "gather and preserve" |

**Translation:** "Act thus, my dear Lucilius: claim yourself for yourself, and the time that until now was either being stolen from you, or snatched away, or simply slipping past — gather it and guard it."

**Commentary:** Three verbs describe three ways we lose time: it is taken openly (*auferebatur*), stolen secretly (*subripiebatur*), or lost through carelessness (*excidebat*). The tricolon escalates from external theft to personal negligence.

#### Passage 2: On the Value of Time (Ep. 1.3)

> **Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est. In huius rei unius fugacis ac lubricae possessionem natura nos misit.**

**Translation:** "Everything, Lucilius, belongs to others; only time is ours. Nature has put us in possession of this one fleeting and slippery thing."

- *aliena* — "belonging to others" (predicate adjective)
- *tempus tantum nostrum est* — "only time is ours"
- *fugacis ac lubricae* — gen. sg. modifying *rei* — "fleeting and slippery"
- *possessionem* — acc. after *in* — "into possession"

#### Passage 3: On Philosophy and Life (Ep. 48.12)

> **Non vitae sed scholae discimus.**

**Translation:** "We learn not for life but for school."

This is Seneca's famous complaint (often quoted in the reversed form *Non scholae sed vitae discimus* — "We learn not for school but for life"). The dative expresses purpose.

### 1.4 Seneca: De Brevitate Vitae

> **Non exiguum temporis habemus, sed multum perdidimus. Satis longa vita et in maximarum rerum consummationem large data est, si tota bene conlocaretur.**

**Translation:** "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste much of it. Life is long enough, and has been given generously enough for the accomplishment of the greatest things, if it were all well invested."

**Key vocabulary:**
- *exiguum* — "small, scanty"
- *perdidimus* — perf. of *perdere* — "we have lost/wasted"
- *consummationem* — "accomplishment, completion"
- *large data est* — "has been generously given"
- *conlocaretur* — impf. subj. pass. — "were invested" (contrary to fact)

## 2. Epicureanism: Lucretius

### 2.1 De Rerum Natura

Lucretius (c. 99–55 BC) wrote *De Rerum Natura* ("On the Nature of Things"), a hexameter poem expounding Epicurean physics and ethics. It is the most sustained philosophical poem in Latin.

### 2.2 Key Epicurean Concepts

| Latin Term | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| *atomus* (from Gr.) / *corpus* | atom, indivisible body |
| *inane* | void, empty space |
| *natura rerum* | the nature of things (= physics) |
| *voluptas* | pleasure (the highest good in Epicureanism) |
| *ataraxia* (Gr.) / *tranquillitas animi* | freedom from anxiety |
| *clinamen* | the swerve (atoms' random deviation — basis of free will) |
| *simulacra* | thin films shed by objects (basis of perception) |
| *mortalitas animae* | mortality of the soul |
| *foedera naturai* | the laws/covenants of nature |

### 2.3 Famous Passages

#### Opening Invocation (1.1–4)

> **Aeneadum genetrix, hominum divumque voluptas,**
> **alma Venus, caeli subter labentia signa**
> **quae mare navigerum, quae terras frugiferentis**
> **concelebras...**

**Translation:** "Mother of the sons of Aeneas, pleasure of humans and gods, nourishing Venus, you who beneath the gliding signs of heaven fill with yourself the ship-bearing sea and the fruit-bearing lands..."

- *Aeneadum genetrix* — "mother of Aeneas's descendants" (= Rome)
- *hominum divumque voluptas* — *voluptas* is programmatic: pleasure as cosmic force
- *alma* — "nourishing" (same root as *alma mater*)

#### On the Fear of Death (3.830)

> **Nil igitur mors est ad nos neque pertinet hilum.**

**Translation:** "Death, therefore, is nothing to us, nor does it matter a bit."

- *nil ... ad nos* — "nothing to us"
- *hilum* — "a trifle, a bit" (colloquial intensifier)

## 3. Marcus Aurelius and Later Stoicism

### 3.1 The Emperor-Philosopher

Although Marcus Aurelius (AD 121–180) wrote his *Meditationes* in Greek, his thought is deeply Roman Stoic. Key ideas in Latin translation:

| Greek Concept | Latin Equivalent | Meaning |
|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| τὸ ἡγεμονικόν | *principale animi* | The ruling faculty of the mind |
| κοσμοπολίτης | *civis mundi* | Citizen of the world |
| ἀνεχου καὶ ἀπέχου | *sustine et abstine* | Bear and forbear |
| προαίρεσις | *voluntas / arbitrium* | Moral choice, will |

### 3.2 Boethius: Bridge to the Middle Ages

Boethius (c. AD 480–524) wrote *De Consolatione Philosophiae* in prison, combining Stoic and Neoplatonic thought. His famous lines:

> **In omni adversitate fortunae, infelicissimum genus infortunii est fuisse felicem.**

**Translation:** "In every adversity of fortune, the most unhappy kind of misfortune is to have been happy."

## 4. Legal Latin: Fundamental Principles

### 4.1 Sources of Roman Law

| Source | Latin | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Statutes | *leges* | Laws passed by popular assemblies |
| Plebiscites | *plebiscita* | Laws passed by the plebeian assembly |
| Senatorial decrees | *senatus consulta* | Decrees of the Senate |
| Imperial edicts | *constitutiones principum* | Edicts, rescripts, and decrees of the emperor |
| Praetorian edict | *edictum praetoris* | Annual judicial program of the praetor |
| Juristic opinion | *responsa prudentium* | Opinions of authorized jurists |
| Custom | *consuetudo* | Unwritten customary law |

### 4.2 Divisions of Law

| Division | Latin | Governs |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Public law | *ius publicum* | The state, magistracies, religion |
| Private law | *ius privatum* | Relations between individuals |
| Persons | *ius personarum* | Status: free/slave, citizen/non-citizen |
| Things | *ius rerum* | Property, obligations, inheritance |
| Actions | *ius actionum* | Legal procedure and remedies |

### 4.3 Key Legal Vocabulary

| Latin | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| *actor* | plaintiff |
| *reus* | defendant |
| *iudex* | judge |
| *testis* | witness |
| *sententia* | judgment, verdict |
| *poena* | penalty, punishment |
| *delictum* | offense, wrong |
| *contractus* | contract |
| *obligatio* | obligation |
| *dominium* | ownership |
| *possessio* | possession |
| *hereditas* | inheritance |
| *fideicommissum* | trust (entrusted legacy) |
| *mandatum* | mandate, commission |
| *culpa* | fault, negligence |
| *dolus* | deceit, fraud |

## 5. Legal Maxims Still in Use

### 5.1 Criminal and Procedural Law

| Maxim | Literal Meaning | Modern Application |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| *habeas corpus* | "you may have the body" | Right to be brought before a court |
| *stare decisis* | "to stand by things decided" | Legal precedent |
| *prima facie* | "at first face/appearance" | Evidence sufficient on its face |
| *corpus delicti* | "body of the crime" | Material evidence of a crime |
| *mens rea* | "guilty mind" | Criminal intent |
| *actus reus* | "guilty act" | The criminal act itself |
| *nolo contendere* | "I do not wish to contend" | No contest plea |
| *nolle prosequi* | "to be unwilling to pursue" | Prosecutor drops charges |
| *subpoena* (*sub poena*) | "under penalty" | Compulsory court summons |
| *alibi* | "elsewhere" | Defense of being in another place |

### 5.2 Civil and Property Law

| Maxim | Literal Meaning | Modern Application |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| *pro bono* (*publico*) | "for the (public) good" | Free professional services |
| *de facto* | "from the fact" | In practice, regardless of law |
| *de iure* (*de jure*) | "from the law" | By right, legally |
| *caveat emptor* | "let the buyer beware" | Buyer assumes risk |
| *quid pro quo* | "something for something" | Exchange, consideration |
| *pro rata* | "according to the calculated (share)" | Proportional distribution |
| *bona fide* | "in good faith" | Honest intent |
| *mala fide* | "in bad faith" | Dishonest intent |
| *inter vivos* | "between the living" | Gifts or trusts during one's lifetime |
| *post mortem* | "after death" | After-death examination |

### 5.3 Constitutional and International Law

| Maxim | Literal Meaning | Modern Application |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| *veto* | "I forbid" | Executive override |
| *quorum* | "of whom" | Minimum number for valid proceedings |
| *ad hoc* | "for this" | For a specific purpose |
| *ex officio* | "from the office" | By virtue of one's position |
| *persona non grata* | "person not welcome" | Diplomatic rejection |
| *casus belli* | "occasion of war" | Justification for war |
| *status quo ante* | "the state in which before" | Restoring previous conditions |
| *modus vivendi* | "way of living" | Practical compromise |
| *ultra vires* | "beyond the powers" | Exceeding legal authority |

## 6. Medical and Scientific Latin

### 6.1 Anatomical Terminology

Latin (and Greek via Latin) forms the basis of anatomical nomenclature:

| Latin Term | Body Part | Derivation |
|------------|-----------|------------|
| *cranium* | skull | Gr. *kranion* |
| *vertebra* | spinal bone | *vertere* ("to turn") |
| *humerus* | upper arm bone | "shoulder" |
| *femur* | thigh bone | "thigh" |
| *tibia* | shinbone | "flute, pipe" |
| *patella* | kneecap | diminutive of *patina* ("dish") |
| *sternum* | breastbone | Gr. *sternon* |
| *pectus, pectoris* | chest | "breast, chest" |
| *cor, cordis* | heart | "heart" (cf. *cordial*) |
| *pulmo, pulmonis* | lung | "lung" (cf. *pulmonary*) |
| *ren, renis* | kidney | "kidney" (cf. *renal*) |
| *hepar, hepatis* | liver | Gr. *hepar* (cf. *hepatitis*) |

### 6.2 Linnean Taxonomy

Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778) established the binomial naming system using Latin:

**Format:** *Genus species*

| Scientific Name | Common Name | Latin Meaning |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| *Homo sapiens* | Modern human | "wise man" |
| *Canis lupus* | Gray wolf | "dog wolf" |
| *Felis catus* | Domestic cat | "cat cat" (cunning) |
| *Aquila chrysaetos* | Golden eagle | "eagle golden-eagle" |
| *Quercus robur* | English oak | "oak strength" |
| *Rosa canina* | Dog rose | "rose of the dog" |
| *Tyrannosaurus rex* | — | "tyrant lizard king" |

**Taxonomy hierarchy in Latin:**

| Level | Latin | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Kingdom | *Regnum* | Animalia |
| Phylum | *Phylum* | Chordata |
| Class | *Classis* | Mammalia |
| Order | *Ordo* | Primates |
| Family | *Familia* | Hominidae |
| Genus | *Genus* | Homo |
| Species | *Species* | sapiens |

### 6.3 Pharmaceutical Latin

| Abbreviation | Latin | Meaning | Usage |
|-------------|-------|---------|-------|
| Rx | *recipe* | "take" (imperative) | Prescription heading |
| b.i.d. | *bis in die* | "twice a day" | Dosage |
| t.i.d. | *ter in die* | "three times a day" | Dosage |
| q.i.d. | *quater in die* | "four times a day" | Dosage |
| p.r.n. | *pro re nata* | "as the situation arises" | As needed |
| p.o. | *per os* | "by mouth" | Route |
| stat. | *statim* | "immediately" | Urgency |
| q.h. | *quaque hora* | "every hour" | Frequency |

### Exercise 1: Seneca Translation

Translate the following passage from Seneca (*Ep.* 2.4):

> **Lectio certa prodest, varia delectat. Qui quo destinavit pervenire vult, unam sequatur viam, non per multas vagetur.**

*Hints:*
- *lectio certa* — "focused/fixed reading"
- *varia* — "varied (reading)"
- *destinavit* — perfect of *destinare* ("has decided, aimed")
- *sequatur ... vagetur* — jussive subjunctive

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

**Translation:** "Focused reading is beneficial; varied reading is delightful. He who wishes to arrive where he has aimed should follow one path, not wander through many."

**Parsing:**
- *certa ... varia* — contrasting adjectives modifying *lectio* (understood in second clause)
- *prodest* — "is useful" / *delectat* — "delights"
- *Qui ... vult* — "he who wishes" (relative clause as subject)
- *quo destinavit pervenire* — "to arrive where he has aimed" (*quo* = "to where")
- *unam sequatur viam* — jussive: "let him follow one road"
- *non ... vagetur* — "let him not wander" (*vagor*, deponent)

### Exercise 6: Lucretius Translation

Translate:

> **Nil igitur mors est ad nos neque pertinet hilum, quandoquidem natura animi mortalis habetur.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

**Translation:** "Death, therefore, is nothing to us, nor does it matter a bit, since the nature of the mind is held to be mortal."

**Parsing:**
- *Nil ... est ad nos* — "is nothing to us"
- *neque pertinet hilum* — "nor does it pertain a bit" (*hilum* = acc. of extent)
