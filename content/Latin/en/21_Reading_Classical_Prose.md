## 1. Introduction to Classical Latin Authors

The "Golden Age" of Latin prose spans roughly from 80 BC to AD 14. Three authors represent its major currents:

| Author | Dates | Major Works | Style |
|--------|-------|-------------|-------|
| **Caesar** | 100–44 BC | *Bellum Gallicum*, *Bellum Civile* | Clear, direct, military precision |
| **Cicero** | 106–43 BC | Orations, philosophical works, letters | Elaborate, periodic, rhetorical |
| **Livy** | 59 BC–AD 17 | *Ab Urbe Condita* (142 books) | Flowing narrative, patriotic tone |

### 1.1 Why Start with These Authors?

Caesar is traditionally the first author students read because his style is straightforward. Cicero represents the height of rhetorical Latin and is essential for understanding formal prose. Livy bridges prose and storytelling, with a dramatic, almost poetic quality.

### 1.2 Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature | Caesar | Cicero | Livy |
|---------|--------|--------|------|
| Sentence length | Moderate | Very long | Long |
| Subordination | Moderate | Heavy | Moderate-heavy |
| Vocabulary | Military, plain | Rich, formal | Archaic touches |
| Narrative voice | 3rd person (self) | 1st/2nd person | 3rd person |
| Main constructions | Ablative absolute, indirect discourse | Periodic sentences, subjunctive clauses | Dramatic speeches, vivid description |

## 2. Caesar's Bellum Gallicum

### 2.1 Style Analysis

Caesar wrote commentarii — campaign reports presented in the third person. His hallmarks:

- **Third-person self-reference**: Caesar always calls himself "Caesar," never "I"
- **Clear subordination**: Temporal and causal clauses nest logically
- **Military vocabulary**: *castra* (camp), *acies* (battle line), *impetus* (attack), *copiae* (forces)
- **Ablative absolute**: Heavily used for background circumstances
- **Indirect discourse**: For speeches and reports from others

### 2.2 Passage 1: The Opening of the Bellum Gallicum (BG 1.1)

> **Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres, quarum unam incolunt Belgae, aliam Aquitani, tertiam qui ipsorum lingua Celtae, nostra Galli appellantur.**

**Word-by-word analysis:**

| Latin | Form | Translation |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Gallia | nom. sg. f. (1st decl.) | Gaul |
| est ... divisa | perf. pass. participle + *esse* | is divided |
| omnis | nom. sg. f. adj. | all, as a whole |
| in partes tres | acc. pl. + numeral | into three parts |
| quarum | gen. pl. f. (relative) | of which |
| unam | acc. sg. f. | one |
| incolunt | 3rd pl. pres. act. ind. | inhabit |
| Belgae | nom. pl. m. | the Belgae |
| aliam | acc. sg. f. | another |
| Aquitani | nom. pl. m. | the Aquitani |
| tertiam | acc. sg. f. | the third |
| qui | nom. pl. m. (relative) | who |
| ipsorum lingua | abl. sg. (means) + gen. pl. | in their own language |
| Celtae | nom. pl. m. | Celts |
| nostra | abl. sg. f. (adj. modifying *lingua* understood) | in our (language) |
| Galli | nom. pl. m. | Gauls |
| appellantur | 3rd pl. pres. pass. ind. | are called |

**Full translation:**
"Gaul as a whole is divided into three parts, of which the Belgae inhabit one, the Aquitani another, and those who in their own language are called Celts, in ours Gauls, the third."

### 2.3 Passage 2: Geography and Character (BG 1.1 continued)

> **Hi omnes lingua, institutis, legibus inter se differunt. Gallos ab Aquitanis Garumna flumen, a Belgis Matrona et Sequana dividit.**

| Latin | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Hi omnes | nom. pl. m. — "All these" |
| lingua, institutis, legibus | abl. of respect — "in language, customs, laws" |
| inter se | "among themselves" |
| differunt | 3rd pl. pres. act. — "differ" |
| Gallos | acc. pl. — direct object |
| ab Aquitanis | abl. of separation — "from the Aquitani" |
| Garumna flumen | nom. sg. — subject — "the river Garonne" |
| a Belgis | abl. of separation — "from the Belgae" |
| Matrona et Sequana | nom. pl. — "the Marne and the Seine" |
| dividit | 3rd sg. pres. act. — "separates" |

**Translation:** "All these differ among themselves in language, customs, and laws. The river Garonne separates the Gauls from the Aquitani; the Marne and Seine separate them from the Belgae."

### 2.4 Passage 3: The Helvetii (BG 1.2)

> **Apud Helvetios longe nobilissimus fuit et ditissimus Orgetorix. Is M. Messala et M. Pisone consulibus regni cupiditate inductus coniurationem nobilitatis fecit.**

**Translation:** "Among the Helvetii, Orgetorix was by far the most noble and the wealthiest. He, when M. Messala and M. Piso were consuls, induced by desire for royal power, formed a conspiracy of the nobility."

**Key constructions:**
- *longe nobilissimus* — superlative strengthened by *longe* ("by far")
- *M. Messala et M. Pisone consulibus* — ablative absolute (dating by consulship)
- *regni cupiditate inductus* — perfect passive participle + ablative of cause

## 3. Cicero's Orations and Letters

### 3.1 The Periodic Sentence

Cicero's trademark is the **periodic sentence** (*periodus*): the main idea is suspended until the end, with subordinate clauses building suspense.

**Structure pattern:**
```
[When/Although/Because clause] + [participial phrase] + [relative clause] + MAIN VERB
```

The reader or listener must hold multiple ideas in mind until the sentence resolves.

### 3.2 Rhetorical Devices in Cicero

| Device | Latin Term | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Rhetorical question | *interrogatio* | *Quo usque tandem...?* |
| Repetition (beginning) | *anaphora* | *Nihil... nihil... nihil...* |
| Ascending series | *climax / gradatio* | citizen → senator → consul |
| Paired contrast | *antithesis* | *Non tibi, sed rei publicae* |
| Direct address | *apostrophe* | *O tempora! O mores!* |
| Ironic understatement | *litotes* | *non ignorabas* ("you were not unaware") |
| Tricolon | *tricolon* | Three parallel phrases |

### 3.3 Passage: In Catilinam I.1 (Opening)

> **Quo usque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra? Quam diu etiam furor iste tuus nos eludet? Quem ad finem sese effrenata iactabit audacia?**

| Latin | Analysis |
|-------|----------|
| Quo usque tandem | "How far, finally" (interrogative) |
| abutere = abuteris | 2nd sg. fut. dep. (*abutor* + abl.) — "will you abuse" |
| Catilina | vocative sg. |
| patientia nostra | abl. (object of *abutor*) — "our patience" |
| Quam diu | "How long" |
| etiam | "still, even" |
| furor iste tuus | nom. sg. — "that madness of yours" (*iste* = contemptuous) |
| nos eludet | "will mock us" |
| Quem ad finem | "To what end" |
| sese ... iactabit | "will ... flaunt itself" (future) |
| effrenata audacia | nom. sg. — "unbridled audacity" |

**Translation:** "How far, I ask, will you abuse our patience, Catiline? How long will that madness of yours still mock us? To what end will your unbridled audacity flaunt itself?"

**Rhetorical analysis:**
- **Tricolon**: Three questions of escalating intensity
- **Anaphora**: Each question begins with an interrogative
- **Deictic pronoun** *iste*: implies contempt — "that ... of yours"

### 3.4 Passage: In Catilinam I.2

> **O tempora! O mores! Senatus haec intellegit, consul vidit; hic tamen vivit. Vivit? Immo vero etiam in senatum venit, fit publici consilii particeps.**

**Translation:** "O the times! O the customs! The senate understands these things, the consul sees them; yet this man lives. Lives? Indeed, he even comes into the senate, he becomes a participant in public deliberation."

**Key features:**
- *O tempora! O mores!* — exclamatory accusative (*apostrophe*)
- *Vivit? Immo vero...* — question and answer (*subiectio*)
- Asyndeton: short, punchy clauses without conjunctions

### 3.5 Cicero's Letters: A Different Style

In his letters (especially *Ad Atticum*), Cicero writes informally:

> **Nihil mihi nunc scito tam deesse quam hominem eum quocum omnia quae me cura aliqua afficiunt una communicem.** (Att. 1.18.1)

**Translation:** "Know that nothing is so lacking to me now as a person with whom I might share all the things that cause me any concern."

Note the contrast: colloquial tone, but still complex subordination (*quocum ... communicem* — subjunctive in a relative clause of characteristic).

## 4. Livy's Ab Urbe Condita

### 4.1 Style Characteristics

Livy wrote 142 books of Roman history from the founding of the city (753 BC). His style features:

- **Dramatic speeches**: Characters deliver vivid orations
- **Archaic vocabulary**: Deliberately echoes older Latin
- **Variatio**: Avoids repeating the same construction
- **Patavinity**: Ancient critics noted his provincial style (*Patavinitas*)
- **Moralizing tone**: History as moral exemplum

### 4.2 Passage: The Rape of Lucretia (1.58)

> **Lucretia maesta tanto malo nuntium Romam eundem ad patrem Ardeamque ad virum mittit, ut cum singulis fidelibus amicis veniant; ita facto maturatoque opus esse; rem atrocem incidisse.**

**Translation:** "Lucretia, grieving at so great a wrong, sends a messenger to Rome to her father and to Ardea to her husband, asking that each come with a single trusted friend; that it was necessary to act and to act quickly; that a terrible thing had happened."

**Key constructions:**
- *maesta tanto malo* — ablative of cause with adjective
- *ut ... veniant* — indirect command (subjunctive)
- *facto maturatoque opus esse* — indirect discourse with ablative supine (*opus esse* + abl.)
- *rem atrocem incidisse* — indirect statement (acc. + inf.)

### 4.3 Passage: Horatius at the Bridge (2.10)

> **Tum Cocles "Tiberine pater," inquit, "te sancte precor, haec arma et hunc militem propitio flumine accipias."**

**Translation:** "Then Cocles said, 'Father Tiber, I solemnly pray to you: may you receive these arms and this soldier in a favorable stream.'"

- *Tiberine pater* — vocative (personification of the river)
- *te ... precor* — "I pray to you"
- *accipias* — present subjunctive (volitive / prayer)
- *propitio flumine* — ablative of manner/place

## 5. Prose Reading Strategy

### 5.1 The Five-Step Method

When facing a complex Latin sentence, follow this systematic approach:

**Step 1: Find the main verb**
Look for a finite verb not inside a subordinate clause. This anchors the sentence.

**Step 2: Find the subject**
The nominative noun or pronoun that goes with the main verb. It may be implicit in the verb ending.

**Step 3: Identify subordinate clauses**
Look for clause markers:

| Marker | Clause Type |
|--------|-------------|
| *cum* | temporal/causal/concessive |
| *ut / ne* | purpose/result/indirect command |
| *quod, quia, quoniam* | causal |
| *si, nisi* | conditional |
| *qui, quae, quod* | relative |
| *dum, donec, antequam* | temporal |

**Step 4: Parse within each clause**
For each clause, find its own verb, subject, and complements.

**Step 5: Reassemble in English**
Start with the main clause, then attach subordinate clauses in logical English order.

### 5.2 Worked Example

Consider this sentence from Caesar (BG 1.3):

> **His rebus adducti et auctoritate Orgetorigis permoti, constituerunt ea quae ad proficiscendum pertinerent comparare.**

**Step 1 — Main verb:** *constituerunt* ("they decided")

**Step 2 — Subject:** Implied *Helvetii* (from context), nom. pl.

**Step 3 — Subordinate elements:**
- *His rebus adducti* — participle phrase (abl. abs. or nom. pl. modifying subject)
- *auctoritate Orgetorigis permoti* — participle phrase (nom. pl.)
- *quae ad proficiscendum pertinerent* — relative clause (subjunctive — characteristic/purpose)

**Step 4 — Parse each part:**
- *adducti* (nom. pl. m. PPP) + *his rebus* (abl. of cause) = "led by these things"
- *permoti* (nom. pl. m. PPP) + *auctoritate Orgetorigis* (abl. of means + gen.) = "moved by the authority of Orgetorix"
- *ea ... comparare* = "to prepare those things"
- *quae pertinerent ad proficiscendum* = "which pertained to setting out"

**Step 5 — Translation:** "Led by these considerations and moved by the authority of Orgetorix, they decided to prepare what was needed for their departure."

### 5.3 Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Translating word by word left to right | Find the verb first; Latin word order is flexible |
| Confusing ablative absolute with main clause | Check whether the participle's subject is different from the main subject |
| Missing nested relative clauses | Track each *qui/quae/quod* to its antecedent |
| Ignoring subjunctive mood | Subjunctive in subordinate clauses signals purpose, result, or indirect speech |
| Forgetting that perfect passive = PPP + *esse* | *Divisa est* = "has been divided" (not two separate words) |

### Exercise 1: Caesar (BG 1.7)

Translate the following passage:

> **Caesar, cum id nuntiatum esset eos per provinciam nostram iter facere conari, maturat ab urbe proficisci et quam maximis potest itineribus in Galliam ulteriorem contendit.**

*Hints:*
- *cum ... esset* — cum + subjunctive (circumstantial)
- *id nuntiatum esset* — impersonal passive ("it had been announced")
- *eos ... conari* — acc. + inf. (indirect statement after *nuntiatum*)
- *iter facere* — "to march" (lit. "to make a journey")
- *maturat* — "he hastens"
- *quam maximis ... itineribus* — "by the greatest marches possible"

**Translation:** "When it had been reported to Caesar that they were attempting to march through our province, he hastened to set out from the city and advanced to Further Gaul by the longest marches he could."

**Parsing:**
- *cum id nuntiatum esset* — cum-clause (pluperfect subjunctive passive)
- *eos ... iter facere conari* — indirect statement: subject (*eos*), infinitive (*conari*), complementary infinitive (*iter facere*)
- *maturat ... proficisci* — main verb + complementary infinitive
- *quam maximis potest itineribus* — ablative of means; *quam* + superlative = "as great as possible"
- *contendit* — "he pressed on / marched"

### Exercise 2: Cicero (In Catilinam I.10)

Translate:

> **Castra sunt in Italia contra populum Romanum in Etruriae faucibus conlocata; crescit in dies singulos hostium numerus; eorum autem castrorum imperatorem ducemque hostium intra moenia atque adeo in senatu videmus.**

<details>

**Translation:** "A camp has been established in Italy against the Roman people in the passes of Etruria; the number of enemies grows daily; yet we see the commander of that camp and leader of the enemy within the walls and even in the senate."

**Key points:**
- *Castra sunt ... conlocata* — perfect passive (*conlocare*)
- *in Etruriae faucibus* — "in the passes of Etruria" (gen. + abl.)
- *in dies singulos* — "from day to day" (distributive)
- *eorum ... castrorum* — gen. pl. — "of that camp"
- *intra moenia atque adeo in senatu* — escalating: "within the walls and even in the senate"
</details>

### Exercise 3: Livy (Praefatio 9)

Translate:

> **Ad illa mihi pro se quisque acriter intendat animum, quae vita, qui mores fuerint, per quos viros quibusque artibus domi militiaeque et partum et auctum imperium sit.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

**Translation:** "Let each reader for himself keenly direct his attention to these questions: what manner of life, what customs there were; through what men and by what arts, at home and in the field, dominion was both won and extended."

**Parsing:**
- *pro se quisque* — "each person for himself" (idiomatic)
- *intendat* — jussive subjunctive ("let him direct")
- *quae vita, qui mores fuerint* — indirect question (perf. subj.)
- *per quos viros quibusque artibus* — indirect question (abl. of means)
- *domi militiaeque* — locative + *-que* — "at home and in the field"
- *partum et auctum ... sit* — perf. pass. subjunctive (*parere*, *augere*)
</details>
