# 18. Conditions and Complex Syntax (Conditiōnēs et Syntaxis Complexa)

## Learning Objectives
- Recognize and form the five standard types of Latin conditional sentences
- Distinguish between simple, future more vivid, future less vivid, present contrary-to-fact, and past contrary-to-fact conditions
- Handle mixed conditions where protasis and apodosis differ in type
- Construct relative clauses of characteristic using **quī** + subjunctive
- Form relative clauses of purpose and result
- Express fear clauses with **timeō nē/ut**
- Use proviso clauses with **dum/modo/dummodo** + subjunctive

## 1. Simple Conditions

Simple (or "open") conditions state a straightforward if-then relationship. The speaker takes no position on whether the condition is or will be fulfilled.

### 1.1 Present Simple

**sī + present indicative → present indicative**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** hoc dīcis, errās. | **If** you say this, you are wrong. |
| **Sī** pluit, domī maneō. | **If** it is raining, I stay at home. |

### 1.2 Past Simple

**sī + any past indicative → any past indicative**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** hoc dīxit, errāvit. | **If** he said this, he was wrong. |
| **Sī** id vīdistī, fēlīx fuistī. | **If** you saw it, you were fortunate. |

> **Key:** Both clauses use the **indicative**. The speaker presents the condition as a possible fact.

---

## 2. Future More Vivid

The speaker presents the condition as **likely** or **probable** in the future.

**sī + future indicative → future indicative**
(or: sī + future perfect → future indicative)

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** veniēs, gaudēbō. | **If** you (will) come, I shall rejoice. |
| **Sī** hoc fēceris, poenās dabis. | **If** you do (will have done) this, you will pay the penalty. |
| **Sī** mē rogābis, respondēbō. | **If** you ask me, I shall answer. |

> **Note:** The future perfect in the *sī*-clause (fēceris) emphasizes the completion of the condition before the result occurs. English often translates it as a simple present.

---

## 3. Future Less Vivid

The speaker presents the condition as **hypothetical** or **less likely** — a "should...would" scenario.

**sī + present subjunctive → present subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** hoc dīcās, errēs. | **If** you **should** say this, you **would** be wrong. |
| **Sī** veniat, gaudeam. | **If** he **should** come, I **would** rejoice. |
| **Sī** quis hoc faciat, stultus sit. | **If** anyone **were to** do this, he **would** be foolish. |

> **Tip:** This type is common with indefinite subjects (sī quis — "if anyone").

---

## 4. Present Contrary-to-Fact

The speaker asserts that the condition is **not true now** — "if X were the case (but it is not)..."

**sī + imperfect subjunctive → imperfect subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** adessēs, gaudērem. | **If** you **were** here, I **would** rejoice. (But you are not here.) |
| **Sī** hoc scīret, nōn errāret. | **If** he **knew** this, he **would not** be wrong. (But he does not know.) |
| **Sī** dīves essem, tē adiuvārem. | **If** I **were** rich, I **would** help you. (But I am not rich.) |

---

## 5. Past Contrary-to-Fact

The speaker asserts that the condition was **not true in the past** — "if X had been the case (but it was not)..."

**sī + pluperfect subjunctive → pluperfect subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** adfuissēs, gavīsus essem. | **If** you **had been** here, I **would have** rejoiced. |
| **Sī** hoc scīvisset, nōn errāvisset. | **If** he **had known** this, he **would not have** been wrong. |
| **Sī** Caesarem monuissēmus, vīxisset. | **If** we **had warned** Caesar, he **would have** lived. |

---

## 6. Mixed Conditions

Sometimes the protasis (if-clause) and apodosis (then-clause) belong to different condition types. This is common when the time frames differ.

### 6.1 Past Condition → Present Result

**sī + pluperfect subjunctive → imperfect subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** mīlitēs mīsisset, nunc tūtī essēmus. | **If** he **had sent** soldiers, we **would** now **be** safe. |
| **Sī** hoc didicissēs, nunc scīrēs. | **If** you **had learned** this, you **would** now **know**. |

### 6.2 Present Condition → Past Result

**sī + imperfect subjunctive → pluperfect subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sī** vir fortis essēs, nōn fūgissēs. | **If** you **were** a brave man, you **would not have** fled. |

## 7. Relative Clauses of Characteristic

A relative clause of characteristic describes a type or class of person/thing rather than a specific individual. It uses the **subjunctive**.

### 7.1 Formation

**antecedent + quī/quae/quod + subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Sunt **quī** hoc crēdant. | There are (those) **who** believe this. (a type of person) |
| Nēmō est **quī** hoc nesciat. | There is no one **who** does not know this. |
| Quis est **quī** hoc faciat? | Who is there **who** would do this? |
| Dignus est **quī** laudētur. | He is worthy **to be praised** (who should be praised). |
| Is nōn est **quī** mentīrī soleat. | He is not the sort of man **who** is accustomed to lie. |

### 7.2 Common Triggers

Relative clauses of characteristic are common after:

| Trigger | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Negative antecedents** (nēmō, nihil, nūllus) | Nēmō est quī hoc faciat. |
| **Indefinite antecedents** (sunt quī, is quī) | Sunt quī putent... |
| **Interrogative antecedents** (quis est quī?) | Quis est quī nesciat? |
| **Adjectives of worth** (dignus, indignus, idōneus) | Dignus est quī laudētur. |
| **Superlatives** or **ūnus, sōlus** | Sōlus est quī intellegat. |

## 8. Relative Clauses of Purpose and Result

### 8.1 Relative Clause of Purpose

A relative pronoun can replace **ut** in a purpose clause when the clause has a clear antecedent (see also Lesson 16):

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Lēgātōs mīsit **quī** pācem peterent. | He sent envoys **to seek** peace. |
| Locum invēnit **ubi** cōnsīderet. | He found a place **to settle**. |

### 8.2 Relative Clause of Result

After signal words (tam, tantus, is, tālis), the relative pronoun + subjunctive can express result:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Nūllum est perīculum **quod** nōn subeam. | There is no danger **that** I would not undergo. |
| Nōn is sum **quī** hoc timeam. | I am not the sort (of person) **who** would fear this. |

> **Overlap:** Relative clauses of characteristic and relative clauses of result can be hard to distinguish. Context and signal words guide interpretation.

---

## 9. Fear Clauses

Fear clauses have a **counterintuitive** negation pattern: **nē** = "that" (positive fear), **ut** (or **nē nōn**) = "that not" (negative fear).

### 9.1 Formation

**Verb of fearing + nē/ut + subjunctive**

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Timeō **nē veniat**. | I fear **that** he will come. |
| Timeō **ut veniat**. | I fear **that** he will **not** come. |
| Timeō **nē nōn veniat**. | I fear **that** he will **not** come. (= timeō ut veniat) |
| Verēbātur **nē caperētur**. | He was afraid **that** he would be captured. |
| Metuēbāmus **nē hostēs vincērent**. | We feared **that** the enemy would win. |

### 9.2 Verbs of Fearing

| Latin | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **timeō, timēre** | fear |
| **metuō, metuere** | fear, dread |
| **vereor, verērī** | fear, be anxious |
| **perīculum est** | there is danger |

### 9.3 Memory Aid

Think of it this way:
- **Timeō nē veniat** = "I fear lest he come" → I fear that he WILL come.
- **Timeō ut veniat** = "I fear as to whether he will come" → I fear that he will NOT come.

## 10. Proviso Clauses

Proviso clauses express a condition or stipulation ("provided that," "as long as"). They use **dum, modo, dummodo** + subjunctive.

### 10.1 Formation

| Conjunction | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| **dum** (+ subj.) | provided that |
| **modo** (+ subj.) | provided that, if only |
| **dummodo** (+ subj.) | provided that |

### 10.2 Negation

Proviso clauses are negated with **nē** (not *nōn*):

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Dum** nē ab amīcīs dēserar, nihil timeō. | **Provided that** I am not deserted by friends, I fear nothing. |

### 10.3 Examples

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ōderint, **dum** metuant. | Let them hate, **provided that** they fear. (Accius / Caligula) |
| Maneat, **modo** taceat. | Let him stay, **provided that** he is silent. |
| Omnia faciam, **dummodo** rēs pūblica salva sit. | I will do everything, **provided that** the republic is safe. |

## 11. Practice Exercises

### Exercise A: Identify the Condition Type

Classify each conditional as: **simple**, **future more vivid**, **future less vivid**, **present CTF**, or **past CTF**:

1. Sī hoc facis, bene facis.
2. Sī hoc fēceris, bene fēcerīs.
3. Sī hoc faciās, bene faciās.
4. Sī hoc facerēs, bene facerēs.
5. Sī hoc fēcissēs, bene fēcissēs.
6. Sī Caesar adesset, nōs dēfenderet.
7. Sī veniet, laetī erimus.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **Simple (present)** — sī + present indicative → present indicative. "If you are doing this, you are doing well."
2. **Future more vivid** — sī + future perfect → future perfect. "If you do this, you will have done well."
3. **Future less vivid** — sī + present subjunctive → present subjunctive. "If you should do this, you would do well."
4. **Present contrary-to-fact** — sī + imperfect subjunctive → imperfect subjunctive. "If you were doing this, you would be doing well." (But you are not.)
5. **Past contrary-to-fact** — sī + pluperfect subjunctive → pluperfect subjunctive. "If you had done this, you would have done well." (But you did not.)
6. **Present contrary-to-fact** — sī + imperfect subjunctive → imperfect subjunctive. "If Caesar were here, he would defend us." (But he is not.)
7. **Future more vivid** — sī + future indicative → future indicative. "If he comes, we shall be happy."

</details>

### Exercise B: Fear Clauses

Translate into English, paying careful attention to **nē** vs **ut**:

1. Timeō nē pater veniat.
2. Timeō ut pater veniat.
3. Verēbāmur nē hostēs urbem caperent.
4. Metuēbat nē nōn satis temporis esset.
5. Perīculum est nē omnēs pereāmus.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. "I fear **that** my father **will** come." (*nē* = positive fear)
2. "I fear **that** my father **will not** come." (*ut* = negative fear)
3. "We feared **that** the enemy **would** capture the city."
4. "He was afraid **that** there **would not be** enough time." (*nē nōn* = fear of negative)
5. "There is danger **that** we may all perish."

</details>

### Exercise C: Translate into Latin

1. If he were wise, he would not do this. (present CTF)
2. If you had come, you would have seen the spectacle. (past CTF)
3. There is no one who does not know this. (relative clause of characteristic)
4. I fear that the enemy will attack. (fear clause)
5. Let them hate, provided that they fear. (proviso clause)
6. If she should ask, I would tell the truth. (future less vivid)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **Sī sapiēns esset, hoc nōn faceret.** (sī + imperfect subj. → imperfect subj.)
2. **Sī vēnissēs, spectāculum vīdissēs.** (sī + pluperfect subj. → pluperfect subj.)
3. **Nēmō est quī hoc nesciat.** (relative clause of characteristic; present subj.)
4. **Timeō nē hostēs oppugnent.** (fear clause; nē + present subj.)
5. **Ōderint, dum metuant.** (proviso; dum + present subj.)
6. **Sī roget, vērum dīcam.** (sī + present subj. → present subj.)

</details>

### Exercise D: Mixed Conditions and Complex Syntax

Translate the following into English:

1. Sī hoc tum fēcissēs, nunc tūtus essēs.
2. Dignus est quī laudētur.
3. Metuēbant nē Caesar revenīret.
4. Omnia faciam dummodo mē adiuvēs.
5. Nōn is sum quī mentiar.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. "If you had done this then, you would now be safe." (Mixed: past protasis + present apodosis)
2. "He is worthy to be praised." (Relative clause of characteristic with *dignus*)
3. "They were afraid that Caesar would return." (Fear clause, secondary sequence)
4. "I will do everything provided that you help me." (Proviso clause)
5. "I am not the sort of person who would lie." (Relative clause of characteristic)

</details>

## Quick Reference Card

### Conditional Types

| Type | Protasis (sī-clause) | Apodosis (then-clause) |
|------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Simple** | sī + indicative (any tense) | indicative (any tense) |
| **Future More Vivid** | sī + future / fut. perf. | future indicative |
| **Future Less Vivid** | sī + present subjunctive | present subjunctive |
| **Present CTF** | sī + imperfect subjunctive | imperfect subjunctive |
| **Past CTF** | sī + pluperfect subjunctive | pluperfect subjunctive |

### Special Clause Types

| Clause | Formation | Negation |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Relative of Characteristic | quī + subjunctive | quī nōn |
| Fear Clause | timeō + nē/ut + subj. | nē = "that"; ut = "that not" |
| Proviso | dum/modo/dummodo + subj. | nē |
