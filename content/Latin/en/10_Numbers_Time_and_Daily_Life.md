# 10. Numbers, Time, and Daily Life (Numeri, Tempus, Vita Cotidiana)

## Learning Objectives

- Count in Latin using cardinal numbers from 1 to 1000
- Decline the irregular numerals ūnus, duo, trēs, and mīlle
- Use ordinal numbers (prīmus through decimus) with correct agreement
- Understand the Roman calendar system (Kalendae, Nōnae, Īdūs)
- Tell time using Roman day divisions (hora, vigilia)
- Build vocabulary for daily life in Rome (food, clothing, housing, family, occupations)
- Read and translate passages about Roman daily life

## 1. Cardinal Numbers

### Numbers 1-20

| Number | Latin | Number | Latin |
|--------|-------|--------|-------|
| 1 | ūnus, ūna, ūnum | 11 | ūndecim |
| 2 | duo, duae, duo | 12 | duodecim |
| 3 | trēs, tria | 13 | trēdecim |
| 4 | quattuor | 14 | quattuordecim |
| 5 | quīnque | 15 | quīndecim |
| 6 | sex | 16 | sēdecim |
| 7 | septem | 17 | septendecim |
| 8 | octō | 18 | duodēvīgintī |
| 9 | novem | 19 | ūndēvīgintī |
| 10 | decem | 20 | vīgintī |

> **Note**: 18 and 19 are formed by subtraction: **duodēvīgintī** = "two from twenty" and **ūndēvīgintī** = "one from twenty." This subtractive pattern also appears in 28/29, 38/39, etc.

### Tens, Hundreds, Thousands

| Number | Latin | Number | Latin |
|--------|-------|--------|-------|
| 10 | decem | 100 | centum |
| 20 | vīgintī | 200 | ducentī, -ae, -a |
| 30 | trīgintā | 300 | trecentī, -ae, -a |
| 40 | quadrāgintā | 400 | quadringentī, -ae, -a |
| 50 | quīnquāgintā | 500 | quīngentī, -ae, -a |
| 60 | sexāgintā | 600 | sescentī, -ae, -a |
| 70 | septuāgintā | 700 | septingentī, -ae, -a |
| 80 | octōgintā | 800 | octingentī, -ae, -a |
| 90 | nōnāgintā | 900 | nōngentī, -ae, -a |
| 100 | centum | 1000 | mīlle |

> Numbers 4-100 and mīlle (singular) are **indeclinable** — they do not change form. Numbers 200-900 decline like 1st/2nd declension plural adjectives.

### Declining ūnus, duo, trēs

**ūnus, ūna, ūnum** — *one* (declines like a 1st/2nd declension adjective, singular only)

| Case | M. | F. | N. |
|------|-----|-----|-----|
| Nom. | ūnus | ūna | ūnum |
| Gen. | ūnīus | ūnīus | ūnīus |
| Dat. | ūnī | ūnī | ūnī |
| Acc. | ūnum | ūnam | ūnum |
| Abl. | ūnō | ūnā | ūnō |

> **Note**: The gen. sg. **ūnīus** and dat. sg. **ūnī** follow the pronominal pattern (like ille: illīus, illī).

**duo, duae, duo** — *two*

| Case | M. | F. | N. |
|------|------|------|------|
| Nom. | duo | duae | duo |
| Gen. | duōrum | duārum | duōrum |
| Dat. | duōbus | duābus | duōbus |
| Acc. | duōs (duo) | duās | duo |
| Abl. | duōbus | duābus | duōbus |

**trēs, tria** — *three*

| Case | M./F. | N. |
|------|-------|----|
| Nom. | trēs | tria |
| Gen. | trium | trium |
| Dat. | tribus | tribus |
| Acc. | trēs | tria |
| Abl. | tribus | tribus |

### Mīlle vs. Mīlia

```
MĪLLE: A SPECIAL NUMBER
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Singular: mīlle = indeclinable ADJECTIVE     │
│   mīlle mīlitēs = a thousand soldiers        │
│   mīlle passūs = a thousand paces (= 1 mile) │
│                                              │
│ Plural: mīlia = declinable NEUTER NOUN       │
│   (3rd decl. i-stem neuter)                  │
│   mīlia, mīlium, mīlibus, mīlia, mīlibus   │
│                                              │
│   tria mīlia mīlitum =                       │
│   three thousands OF soldiers (+ gen.)        │
│   = three thousand soldiers                   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Example Sentences

- **Ūnus vir vēnit.** — *One man came.*
- **Duae fēminae in hortō ambulābant.** — *Two women were walking in the garden.*
- **Trēs librōs legī.** — *I read three books.*
- **Centum mīlitēs in castrīs erant.** — *A hundred soldiers were in the camp.*
- **Mīlle passūs ambulāvimus.** — *We walked a thousand paces (one mile).*
- **Quinque mīlia passuum iter fēcērunt.** — *They marched five thousand paces (five miles).*

## 2. Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal numbers are **adjectives** and decline like regular 1st/2nd declension adjectives (-us, -a, -um).

| Cardinal | Ordinal | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| 1 - ūnus | **prīmus, -a, -um** | first |
| 2 - duo | **secundus, -a, -um** (alter) | second |
| 3 - trēs | **tertius, -a, -um** | third |
| 4 - quattuor | **quārtus, -a, -um** | fourth |
| 5 - quīnque | **quīntus, -a, -um** | fifth |
| 6 - sex | **sextus, -a, -um** | sixth |
| 7 - septem | **septimus, -a, -um** | seventh |
| 8 - octō | **octāvus, -a, -um** | eighth |
| 9 - novem | **nōnus, -a, -um** | ninth |
| 10 - decem | **decimus, -a, -um** | tenth |
| 20 - vīgintī | **vīcēsimus, -a, -um** | twentieth |
| 100 - centum | **centēsimus, -a, -um** | hundredth |
| 1000 - mīlle | **mīlēsimus, -a, -um** | thousandth |

> **alter** (the other of two) is sometimes used instead of **secundus**. It declines: alter, altera, alterum (gen. alterīus, dat. alterī).

### Example Sentences

- **Prīmus in bellō, prīmus in pāce.** — *First in war, first in peace.*
- **Legiō decima fortissima erat.** — *The tenth legion was the bravest.*
- **Tertium cōnsulem creāvērunt.** — *They elected (him) consul for the third time.*
- **Annō quārtō bellī Pūnicī secundī...** — *In the fourth year of the Second Punic War...*

## 3. The Roman Calendar

The Roman calendar was fundamentally different from our modern one. Dates were counted **backward** from three fixed reference points in each month.

### The Three Reference Points

| Latin Name | English | March, May, Jul, Oct | Other Months |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Kalendae** (Kal.) | Kalends | 1st | 1st |
| **Nōnae** (Non.) | Nones | 7th | 5th |
| **Īdūs** (Id.) | Ides | 15th | 13th |

> **Mnemonic**: "In March, July, October, May / the Nones fall on the 7th day" (and Ides on the 15th). All other months: Nones on the 5th, Ides on the 13th.

### How Roman Dating Worked

Romans counted **backward inclusively** from the next reference point:

```
ROMAN DATE CALCULATION
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. If the date IS a reference point:             │
│    Kalendīs Iānuāriīs = On the Kalends of Jan.  │
│    (= January 1st)                               │
│                                                  │
│ 2. If the day BEFORE a reference point:           │
│    Prīdiē Kalendās Februāriās                    │
│    = The day before the Kalends of Feb.           │
│    (= January 31st)                              │
│                                                  │
│ 3. Otherwise, count BACKWARD (inclusive):         │
│    ante diem [number] [reference] [month]         │
│                                                  │
│    ante diem III Nōnās Iānuāriās                  │
│    = 3rd day before the Nones of January          │
│    Nones = Jan. 5th; counting back: 5,4,3         │
│    = January 3rd                                  │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Declension of Calendar Terms

- **Kalendae, -ārum** (f. pl.) — 1st declension plural
- **Nōnae, -ārum** (f. pl.) — 1st declension plural
- **Īdūs, -uum** (f. pl.) — 4th declension plural

### Example Dates

| Roman Expression | Modern Date |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Kalendīs Iānuāriīs | January 1 |
| Nōnīs Mārtiīs | March 7 |
| Īdibus Mārtiīs | March 15 (the famous "Ides of March") |
| ante diem IV Kalendās Aprīlēs | March 29 |
| prīdiē Kalendās Maiās | April 30 |
| ante diem VIII Īdūs Decembrēs | December 6 |

> **한국어 화자 참고**: The Roman system is like counting "5일 전" (5 days before) from a fixed date, but counting inclusively (including both the start and end dates). For example, ante diem III Nōnās = Nones minus 3 + 1 in our counting.

### The Roman Months

| Latin | English | Named After |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Iānuārius | January | Iānus (god of beginnings) |
| Februārius | February | Februa (purification rites) |
| Mārtius | March | Mārs (god of war) |
| Aprīlis | April | aperīre (to open) or Aphrodite |
| Māius | May | Māia (goddess of growth) |
| Iūnius | June | Iūnō (queen of gods) |
| Iūlius | July | Iūlius Caesar |
| Augustus | August | Augustus Caesar |
| September | September | septem (seven — originally 7th month) |
| Octōber | October | octō (eight) |
| November | November | novem (nine) |
| December | December | decem (ten) |

## 4. Telling Time

### The Roman Hour (Hora)

Romans divided daylight into **12 equal hours** (hōrae) and nighttime into **4 watches** (vigiliae). Since the hours were proportional to available daylight, their length varied by season.

```
THE ROMAN DAY
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ DAYLIGHT: 12 hōrae (sunrise to sunset)  │
│                                          │
│ Hōra prīma    ≈ 6:00 AM (sunrise)       │
│ Hōra tertia   ≈ 9:00 AM                 │
│ Hōra sexta    ≈ 12:00 PM (noon)          │
│ Hōra nōna     ≈ 3:00 PM                 │
│ Hōra duodecima ≈ 6:00 PM (sunset)        │
│                                          │
│ NIGHTTIME: 4 vigiliae (watches)          │
│                                          │
│ Vigilia prīma  ≈ 6 PM - 9 PM            │
│ Vigilia secunda ≈ 9 PM - 12 AM           │
│ Vigilia tertia  ≈ 12 AM - 3 AM           │
│ Vigilia quārta  ≈ 3 AM - 6 AM            │
└──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Key Time Vocabulary

| Latin | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| hōra, -ae (f.) | hour |
| diēs, diēī (m.) | day |
| nox, noctis (f.) | night |
| lūx, lūcis (f.) | light, daylight |
| merīdiēs, -ēī (m.) | midday, noon |
| prīma lūce | at first light, at dawn |
| mediā nocte | at midnight |
| māne | in the morning (indecl.) |
| vesperī | in the evening |
| herī | yesterday |
| hodiē | today |
| crās | tomorrow |
| cotīdiē | every day, daily |

### Example Sentences

- **Hōrā tertiā ad forum vēnit.** — *He came to the forum at the third hour (≈ 9 AM).*
- **Prīmā lūce exercitus profectus est.** — *At first light, the army set out.*
- **Mediā nocte vigilēs mūrōs custōdiēbant.** — *At midnight, the watchmen were guarding the walls.*
- **Crās Rōmam iter faciēmus.** — *Tomorrow we will journey to Rome.*

## 6. Reading Practice

### Passage: Diēs Rōmānī — A Roman Day

> Prīmā lūce Mārcus surgit. Iēntāculum parvum — pānem et cāseum cum melle — edit. Tunicam et togam induit. Hōrā secundā ad forum cum servō ambulat. Ibi amīcōs salūtat et negōtia agit.

> Hōrā sextā prandium breve sūmit: olīvās et pānem. Post prandium ad balnea it. In balneīs cum amīcīs loquitur et corpus exercet.

> Hōrā nōnā domum redit. Cēna magna in triclīniō parātur: piscis, carō, ōva, vīnum cum aquā. Familia tōta in lectīs recumbit et cēnat. Post cēnam fābulās narrant.

> Nocte, prīmā vigiliā, Mārcus in cubiculum it et dormit.

Translation:

> At first light, Marcus gets up. He eats a small breakfast — bread and cheese with honey. He puts on his tunic and toga. At the second hour he walks to the forum with his slave. There he greets friends and conducts business.

> At the sixth hour he takes a brief lunch: olives and bread. After lunch he goes to the baths. In the baths he talks with friends and exercises his body.

> At the ninth hour he returns home. A grand dinner is prepared in the dining room: fish, meat, eggs, wine with water. The whole family reclines on couches and dines. After dinner they tell stories.

> At night, during the first watch, Marcus goes to his bedroom and sleeps.
