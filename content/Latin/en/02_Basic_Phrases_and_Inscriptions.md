### 1.1 Greetings

| Latin | Pronunciation (Classical) | Meaning | Usage |
|-------|--------------------------|---------|-------|
| Salvē! | /ˈsal.weː/ | Hello! (to one person) | Standard greeting |
| Salvēte! | /sal.ˈweː.tɛ/ | Hello! (to multiple people) | Plural greeting |
| Avē! | /ˈa.weː/ | Hail! (to one person) | More formal greeting |
| Avēte! | /a.ˈweː.tɛ/ | Hail! (to multiple people) | Formal plural |

| Quid agitis? | /kwɪd a.ˈgɪ.tɪs/ | How are you? (pl.) | Plural form |

### 1.2 Responses

| Latin | Pronunciation | Meaning |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| Bene! | /ˈbɛ.nɛ/ | Well! |

### 1.3 Farewells

| Latin | Pronunciation | Meaning | Usage |
|-------|---------------|---------|-------|
| Valē! | /ˈwa.leː/ | Farewell! (to one) | Standard goodbye |
| Valēte! | /wa.ˈleː.tɛ/ | Farewell! (to many) | Plural goodbye |
| Cūrā ut valeās | /ˈkuː.ra ʊt wa.ˈlɛ.aːs/ | Take care! | Literally "See to it that you are well" |
| In pace | /ɪn ˈpa.kɛ/ | In peace | Formal parting |
| Bonā nocte | /ˈbɔ.naː ˈnɔk.tɛ/ | Good night | Evening farewell |

> **Note**: *Salvē* literally means "Be well!" (imperative of *salveō*). Similarly, *valē* means "Be strong/healthy!" (imperative of *valeō*). Latin greetings are essentially wishes for the other person's well-being.

### 1.4 Introductions

```
Asking someone's name:
  Quid nōmen tibi est?    /kwɪd ˈnoː.mɛn ˈtɪ.bɪ ɛst/
  "What is your name?"     (Literally: "What name is to you?")

Responding:
  Mihi nōmen est Marcus.  /ˈmɪ.hɪ ˈnoː.mɛn ɛst ˈmar.kʊs/
  "My name is Marcus."    (Literally: "To me the name is Marcus.")

  Nōmen mihi est Iūlia.   /ˈnoː.mɛn ˈmɪ.hɪ ɛst ˈjuː.lɪ.a/
  "My name is Julia."

Meeting someone:
  Tē salūtō.              /teː sa.ˈluː.toː/
  "I greet you."
```

## 4. Reading Roman Inscriptions

### 4.1 SPQR and Political Abbreviations

> **S . P . Q . R**
>
> S(enātus) P(opulus)Q(ue) R(ōmānus)
> /sɛ.ˈnaː.tʊs pɔ.pʊ.ˈlʊs.kwɛ roː.ˈmaː.nʊs/
>
> "The Senate and People of Rome"
>
> This was the official emblem of the Roman Republic and Empire. It appeared on standards, buildings, coins, and official documents. It remains the symbol of the city of Rome today — you can see it on manhole covers and public buildings throughout the modern city.
>
> -que = "and" (enclitic, attached to the second word)
> Compare: et = "and" (separate word)
> Senātus et Populus = Senātus Populusque

## 6. Simple Sentence Patterns

### 6.1 Basic Word Order: Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)

> **Latin Default Word Order: SOV**
>
> Latin's default word order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV), the same as Korean and Japanese!
>
> - Korean: 마르쿠스가 율리아를 사랑한다.
> - Latin: Mārcus Iūliam amat.
> - English: Marcus loves Julia.
> - (S) (O) (V)
>
> However, because Latin uses case endings (not word order) to show grammatical function, word order is flexible. All of these mean the same thing:
> - Mārcus Iūliam amat. (SOV — default)
> - Mārcus amat Iūliam. (SVO)
> - Iūliam Mārcus amat. (OSV)
> - Amat Mārcus Iūliam. (VSO)
>
> The case endings (-us = subject, -am = object) tell you WHO does WHAT regardless of word position.

### 6.2 Pattern 1: Subject + Verb

The simplest Latin sentences have just a subject and a verb. Often the subject pronoun is dropped (like Korean and Spanish).

| Latin | Literal | Translation |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Puella cantat. | Girl sings. | The girl sings. |
| Puer ambulat. | Boy walks. | The boy walks. |
| Nauta labōrat. | Sailor works. | The sailor works. |
| Fēmina vocat. | Woman calls. | The woman calls. |
| Agricola arat. | Farmer plows. | The farmer plows. |

> **Note on articles**: Latin has NO articles (no "the" or "a"). *Puella* can mean "the girl," "a girl," or simply "girl" depending on context — similar to how Korean lacks articles.

### 6.3 Pattern 2: Subject + Verb "to be" + Complement

| Latin | Literal | Translation |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Rōma est magna. | Rome is great. | Rome is great. |
| Puella est laeta. | Girl is happy. | The girl is happy. |
| Poēta est bonus. | Poet is good. | The poet is good. |
| Aqua est frīgida. | Water is cold. | The water is cold. |
| Vir est Rōmānus. | Man is Roman. | The man is Roman. |

### 6.4 Pattern 3: Subject + Object + Verb

| Latin | Literal | Translation |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Puella rosam amat. | Girl rose loves. | The girl loves the rose. |
| Nauta terram videt. | Sailor land sees. | The sailor sees the land. |
| Agricola aquam portat. | Farmer water carries. | The farmer carries water. |
| Poēta fābulam nārrat. | Poet story tells. | The poet tells a story. |
| Fēmina viam mōnstrat. | Woman road shows. | The woman shows the road. |

## Quick Reference Card

> **Basic Latin Phrases Quick Reference**
>
> **GREETINGS:**
> - Salvē / Salvēte = Hello (sg. / pl.)
> - Avē / Avēte = Hail (sg. / pl.)
> - Quid agis? = How are you?
> - Bene! = Well!
>
> **FAREWELLS:**
> - Valē / Valēte = Farewell (sg. / pl.)
>
> **ESSENTIAL:**
> - Grātiās tibi agō = Thank you
> - Quaesō = Please
> - Ita / Minimē = Yes / No
> - Ignōsce mihi = Excuse me
>
> **FAMOUS PHRASES:**
> - Carpe diem = Seize the day
> - Vēnī, vīdī, vīcī = I came, I saw, I conquered
> - Cogitō ergō sum = I think, therefore I am
> - Tempus fugit = Time flies
>
> **SENTENCE PATTERNS:**
> - S + V: Puella cantat. (The girl sings.)
> - S + est + C: Rōma est magna. (Rome is great.)
> - S + O + V: Puer rosam videt. (The boy sees the rose.)
> - Default order: SOV (like Korean!)
