## 1. Introduction to the Case System

### 1.1 What Are Cases?

In English, word order tells you who does what: "The dog bites the man" vs. "The man bites the dog." In Latin, **case endings** on nouns serve this function. The ending of a noun tells you its role in the sentence, regardless of word order.

> **The Six Latin Cases**
>
> - **Nominative**: Subject (Korean: 이/가)
> - **Genitive**: Possession, "of" (Korean: 의)
> - **Dative**: Indirect object, "to" (Korean: 에게/한테)
> - **Accusative**: Direct object (Korean: 을/를)
> - **Ablative**: Various, "by/with/in" (Korean: 으로/에서)
> - **Vocative**: Direct address (Korean: 아/야)
>
> ※ The parallel to Korean particles (조사) is remarkably close. Both languages use endings/particles instead of relying on word order alone.

### 1.2 Case Functions in Detail

| Case | Abbreviation | Primary Function | Example (English) |
|------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Nominative** | Nom. | Subject of the sentence | **The girl** sings. |
| **Genitive** | Gen. | Possession, "of" | The girl **of the farmer** (= the farmer's girl) |
| **Dative** | Dat. | Indirect object, "to/for" | He gives a rose **to the girl**. |
| **Accusative** | Acc. | Direct object | He sees **the girl**. |
| **Ablative** | Abl. | Means, manner, place, time ("by/with/from/in") | He walks **with the girl**. |
| **Vocative** | Voc. | Direct address | **O girl**, come here! |

### 1.3 How Case Endings Work

```
Example with puella (girl):

  Puella cantat.             The girl (nom.) sings.
  Fīlia agricolae cantat.   The daughter of the farmer (gen.) sings.
  Puellae rosam dat.         He gives a rose to the girl (dat.).
  Puellam videt.             He sees the girl (acc.).
  Cum puellā ambulat.        He walks with the girl (abl.).
  Puella, venī!              Girl (voc.), come!

Notice: the ENDING of "puella" changes based on its role:
  puella  (nom.)  →  puellae  (gen.)  →  puellae  (dat.)
  puellam (acc.)  →  puellā   (abl.)  →  puella   (voc.)
```

### 1.4 Number: Singular and Plural

Every noun form has both a case AND a number (singular or plural). This means each declension has 12 forms total (6 cases × 2 numbers), though some forms are identical.

## 2. Gender in Latin

### 2.1 Three Genders

Latin has three grammatical genders: **masculine**, **feminine**, and **neuter**.

> **Latin Grammatical Gender**
>
> - **Masculine (M)**: servus (slave), amīcus (friend), puer (boy)
> - **Feminine (F)**: puella (girl), rosa (rose), terra (earth)
> - **Neuter (N)**: bellum (war), dōnum (gift), templum (temple)
>
> Gender is partly semantic, partly arbitrary:
>
> Semantic (logical):
> - Male beings → masculine: vir (man), fīlius (son)
> - Female beings → feminine: fēmina (woman), fīlia (daughter)
>
> Arbitrary (grammatical):
> - Why is mūrus (wall) masculine?
> - Why is via (road) feminine?
> - Why is bellum (war) neuter?
> - → No logical reason; you must memorize the gender.
>
> The DECLENSION helps predict gender:
> - 1st Declension (-a) → mostly feminine
> - 2nd Declension (-us, -er) → mostly masculine
> - 2nd Declension (-um) → always neuter

### 2.2 Why Gender Matters

Gender affects:
1. **Adjective agreement** — Adjectives must match their noun's gender, number, and case.
2. **Pronoun reference** — Pronouns must match the gender of their antecedent.
3. **Declension pattern** — Gender often determines which set of endings a noun uses.

> **For Korean speakers**: Korean does not have grammatical gender, so this concept may feel unfamiliar. Think of gender as a classification system — each noun belongs to a "group" that determines how other words around it behave.

## 3. First Declension

### 3.1 Overview

First Declension nouns are characterized by the stem vowel **-a-**. They are mostly feminine, with a few masculine exceptions (mainly occupations and natural males).

```
Stem vowel: -a-
Gender: Almost always FEMININE
Dictionary entry: puella, -ae, f. (girl)
                  ↑       ↑    ↑
                  nom.sg  gen.sg  gender
```

### 3.2 Full Paradigm: puella, -ae, f. (girl)

| Case | Singular | Plural | Function |
|------|----------|--------|----------|
| Nominative | puell**a** | puell**ae** | Subject |
| Genitive | puell**ae** | puell**ārum** | "of" / possession |
| Dative | puell**ae** | puell**īs** | "to/for" |
| Accusative | puell**am** | puell**ās** | Direct object |
| Ablative | puell**ā** | puell**īs** | "by/with/from/in" |
| Vocative | puell**a** | puell**ae** | Direct address |

> **First Declension Endings (memorize these!)**
>
> | | Singular | Plural |
> |---|---|---|
> | Nom. | -a | -ae |
> | Gen. | -ae | -ārum |
> | Dat. | -ae | -īs |
> | Acc. | -am | -ās |
> | Abl. | -ā | -īs |
> | Voc. | -a | -ae |
>
> ※ Nom. sg. and Voc. sg. are identical (-a)
> ※ Gen. sg., Dat. sg., Nom. pl., and Voc. pl. are all -ae
> ※ Dat. pl. and Abl. pl. are identical (-īs)
> ※ Abl. sg. (-ā) has a LONG vowel (distinguishes from nom.)

### 3.3 Common First Declension Nouns

| Latin | Gen. sg. | Gender | Meaning |
|-------|----------|--------|---------|
| aqua | aquae | f. | water |
| fēmina | fēminae | f. | woman |
| fīlia | fīliae | f. | daughter |
| fortūna | fortūnae | f. | fortune, luck |
| Itālia | Itāliae | f. | Italy |
| lingua | linguae | f. | tongue, language |
| lūna | lūnae | f. | moon |
| patria | patriae | f. | fatherland, country |
| pecūnia | pecūniae | f. | money |
| poēta | poētae | **m.** | poet (masculine!) |
| puella | puellae | f. | girl |
| rosa | rosae | f. | rose |
| terra | terrae | f. | earth, land |
| via | viae | f. | road, way |
| vīta | vītae | f. | life |

### 3.4 Masculine First Declension Nouns

A small number of 1st declension nouns are **masculine** — typically male occupations or male persons:

| Latin | Meaning | Note |
|-------|---------|------|
| agricola, -ae, m. | farmer | Most common example |
| nauta, -ae, m. | sailor | |
| poēta, -ae, m. | poet | From Greek |
| pīrāta, -ae, m. | pirate | From Greek |
| incola, -ae, m. | inhabitant | |

> These decline exactly like *puella* — the **endings** are the same; only the **gender** differs. This matters for adjective agreement: *bonus agricola* (good farmer) uses a masculine adjective.

### 3.5 First Declension in Action

```
Puella rosam amat.
puella (nom. sg.) = "the girl" → subject
rosam (acc. sg.)  = "the rose" → direct object
amat              = "loves"    → verb
→ "The girl loves the rose."

Agricolae fīlia cantat.
agricolae (gen. sg.) = "of the farmer" → possession
fīlia (nom. sg.)     = "the daughter"  → subject
cantat               = "sings"         → verb
→ "The farmer's daughter sings."

Fēminae pecūniam dant.
fēminae (dat. sg.)  = "to the woman"  → indirect object
pecūniam (acc. sg.)  = "money"         → direct object
dant                  = "they give"     → verb
→ "They give money to the woman."

Cum puellīs ambulat.
cum (prep. + abl.)   = "with"
puellīs (abl. pl.)  = "the girls"
ambulat              = "he/she walks"
→ "He/She walks with the girls."
```

## 4. Second Declension Masculine

### 4.1 Overview

Second Declension nouns have a stem vowel of **-o-** (which sometimes appears as -u-). Masculine nouns of this declension typically end in **-us** or **-er** in the nominative singular.

### 4.2 Type 1: -us Nouns (amīcus pattern)

Full paradigm: **amīcus, -ī, m.** (friend)

| Case | Singular | Plural | Function |
|------|----------|--------|----------|
| Nominative | amīc**us** | amīc**ī** | Subject |
| Genitive | amīc**ī** | amīc**ōrum** | "of" / possession |
| Dative | amīc**ō** | amīc**īs** | "to/for" |
| Accusative | amīc**um** | amīc**ōs** | Direct object |
| Ablative | amīc**ō** | amīc**īs** | "by/with/from/in" |
| Vocative | amīc**e** | amīc**ī** | Direct address |

> **Second Declension -us Endings (memorize these!)**
>
> | | Singular | Plural |
> |---|---|---|
> | Nom. | -us | -ī |
> | Gen. | -ī | -ōrum |
> | Dat. | -ō | -īs |
> | Acc. | -um | -ōs |
> | Abl. | -ō | -īs |
> | Voc. | -e | -ī |
>
> ※ Gen. sg., Nom. pl., and Voc. pl. are all -ī
> ※ Dat. sg. and Abl. sg. are identical (-ō)
> ※ Dat. pl. and Abl. pl. are identical (-īs)
> ※ Voc. sg. is -e (unique among declensions!)
> ※ Special: nouns in -ius have Voc. sg. in -ī: fīlius → fīlī (O son!)

### 4.3 Type 2: -er Nouns

Some 2nd declension masculine nouns end in **-er** in the nominative. There are two subtypes:

**Subtype A — keeps the -e- throughout (puer pattern):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | pu**er** | pu**erī** |
| Gen. | pu**erī** | pu**erōrum** |
| Dat. | pu**erō** | pu**erīs** |
| Acc. | pu**erum** | pu**erōs** |
| Abl. | pu**erō** | pu**erīs** |
| Voc. | pu**er** | pu**erī** |

**Subtype B — drops the -e- after nominative (ager pattern):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | ag**er** | agr**ī** |
| Gen. | agr**ī** | agr**ōrum** |
| Dat. | agr**ō** | agr**īs** |
| Acc. | agr**um** | agr**ōs** |
| Abl. | agr**ō** | agr**īs** |
| Voc. | ag**er** | agr**ī** |

> **How to tell which -er subtype? Check the GENITIVE SINGULAR.**
>
> - puer, puerī → stem = puer- (keeps -e-)
> - ager, agrī → stem = agr- (drops -e-)
>
> The genitive singular (the second form in a dictionary entry) reveals the true stem. Always learn nouns with their genitive!
>
> - **Keeps -e-**: puer (boy), socer (father-in-law), līber (free — as adjective), vir\* (man)
> - **Drops -e-**: ager (field), liber (book), magister (teacher), minister (servant)
>
> \*vir, virī is slightly irregular (2nd decl., not -er type)

## 5. Second Declension Neuter

### 5.1 Overview

Second Declension neuter nouns end in **-um** in the nominative singular. They follow the same basic pattern as the -us masculines but with two critical differences.

### 5.2 Full Paradigm: bellum, -ī, n. (war)

| Case | Singular | Plural | Function |
|------|----------|--------|----------|
| Nominative | bell**um** | bell**a** | Subject |
| Genitive | bell**ī** | bell**ōrum** | "of" / possession |
| Dative | bell**ō** | bell**īs** | "to/for" |
| Accusative | bell**um** | bell**a** | Direct object |
| Ablative | bell**ō** | bell**īs** | "by/with/from/in" |
| Vocative | bell**um** | bell**a** | Direct address |

> **The NEUTER RULE (applies to ALL declensions!)**
>
> In ALL neuter nouns, across ALL five declensions:
>
> 1. Nominative = Accusative = Vocative (always identical)
>    - Singular: -um = -um = -um
>    - Plural: -a = -a = -a
>
> 2. Nominative/Accusative PLURAL always ends in -a
>
> This is one of the most reliable rules in Latin grammar. Memorize it now — it will save you many times.
>
> Compare masculine vs neuter:
>
> | | Masculine (-us) | Neuter (-um) |
> |---|---|---|
> | Nom. sg. | amīcus | bellum |
> | Acc. sg. | amīcum | bellum (same as nom.) |
> | Nom. pl. | amīcī | bella (ends in -a) |
> | Acc. pl. | amīcōs | bella (same as nom.) |

### 5.3 Common Second Declension Neuter Nouns

| Latin | Gen. sg. | Meaning |
|-------|----------|---------|
| arma, -ōrum (pl. only) | armōrum | arms, weapons |
| bellum, -ī | bellī | war |
| cōnsilium, -ī | cōnsiliī | plan, advice |
| dōnum, -ī | dōnī | gift |
| factum, -ī | factī | deed, act |
| forum, -ī | forī | forum, marketplace |
| imperium, -ī | imperiī | command, empire |
| oppidum, -ī | oppidī | town |
| perīculum, -ī | perīculī | danger |
| praemium, -ī | praemiī | reward, prize |
| regnum, -ī | regnī | kingdom, realm |
| studium, -ī | studiī | eagerness, study |
| templum, -ī | templī | temple |
| verbum, -ī | verbī | word |
| vīnum, -ī | vīnī | wine |

## 6. Noun-Case Function Summary

### 6.1 Complete Function Chart

> **Case Functions — Summary with Examples**
>
> **NOMINATIVE** — Subject / Predicate Nominative
> - Puella cantat. — The girl sings.
> - Puella est poēta. — The girl is a poet.
>
> **GENITIVE** — Possession / "of" / Partitive
> - Fīlia agricolae — The daughter of the farmer
> - Magna pars populī — A great part of the people
>
> **DATIVE** — Indirect Object / "to, for"
> - Puerō librum dat. — He gives a book to the boy.
> - Patriae labōrat. — He works for the country.
>
> **ACCUSATIVE** — Direct Object / Motion Toward
> - Puellam videt. — He sees the girl.
> - Ad Italiam nāvigat. — He sails to Italy.
>
> **ABLATIVE** — Means / Manner / Accompaniment / Place / Time
> - Gladiō pugnat. — He fights with a sword. (means)
> - Cum amīcō ambulat. — He walks with a friend. (accomp.)
> - In Italiā habitat. — He lives in Italy. (place)
> - Prīmō annō — In the first year (time)
>
> **VOCATIVE** — Direct Address
> - Mārce, venī! — Marcus, come!
> - Ō amīcī, audīte! — O friends, listen!

### 6.2 Prepositions with Cases

Latin prepositions take either the accusative or the ablative:

| Preposition | Case | Meaning |
|------------|------|---------|
| ad | + acc. | to, toward |
| ante | + acc. | before, in front of |
| contrā | + acc. | against |
| in | + acc. | into, onto (motion toward) |
| per | + acc. | through, by means of |
| post | + acc. | after, behind |
| trāns | + acc. | across |
| ā / ab | + abl. | away from, by (agent) |
| cum | + abl. | with |
| dē | + abl. | down from, about, concerning |
| ex / ē | + abl. | out of, from |
| in | + abl. | in, on (location, no motion) |
| prō | + abl. | in front of, on behalf of |
| sine | + abl. | without |

> **Note**: The preposition *in* takes **two different cases** with different meanings: *in + acc.* = "into" (motion), *in + abl.* = "in/on" (location). Compare Korean 에 (location) vs 으로 (direction).

## 7. Practice with Reading Simple Sentences

### 7.1 First Declension Sentences

Read and translate these sentences. Identify the case of each noun.

```
1. Puella rosam portat.
   puella (nom.) rosam (acc.) portat (verb)
   → The girl carries a rose.

2. Poēta fābulam nārrat.
   poēta (nom., m.) fābulam (acc.) nārrat (verb)
   → The poet tells a story.

3. Fēminae in viā ambulant.
   fēminae (nom. pl.) in viā (abl.) ambulant (verb)
   → The women walk on the road.

4. Nautae patriam amant.
   nautae (nom. pl.) patriam (acc.) amant (verb)
   → The sailors love their country.

5. Agricola fīliārum fortūnam laudat.
   agricola (nom.) fīliārum (gen. pl.) fortūnam (acc.) laudat (verb)
   → The farmer praises the fortune of his daughters.
```

### 7.2 Second Declension Sentences

```
1. Servus dominō pecūniam dat.
   servus (nom.) dominō (dat.) pecūniam (acc.) dat (verb)
   → The slave gives money to the master.

2. Fīlius magistrī est puer bonus.
   fīlius (nom.) magistrī (gen.) est (verb) puer (nom.) bonus (nom.)
   → The son of the teacher is a good boy.

3. Bellum est perīculum magnum.
   bellum (nom.) est (verb) perīculum (nom.) magnum (nom.)
   → War is a great danger.

4. Populī Rōmānī arma sunt multa.
   populī (gen.) Rōmānī (gen.) arma (nom. pl. n.) sunt (verb) multa
   → The weapons of the Roman people are many.

5. In templō deōrum dōna sunt.
   in templō (abl.) deōrum (gen. pl.) dōna (nom. pl. n.) sunt (verb)
   → In the temple there are gifts of the gods.
```

### 7.3 Mixed Declension Sentences

```
1. Puella fīliō agricolae rosam dat.
   puella (nom. 1st) fīliō (dat. 2nd) agricolae (gen. 1st)
   rosam (acc. 1st) dat (verb)
   → The girl gives a rose to the farmer's son.

2. Dominus servīs pecūniam et cibum dat.
   dominus (nom. 2nd) servīs (dat. 2nd) pecūniam (acc. 1st)
   et cibum (acc. 2nd) dat (verb)
   → The master gives money and food to the slaves.

3. Magister puerōs et puellās docet.
   magister (nom. 2nd) puerōs (acc. 2nd) et puellās (acc. 1st)
   docet (verb)
   → The teacher teaches the boys and girls.
```

### Exercise 6: Case Function Identification

For each underlined noun, name its case and explain its grammatical function.

```
1. Agricola terram arat.
   (a) Agricola = ?    (b) terram = ?

2. Fīlia nautae rosam portat.
   (a) Fīlia = ?    (b) nautae = ?    (c) rosam = ?

3. Servus dominō cibum dat.
   (a) Servus = ?    (b) dominō = ?    (c) cibum = ?

4. In oppidō cum amīcīs ambulāmus.
   (a) oppidō = ?    (b) amīcīs = ?
```

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

```
1. (a) Agricola = nominative sg. → subject ("the farmer")
   (b) terram = accusative sg. → direct object ("the land")

2. (a) Fīlia = nominative sg. → subject ("the daughter")
   (b) nautae = genitive sg. → possession ("of the sailor")
   (c) rosam = accusative sg. → direct object ("a rose")

3. (a) Servus = nominative sg. → subject ("the slave")
   (b) dominō = dative sg. → indirect object ("to the master")
   (c) cibum = accusative sg. → direct object ("food")

4. (a) oppidō = ablative sg. → place where (after "in")
   (b) amīcīs = ablative pl. → accompaniment (after "cum")
```

## Quick Reference Card

> **First and Second Declension Quick Reference**
>
> **1ST DECLENSION** (-a stems, mostly feminine):
>
> | | Sg. | Pl. | | Sg. | Pl. |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | Nom. | -a | -ae | Gen. | -ae | -ārum |
> | Dat. | -ae | -īs | Acc. | -am | -ās |
> | Abl. | -ā | -īs | Voc. | -a | -ae |
>
> **2ND DECLENSION MASC** (-us / -er):
>
> | | Sg. | Pl. | | Sg. | Pl. |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | Nom. | -us | -ī | Gen. | -ī | -ōrum |
> | Dat. | -ō | -īs | Acc. | -um | -ōs |
> | Abl. | -ō | -īs | Voc. | -e | -ī |
>
> **2ND DECLENSION NEUTER** (-um):
>
> | | Sg. | Pl. | | Sg. | Pl. |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | Nom. | -um | -a | Gen. | -ī | -ōrum |
> | Dat. | -ō | -īs | Acc. | -um | -a |
> | Abl. | -ō | -īs | Voc. | -um | -a |
>
> **NEUTER RULE**: Nom. = Acc. = Voc. (always!) / Plural Nom./Acc./Voc. always end in -a
>
> **SIX CASES**: Nom. (subject), Gen. (of), Dat. (to/for), Acc. (object), Abl. (by/with/in), Voc. (address)
