# 8. Pronouns and Prepositions (Pronomina et Praepositiones)

## Learning Objectives

- Decline all personal pronouns (ego, tū, nōs, vōs) in every case
- Master the four demonstrative pronouns (hic, ille, is, iste) with full declension
- Use the relative pronoun (quī, quae, quod) to form relative clauses
- Distinguish interrogative pronouns (quis, quid) in questions
- Categorize prepositions by the case they govern (accusative vs. ablative)
- Understand the dual behavior of *in* and *sub* with accusative and ablative
- Combine pronouns and prepositions in complete Latin sentences

## 1. Personal Pronouns

Latin uses personal pronouns for **emphasis** or **contrast** — the verb ending already indicates the subject, so the pronoun is not required.

### First Person: ego (I) / nōs (we)

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | ego | nōs |
| Gen. | meī | nostrum / nostrī |
| Dat. | mihi | nōbīs |
| Acc. | mē | nōs |
| Abl. | mē | nōbīs |

> **Gen. pl. distinction**: **nostrum** = partitive ("of us," i.e., "one of us"); **nostrī** = objective ("of us" as object, e.g., "memory of us").

### Second Person: tū (you) / vōs (you all)

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | tū | vōs |
| Gen. | tuī | vestrum / vestrī |
| Dat. | tibi | vōbīs |
| Acc. | tē | vōs |
| Abl. | tē | vōbīs |

### Third Person Reflexive: sē (himself/herself/itself/themselves)

Latin has no dedicated third person non-reflexive pronoun. Instead, it uses the demonstrative **is, ea, id** (see Section 2). The reflexive **sē** refers back to the subject.

| Case | Singular & Plural |
|------|-------------------|
| Nom. | — (none) |
| Gen. | suī |
| Dat. | sibi |
| Acc. | sē (sēsē) |
| Abl. | sē (sēsē) |

### Using *cum* with Pronouns

When **cum** (with) is used with personal/reflexive pronouns, it is **attached as a suffix**:

| Separate | Attached | Meaning |
|----------|----------|---------|
| cum + mē | **mēcum** | with me |
| cum + tē | **tēcum** | with you |
| cum + sē | **sēcum** | with himself |
| cum + nōbīs | **nōbīscum** | with us |
| cum + vōbīs | **vōbīscum** | with you (pl.) |

### Example Sentences

- **Ego tē amō, tū mē amās.** — *I love you, you love me.*
- **Quis nostrum hoc fēcit?** — *Which one of us did this?*
- **Venī mēcum!** — *Come with me!*
- **Dux sē dēfendit.** — *The leader defended himself.*
- **Tibi librōs dabō.** — *I will give you the books.*

## 2. Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns point to specific nouns. They can function as **pronouns** (standing alone) or **adjectives** (modifying a noun).

### 2.1 hic, haec, hoc — *this* (near the speaker)

| Case | M. Sg. | F. Sg. | N. Sg. | M. Pl. | F. Pl. | N. Pl. |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nom. | hic | haec | hoc | hī | hae | haec |
| Gen. | huius | huius | huius | hōrum | hārum | hōrum |
| Dat. | huic | huic | huic | hīs | hīs | hīs |
| Acc. | hunc | hanc | hoc | hōs | hās | haec |
| Abl. | hōc | hāc | hōc | hīs | hīs | hīs |

> **한국어 화자 참고**: **hic** is like 이 (this, near me). The genitive singular **huius** (one syllable: *hoo-yus*) is the same for all three genders — a pattern shared by all demonstratives.

### 2.2 ille, illa, illud — *that* (away from both speaker and listener)

| Case | M. Sg. | F. Sg. | N. Sg. | M. Pl. | F. Pl. | N. Pl. |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nom. | ille | illa | illud | illī | illae | illa |
| Gen. | illīus | illīus | illīus | illōrum | illārum | illōrum |
| Dat. | illī | illī | illī | illīs | illīs | illīs |
| Acc. | illum | illam | illud | illōs | illās | illa |
| Abl. | illō | illā | illō | illīs | illīs | illīs |

> **ille** is like 저 (that, over there). It is also used to mean "that famous person" — **ille Caesar** = "that (famous) Caesar."

### 2.3 is, ea, id — *this/that; he, she, it*

This is the **most common** demonstrative and serves as the standard third person pronoun.

| Case | M. Sg. | F. Sg. | N. Sg. | M. Pl. | F. Pl. | N. Pl. |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nom. | is | ea | id | eī (iī) | eae | ea |
| Gen. | eius | eius | eius | eōrum | eārum | eōrum |
| Dat. | eī | eī | eī | eīs (iīs) | eīs (iīs) | eīs (iīs) |
| Acc. | eum | eam | id | eōs | eās | ea |
| Abl. | eō | eā | eō | eīs (iīs) | eīs (iīs) | eīs (iīs) |

> **is/ea/id** is the pronoun you use for "he," "she," and "it" in Latin. It is weaker in pointing force than hic or ille.

### 2.4 iste, ista, istud — *that (of yours); that (pejorative)*

| Case | M. Sg. | F. Sg. | N. Sg. | M. Pl. | F. Pl. | N. Pl. |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nom. | iste | ista | istud | istī | istae | ista |
| Gen. | istīus | istīus | istīus | istōrum | istārum | istōrum |
| Dat. | istī | istī | istī | istīs | istīs | istīs |
| Acc. | istum | istam | istud | istōs | istās | ista |
| Abl. | istō | istā | istō | istīs | istīs | istīs |

> **iste** often carries a **negative or contemptuous** tone: **iste homō** = "that man (of yours)" or "that fellow." It follows the same pattern as ille.

### Demonstrative Summary

> **DEMONSTRATIVE DISTANCE**
>
> - hic  = THIS (near speaker)     → 이
> - iste = THAT (near listener)    → 그
> - ille = THAT (far from both)    → 저
> - is   = he/she/it (neutral)     → 그/그녀
>
> Shared pattern in gen./dat. sg.:
> - Gen. sg.: -īus (all genders)
> - Dat. sg.: -ī (all genders)

### Example Sentences

- **Hic liber bonus est.** — *This book is good.*
- **Illa fēmina est māter mea.** — *That woman is my mother.*
- **Is vir est amīcus meus.** — *That man is my friend.* / *He is my friend.*
- **Eius filium cognōscō.** — *I know his/her son.*
- **Iste canis mē terret!** — *That dog (of yours) frightens me!*

## 3. Relative Pronouns

The relative pronoun **quī, quae, quod** introduces relative clauses. It agrees with its **antecedent** in gender and number, but takes its **case from its function in the relative clause**.

### Full Declension

| Case | M. Sg. | F. Sg. | N. Sg. | M. Pl. | F. Pl. | N. Pl. |
|------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Nom. | quī | quae | quod | quī | quae | quae |
| Gen. | cuius | cuius | cuius | quōrum | quārum | quōrum |
| Dat. | cui | cui | cui | quibus | quibus | quibus |
| Acc. | quem | quam | quod | quōs | quās | quae |
| Abl. | quō | quā | quō | quibus | quibus | quibus |

### How Relative Clauses Work

> **RELATIVE CLAUSE AGREEMENT**
>
> Gender & Number → from the ANTECEDENT
> Case → from the pronoun's role IN ITS CLAUSE
>
> Example:
> Vir quem vidēs est senātor.
> (The man whom you see is a senator.)
>
> quem = masc. sg. (agrees with vir)
>      = accusative (object of vidēs)

### Example Sentences

- **Puella quae cantat est soror mea.** — *The girl who is singing is my sister.*
- **Liber quem legō bonus est.** — *The book which I am reading is good.*
- **Mīlitēs quōrum arma vidēmus fortēs sunt.** — *The soldiers whose weapons we see are brave.*
- **Urbs in quā habitāmus magna est.** — *The city in which we live is large.*
- **Omnia quae dīxit vēra erant.** — *Everything that he said was true.*

## 4. Interrogative Pronouns

### Substantive (Pronoun): quis, quid — *who? what?*

Used when asking a question **without** a noun (stands alone).

| Case | M./F. | N. |
|------|-------|----|
| Nom. | quis | quid |
| Gen. | cuius | cuius |
| Dat. | cui | cui |
| Acc. | quem | quid |
| Abl. | quō | quō |

> The plural is identical to the relative pronoun: quī, quae, quae, etc.

### Adjective: quī, quae, quod — *which? what?*

Used when modifying a noun (identical to the relative pronoun in form).

- **Quis es?** — *Who are you?*
- **Quid facis?** — *What are you doing?*
- **Quem librum legis?** — *Which book are you reading?*
- **Cuius est hic canis?** — *Whose is this dog?*
- **Cui pecūniam dedistī?** — *To whom did you give the money?*

## 5. Prepositions with Accusative

Prepositions governing the **accusative** generally express **motion toward** or **direction**.

| Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **ad** | to, toward, at | *Ad urbem vēnit.* — He came to the city. |
| **ante** | before, in front of | *Ante templum stābat.* — He stood before the temple. |
| **apud** | at, near, among, at the house of | *Apud amīcum cēnābat.* — He dined at a friend's house. |
| **circum** | around | *Circum oppidum ambulāvit.* — He walked around the town. |
| **contrā** | against | *Contrā hostēs pugnāvērunt.* — They fought against the enemies. |
| **in** | into, onto (+ acc.) | *In urbem cucurrit.* — He ran into the city. |
| **inter** | between, among | *Inter amīcōs sedēbat.* — He sat among friends. |
| **ob** | on account of | *Ob metum fūgit.* — He fled on account of fear. |
| **per** | through, by means of | *Per silvam ambulāvit.* — He walked through the forest. |
| **post** | after, behind | *Post bellum pāx vēnit.* — After the war, peace came. |
| **praeter** | past, beyond, except | *Praeter castra iter fēcit.* — He marched past the camp. |
| **prope** | near | *Prope flūmen habitat.* — He lives near the river. |
| **propter** | because of | *Propter perīculum fūgit.* — He fled because of danger. |
| **trāns** | across | *Trāns flūmen nāvigāvit.* — He sailed across the river. |

## 6. Prepositions with Ablative

Prepositions governing the **ablative** generally express **location**, **separation**, or **accompaniment**.

| Preposition | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **ā / ab** | from, by (agent) | *Ab urbe discessit.* — He departed from the city. |
| **cum** | with | *Cum amīcīs ambulat.* — He walks with friends. |
| **dē** | down from, about, concerning | *Dē bellō scrīpsit.* — He wrote about the war. |
| **ē / ex** | out of, from | *Ex oppidō exiit.* — He went out of the town. |
| **in** | in, on (+ abl.) | *In urbe habitat.* — He lives in the city. |
| **prae** | before, in comparison with | *Prae timōre fugit.* — He fled before/because of fear. |
| **prō** | in front of, for, on behalf of | *Prō patriā pugnat.* — He fights for the fatherland. |
| **sine** | without | *Sine aquā vīvere nōn possumus.* — We cannot live without water. |
| **sub** | under (+ abl. = location) | *Sub arbore sedēbat.* — He sat under a tree. |

### The Dual Prepositions: in and sub

> **IN + ACCUSATIVE vs. IN + ABLATIVE**
>
> - in + ACC = motion INTO / ONTO
>   In urbem ambulat. (He walks into the city)
> - in + ABL = location IN / ON
>   In urbe habitat. (He lives in the city)
> - sub + ACC = motion UNDER
>   Sub aquam mersit. (He plunged under water)
> - sub + ABL = location UNDER
>   Sub aquā nātat. (He swims under water)

> **한국어 화자 참고**: This is similar to how Korean uses different particles for location (에서) vs. direction (으로/에). The Latin case (accusative vs. ablative) serves the same function as these Korean particles.

## 7. Reading Practice

### Passage: Epistula ad Amīcum

> Ego, quī in hāc urbe habitō, ad tē haec verba scrībō. Ille liber, quem mihi dedistī, bonus est. Cum amīcīs meīs per forum ambulāvī. Ante templum Iovis stābāmus. Post cēnam domum rediimus. Sine tē omnia trīstia sunt. Venī ad nōs! Nōbīscum cēnā et dē rēbus nostrīs loquāmur.

Translation:

> I, who live in this city, am writing these words to you. That book, which you gave me, is good. I walked through the forum with my friends. We stood before the temple of Jupiter. After dinner we returned home. Without you, all things are sad. Come to us! Dine with us and let us talk about our affairs.

6. quī — dative singular (any gender)

### Exercise 2: Choose the Correct Pronoun
