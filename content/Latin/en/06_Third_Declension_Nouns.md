## 1. Overview of the Third Declension

The third declension is the **largest and most varied** declension in Latin. Unlike the first and second declensions, which have predictable stems (-a and -o/-u), third declension nouns come from many different stem types.

### Key Characteristics

- **Genitive singular** always ends in **-is** (this is how you identify a 3rd declension noun)
- The **nominative singular** is highly irregular — you must memorize it
- The **stem** is found by removing **-is** from the genitive singular
- Contains **all three genders**: masculine, feminine, and neuter

### General Endings: Consonant Stems

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nominative | (varies) | -ēs |
| Genitive | -is | -um |
| Dative | -ī | -ibus |
| Accusative | -em | -ēs |
| Ablative | -e | -ibus |
| Vocative | (= nom.) | -ēs |

> **Note for Korean speakers (한국어 화자 참고)**: The third declension is comparable to irregular noun patterns. Unlike the predictable first (-a) and second (-us/-um) declensions, you must learn each noun with its genitive form, much like learning 은/는 vs 이/가 particle usage requires memorization of context.

## 2. Consonant-Stem Nouns

Consonant-stem nouns have stems ending in a consonant. They are subdivided by the final consonant of the stem.

### 2.1 Guttural Stems (c/g-stems)

The nominative is formed by adding **-s** to the stem, which combines with c/g to produce **-x**.

**rēx, rēgis** (m.) — *king*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | rēx | rēg-ēs |
| Gen. | rēg-is | rēg-um |
| Dat. | rēg-ī | rēg-ibus |
| Acc. | rēg-em | rēg-ēs |
| Abl. | rēg-e | rēg-ibus |

> Stem: **rēg-** (found from gen. sg. rēg-is)

**vōx, vōcis** (f.) — *voice*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | vōx | vōc-ēs |
| Gen. | vōc-is | vōc-um |
| Dat. | vōc-ī | vōc-ibus |
| Acc. | vōc-em | vōc-ēs |
| Abl. | vōc-e | vōc-ibus |

Other c/g-stem nouns:
- **dux, ducis** (m.) — *leader* (stem: duc-)
- **lēx, lēgis** (f.) — *law* (stem: lēg-)
- **pāx, pācis** (f.) — *peace* (stem: pāc-)
- **iūdex, iūdicis** (m.) — *judge* (stem: iūdic-)

### 2.2 Dental Stems (t/d-stems)

The dental consonant (t or d) **drops before -s** in the nominative singular.

**mīles, mīlitis** (m.) — *soldier*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | mīles | mīlit-ēs |
| Gen. | mīlit-is | mīlit-um |
| Dat. | mīlit-ī | mīlit-ibus |
| Acc. | mīlit-em | mīlit-ēs |
| Abl. | mīlit-e | mīlit-ibus |

> Stem: **mīlit-** (the -t- drops in the nominative: mīlit-s → mīles)

**virtūs, virtūtis** (f.) — *courage, virtue*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | virtūs | virtūt-ēs |
| Gen. | virtūt-is | virtūt-um |
| Dat. | virtūt-ī | virtūt-ibus |
| Acc. | virtūt-em | virtūt-ēs |
| Abl. | virtūt-e | virtūt-ibus |

Other t/d-stem nouns:
- **pēs, pedis** (m.) — *foot* (stem: ped-)
- **caput, capitis** (n.) — *head* (stem: capit-)
- **laus, laudis** (f.) — *praise* (stem: laud-)

### 2.3 S-Stems (Rhotacism)

In s-stem nouns, the **-s-** between vowels becomes **-r-** through a process called **rhotacism**.

**corpus, corporis** (n.) — *body*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | corpus | corpor-a |
| Gen. | corpor-is | corpor-um |
| Dat. | corpor-ī | corpor-ibus |
| Acc. | corpus | corpor-a |
| Abl. | corpor-e | corpor-ibus |

> Original stem: **corpos-** → rhotacism → **corpor-** (s between vowels → r)

Other s-stem nouns:
- **tempus, temporis** (n.) — *time* (tempos- → tempor-)
- **genus, generis** (n.) — *kind, race* (genes- → gener-)
- **opus, operis** (n.) — *work* (opes- → oper-)
- **flōs, flōris** (m.) — *flower* (flōs- → flōr-)

### 2.4 N-Stems

**nōmen, nōminis** (n.) — *name*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | nōmen | nōmin-a |
| Gen. | nōmin-is | nōmin-um |
| Dat. | nōmin-ī | nōmin-ibus |
| Acc. | nōmen | nōmin-a |
| Abl. | nōmin-e | nōmin-ibus |

Other n-stem nouns:
- **flūmen, flūminis** (n.) — *river*
- **carmen, carminis** (n.) — *song, poem*
- **lūmen, lūminis** (n.) — *light*
- **homō, hominis** (m.) — *human being, person*

### 2.5 Liquid Stems (l/r-stems)

**pater, patris** (m.) — *father*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | pater | patr-ēs |
| Gen. | patr-is | patr-um |
| Dat. | patr-ī | patr-ibus |
| Acc. | patr-em | patr-ēs |
| Abl. | patr-e | patr-ibus |

Other liquid-stem nouns:
- **māter, mātris** (f.) — *mother*
- **frāter, frātris** (m.) — *brother*
- **soror, sorōris** (f.) — *sister*
- **uxor, uxōris** (f.) — *wife*
- **cōnsul, cōnsulis** (m.) — *consul*

## 3. I-Stem Nouns

Some third declension nouns have stems ending in **-i-**. These nouns show slight differences in their endings compared to consonant stems.

### 3.1 How to Identify I-Stems

```
Rules for Identifying I-Stem Nouns:
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ MASCULINE / FEMININE I-STEMS                    │
│                                                 │
│ 1. Parisyllabic: same number of syllables in    │
│    nom. sg. and gen. sg.                        │
│    e.g., cīvis / cīv-is (2 / 2 syllables)      │
│                                                 │
│ 2. Stem ends in two consonants before -is       │
│    e.g., urbs / urb-is, mors / mort-is          │
│                                                 │
│ NEUTER I-STEMS                                  │
│                                                 │
│ 3. Nom. sg. ends in -e, -al, or -ar            │
│    e.g., mare / mar-is, animal / animāl-is,     │
│          calcar / calcār-is                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 3.2 I-Stem Endings vs. Consonant-Stem Endings

| Case | Consonant Stem | Masc./Fem. I-Stem | Neuter I-Stem |
|------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Nom. Sg. | (varies) | (varies) | -e / -al / -ar |
| Gen. Sg. | -is | -is | -is |
| Dat. Sg. | -ī | -ī | -ī |
| Acc. Sg. | -em | -em | = nom. |
| Abl. Sg. | **-e** | **-e** | **-ī** |
| Nom. Pl. | -ēs | -ēs | **-ia** |
| Gen. Pl. | **-um** | **-ium** | **-ium** |
| Dat. Pl. | -ibus | -ibus | -ibus |
| Acc. Pl. | -ēs | **-ēs / -īs** | **-ia** |
| Abl. Pl. | -ibus | -ibus | -ibus |

> **Key differences** are in bold: ablative singular (-ī for neuters), genitive plural (-ium), and nominative/accusative plural (-ia for neuters).

| Nom. | cīvis | cīv-ēs |
| Gen. | cīv-is | cīv-ium |
| Dat. | cīv-ī | cīv-ibus |
| Acc. | cīv-em | cīv-ēs |
| Abl. | cīv-e | cīv-ibus |

**urbs, urbis** (f.) — *city*

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | urbs | urb-ēs |
| Gen. | urb-is | urb-ium |
| Dat. | urb-ī | urb-ibus |
| Acc. | urb-em | urb-ēs |
| Abl. | urb-e | urb-ibus |

Other masc./fem. i-stems:
- **hostis, hostis** (m.) — *enemy*

- **mors, mortis** (f.) — *death*
- **pars, partis** (f.) — *part*
- **nox, noctis** (f.) — *night*

## 4. The Neuter Rule

The **neuter rule** is one of the most important principles in Latin grammar. It applies across **all** declensions.

```
THE NEUTER RULE (Rēgula Neutrōrum)
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. Nom. = Acc. (always, singular and plural) │
│                                              │
│ 2. Nom./Acc. plural always ends in -a        │
│    (2nd decl: -a, 3rd cons: -a, 3rd i: -ia)  │
│                                              │
│ 3. Neuter plural subject takes a             │
│    SINGULAR verb                             │
│    e.g., Bella longa sunt. BUT also:         │
│    Arma virumque canō.                       │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Examples Across Declensions

| Declension | Nom./Acc. Sg. | Nom./Acc. Pl. | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------|
| 2nd | -um | -a | bellum → bella |
| 3rd (cons.) | (varies) | -a | corpus → corpora |
| 3rd (i-stem) | -e/-al/-ar | -ia | mare → maria |

### Example Sentences

- **Corpora mīlitum in viā iacent.** — *The bodies of the soldiers lie in the road.*
- **Nōmina rēgum scrīpta sunt.** — *The names of the kings have been written.*
- **Maria lāta et pulchra sunt.** — *The seas are wide and beautiful.*
- **Omnia animālia aquam quaerunt.** — *All animals seek water.*

## 5. Third Declension Patterns Summary

### Quick Reference: All Stem Types

| Stem Type | Nom. Sg. | Gen. Sg. | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|----------|----------|---------|---------|
| c/g-stem | -x | -cis/-gis | rēx, rēgis | king |
| t/d-stem | -s / varies | -tis/-dis | mīles, mīlitis | soldier |
| s-stem | -us/-ōs | -oris/-eris | corpus, corporis | body |
| n-stem | -en/-ō | -inis | nōmen, nōminis | name |
| r/l-stem | -er/-or/-ul | -ris/-ōris/-ulis | pater, patris | father |
| i-stem (m/f) | varies | -is (-ium pl.) | cīvis, cīvis | citizen |
| i-stem (n) | -e/-al/-ar | -is (-ium pl.) | mare, maris | sea |

### Gender Tendencies in the Third Declension

```
GENDER PATTERNS (3rd Declension)
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ MASCULINE tend to be:                   │
│   - Agents: mīles, rēx, dux, homō      │
│   - Rivers: some proper nouns           │
│                                         │
│ FEMININE tend to be:                    │
│   - Abstract nouns: virtūs, pāx, lēx    │
│   - Trees: arbor (but this is f.)       │
│   - -tās, -tūs, -tiō endings           │
│                                         │
│ NEUTER tend to be:                      │
│   - -us/-oris: corpus, tempus, genus    │
│   - -en/-inis: nōmen, flūmen, carmen    │
│   - -e, -al, -ar: mare, animal, calcar │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## 6. Reading Practice

### Passage: Dē Urbe Rōmā

> **Urbs** Rōma magna est. In **urbe** multī **cīvēs** habitant. **Rēx** ōlim urbem regēbat, sed nunc **cōnsulēs** rem pūblicam regunt. **Mīlitēs** fortēs urbem dēfendunt. **Nōmina** ducum in **tempore** bellī nōta sunt. **Patria** nostra multa **flūmina** et **maria** habet. **Corpora** **mīlitum** fortia sunt.

Translation:

> The **city** of Rome is great. Many **citizens** live in the **city**. A **king** once ruled the city, but now **consuls** govern the republic. Brave **soldiers** defend the city. The **names** of the leaders are known in **time** of war. Our homeland has many **rivers** and **seas**. The **bodies** of the **soldiers** are strong.

> All bolded words are third declension nouns — notice the variety of stem types!
