## 1. Introduction to Latin Meter

### 1.1 Quantity vs. Stress

English poetry is **stress-based** — rhythm comes from alternating stressed and unstressed syllables (*To BE or NOT to BE*). Latin poetry is **quantity-based** — rhythm comes from alternating **long** (—) and **short** (∪) syllables.

| System | Basis | Example Language |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Quantitative | Vowel/syllable length | Latin, Greek, Sanskrit |
| Accentual | Word stress | English, German |
| Syllabic | Syllable count | French, Japanese |

### 1.2 Syllable Quantity Rules

A syllable is **long** if it contains:
1. A **long vowel** or **diphthong** (ae, au, oe, ei, eu, ui)
2. A **short vowel followed by two or more consonants** (long "by position")

A syllable is **short** if it contains:
- A short vowel followed by a single consonant (or no consonant before a vowel)

**Special rules:**
- A mute (b, c, d, g, p, t) + liquid (l, r) combination may leave the preceding syllable short or long (*muta cum liquida* rule)
- *h* does not count as a consonant
- *x* and *z* count as double consonants (ks, dz)
- *qu* counts as a single consonant

### 1.3 Metrical Notation

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| — | Long syllable (*longum*) |
| ∪ | Short syllable (*breve*) |
| ∪∪ | Two shorts (equivalent to one long) |
| ‖ | Caesura (word break within a foot) |
| \| | Foot boundary |
| × | *anceps* (long or short) |

## 2. Dactylic Hexameter

### 2.1 The Epic Meter

Dactylic hexameter is the meter of epic poetry (Homer, Virgil, Lucretius) and the most important Latin meter. Each line has **six feet**.

**The basic pattern:**

```
Foot:    1       2       3       4       5       6
Pattern: —∪∪ |  —∪∪ |  —∪∪ |  —∪∪ |  —∪∪ |  —×
```

**Substitution rule:** In feet 1–4, a **spondee** (— —) may replace a dactyl (—∪∪). The 5th foot is almost always a dactyl. The 6th foot is always a spondee or trochee (—×).

### 2.2 Caesura

A **caesura** is a word-break within a foot, creating a natural pause. The main caesurae in hexameter:

| Type | Position | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Penthemimeral** | After 5th half-foot | After the longum of foot 3 |
| **Trithemimeral** | After 3rd half-foot | After the longum of foot 2 |
| **Hephthemimeral** | After 7th half-foot | After the longum of foot 4 |
| **Feminine / trochaic** | After 1st short of foot 3 | *bucolic diaeresis* if between feet 4–5 |

Most lines have a **strong (penthemimeral) caesura** in the third foot:

```
—∪∪ | —∪∪ | — ‖ ∪∪ | —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —×
                ↑
          main caesura
```

### 2.3 Scansion Step by Step

1. **Mark elisions** (see Section 8)
2. **Mark all long syllables** you can identify (diphthongs, long vowels, position)
3. **Mark final syllable** as *anceps* (×)
4. **Work backward from the 5th foot** (usually —∪∪)
5. **Fill in remaining feet** as dactyls or spondees

### 2.4 Example Scansion: Aeneid 1.1

> **Arma virumque cano, Troiae qui primus ab oris**

```
Ar-ma vi-|rum-que ca-|nō, Troi-|ae quī |prī-mus ab |ō-rīs
 — ∪ ∪  |  — ∪  ∪  | —  —   |  — —  | — ∪  ∪   | — —
 dact.     dact.      spond.   spond.   dact.       spond.
```

Notes:
- *Troiae* — diphthong *ae* makes it long; *Troi-ae* = two syllables (synizesis of *oi* in some readings, but typically *Trō-iae* with the *o* long)
- *primus* — *prī-* is long by nature
- Final *-is* is *anceps* (treated as long here, end of line)

## 3. Elegiac Couplet

### 3.1 Structure

The elegiac couplet pairs a **hexameter** with a **pentameter**. It is the meter of love poetry (Ovid, Tibullus, Propertius), epigrams, and Ovid's exile poetry.

```
Line 1 (hexameter):  —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —×
Line 2 (pentameter):  —∪∪ | —∪∪ | — ‖ —∪∪ | —∪∪ | ×
```

**Pentameter rules:**
- Two and a half feet + caesura + two and a half feet
- The two halves each end with a half-foot (single long)
- No spondaic substitution in the second half (always —∪∪)
- The pentameter line typically ends with a disyllabic word

### 3.2 Example: Ovid, Amores 1.1.1–2

> **Arma gravi numero violentaque bella parabam**
> **edere, materia conveniente modis.**

```
Hexameter:
Ar-ma gra-|vī nu-me-|rō vi-o-|len-ta-que |bel-la pa-|rā-bam
 — ∪  ∪   | —  ∪ ∪  | — ∪ ∪  |  — ∪  ∪  | — ∪  ∪  | — —

Pentameter:
ē-de-re | mā-te-ri-|ā ‖ con-ve-ni-|en-te mo-|dīs
— ∪ ∪  |  — ∪  ∪  | —   — ∪  ∪  | — ∪  ∪ | —
```

**Translation:** "I was preparing to produce arms and violent wars in heavy meter, / the subject matter fitting the rhythms."

## 4. Lyric Meters

### 4.1 Sapphic Stanza

Used by Horace (following Sappho). Four lines per stanza:

```
Line 1: —∪ | — × | —∪∪ | —∪ | — ×     (Sapphic hendecasyllable)
Line 2: —∪ | — × | —∪∪ | —∪ | — ×     (same)
Line 3: —∪ | — × | —∪∪ | —∪ | — ×     (same)
Line 4: —∪∪ | — ×                        (Adonic)
```

### 4.2 Alcaic Stanza

Also used by Horace (following Alcaeus). Four lines per stanza:

```
Line 1: ×—∪ | — × | — ‖ ∪∪ | —∪ | — ×   (Alcaic hendecasyllable)
Line 2: ×—∪ | — × | — ‖ ∪∪ | —∪ | — ×   (same)
Line 3: ×—∪ | —∪ | —∪ | — ×              (Alcaic enneasyllable)
Line 4: —∪∪ | —∪∪ | —∪ | — ×             (Alcaic decasyllable)
```

### 4.3 Hendecasyllable (Phalaecean)

Catullus's favorite meter. Eleven syllables per line:

```
× × | —∪∪ | —∪ | —∪ | — ×
```

Example — Catullus 5.1: *Vivamus, mea Lesbia, atque amemus*

## 5. Virgil's Aeneid

### 5.1 The Opening Lines (Aeneid 1.1–7)

> **Arma virumque cano, Troiae qui primus ab oris**
> **Italiam, fato profugus, Laviniaque venit**
> **litora, multum ille et terris iactatus et alto**
> **vi superum saevae memorem Iunonis ob iram;**
> **multa quoque et bello passus, dum conderet urbem,**
> **inferretque deos Latio, genus unde Latinum,**
> **Albanique patres, atque altae moenia Romae.**

**Translation:**
"I sing of arms and the man who, an exile by fate, first came from the shores of Troy to Italy and the Lavinian coast — much buffeted both on land and on the deep by the force of the gods above, on account of the unforgetting wrath of cruel Juno; having suffered much also in war, until he could found a city and bring his gods to Latium — whence came the Latin race, the Alban fathers, and the walls of lofty Rome."

### 5.2 Analysis of the Opening

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Model** | Homer's *Iliad* ("arms") + *Odyssey* ("the man") combined |
| **Main verb** | *cano* — first word = subject matter; first person unlike Homer |
| **Enjambment** | *Italiam* dramatically placed at start of line 2 |
| **Key themes** | *fato* (fate), *iram* (wrath), *urbem* (city-founding) |
| **Syntax** | Single sentence spanning 7 lines — periodic structure |
| **Meter** | Mix of dactyls and spondees, reflecting both speed and weight |

### 5.3 Passage: The Fall of Troy (Aeneid 2.1–6)

> **Conticuere omnes intentique ora tenebant.**
> **Inde toro pater Aeneas sic orsus ab alto:**
> **"Infandum, regina, iubes renovare dolorem."**

**Translation:** "All fell silent and held their faces attentively. Then father Aeneas from the high couch thus began: 'Unspeakable, O queen, is the grief you bid me renew.'"

**Metrical note on line 1:**
```
Con-ti-cu-|ē-re om-|nēs in-|ten-tī-que |ō-ra te-|nē-bant
 — ∪  ∪  | — ∪ ∪  | — —  |  — — ∪   | — ∪  ∪ | — —
```
- Four spondees create a heavy, solemn opening — all fall silent
- The dactyls in foot 1, 2, 5 provide necessary variety

## 6. Ovid's Metamorphoses

### 6.1 Ovid's Style

Ovid writes in hexameter but with a lighter, more fluent touch than Virgil:

- Frequent enjambment
- Witty wordplay and etymological puns
- Symmetrical balanced lines
- Narrative speed — dactylic rhythm predominates

### 6.2 Passage: The Opening (Met. 1.1–4)

> **In nova fert animus mutatas dicere formas**
> **corpora; di, coeptis (nam vos mutastis et illas)**
> **adspirate meis primaque ab origine mundi**
> **ad mea perpetuum deducite tempora carmen.**

**Translation:** "My mind moves me to tell of forms changed into new bodies; gods, favor my undertakings (for you have changed even these), and from the first origin of the world to my own times, spin out an unbroken song."

**Key features:**
- *In nova ... corpora* — enjambment separates adjective (*nova*) from noun (*corpora*)
- *mutatas ... formas / corpora* — interlocked word order (*synchysis*): changed-forms / new-bodies
- *perpetuum ... carmen* — "continuous song" frames the epic scope
- *deducite* — metaphor from spinning thread (*deducere filum*)

### 6.3 Passage: Apollo and Daphne (Met. 1.525–530)

> **Sic deus in somnis "Ego sum, ne time, Daphne,"**
> — Actually, the pursuit passage is more famous:

> **Phoebus amat visaeque cupit conubia Daphnes,**
> **quodque cupit sperat, suaque illum oracula fallunt.**

**Translation:** "Phoebus loves and, having seen Daphne, desires marriage with her; and what he desires he hopes for, and his own oracles deceive him."

- Irony: the god of prophecy cannot foresee his own failure
- *visae ... Daphnes* — Greek genitive (*Daphnes* rather than *Daphnae*)
- Tricolon: *amat ... cupit ... sperat* — escalating desire

## 7. Horace and Catullus

### 7.1 Catullus 5 (Hendecasyllables)

> **Vivamus, mea Lesbia, atque amemus,**
> **rumoresque senum severiorum**
> **omnes unius aestimemus assis!**
> **Soles occidere et redire possunt;**
> **nobis cum semel occidit brevis lux,**
> **nox est perpetua una dormienda.**

**Translation:**
"Let us live, my Lesbia, and let us love, / and let us value at a single penny / all the rumors of stern old men! / Suns can set and rise again; / for us, once our brief light has set, / there is one perpetual night that must be slept through."

**Analysis:**
- Jussive subjunctives: *vivamus, amemus, aestimemus*
- Contrast: *soles* (suns, plural, renewable) vs. *nobis ... brevis lux* (our brief light, singular)
- *una nox perpetua dormienda* — gerundive: "one night that must be slept" — euphemism for death

### 7.2 Horace: Odes 1.11 (*Carpe Diem*)

> **Tu ne quaesieris, scire nefas, quem mihi, quem tibi**
> **finem di dederint, Leuconoe, nec Babylonios**
> **temptaris numeros.**

**Translation:** "Do not ask — it is forbidden to know — what end the gods have given to me, to you, Leuconoe, and do not try Babylonian calculations."

- *ne quaesieris ... nec temptaris* — perfect subjunctive (negative command)
- The ode ends with the famous: ***carpe diem, quam minimum credula postero*** — "seize the day, trusting as little as possible in tomorrow"

### 7.3 Catullus 85

The shortest and most famous Latin poem (elegiac couplet):

> **Odi et amo. Quare id faciam, fortasse requiris.**
> **Nescio, sed fieri sentio et excrucior.**

**Translation:** "I hate and I love. Why I do this, perhaps you ask. / I do not know, but I feel it happening and I am tortured."

- Five verbs in two lines: *odi, amo, faciam, requiris, nescio, fieri, sentio, excrucior* — actually eight
- Emotional compression: the entire drama of a relationship in a couplet
- *excrucior* — passive: "I am crucified" — metaphor of suffering

## 8. Poetic License

### 8.1 Elision

When a word ending in a vowel (or vowel + *m*) is followed by a word beginning with a vowel (or *h* + vowel), the final syllable of the first word is **elided** (not pronounced in scansion).

| Pattern | Example | Scansion |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Vowel + Vowel | *mult(um) ille* | *mult' ille* |
| Vowel + *h* | *monstrat(e) hanc* | *monstrat' hanc* |
| *-m* + Vowel | *arma(m) et* | *arm' et* |
| *-m* + *h* | *terra(m) Hesperiam* | *terr' Hesperiam* |

**Example — Aeneid 1.3:**
> *multum ill(e) et terris iactatus et alto*

The *-e* of *ille* is elided before *et*.

### 8.2 Synizesis

Two vowels within a single word are pronounced as one syllable:

- *deinde* → *dein-de* (2 syllables instead of 3)
- *eodem* → *eō-dem* (2 syllables)
- *deos* → *deos* (1 syllable in some contexts)

### 8.3 Hiatus

The absence of elision — rare and usually deliberate for emphasis:

> *et vera incessu patuit dea. Ille ubi matrem* (Aen. 1.405)

*dea. Ille* — hiatus at the caesura, marking the dramatic pause of recognition.

### 8.4 Other Licenses

| License | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Systole** | Long vowel treated as short | *steterunt* (3rd syllable shortened) |
| **Diastole** | Short vowel treated as long | *vidēt* for *videt* |
| **Tmesis** | Compound word split | *quo ... cumque* for *quocumque* |
| **Archaism** | Old forms for metrical convenience | *olli* for *illi*, *ast* for *at* |
| **Greek forms** | Greek endings retained | *Daphnes* (gen.), *aethera* (acc.) |

### Exercise 1: Mark the Scansion

Scan the following hexameter lines. Mark long (—), short (∪), and foot divisions (|).

**a)** *Tityre, tu patulae recubans sub tegmine fagi* (Ecl. 1.1)

**b)** *Infandum, regina, iubes renovare dolorem* (Aen. 2.3)

**c)** *Tantae molis erat Romanam condere gentem* (Aen. 1.33)

<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

**a)** *Tī-ty-re | tū pa-tu-|lae re-cu-|bāns sub | tēg-mi-ne | fā-gī*
```
— ∪ ∪ | — ∪  ∪ | — ∪  ∪ | — —   | — ∪  ∪ | — —
dact.    dact.    dact.    spond.   dact.    spond.
```
Note: *patulae* — *pa* short, *tu* short, *lae* long (diphthong)

**b)** *Īn-fan-|dum re-gī-|na iu-|bēs re-no-|vā-re do-|lō-rem*
```
— —   | — ∪  — | ∪ ∪ | —  ∪  ∪ | — ∪  ∪ | — —
spond.   — ∪ —    —     dact.     dact.    spond.
```
Corrected full scansion:
```
Īn-fan-|dum re-|gī-na iu-|bēs re-no-|vā-re do-|lō-rem
 — —  | — ∪  |  — ∪  ∪ | —  ∪  ∪ | — ∪  ∪ | — —
spond.  spond.   dact.     dact.     dact.    spond.
```
Note: *regina* — *re* short before single consonant, *gī* long, *na iu-* elision does NOT occur here (no elision since *na* ends in *a* and *iu-* begins with consonantal *i*)

**c)** *Tan-tae | mō-lis e-|rat Rō-mā-|nam con-de-|re gen-|tem*
```
— —   | — ∪  ∪ | — — —  | — — ∪  | ∪ —  | —
spond.   dact.    spond.    — ∪ ∪   dact.  spond.
```
Corrected:
```
Tan-tae | mō-lis e-|rāt Rō-|mā-nam con-|de-re gen-|tem
 — —   | — ∪   ∪ | — —   | — —  —    | ∪ ∪  —  | —
spond.    dact.     spond.   spond.      dact.    (cat.)
```
This is a famously spondaic line — the heavy spondees mirror the "great effort" (*tantae molis*) of founding Rome.

### Exercise 2: Identify the Meter

What meter is each of the following written in?

**a)** Two-line stanza: first line scans as hexameter, second line has a break in the middle and two dactylic halves.

**b)** Four-line stanza: three identical eleven-syllable lines followed by a short five-syllable line.

**c)** Every line has exactly eleven syllables beginning with two variable syllables then a dactyl.

### Exercise 3: Translate with Metrical Awareness

Translate the following, paying attention to how word order serves the meter:

> **Forsan et haec olim meminisse iuvabit.** (Aen. 1.203)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

**Translation:** "Perhaps one day it will be a joy to remember even these things."

**Notes:**
- *Forsan* — "perhaps" (poetic for *fortasse*)
- *et haec* — "even these things" (acc. pl. n.)
- *olim* — "one day, someday"
- *meminisse* — perfect infinitive of *memini* ("to remember")
- *iuvabit* — future of *iuvo* ("it will delight / be a joy") — impersonal

Aeneas speaks this to his men after the storm, encouraging them that their suffering will become a good memory. The line is one of the most quoted in all Latin literature.
</details>

### Exercise 4: Poetic License Identification

Identify the poetic license in each example:

**a)** *mult(um) ille et terris iactatus et alto* — what happens to the *-um* of *multum*?

**b)** *deinde* scanned as two syllables in a hexameter line.

**c)** *terrai* used for *terrae* (genitive singular).

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

- **a)** **Elision** — the final *-um* of *multum* is elided before *ille* (which begins with a vowel)
- **b)** **Synizesis** — the two vowels *e-i* in *deinde* are pronounced as a single syllable
- **c)** **Archaism** — *terrai* is the archaic genitive singular of *terra* (used by Lucretius and Ennius); the classical form is *terrae*
</details>

## Quick Reference Card

| Meter | Pattern | Used By |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Dactylic hexameter | 6 feet: —∪∪ (or — —), last foot —× | Virgil, Lucretius, Ovid (*Met.*) |
| Elegiac couplet | Hexameter + pentameter | Ovid (*Am.*), Catullus, Tibullus |
| Sapphic stanza | 3 × hendecasyllable + Adonic | Horace, Catullus 11 |
| Alcaic stanza | 2 × hendecasyllable + 9-syl. + 10-syl. | Horace (*Odes*) |
| Hendecasyllable | × × —∪∪ —∪ —∪ —× | Catullus 1–60 |

| Key Term | Definition |
|----------|------------|
| Dactyl | —∪∪ (long-short-short) |
| Spondee | — — (long-long) |
| Caesura | Word break within a foot |
| Elision | Suppression of final vowel/vowel+m before initial vowel |
| Synizesis | Two vowels in one word pronounced as one syllable |
| Enjambment | Sentence running over the line break |
| Anceps | Syllable that may be long or short (×) |
