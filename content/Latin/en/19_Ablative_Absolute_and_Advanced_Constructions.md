# 19. Ablative Absolute and Advanced Constructions (Ablātīvus Absolūtus et Cōnstrūctiōnēs Prōgredientēs)

## Learning Objectives
- Recognize and construct ablative absolute phrases with present participle, perfect passive participle, and noun+noun/adjective patterns
- Understand why the ablative absolute is "absolute" (grammatically independent from the main clause)
- Form and use the supine in both accusative (purpose of motion) and ablative (respect) constructions
- Identify and use the locative case for cities, small islands, and special nouns
- Master verbs that govern the dative, ablative, or genitive instead of the expected accusative
- Understand and translate impersonal verb constructions
- Apply these advanced constructions in reading and composition

## 1. Ablative Absolute

### 1.1 What Is the Ablative Absolute?

The ablative absolute is a phrase in the **ablative case** consisting of a **noun (or pronoun)** + a **participle** (or another noun/adjective), grammatically **independent** from the rest of the sentence. It functions as a subordinate clause expressing **time, cause, condition, or concession**.

> **"Absolute"** comes from Latin *absolūtus* — "freed, loosened" — because the phrase is syntactically "loosened" from the main clause. The noun in the ablative absolute must NOT be the subject or object of the main clause.

### 1.2 Basic Pattern

```
[Noun/Pronoun (ablative)] + [Participle (ablative)] , [Main clause]
```

| Latin | English | Clause Sense |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Urbe captā**, mīlitēs gāvīsī sunt. | **The city having been captured**, the soldiers rejoiced. | Temporal: After/When |
| **Caesare duce**, Rōmānī vīcērunt. | **With Caesar as leader**, the Romans conquered. | Circumstantial |
| **Hostibus fugientibus**, castra cēpimus. | **The enemy fleeing**, we seized the camp. | Temporal: While |

### 1.3 The Independence Rule

The noun of the ablative absolute must NOT serve as subject, object, or any other grammatical element of the main clause.

**Correct ablative absolute:**
- **Urbe captā**, mīlitēs gāvīsī sunt. (Urbe is not subject or object of the main clause.)

**NOT ablative absolute (use participle agreeing with subject instead):**
- ~~Caesare veniente, Caesar dīxit...~~ → **Caesar veniēns dīxit...** (Caesar is the subject of the main clause, so use the nominative participle.)

### 1.4 Translation Strategies

The ablative absolute can be rendered in English as:

| Strategy | Example Translation of *Urbe captā* |
|----------|--------------------------------------|
| Literal ("having been...") | The city having been captured, ... |
| Temporal clause | After/When the city was captured, ... |
| Causal clause | Because/Since the city had been captured, ... |
| Concessive clause | Although the city was captured, ... |
| Prepositional phrase | After the capture of the city, ... |

Choose the strategy that best fits the context.

## 2. Types of Ablative Absolute

### 2.1 With Present Active Participle

Expresses an action **happening at the same time** as the main verb:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Sole oriente**, profectī sumus. | **As the sun was rising**, we set out. |
| **Omnibus audientibus**, ōrātiōnem habuit. | **With everyone listening**, he delivered a speech. |
| **Hostibus appropin­quantibus**, portās clausērunt. | **As the enemy was approaching**, they closed the gates. |

### 2.2 With Perfect Passive Participle (PPP)

Expresses an action **completed before** the main verb:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Bellō cōnfectō**, domum rediit. | **The war having been finished**, he returned home. |
| **Hīs rēbus cognitīs**, Caesar profectus est. | **These things having been learned**, Caesar set out. |
| **Signō datō**, mīlitēs impetum fēcērunt. | **The signal having been given**, the soldiers attacked. |

### 2.3 Noun + Noun or Noun + Adjective

When no participle is available (e.g., **esse** has no present participle), a noun + noun or noun + adjective construction fills the gap:

| Latin | English | Pattern |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Caesare duce** | With Caesar (as) leader | noun + noun |
| **Cicerōne cōnsule** | When Cicero was consul | noun + noun |
| **Mē invītō** | Against my will (with me unwilling) | pronoun + adjective |
| **Caelō serēnō** | The sky being clear | noun + adjective |
| **Nūllā morā factā** | No delay having been made | noun(+adj) + PPP |

### 2.4 Common Ablative Absolute Phrases

These set phrases appear frequently in Latin prose, especially in Caesar and Livy:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **hīs rēbus cognitīs** | when these things were learned |
| **quibus rēbus cognitīs** | when these things were learned |
| **proeliō commissō** | when battle was joined |
| **ponte factō** | when a bridge was built |
| **cōnsiliō captō** | a plan having been adopted |
| **omnibus rēbus parātīs** | all things having been prepared |
| **pāce factā** | peace having been made |
| **operiō prīmā lūce** | at first light |

## 3. The Supine

The supine is a **verbal noun** of the 4th declension, formed from the 4th principal part. It exists only in the **accusative** and **ablative** singular.

### 3.1 Accusative Supine (Purpose with Verbs of Motion)

The accusative supine (ending in **-um**) expresses **purpose** after verbs of motion:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Vēnit **spectātum**. | He came **to watch**. |
| Legiōnēs mīsit **frūmentum** **petītum**. | He sent the legions **to seek grain**. |
| Eō **dormītum**. | I am going **to sleep**. |
| Vēnērunt **auxilium** **rogātum**. | They came **to ask for help**. |
| Īvit **lūsum**. | He went **to play**. |

> **Note:** The accusative supine can take a direct object: *mīsit lēgātōs pācem **petītum*** — "he sent envoys to seek peace."

### 3.2 Ablative Supine (Respect / Specification)

The ablative supine (ending in **-ū**) is used with certain adjectives to express **in what respect** something is true:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Hoc est facile **dictū**. | This is easy **to say**. |
| Rēs est difficilis **factū**. | The thing is difficult **to do**. |
| Mīrābile **vīsū**! | Wonderful **to see / to behold**! |
| Horribile **audītū**! | Horrible **to hear**! |
| Optimum **cognitū** | Best **to know** |

Common adjectives used with the ablative supine: **facilis** (easy), **difficilis** (difficult), **mīrābilis** (wonderful), **horribilis** (horrible), **optimus** (best), **turpis** (shameful), **incrēdibilis** (incredible).

## 4. The Locative Case

The locative is a vestigial case expressing **place where** without a preposition. It survives for:

### 4.1 Cities and Small Islands

| Declension | Locative Ending | Example | Meaning |
|-----------|----------------|---------|---------|
| 1st sing. | **-ae** | **Rōmae** | at/in Rome |
| 2nd sing. | **-ī** | **Corinthī** | at/in Corinth |
| 3rd sing. | **-ī** or **-e** | **Carthāginī** / **Carthāgine** | at/in Carthage |
| Plural (any) | **-īs** | **Athēnīs** | at/in Athens |
| | | **Syrācūsīs** | at/in Syracuse |

### 4.2 Special Common Nouns

A few common nouns retain the locative:

| Latin | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **domī** | at home |
| **rūrī** (or **rūre**) | in the country |
| **humī** | on the ground |
| **bellī** (archaic) | in war (rare) |
| **mīlitiae** | in military service |

### 4.3 Place Constructions Summary

| Concept | Cities/Small Islands | Common Nouns |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Place where** | Locative (Rōmae) | in + ablative (in urbe) |
| **Place to which** | Accusative alone (Rōmam) | ad/in + accusative (ad urbem) |
| **Place from which** | Ablative alone (Rōmā) | ā/ab/ē/ex + ablative (ex urbe) |
| **Special** | domī, rūrī, humī (locative) | domum (to home), domō (from home) |

### 4.4 Examples

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Rōmae** habitat. | He lives **in Rome**. |
| **Rōmam** proficīscitur. | He sets out **for Rome**. |
| **Rōmā** discessit. | He departed **from Rome**. |
| **Domī** manēbō. | I will stay **at home**. |
| **Domum** revertit. | He returned **home**. |
| **Domō** exiit. | He went out **from home**. |

## 5. Special Adjective and Verb Constructions

### 5.1 Verbs Governing the Dative

Many Latin verbs that translate as "help," "please," "harm," "trust," etc. take a **dative** object instead of the accusative:

| Latin | Meaning | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **crēdō** | believe, trust | Tibi **crēdō**. — I trust **you**. |
| **noceō** | harm | Nēminī **nocēbō**. — I will harm **no one**. |
| **pāreō** | obey | Lēgibus **pārē**. — Obey **the laws**. |
| **placeō** | please | Hoc mihi **placet**. — This **pleases** me. |
| **persuādeō** | persuade | Eī **persuāsit**. — He persuaded **him**. |
| **imperō** | command | Mīlitibus **imperāvit**. — He commanded **the soldiers**. |
| **serviō** | serve | Patriae **servīmus**. — We serve **the fatherland**. |
| **studeō** | be eager for | Litterīs **studet**. — He is devoted to **literature**. |
| **resistō** | resist | Hostibus **resistunt**. — They resist **the enemy**. |
| **faveō** | favor | Populō **favet**. — He favors **the people**. |
| **invideō** | envy | Nēminī **invideō**. — I envy **no one**. |
| **ignōscō** | forgive | Mihi **ignōsce**. — Forgive **me**. |

### 5.2 Verbs Governing the Ablative

| Latin | Meaning | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **ūtor** | use | Gladiō **ūtitur**. — He uses **a sword**. |
| **fruor** | enjoy | Vītā **fruimur**. — We enjoy **life**. |
| **fungor** | perform | Officiō **fungitur**. — He performs **his duty**. |
| **potior** | gain possession of | Urbe **potītus est**. — He gained possession of **the city**. |
| **vescor** | feed on | Carne **vescitur**. — He feeds on **meat**. |

> **Memory aid: UFFPV** — Ūtor, Fruor, Fungor, Potior, Vescor (all deponent, all + ablative).

### 5.3 Verbs and Adjectives Governing the Genitive

| Type | Word | Meaning | Example |
|------|------|---------|---------|
| Verb | **meminī** | remember | Patriae **meminī**. — I remember **the fatherland**. |
| Verb | **oblīvīscor** | forget | Iniūriārum **oblīvīscere**. — Forget **the injuries**. |
| Verb | **misereor** | pity | Nostrī **miserēre**. — Pity **us**. |
| Adjective | **plēnus** | full of | Plēna **perīculī**. — Full **of danger**. |
| Adjective | **cupidus** | desirous of | Cupidus **glōriae**. — Desirous **of glory**. |
| Adjective | **perītus** | skilled in | Perītus **bellī**. — Skilled **in war**. |
| Adjective | **memor** | mindful of | Memor **beneficiōrum**. — Mindful **of kindnesses**. |

## 6. Impersonal Verbs

Impersonal verbs have no personal subject and appear only in the **3rd person singular** (with infinitives or subjunctive clauses as their logical subjects).

### 6.1 Common Impersonal Verbs

| Latin | Meaning | Construction | Example |
|-------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **licet** | it is permitted | dat. + inf. | Mihi **licet** abīre. — I am **permitted** to leave. |
| **oportet** | it is necessary, ought | acc. + inf. | Tē **oportet** studēre. — You **ought** to study. |
| **decet** | it is fitting | acc. + inf. | Tē **decet** esse fortem. — It **befits** you to be brave. |
| **dēdecet** | it is unfitting | acc. + inf. | Hoc tē **dēdecet**. — This is **unfitting** for you. |
| **placet** | it pleases, is decided | dat. + inf. | Mihi **placet** manēre. — I am **pleased** to stay. |
| **piget** | it irks | acc. + gen. | Mē **piget** stultitiae. — I am **ashamed** of (my) folly. |
| **pudet** | it shames | acc. + gen. | Mē **pudet** factī. — I am **ashamed** of the deed. |
| **paenitet** | it causes regret | acc. + gen. | Mē **paenitet** errōris. — I **regret** the mistake. |
| **taedet** | it wearies | acc. + gen. | Mē **taedet** vītae. — I am **weary** of life. |
| **miseret** | it causes pity | acc. + gen. | Mē **miseret** tuī. — I **pity** you. |

### 6.2 Necesse Est and Opus Est

| Construction | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **necesse est** + dat. + inf. | it is necessary | Mihi **necesse est** proficīscī. — I **must** set out. |
| **necesse est** + subj. | it is necessary that | **Necesse est** ut abeās. — It is necessary **that** you leave. |
| **opus est** + abl. | there is need of | Cōnsiliō **opus est**. — There is **need of** a plan. |
| **opus est** + dat. + abl. | someone needs something | Mihi pecūniā **opus est**. — I **need** money. |

### 6.3 Impersonal Passives

Any intransitive verb can be used impersonally in the passive to express a general action:

| Latin | Literal | Natural English |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| **Pugnātum est.** | It was fought. | There was fighting. / A battle was fought. |
| **Ventum est.** | It was come. | They arrived. / People came. |
| **Curritur.** | It is run. | There is running. / People run. |
| **Ā mīlitibus fortiter **pugnātum est**.** | It was fought bravely by the soldiers. | The soldiers fought bravely. |

2. Sole occidente, domum rediimus.
3. Caesare cōnsule, multae lēgēs lātae sunt.
4. Hīs rēbus cognitīs, senātus bellum dēcrēvit.
5. Mē invītō, profectus est.
6. Proeliō commissō, mīlitēs fortiter pugnāvērunt.

4. "When these things were learned, the senate decreed war." (PPP; common Caesarian phrase)

### Exercise C: Special Verb Constructions

Supply the correct case for the word in parentheses:

1. Tibi (crēdō). — The object of crēdō should be in the ________ case.
2. Gladiō (ūtor). — The object of ūtor should be in the ________ case.
3. Patriae (meminī). — The object of meminī should be in the ________ case.
4. Mihi (licet) abīre. — Mihi is in the ________ case.
5. Mē (piget) errōris. — Mē is ________; errōris is ________.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **Dative** — crēdō takes a dative object. "I trust you."
2. **Ablative** — ūtor takes an ablative object. "He uses a sword."
3. **Genitive** — meminī takes a genitive object. "I remember the fatherland."
4. **Dative** — licet is impersonal; the person permitted is in the dative. "I am permitted to leave."
5. **Accusative**; **genitive** — piget takes an accusative of the person affected and genitive of the cause. "I am ashamed of my mistake."

### Exercise D: Translate into Latin

1. With the bridge having been built, Caesar led the army across.
2. It is easy to say, difficult to do.
3. He lives at home but went to Athens.
4. You ought to obey the laws.
5. There was fighting for many hours.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

5. **Multās hōrās pugnātum est.** (Impersonal passive)

### Exercise E: Identify the Construction

Label each underlined element as: **ablative absolute**, **supine**, **locative**, **impersonal verb**, or **special case construction**:

1. _Rēbus omnibus parātīs_, profectī sumus.
2. Vēnit _spectātum_.
