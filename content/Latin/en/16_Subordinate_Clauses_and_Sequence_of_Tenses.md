# 16. Subordinate Clauses and Sequence of Tenses (Sententiae Subiunctae et Consecutio Temporum)

## Learning Objectives
- Recognize and construct purpose clauses using **ut/ne** + subjunctive
- Distinguish result clauses from purpose clauses in form and meaning
- Form causal clauses with **quod/quia/quoniam** (indicative) vs **cum** (subjunctive)
- Use temporal clauses with **cum, postquam, antequam, dum, donec**
- Understand concessive clauses with **quamquam, quamvis, cum**
- Master the **sequence of tenses** (primary vs secondary) for subordinate subjunctive clauses
- Translate complex Latin sentences containing multiple subordinate clauses

## 1. Purpose Clauses

Purpose clauses express the **reason for which** an action is performed ("in order to," "so that"). In Latin they are introduced by **ut** (positive) or **ne** (negative) followed by the **subjunctive**.

### 1.1 Positive Purpose (ut + subjunctive)

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Venimus **ut videāmus**. | We come **in order to see** / **so that we may see**. |
| Mīlitēs mīsit **ut urbem caperet**. | He sent soldiers **to capture the city**. |
| Festīnā **ut pervenias**. | Hurry **so that you may arrive**. |

### 1.2 Negative Purpose (ne + subjunctive)

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Fugit **ne caperētur**. | He fled **so that he would not be captured**. |
| Tacē **ne audiāris**. | Be silent **lest you be heard**. |
| Pecūniam cēlāvit **ne invenīrētur**. | He hid the money **so it would not be found**. |

### 1.3 Relative Clauses of Purpose

A relative pronoun (**quī, quae, quod**) can replace **ut** to introduce a purpose clause when the clause refers to a definite antecedent:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Lēgātōs mīsit **quī pācem peterent**. | He sent envoys **to seek peace** (who would seek peace). |
| Locum dēlēgit **quō facilius sē dēfenderet**. | He chose a place **to defend himself more easily**. |

> **Note:** When the purpose clause contains a comparative (facilius, melius), **quō** replaces **ut**.

### 1.4 Key Signals

- The main clause describes an **action**; the purpose clause explains **why**.
- Purpose clauses **never** use the indicative mood.
- Negative purpose always uses **ne**, never **ut nōn**.

## 2. Result Clauses

Result clauses express the **consequence** or **outcome** of an action ("so that," "with the result that"). They use **ut** (positive) or **ut nōn** (negative) + subjunctive.

### 2.1 Signal Words in the Main Clause

Result clauses are almost always preceded by a signal word in the main clause:

| Signal Word | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **tam** | so (with adj/adv) | Tam fortis erat **ut nēmō vinceret**. |
| **tantus, -a, -um** | so great | Tanta erat tempestās **ut nāvēs frangerentur**. |
| **tālis, -e** | of such a kind | Tālis erat **ut omnēs eum amārent**. |
| **tot** | so many | Tot hostēs erant **ut fūgērēmus**. |
| **ita / sīc** | so, in such a way | Ita clāmāvit **ut omnēs audīrent**. |
| **adeō** | to such a degree | Adeō timēbat **ut tremeret**. |

### 2.2 Distinguishing Purpose from Result

| Feature | Purpose Clause | Result Clause |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| **Signal word** in main clause? | Usually none | Almost always present (tam, tantus, ita, etc.) |
| **Negation** | **ne** | **ut nōn** |
| **Reflexive pronoun** | Refers to subject of main verb | Refers to subject of subordinate verb |
| **Meaning** | Intention (looking forward) | Actual consequence |

**Compare:**

- **Purpose:** Vēnit **ut vidēret**. — He came **in order to see**.
- **Result:** Tam clārus erat **ut omnēs vidērent**. — He was so famous **that everyone saw** (him).

### 2.3 Negative Result Clauses

Negative result uses **ut nōn** (not **ne**), and negative pronouns/adverbs remain in their standard forms:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Tam stultus erat **ut nōn intellegeret**. | He was so foolish **that he did not understand**. |
| Tam paucī erant **ut nēmō resisteret**. | There were so few **that no one resisted**. |
| Ita ēgit **ut numquam errāret**. | He acted in such a way **that he never made a mistake**. |

## 3. Causal Clauses

Causal clauses express the **reason** or **cause** for the action in the main clause.

### 3.1 With Indicative (Stated Fact)

**Quod, quia, quoniam** + indicative state a reason the author presents as factual:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Quod** bonus est, eum amāmus. | **Because** he is good, we love him. |
| **Quia** Caesar abiit, hostēs vēnērunt. | **Because** Caesar departed, the enemies came. |
| **Quoniam** hoc vērum est, crēdō. | **Since** this is true, I believe (it). |

### 3.2 With Subjunctive (Alleged Reason)

**Cum** (since) + subjunctive gives a reason presented as part of the speaker's or subject's thinking, or as circumstantial background:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Cum** id vidisset, discessit. | **Since** he had seen this, he departed. |
| **Cum** mīles fortis esset, pugnāvit. | **Since** the soldier was brave, he fought. |

**Quod/quia** + subjunctive can indicate an alleged or reported reason (not the author's own assertion):

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| Sōcratēs damnātus est **quod iuventūtem corrumperet**. | Socrates was condemned **because (allegedly) he was corrupting the youth**. |

### 3.3 Summary Table

| Conjunction | Mood | Nuance |
|-------------|------|--------|
| quod, quia, quoniam | Indicative | Author's stated fact |
| quod, quia | Subjunctive | Reported/alleged reason |
| cum (causal) | Subjunctive | Circumstantial cause |

## 4. Temporal Clauses

Temporal clauses describe **when** the action of the main clause occurs.

### 4.1 Cum (When)

**Cum** has different meanings depending on mood and tense:

| Construction | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| cum + indicative (present/future) | when (definite time) | **Cum** veniet, gaudēbō. — **When** he comes, I shall rejoice. |
| cum + imperfect subjunctive | when / while (circumstantial) | **Cum** Rōmae esset, Cicerōnem vīdit. — **When** he was in Rome, he saw Cicero. |
| cum + pluperfect subjunctive | when / after (circumstantial) | **Cum** hoc audīvisset, respondit. — **When** he had heard this, he replied. |
| cum + indicative (with tum/tamen) | at the time when | **Cum** tacent, clāmant. — **When** they are silent, they cry out. (Cicero) |

### 4.2 Postquam, Ubi, Ut (After, When)

These conjunctions typically take the **perfect indicative**, even for past narrative:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Postquam** id audīvit, profectus est. | **After** he heard this, he set out. |
| **Ubi** Caesar pervēnit, castra posuit. | **When** Caesar arrived, he pitched camp. |

### 4.3 Antequam / Priusquam (Before)

| Construction | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| antequam/priusquam + indicative | Before (simple fact) |
| antequam/priusquam + subjunctive | Before (anticipation, prevention) |

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Antequam** lūx vēnit, profectī sunt. | **Before** the light came, they set out. (fact) |
| **Priusquam** hostēs venīrent, mūnītiōnēs fēcit. | **Before** the enemy could come, he built fortifications. (anticipation) |

### 4.4 Dum, Dōnec, Quoad (While, Until)

| Conjunction | Mood | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|------|---------|---------|
| **dum** | present indicative | while (during) | **Dum** scrībō, lēgis. — **While** I write, you read. |
| **dum / dōnec** | indicative | until (fact) | Exspectā **dum** veniō. — Wait **until** I come. |
| **dum / dōnec** | subjunctive | until (anticipated) | Exspectāvit **dōnec** venīret. — He waited **until** she should come. |
| **quoad** | indicative | as long as | **Quoad** vīxit, fēlīx fuit. — **As long as** he lived, he was happy. |

## 5. Concessive Clauses

Concessive clauses express **concession** ("although," "even though").

### 5.1 Types of Concessive Clauses

| Conjunction | Mood | Nuance | Example |
|-------------|------|--------|---------|
| **quamquam** | Indicative | although (acknowledged fact) | **Quamquam** fessus erat, labōrābat. — **Although** he was tired, he kept working. |
| **quamvīs** | Subjunctive | however much, even though | **Quamvīs** fortis sit, vincī potest. — **However** brave he may be, he can be conquered. |
| **cum** (concessive) | Subjunctive | although (circumstantial) | **Cum** id scīret, tacuit. — **Although** he knew this, he was silent. |
| **etsī / tametsī** | Indicative | even if (factual) | **Etsī** difficile est, temptābō. — **Even if** it is difficult, I shall try. |
| **etiamsī** | Subjunctive | even if (hypothetical) | **Etiamsī** veniat, nōn manēbō. — **Even if** he comes, I shall not stay. |

### 5.2 Correlatives

Concessive clauses often pair with **tamen** ("nevertheless") in the main clause:

| Latin | English |
|-------|---------|
| **Quamquam** senex erat, **tamen** pugnāvit. | **Although** he was old, **nevertheless** he fought. |
| **Cum** omnia parāta essent, **tamen** discessit. | **Although** everything was prepared, **nevertheless** he departed. |

## 6. Sequence of Tenses

The **consecutio temporum** (sequence of tenses) governs which subjunctive tense appears in a subordinate clause, based on the tense of the main verb.

### 6.1 Primary Sequence

When the main verb is in a **primary** (present, future, future perfect, or true perfect) tense:

| Subordinate Time | Subjunctive Tense | Example |
|------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Same time / after | **Present** subjunctive | Rogat **quid faciās**. — He asks what you **are doing**. |
| Before | **Perfect** subjunctive | Rogat **quid fēceris**. — He asks what you **did / have done**. |

### 6.2 Secondary Sequence

When the main verb is in a **secondary** (imperfect, perfect [historical], pluperfect) tense:

| Subordinate Time | Subjunctive Tense | Example |
|------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Same time / after | **Imperfect** subjunctive | Rogāvit **quid facerēs**. — He asked what you **were doing**. |
| Before | **Pluperfect** subjunctive | Rogāvit **quid fēcissēs**. — He asked what you **had done**. |

### 6.3 Complete Sequence Table

| | **Contemporaneous / Subsequent** | **Prior** |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Sequence** (present, future, future perfect, true perfect) | Present subjunctive (-e-/-a-) | Perfect subjunctive (-erim) |
| **Secondary Sequence** (imperfect, historical perfect, pluperfect) | Imperfect subjunctive (-rem) | Pluperfect subjunctive (-issem) |

### 6.4 Applying the Sequence

**Step 1:** Identify the tense of the main verb.
**Step 2:** Determine if it is primary or secondary.
**Step 3:** Determine the time relationship (same time / before).
**Step 4:** Select the appropriate subjunctive tense.

**Example walkthrough:**

- **Mittit** lēgātōs **quī pācem petant**. (Primary: mittit = present; contemporaneous: present subjunctive)
  - "He sends envoys to seek peace."
- **Mīsit** lēgātōs **quī pācem peterent**. (Secondary: mīsit = perfect; contemporaneous: imperfect subjunctive)
  - "He sent envoys to seek peace."
- **Mittit** lēgātōs **quī pācem petīverint**. (Primary: prior action: perfect subjunctive)
  - "He sends envoys who have sought peace."
- **Mīsit** lēgātōs **quī pācem petīvissent**. (Secondary: prior action: pluperfect subjunctive)
  - "He sent envoys who had sought peace."

## 7. Practice Exercises

### Exercise A: Identify the Clause Type

Identify each subordinate clause as **purpose**, **result**, **causal**, **temporal**, or **concessive**:

1. Tam celeriter cucurrit ut nēmō eum caperet.
2. Caesar mīlitēs mīsit ut pontem facerent.
3. Cum hostēs vēnissent, portās clausērunt.
4. Quamquam perīculum magnum erat, tamen prōgressī sunt.
5. Quod bonus imperātor erat, mīlitēs eum amābant.
6. Festīnāvit nē sērō venīret.
7. Adeō territus erat ut fugeret.
8. Dum Rōmae habitat, multōs amīcōs invēnit.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **Result** — signal word *tam*, negative *nēmō* (not *ne*). "He ran so quickly that no one caught him."
2. **Purpose** — *ut* + subjunctive with no signal word. "Caesar sent soldiers to build a bridge."
3. **Temporal** (*cum* circumstantial) — *cum* + pluperfect subjunctive. "When the enemies had come, they closed the gates."
4. **Concessive** — *quamquam* + indicative, paired with *tamen*. "Although the danger was great, nevertheless they advanced."
5. **Causal** — *quod* + indicative (stated fact). "Because he was a good commander, the soldiers loved him."
6. **Purpose** (negative) — *nē* + subjunctive. "He hurried so that he would not arrive late."
7. **Result** — signal word *adeō*. "He was so terrified that he fled."
8. **Temporal** — *dum* + present indicative (during). "While he lived in Rome, he found many friends."

</details>

### Exercise B: Sequence of Tenses

Supply the correct subjunctive form of the verb in parentheses:

1. Rogat quid ________. (facere, 2nd sg, contemporaneous)
2. Rogāvit quid ________. (facere, 2nd sg, contemporaneous)
3. Nescit cūr Caesar ________. (venīre, 3rd sg, prior)
4. Nesciēbat cūr Caesar ________. (venīre, 3rd sg, prior)
5. Mittit nūntiōs quī hoc ________. (nūntiāre, 3rd pl, contemporaneous)
6. Mīsit nūntiōs quī hoc ________. (nūntiāre, 3rd pl, contemporaneous)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **faciās** — Primary sequence (rogat = present), contemporaneous → present subjunctive.
2. **facerēs** — Secondary sequence (rogāvit = perfect), contemporaneous → imperfect subjunctive.
3. **vēnerit** — Primary sequence (nescit = present), prior → perfect subjunctive.
4. **vēnisset** — Secondary sequence (nesciēbat = imperfect), prior → pluperfect subjunctive.
5. **nūntient** — Primary sequence (mittit = present), contemporaneous → present subjunctive.
6. **nūntiārent** — Secondary sequence (mīsit = perfect), contemporaneous → imperfect subjunctive.

</details>

### Exercise C: Translate into Latin

1. He fled so that he would not be seen.
2. The storm was so great that no one could sail.
3. Although the soldiers were tired, they fought bravely.
4. After Caesar arrived, he pitched camp.
5. She asks why he came.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **Fūgit nē vidērētur.** (Purpose, negative, secondary sequence)
2. **Tanta erat tempestās ut nēmō nāvigāre posset.** (Result clause with signal word *tanta*)
3. **Quamquam mīlitēs fessī erant, tamen fortiter pugnāvērunt.** (Concessive, *quamquam* + indicative)
4. **Postquam Caesar pervēnit, castra posuit.** (Temporal, *postquam* + perfect indicative)
5. **Rogat cūr vēnerit.** (Indirect question, primary sequence, prior action → perfect subjunctive)

</details>

### Exercise D: Purpose or Result?

Translate each sentence and state whether the **ut** clause is purpose or result:

1. Vēnit ut disceret.
2. Tam doctus erat ut omnēs eum laudārent.
3. Imperātor exercitum parāvit ut bellum gereret.
4. Ita fortiter pugnāvērunt ut hostēs fugārent.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. "He came in order to learn." — **Purpose** (no signal word; intention)
2. "He was so learned that everyone praised him." — **Result** (signal word *tam*)
3. "The general prepared an army to wage war." — **Purpose** (intention; no signal word)
4. "They fought so bravely that they put the enemy to flight." — **Result** (signal word *ita*)

</details>
