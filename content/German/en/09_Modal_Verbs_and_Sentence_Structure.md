- Learn all six German modal verbs: können, müssen, dürfen, sollen, wollen, mögen/möchten

- Distinguish between müssen (necessity) and sollen (obligation/recommendation)
- Understand the difference between mögen (to like) and möchten (would like)
- Form negative sentences with modal verbs using nicht and kein
- Learn the simple past tense (Präteritum) of modal verbs, the form most commonly used even in spoken German
- Use modal verbs in common everyday expressions and idiomatic phrases
- Construct complex sentences with correct word order using modal verbs

## 1. What Are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs (Modalverben) are a special group of auxiliary verbs that modify the meaning of the main verb in a sentence. Rather than describing an action by themselves, they express the speaker's attitude toward that action — whether it is possible, necessary, permitted, desired, or recommended. German has **six modal verbs**:

| Modal Verb | Core Meaning | English Equivalent |
|------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **können** | ability, possibility | can, to be able to |
| **müssen** | necessity, compulsion | must, to have to |
| **dürfen** | permission | may, to be allowed to |
| **sollen** | obligation, recommendation | should, to be supposed to |
| **wollen** | desire, intention | to want to |
| **mögen** | liking, preference | to like |

Additionally, **möchten** (would like to) functions as a separate modal verb in everyday usage, although it is historically the Konjunktiv II (subjunctive) form of mögen.

### 1.1 Shared Grammatical Properties

All modal verbs share several distinctive conjugation features that set them apart from regular verbs:

1. **Stem-vowel change in the singular**: In the ich, du, and er/sie/es forms, most modals change their stem vowel compared to the infinitive (the exception is sollen).
2. **No ending in 1st and 3rd person singular**: The ich-form and the er/sie/es-form are identical and take no personal ending (unlike regular verbs, where er/sie/es takes -t).
3. **Paired with an infinitive**: Modal verbs almost always appear together with a second verb in its infinitive form, which goes to the **end** of the sentence.

> **Key insight**: Unlike English, where modal verbs are followed directly by the base verb ("I can swim"), German places the infinitive at the very end of the clause, creating a bracket structure around the rest of the sentence. This is one of the most distinctive features of German syntax.

## 2. The Sentence Bracket (Satzklammer)

The most important structural concept to master with modal verbs is the **Satzklammer** (sentence bracket or sentence frame). The conjugated modal verb occupies the **second position** (V2 position) in a main clause, while the infinitive of the main verb goes to the very **end** of the sentence.

### 2.1 Basic Structure

```
Position 1       Position 2 (V2)      Middle Field        End
(Subject/other)   (Modal verb)        (Objects, etc.)     (Infinitive)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Ich                kann                gut                 schwimmen.
I                  can                 well                swim.
```

The modal verb and the infinitive form a "bracket" that encloses the middle of the sentence:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                       SATZKLAMMER                                │
│                                                                  │
│  Subject ──► [MODAL VERB] ──── middle field ──── [INFINITIVE]    │
│              (conjugated)                        (unchanged)     │
│                                                                  │
│  Ich         kann              Deutsch             sprechen.     │
│  Wir         müssen            heute Abend          arbeiten.    │
│  Du          darfst            hier nicht            rauchen.    │
│  Er          will              ein neues Buch        lesen.      │
│  Sie         soll              den Arzt              anrufen.    │
│  Ich         möchte            einen Kaffee          trinken.    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 2.2 Statement Examples

- **Ich kann** Deutsch **sprechen**. — I can speak German.
- **Wir müssen** morgen früh **aufstehen**. — We must get up early tomorrow.
- **Du darfst** hier nicht **rauchen**. — You may not smoke here.
- **Er will** nach Berlin **fahren**. — He wants to drive to Berlin.
- **Sie soll** den Arzt **anrufen**. — She is supposed to call the doctor.
- **Ich möchte** ein Glas Wasser **trinken**. — I would like to drink a glass of water.
- **Die Kinder können** im Garten **spielen**. — The children can play in the garden.
- **Ihr müsst** eure Hausaufgaben **machen**. — You all must do your homework.

### 2.3 Yes/No Questions with Modal Verbs

In **yes/no questions**, the modal verb moves to **position 1** (the very beginning of the sentence):

```
[MODAL VERB] ── Subject ── middle field ── [INFINITIVE]?

Kannst           du          Deutsch       sprechen?
Can              you         German        speak?
```

More examples:

- **Kannst** du mir **helfen**? — Can you help me?
- **Müssen** wir jetzt **gehen**? — Do we have to go now?
- **Darf** ich hier **sitzen**? — May I sit here?
- **Willst** du mitkommen? — Do you want to come along?
- **Soll** ich das Fenster **öffnen**? — Shall I open the window?
- **Möchten** Sie etwas **trinken**? — Would you like something to drink?

### 2.4 W-Questions (Question-Word Questions)

In **W-questions**, the question word takes position 1, and the modal stays in position 2:

```
W-word     [MODAL VERB]    Subject    middle field    [INFINITIVE]?

Was        kannst          du         gut             kochen?
What       can             you        well            cook?
```

More examples:

- **Was** **willst** du heute **machen**? — What do you want to do today?
- **Wann** **müssen** wir **abfahren**? — When do we have to depart?
- **Wo** **kann** ich das **kaufen**? — Where can I buy that?
- **Warum** **darfst** du nicht **mitkommen**? — Why are you not allowed to come along?
- **Wie** **soll** ich das **machen**? — How should I do that?
- **Wer** **kann** mir **helfen**? — Who can help me?

### 2.5 Modal Verbs Without an Infinitive

Sometimes the infinitive can be omitted when the meaning is clear from context, especially with verbs of motion (gehen, fahren, kommen) or common activities:

- Ich **kann** Deutsch. — I can (speak) German. (*sprechen* is implied)
- Er **muss** nach Hause. — He must (go) home. (*gehen* is implied)
- Ich **will** ein Bier. — I want a beer. (*haben/trinken* is implied)
- Sie **möchte** ins Kino. — She would like (to go) to the cinema. (*gehen* is implied)
- Wir **müssen** zur Arbeit. — We have to (go) to work. (*gehen/fahren* is implied)
- **Darf** ich mal? — May I (do that/try)? (context determines the verb)

> **Note**: This omission is extremely common in everyday speech. The implied verb is always understood from context.

## 3. können — Can, To Be Able To

**Können** expresses **ability**, **possibility**, or **skill**. It is one of the most frequently used modal verbs in German.

### 3.1 Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **kann** | Ich **kann** schwimmen. — I can swim. |
| du | **kannst** | Du **kannst** gut singen. — You can sing well. |
| er/sie/es | **kann** | Er **kann** Gitarre spielen. — He can play guitar. |
| wir | **können** | Wir **können** Deutsch sprechen. — We can speak German. |
| ihr | **könnt** | Ihr **könnt** morgen kommen. — You (all) can come tomorrow. |
| sie/Sie | **können** | Sie **können** uns helfen. — They/You (formal) can help us. |

> **Note the pattern**: The stem vowel changes from **ö** (infinitive: k**ö**nnen) to **a** (ich k**a**nn) in the singular forms. The ich and er/sie/es forms are identical: **kann** (no personal ending).

### 3.2 Meanings and Uses

**Ability / Skill:**
- Ich **kann** Klavier spielen. — I can play piano.
- Sie **kann** drei Sprachen sprechen. — She can speak three languages.
- **Kannst** du kochen? — Can you cook?
- Er **kann** sehr gut zeichnen. — He can draw very well.

**Possibility:**
- Das **kann** passieren. — That can happen.
- Wir **können** morgen ins Kino gehen. — We can go to the cinema tomorrow.
- Es **kann** sein, dass er kommt. — It could be that he comes.
- Das **kann** nicht stimmen. — That cannot be right.

**Polite Request (with du/Sie):**
- **Kannst** du mir bitte helfen? — Can you please help me?
- **Können** Sie das bitte wiederholen? — Can you please repeat that? (formal)
- **Könnt** ihr bitte leiser sein? — Can you (all) please be quieter?

## 4. müssen — Must, To Have To

**Müssen** expresses **necessity**, **compulsion**, or **strong obligation**. It indicates that something is required, unavoidable, or urgently needed.

### 4.1 Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **muss** | Ich **muss** arbeiten. — I must work. |
| du | **musst** | Du **musst** pünktlich sein. — You must be on time. |
| er/sie/es | **muss** | Sie **muss** zum Arzt gehen. — She must go to the doctor. |
| wir | **müssen** | Wir **müssen** das Haus putzen. — We must clean the house. |
| ihr | **müsst** | Ihr **müsst** die Hausaufgaben machen. — You must do the homework. |
| sie/Sie | **müssen** | Sie **müssen** den Vertrag unterschreiben. — You (formal) must sign the contract. |

> **Note the pattern**: The stem vowel changes from **ü** (infinitive: m**ü**ssen) to **u** (ich m**u**ss) in the singular forms.

### 4.2 Meanings and Uses

**Necessity / Compulsion:**
- Ich **muss** jeden Tag zur Arbeit fahren. — I have to drive to work every day.
- Wir **müssen** heute einkaufen gehen. — We have to go shopping today.
- Du **musst** mehr Wasser trinken. — You must drink more water.
- Alle **müssen** die Regeln befolgen. — Everyone must follow the rules.

**Strong Recommendation:**
- Du **musst** diesen Film sehen! — You must see this film! (enthusiastic recommendation)
- Ihr **müsst** unbedingt nach Berlin fahren. — You absolutely must go to Berlin.

**Logical Conclusion / Deduction:**
- Er **muss** krank sein. — He must be sick. (logical deduction)
- Das **muss** ein Fehler sein. — That must be a mistake.
- Sie **muss** schon zu Hause sein. — She must be home already.

> **Important**: Unlike English "must not," the German negation of müssen does **not** express prohibition. This is a critical difference covered in [Section 11: Negation with Modal Verbs](#11-negation-with-modal-verbs).

## 5. dürfen — May, To Be Allowed To

**Dürfen** expresses **permission** or, when negated, **prohibition**. It is the modal verb of rules, regulations, and authority.

### 5.1 Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **darf** | Ich **darf** hier parken. — I may park here. |
| du | **darfst** | Du **darfst** heute länger aufbleiben. — You may stay up longer today. |
| er/sie/es | **darf** | Er **darf** das Auto fahren. — He is allowed to drive the car. |
| wir | **dürfen** | Wir **dürfen** den Raum betreten. — We are allowed to enter the room. |
| ihr | **dürft** | Ihr **dürft** jetzt gehen. — You may go now. |
| sie/Sie | **dürfen** | Sie **dürfen** hier nicht rauchen. — You (formal) may not smoke here. |

> **Note the pattern**: The stem vowel changes from **ü** (infinitive: d**ü**rfen) to **a** (ich d**a**rf) in the singular forms.

### 5.2 Meanings and Uses

**Permission:**
- **Darf** ich hier sitzen? — May I sit here?
- Du **darfst** mein Handy benutzen. — You may use my phone.
- Die Kinder **dürfen** im Garten spielen. — The children are allowed to play in the garden.
- In diesem Bereich **darf** man essen. — In this area, one is allowed to eat.

**Prohibition (negated):**
- Hier **darf** man nicht rauchen. — One may not smoke here. / Smoking is prohibited here.
- Du **darfst** nicht so laut sprechen. — You must not speak so loudly.
- Sie **dürfen** hier nicht parken. — You are not allowed to park here.
- Kinder **dürfen** keinen Alkohol trinken. — Children must not drink alcohol.

**Polite Request:**
- **Darf** ich Sie etwas fragen? — May I ask you something?
- **Dürfte** ich bitte vorbei? — Might I pass, please? (Konjunktiv II, very polite)
- **Darf** ich mich vorstellen? — May I introduce myself?

> **Cultural note**: Germans take rules seriously. Signs saying *Rauchen verboten* (Smoking forbidden) or *Betreten verboten* (Entry forbidden) are common and expected to be followed. Using *dürfen* correctly shows respect for these norms and an understanding of German culture.

## 6. sollen — Should, To Be Supposed To

**Sollen** expresses an **obligation** or **expectation** originating from an external source — someone else wants you to do something, or there is a moral or societal expectation.

### 6.1 Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **soll** | Ich **soll** mehr Sport treiben. — I should exercise more. |
| du | **sollst** | Du **sollst** nicht lügen. — You shall not lie. |
| er/sie/es | **soll** | Er **soll** die E-Mail beantworten. — He is supposed to answer the email. |
| wir | **sollen** | Wir **sollen** um 8 Uhr da sein. — We are supposed to be there at 8 o'clock. |
| ihr | **sollt** | Ihr **sollt** eure Zimmer aufräumen. — You should tidy up your rooms. |
| sie/Sie | **sollen** | Sie **sollen** den Bericht bis Freitag schreiben. — You (formal) are supposed to write the report by Friday. |

> **Note**: Sollen is unique among modal verbs — it does **not** change its stem vowel in the singular. The conjugation is completely regular in this regard.

### 6.2 Meanings and Uses

**External Obligation / Someone Else's Wish:**
- Der Arzt sagt, ich **soll** mehr schlafen. — The doctor says I should sleep more.
- Meine Mutter sagt, ich **soll** anrufen. — My mother says I should call.
- Was **soll** ich machen? — What should I do? / What am I supposed to do?
- Der Chef sagt, wir **sollen** den Bericht fertig machen. — The boss says we should finish the report.

**Moral Obligation / Commandment:**
- Du **sollst** nicht stehlen. — Thou shalt not steal.
- Man **soll** immer die Wahrheit sagen. — One should always tell the truth.
- Du **sollst** deinen Nächsten lieben. — Thou shalt love thy neighbor.

**Suggestion / Offer:**
- **Soll** ich dir helfen? — Shall I help you?
- **Sollen** wir ins Restaurant gehen? — Shall we go to the restaurant?
- **Soll** ich das Fenster aufmachen? — Shall I open the window?

**Reported Speech / Hearsay:**
- Er **soll** sehr reich sein. — He is said to be very rich.
- Das Restaurant **soll** sehr gut sein. — The restaurant is supposed to be very good.
- Es **soll** morgen regnen. — It is supposed to rain tomorrow. (weather forecast)

## 7. wollen — To Want To

**Wollen** expresses a **strong desire**, **intention**, or **plan**. It is more direct and assertive than möchten.

### 7.1 Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **will** | Ich **will** nach Hause gehen. — I want to go home. |
| du | **willst** | Du **willst** immer Recht haben. — You always want to be right. |
| er/sie/es | **will** | Sie **will** Ärztin werden. — She wants to become a doctor. |
| wir | **wollen** | Wir **wollen** im Sommer nach Italien fahren. — We want to go to Italy in summer. |
| ihr | **wollt** | Ihr **wollt** ein neues Auto kaufen. — You want to buy a new car. |
| sie/Sie | **wollen** | Sie **wollen** ein Haus bauen. — They want to build a house. |

> **Note the pattern**: The stem vowel changes from **o** (infinitive: w**o**llen) to **i** (ich w**i**ll) in the singular forms.

### 7.2 Meanings and Uses

**Strong Desire / Intention:**
- Ich **will** Deutsch lernen. — I want to learn German.
- Was **willst** du zum Geburtstag? — What do you want for your birthday?
- Er **will** unbedingt diesen Job haben. — He absolutely wants this job.
- Sie **will** Lehrerin werden. — She wants to become a teacher.

**Plan / Future Intention:**
- Wir **wollen** nächstes Jahr heiraten. — We want to get married next year.
- Ich **will** morgen früh aufstehen. — I want/intend to get up early tomorrow.
- Er **will** ein Haus bauen. — He wants/intends to build a house.

**Refusal (negated):**
- Sie **will** nicht mitkommen. — She does not want to come along.
- Das Kind **will** nicht schlafen. — The child does not want to sleep.
- Ich **will** das nicht hören. — I do not want to hear that.

> **Critical warning**: German **will** is NOT the same as English "will" (future tense). This is one of the most common false friends for English speakers.
>
> | German | English | Meaning |
> |--------|---------|---------|
> | Ich **will** gehen. | I **want to** go. | Desire |
> | Ich **werde** gehen. | I **will** go. | Future tense (uses *werden*) |
>
> Remember: German *will* = English "want(s) to." The German future tense is formed with *werden* (covered in Lesson 16).

### 7.3 wollen vs. möchten — Directness

| wollen (direct) | möchten (polite) |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Ich **will** ein Bier. | Ich **möchte** ein Bier, bitte. |
| I want a beer. (blunt) | I would like a beer, please. (polite) |
| **Willst** du Kaffee? | **Möchtest** du Kaffee? |
| Do you want coffee? (casual) | Would you like coffee? (polite) |

> **Tip**: In restaurants, shops, and formal situations, always prefer **möchte** over **wollen** to sound polite and respectful.

## 8. mögen and möchten — To Like / Would Like

### 8.1 mögen — To Like

**Mögen** expresses a **general liking** or **preference**. In modern German, it is most often used **without an infinitive**, directly with a noun.

#### Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **mag** | Ich **mag** Schokolade. — I like chocolate. |
| du | **magst** | **Magst** du Kaffee? — Do you like coffee? |
| er/sie/es | **mag** | Sie **mag** klassische Musik. — She likes classical music. |
| wir | **mögen** | Wir **mögen** unseren Lehrer. — We like our teacher. |
| ihr | **mögt** | **Mögt** ihr Fußball? — Do you (all) like soccer? |
| sie/Sie | **mögen** | Sie **mögen** die Stadt. — They like the city. |

> **Note the pattern**: The stem vowel changes from **ö** (infinitive: m**ö**gen) to **a** (ich m**a**g) in the singular forms.

#### Typical Uses of mögen

**Liking something (usually without infinitive):**
- Ich **mag** Pizza. — I like pizza.
- Er **mag** sie sehr. — He likes her a lot.
- **Magst** du Hunde oder Katzen? — Do you like dogs or cats?
- Ich **mag** diesen Film nicht. — I do not like this film.
- Wir **mögen** unsere Nachbarn. — We like our neighbors.

**With an infinitive (less common, somewhat literary or expressing reluctance):**
- Ich **mag** nicht darüber sprechen. — I do not like/care to talk about it.
- Er **mag** das nicht hören. — He does not like to hear that.

**Expressing possibility (formal/literary):**
- Das **mag** stimmen. — That may be true. (conceding a point)
- Das **mag** sein. — That may be so.

### 8.2 möchten — Would Like (To)

**Möchten** is technically the Konjunktiv II (subjunctive II) form of mögen, but in practice it functions as an independent modal verb meaning "would like (to)." It is the **polite** way to express a wish or make a request.

#### Present Tense Conjugation

| Person | Conjugation | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| ich | **möchte** | Ich **möchte** einen Kaffee. — I would like a coffee. |
| du | **möchtest** | **Möchtest** du mitkommen? — Would you like to come along? |
| er/sie/es | **möchte** | Er **möchte** nach Hause gehen. — He would like to go home. |
| wir | **möchten** | Wir **möchten** bestellen. — We would like to order. |
| ihr | **möchtet** | **Möchtet** ihr etwas trinken? — Would you like something to drink? |
| sie/Sie | **möchten** | Was **möchten** Sie? — What would you like? (formal) |

> **Note**: Unlike most other modals, möchten does **not** change its stem vowel. All forms use **möcht-** plus personal endings.

#### Typical Uses of möchten

**Polite Wish / Request (without infinitive):**
- Ich **möchte** ein Stück Kuchen, bitte. — I would like a piece of cake, please.
- **Möchten** Sie etwas trinken? — Would you like something to drink?
- Wir **möchten** gern einen Tisch für zwei. — We would like a table for two.

**Polite Desire (with infinitive):**
- Ich **möchte** Deutsch lernen. — I would like to learn German.
- Sie **möchte** in Berlin wohnen. — She would like to live in Berlin.
- **Möchtest** du ins Kino gehen? — Would you like to go to the cinema?
- Wir **möchten** nächsten Sommer reisen. — We would like to travel next summer.

### 8.3 mögen vs. möchten at a Glance

| mögen | möchten |
|-------|---------|
| General, permanent liking | Specific, current wish |
| "I like..." | "I would like..." |
| Usually without infinitive | Often with infinitive |
| Permanent preference | In-the-moment desire |
| Ich **mag** Kaffee. (I like coffee in general.) | Ich **möchte** einen Kaffee. (I would like a coffee right now.) |
| Ich **mag** Hunde. (I like dogs — always.) | Ich **möchte** einen Hund kaufen. (I would like to buy a dog — now.) |

## 9. können vs. dürfen — Ability vs. Permission

This is one of the most important distinctions for learners. In English, "can" is used for both ability and permission. In German, these are two separate modal verbs.

| Situation | German | English |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Physical/mental ability | **können** | can / am able to |
| Permission from authority | **dürfen** | may / am allowed to |

### 9.1 Comparison Examples

| können (Ability) | dürfen (Permission) |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Ich **kann** schwimmen. | Ich **darf** hier schwimmen. |
| I **can** swim. (I have the skill.) | I **may** swim here. (It is permitted.) |
| Er **kann** Auto fahren. | Er **darf** Auto fahren. |
| He **can** drive. (He knows how.) | He **is allowed to** drive. (He has a license.) |
| Sie **kann** gut kochen. | Die Kinder **dürfen** in der Küche kochen. |
| She **can** cook well. (She has the ability.) | The children **are allowed to** cook in the kitchen. |

### 9.2 A Key Scenario

Consider this situation: A 14-year-old has learned to drive on private property.

- Er **kann** Auto fahren. — He can drive. (He has learned how — **ability**.)
- Er **darf** nicht Auto fahren. — He may not drive. (He is too young for a license — **no permission**.)

Both sentences can be true simultaneously! He has the **ability** (können) but not the **permission** (dürfen).

Another scenario at a swimming pool:

- Sie **kann** tauchen. — She can dive. (She has the **skill**.)
- Sie **darf** hier nicht tauchen. — She may not dive here. (It is **forbidden** in this pool.)

### 9.3 Common Mistake

- **Imprecise**: *Kann ich hier rauchen?* (Asking about the physical ability to smoke — always possible.)
- **Correct**: **Darf** ich hier rauchen? (Asking for **permission** to smoke.)

> **Tip**: When you want to say "Can I...?" in English, think about what you really mean. If you are asking for **permission**, use **dürfen**. If you are asking about **ability**, use **können**. In casual speech, Germans sometimes use können for permission too, but dürfen is more precise and polite.

## 10. müssen vs. sollen — Necessity vs. Obligation

Both müssen and sollen can sometimes translate to "should" or "must" in English, but they differ significantly in the **source and strength** of the obligation.

| müssen | sollen |
|--------|--------|
| Internal necessity or unavoidable requirement | External instruction, recommendation, or moral duty |
| Stronger obligation — no real choice | Weaker obligation — an expectation, not compulsion |
| "I have to / I must" | "I should / I am supposed to" |
| The speaker feels compelled by circumstances | Someone else expects it, or it is recommended |

### 10.1 Comparison Examples

| müssen (Strong / Unavoidable) | sollen (Weaker / External) |
|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Ich **muss** zur Arbeit gehen. | Ich **soll** zur Arbeit gehen. |
| I **must/have to** go to work. (no choice) | I **am supposed to** go to work. (boss expects it) |
| Du **musst** die Medizin nehmen. | Du **sollst** die Medizin nehmen. |
| You **must** take the medicine. (vital for health) | You **should** take the medicine. (the doctor advises it) |
| Wir **müssen** den Zug nehmen. | Wir **sollen** den Zug nehmen. |
| We **have to** take the train. (only option) | We **are supposed to** take the train. (someone suggested it) |

### 10.2 Key Scenarios

**At the doctor:**
- Der Arzt sagt, ich **soll** weniger Zucker essen. — The doctor says I **should** eat less sugar. (recommendation from an external authority)
- Ich **muss** weniger Zucker essen, sonst werde ich krank. — I **must** eat less sugar, or I will get sick. (personal conviction about necessity)

**At work:**
- Mein Chef sagt, ich **soll** den Bericht schreiben. — My boss says I **should** write the report. (instruction from outside)
- Ich **muss** den Bericht schreiben, weil morgen die Deadline ist. — I **must** write the report because the deadline is tomorrow. (unavoidable necessity)

**Moral situations:**
- Du **sollst** nicht lügen. — You should not lie. (moral commandment from outside — the Ten Commandments)
- Du **musst** ehrlich sein. — You must be honest. (personal conviction about necessity)

## 11. Negation with Modal Verbs

### 11.1 nicht with Modal Verbs

The word **nicht** is placed in the middle field, typically before the infinitive or before the specific element being negated:

- Ich kann **nicht** schwimmen. — I cannot swim.
- Du musst heute **nicht** arbeiten. — You do not have to work today.
- Er darf hier **nicht** rauchen. — He is not allowed to smoke here.
- Wir wollen **nicht** ins Kino gehen. — We do not want to go to the cinema.
- Sie soll **nicht** so spät kommen. — She should not come so late.
- Ich möchte **nicht** stören. — I would not like to disturb.

### 11.2 The Critical müssen/dürfen Negation Trap

This distinction is one of the most common sources of errors for English speakers. The negation of müssen and dürfen works **differently** than in English:

| German | English | Meaning |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Du **musst** es machen. | You **must** do it. | Obligation |
| Du **musst** es **nicht** machen. | You **don't have to** do it. | No obligation (but you can if you want) |
| Du **darfst** es **nicht** machen. | You **must not** do it. | Prohibition (it is forbidden) |

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  CRITICAL DIFFERENCE:                                               │
│                                                                     │
│  English "must not" = German "dürfen ... nicht"   (PROHIBITION)     │
│  English "don't have to" = German "müssen ... nicht" (OPTIONAL)     │
│                                                                     │
│  Examples:                                                          │
│  Du musst nicht kommen.   = You don't have to come. (your choice)   │
│  Du darfst nicht kommen.  = You must not come. (it is forbidden!)   │
│                                                                     │
│  This is the OPPOSITE of what English speakers expect!              │
│  "nicht müssen" is WEAKER than "nicht dürfen"                       │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 11.3 More Examples of the Distinction

- Du **musst** das **nicht** essen. — You **don't have to** eat that. (It is optional; eat it only if you want.)
- Du **darfst** das **nicht** essen. — You **must not** eat that. (It is forbidden; perhaps you are allergic.)

- Wir **müssen nicht** warten. — We **don't have to** wait. (We can leave.)
- Wir **dürfen nicht** warten. — We **must not** wait. (Waiting is forbidden; we must act now.)

- Ihr **müsst** morgen **nicht** kommen. — You **don't have to** come tomorrow. (It is voluntary.)
- Ihr **dürft** morgen **nicht** kommen. — You **must not** come tomorrow. (It is not allowed.)

### 11.4 kein with Modal Verbs

When negating a noun with an indefinite article (ein/eine), use **kein** instead of nicht:

- Ich kann **kein** Deutsch sprechen. — I cannot speak (any) German.
- Du musst **keinen** Kuchen kaufen. — You do not have to buy (any) cake.
- Er darf **kein** Bier trinken. — He is not allowed to drink (any) beer.
- Sie will **keine** Schokolade essen. — She does not want to eat (any) chocolate.
- Wir können **keine** Hilfe finden. — We cannot find (any) help.

## 12. Modal Verbs in the Past Tense (Präteritum)

While most German verbs use the Perfekt (haben/sein + past participle) in spoken German for the past tense, **modal verbs are an important exception**. Their **Präteritum** (simple past) forms are used even in everyday conversation because they are simpler to construct and sound more natural.

### 12.1 Präteritum Conjugation Table

| Person | können | müssen | dürfen | sollen | wollen | mögen |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|-------|
| ich | **konnte** | **musste** | **durfte** | **sollte** | **wollte** | **mochte** |
| du | **konntest** | **musstest** | **durftest** | **solltest** | **wolltest** | **mochtest** |
| er/sie/es | **konnte** | **musste** | **durfte** | **sollte** | **wollte** | **mochte** |
| wir | **konnten** | **mussten** | **durften** | **sollten** | **wollten** | **mochten** |
| ihr | **konntet** | **musstet** | **durftet** | **solltet** | **wolltet** | **mochtet** |
| sie/Sie | **konnten** | **mussten** | **durften** | **sollten** | **wollten** | **mochten** |

> **Pattern**: All modals lose their umlaut in the Präteritum. The endings follow the regular weak (mixed) verb pattern: -te, -test, -te, -ten, -tet, -ten. The ich and er/sie/es forms are again identical.

### 12.2 Stem Changes Overview

| Infinitive | Präsens (ich) | Präteritum (ich) | Change |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|--------|
| k**ö**nnen | k**a**nn | k**o**nnte | ö → a → o |
| m**ü**ssen | m**u**ss | m**u**sste | ü → u → u |
| d**ü**rfen | d**a**rf | d**u**rfte | ü → a → u |
| s**o**llen | s**o**ll | s**o**llte | o → o → o (no change) |
| w**o**llen | w**i**ll | w**o**llte | o → i → o |
| m**ö**gen | m**a**g | m**o**chte | ö → a → o (+ch) |

### 12.3 Past Tense Examples

- Ich **konnte** gestern nicht kommen. — I could not come yesterday.
- Er **musste** den ganzen Tag arbeiten. — He had to work all day.
- Wir **durften** als Kinder lange aufbleiben. — We were allowed to stay up late as children.
- Du **solltest** doch anrufen! — You were supposed to call!
- Sie **wollte** unbedingt nach Paris fahren. — She absolutely wanted to go to Paris.
- Ich **mochte** Spinat als Kind nicht. — I did not like spinach as a child.
- **Konntest** du die Aufgabe lösen? — Were you able to solve the problem?
- Wir **wollten** eigentlich ins Kino gehen. — We actually wanted to go to the cinema.
- Die Kinder **durften** nicht fernsehen. — The children were not allowed to watch TV.

### 12.4 Sentence Structure in the Past

The bracket structure remains the same in the past tense — the modal (now in Präteritum form) stays in V2 position, and the infinitive remains at the end:

```
Subject    [MODAL Prät.]    middle field          [INFINITIVE]

Ich        konnte           gestern nicht          kommen.
Wir        mussten          den ganzen Tag         arbeiten.
Sie        durfte           als Kind nicht         fernsehen.
Er         wollte           ein neues Buch         kaufen.
```

## 13. Modal Verbs in Subordinate Clauses

In subordinate clauses (introduced by conjunctions like *weil*, *dass*, *wenn*, *ob*, *obwohl*, etc.), the conjugated verb moves to the **end** of the clause. When a modal verb is involved, the word order in the subordinate clause changes:

### 13.1 The Rule

In a subordinate clause with a modal verb:

```
... conjunction + ... + INFINITIVE + MODAL (conjugated, at end)
```

The infinitive comes **before** the conjugated modal, and both are grouped at the end.

### 13.2 Examples

| Main Clause | Subordinate Clause |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Ich weiß, | **dass** er gut Deutsch **sprechen kann**. |
| (I know) | (that he can speak German well.) |
| Er bleibt zu Hause, | **weil** er **arbeiten muss**. |
| (He stays home) | (because he has to work.) |
| Ich frage, | **ob** ich hier **parken darf**. |
| (I ask) | (whether I may park here.) |
| Sie kommt nicht, | **weil** sie nicht **kommen will**. |
| (She is not coming) | (because she does not want to come.) |
| Ich bin froh, | **dass** ich Deutsch **lernen kann**. |
| (I am glad) | (that I can learn German.) |

### 13.3 Comparison: Main Clause vs. Subordinate Clause

| Type | Word Order |
|------|-----------|
| Main clause | Ich **kann** gut Deutsch **sprechen**. |
| Subordinate clause | ..., dass ich gut Deutsch **sprechen kann**. |
| Main clause | Er **muss** heute **arbeiten**. |
| Subordinate clause | ..., weil er heute **arbeiten muss**. |
| Main clause | Sie **darf** hier nicht **rauchen**. |
| Subordinate clause | ..., weil sie hier nicht **rauchen darf**. |

> **Key difference**: In a main clause, the modal is in V2 and the infinitive is at the end. In a subordinate clause, the infinitive comes **before** the conjugated modal, and both are placed at the very end of the clause.

│              MODAL VERBS CHEAT SHEET                             │
+──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────+
│                                                                  │
│  können  = ability/possibility     "can, am able to"             │
│  müssen  = necessity/compulsion    "must, have to"               │

│  - können = ability  vs.  dürfen = permission                    │

│                                                                  │

│  - nicht müssen = don't have to  (optional)                      │

│                                                                  │
│  PAST TENSE: Use Präteritum (not Perfekt):                       │
│  konnte, musste, durfte, sollte, wollte, mochte                  │
│                                                                  │

The following sentences have incorrect word order. Rewrite them with the correct bracket structure.

1. Ich schwimmen kann gut.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>
