## 1. The DACH Region Overview

**DACH** is an acronym for the three main German-speaking countries:

| Letter | Country | German Name | Capital | Population (approx.) |
|--------|---------|-------------|---------|---------------------|
| **D** | Germany | **D**eutschland | Berlin | 84 million |
| **A** | Austria | **Ö**sterreich | Wien (Vienna) | 9 million |
| **CH** | Switzerland | die **Sch**weiz | Bern | 9 million |

> **Note**: The "A" comes from *Austria* (the Latin/English name), and "CH" from *Confoederatio Helvetica*, the official Latin name for Switzerland.

### Additional German-Speaking Areas

German is also an official or co-official language in:

| Country | German Role | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|
| **Liechtenstein** | Sole official language | Tiny principality between Austria and Switzerland, ~40,000 people |
| **Luxembourg** | Co-official (with French, Luxembourgish) | Trilingual country, German used in media and education |
| **Belgium** | Co-official (in Ostbelgien) | Small German-speaking community (~77,000) in eastern Belgium |
| **South Tyrol (Italy)** | Co-official (with Italian) | Province in northern Italy with ~500,000 German speakers |

### Comparative Overview

| Feature | Germany | Austria | Switzerland |
|---------|---------|---------|-------------|
| Government | Federal republic | Federal republic | Federal republic (direct democracy) |
| States/Cantons | 16 Bundesländer | 9 Bundesländer | 26 Kantone |
| EU member | Yes | Yes | No |
| Currency | Euro (€) | Euro (€) | Swiss Franc (CHF) |
| Area | 357,000 km² | 84,000 km² | 41,000 km² |
| Languages | German (official) | German (official) | German, French, Italian, Romansh |

## 3. Key Moments in German History

### Timeline of Major Events

| Year | Event | German Term |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **1871** | German unification under Bismarck — German Empire proclaimed | **Reichsgründung** |
| **1914-1918** | World War I | **Erster Weltkrieg** |
| **1918** | End of monarchy, Kaiser Wilhelm II abdicates | **Abdankung des Kaisers** |
| **1919-1933** | Weimar Republic — Germany's first democracy | **Weimarer Republik** |
| **1933** | Hitler becomes Chancellor, rise of Nazi dictatorship | **Machtergreifung** |
| **1939-1945** | World War II | **Zweiter Weltkrieg** |
| **1945** | Unconditional surrender, end of WWII | **Bedingungslose Kapitulation** |
| **1945-1949** | Allied occupation, division into zones | **Besatzungszeit** |
| **1949** | Two German states created: BRD (West) and DDR (East) | **Teilung Deutschlands** |
| **1961** | Berlin Wall built | **Bau der Berliner Mauer** |
| **1989** | Fall of the Berlin Wall (November 9) | **Mauerfall** |
| **1990** | German reunification (October 3) | **Wiedervereinigung** |

### The Two Germanys (1949-1990)

| | BRD (West Germany) | DDR (East Germany) |
|---|---|---|
| Full name | Bundesrepublik Deutschland | Deutsche Demokratische Republik |
| Capital | Bonn | Ost-Berlin |
| System | Democratic, market economy | Socialist, planned economy |
| Alliance | NATO, Western Bloc | Warsaw Pact, Eastern Bloc |
| Economy | Wirtschaftswunder (economic miracle) | Centrally planned, state-owned |

### October 3: Tag der Deutschen Einheit

October 3, 1990 is Germany's national holiday: the **Tag der Deutschen Einheit** (Day of German Unity). This is the only national holiday established by federal law. All other public holidays are determined by the individual Bundesländer.

> The fall of the Berlin Wall on November 9, 1989 (*der Mauerfall*) is the more emotionally significant date, but October 3 was chosen as the national holiday because November 9 also marks the anniversary of *Kristallnacht* (1938), a night of anti-Jewish pogroms.

## 4. Cultural Traditions and Customs

### Pünktlichkeit (Punctuality)

Punctuality is taken very seriously in German-speaking countries. If you have an appointment at 10:00, you should arrive at 9:55 — arriving at 10:05 is considered late and rude.

> *Pünktlichkeit ist die Höflichkeit der Könige.* — "Punctuality is the politeness of kings."

- Business meetings start exactly on time
- Trains have a reputation for punctuality (the Deutsche Bahn is famous — and sometimes mocked — for this)
- Social events also start on time; arriving "fashionably late" is not appreciated

### Mülltrennung (Recycling and Waste Separation)

Germany has one of the most rigorous recycling systems in the world:

| Bin / Container | Color | What Goes In | German Term |
|----------------|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Restmüll | Gray/Black | Non-recyclable waste | Rest waste |
| Biomüll | Brown | Food scraps, organic waste | Bio waste |
| Papier | Blue | Paper, cardboard | Paper |
| Gelber Sack / Tonne | Yellow | Packaging, plastics, metal | Packaging waste |
| Glas | Green/White/Brown | Glass bottles/jars (by color) | Glass |
| Pfandflaschen | — | Bottles with deposit (return to store) | Deposit bottles |

The **Pfandsystem** (deposit system) is unique: you pay a deposit (usually 0.25 EUR) on most beverage bottles and cans, and get it back when you return them to any store with a *Pfandautomat* (deposit machine).

### Sonntagsruhe (Sunday Rest)

In Germany, Sunday (*Sonntag*) is a legally protected rest day:
- Almost all shops are **closed** on Sundays (grocery stores, clothing stores, etc.)
- Noisy activities (mowing the lawn, loud construction) are forbidden
- Some bakeries may open for a few hours in the morning
- Train stations and airports have shops that are exempt
- Many Germans use Sunday for family time, walks in nature, or *Kaffee und Kuchen* (coffee and cake)

### Other Cultural Norms

| Custom | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Handschlag** (handshake) | Standard greeting in formal and semi-formal contexts |
| **Schuhe ausziehen** | Take off your shoes when entering someone's home |
| **Abendbrot** | Light evening meal, often bread with cold cuts and cheese |
| **Feierabend** | End of the workday — Germans value clear work-life boundaries |
| **Bares ist Wahres** | "Cash is truth" — Germany still uses cash much more than other Western countries |

## 5. Festivals and Holidays

### Oktoberfest

The world's largest folk festival, held annually in **Munich** (München), Bavaria.

- **When**: Late September to early October (about 16 days)
- **Where**: Theresienwiese (locals call it "die Wiesn")
- **What**: Beer tents, traditional Bavarian food, carnival rides, brass music
- **Beer**: Only Munich breweries may serve at Oktoberfest (e.g., Paulaner, Augustiner, Hofbräu)
- **Clothing**: *Dirndl* (women's traditional dress), *Lederhosen* (men's leather trousers)
- **Toast**: *Prost!* (Cheers!)

> The first Oktoberfest took place on October 12, 1810, as a celebration of the marriage of Crown Prince Ludwig of Bavaria.

### Karneval / Fasching / Fastnacht

The German carnival season, celebrated before Lent:

| Name | Region |
|------|--------|
| **Karneval** | Rhineland (Köln, Düsseldorf, Mainz) |
| **Fasching** | Bavaria, Austria |
| **Fastnacht** | Southwest Germany (Schwaben, Black Forest) |

- **Rosenmontag** (Rose Monday): Main carnival parade day
- **Weiberfastnacht**: Women's carnival day (Thursday before Rosenmontag) — women cut men's ties
- Costumes, parades, street parties, and the motto *Alaaf!* (Cologne) or *Helau!* (Düsseldorf)

### Weihnachten (Christmas)

German Christmas traditions include:

| Tradition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Adventskalender** | Calendar with 24 doors, one opened each day from Dec 1-24 |
| **Adventskranz** | Wreath with 4 candles, one lit each Advent Sunday |
| **Weihnachtsmarkt** | Christmas market with Glühwein, Lebkuchen, crafts |
| **Heiligabend** (Dec 24) | Christmas Eve — main gift exchange, family dinner |
| **Nikolaustag** (Dec 6) | St. Nicholas Day — children put shoes out for small gifts |
| **Christkind / Weihnachtsmann** | Gift-bringer: Christkind (south/tradition) or Weihnachtsmann (north) |

### Silvester (New Year's Eve)

- **Bleigießen**: Traditionally melting lead and pouring it in water to read fortune shapes (now done with wax due to lead toxicity concerns)
- **Feuerwerk**: Fireworks at midnight, sold to the public for a few days before
- **Dinner for One**: A British sketch broadcast every New Year's Eve on German TV — a beloved tradition
- Toast: *Frohes Neues Jahr!* or *Prosit Neujahr!*

### Public Holidays (Feiertage)

| Date | Holiday | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Jan 1 | Neujahr | New Year's Day |
| Varies | Karfreitag | Good Friday |
| Varies | Ostermontag | Easter Monday |
| May 1 | Tag der Arbeit | Labor Day |
| Varies | Christi Himmelfahrt | Ascension Day (also "Vatertag" — Father's Day) |
| Varies | Pfingstmontag | Whit Monday |
| Oct 3 | Tag der Deutschen Einheit | German Unity Day |
| Dec 25-26 | Weihnachten | Christmas (two days!) |

> Many additional holidays vary by Bundesland. Bavaria has the most public holidays (up to 13), while northern states like Hamburg have the fewest (9).

## 6. German Food Culture

### Das Brot (Bread)

Germany is the world capital of bread, with over **3,000 registered bread varieties**:

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Vollkornbrot** | Whole grain bread, dense and dark |
| **Roggenbrot** | Rye bread |
| **Pumpernickel** | Very dark, dense Westphalian rye bread |
| **Brötchen** | Small bread rolls (called *Semmel* in Bavaria, *Schrippe* in Berlin) |
| **Brezel / Pretzel** | Soft pretzel with salt, a Bavarian staple |
| **Schwarzbrot** | Dark bread |

### Die Wurst (Sausage)

| Type | Region | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Bratwurst** | Throughout Germany | Grilled sausage, many regional varieties |
| **Currywurst** | Berlin | Sausage with curry ketchup and curry powder |
| **Weißwurst** | Bavaria | White sausage, eaten before noon with sweet mustard and Brezel |
| **Thüringer Rostbratwurst** | Thuringia | Grilled sausage seasoned with marjoram |
| **Frankfurter / Wiener** | Frankfurt / Vienna | Smoked, thin sausage (what Americans call a "hot dog") |

### Regional Dishes

| Region | Dish | Description |
|--------|------|-------------|
| Bavaria | **Schweinebraten** | Roast pork with Knödel (dumplings) |
| Swabia | **Maultaschen** | Swabian ravioli/dumplings |
| Swabia | **Spätzle** | Egg noodles, often with cheese (Käsespätzle) |
| Rhineland | **Sauerbraten** | Marinated pot roast |
| Hamburg | **Labskaus** | Corned beef, beetroot, and potato mash |
| Berlin | **Eisbein** | Pickled pork knuckle |
| Throughout | **Schnitzel** | Breaded and fried cutlet (pork or veal) |

### Das Bier (Beer)

Germany's beer culture is legendary, governed by the **Reinheitsgebot** (Beer Purity Law) of 1516, which originally allowed only water, barley, and hops (yeast was added later).

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Pils / Pilsner** | Most popular in Germany, light and hoppy |
| **Weizenbier / Weißbier** | Wheat beer, popular in Bavaria |
| **Kölsch** | Light beer from Cologne, served in small glasses |
| **Altbier** | Dark beer from Düsseldorf |
| **Helles** | Light lager, popular in Munich |
| **Bockbier** | Strong beer, often seasonal |

### Kaffee und Kuchen (Coffee and Cake)

The tradition of afternoon coffee and cake (usually around 3-4 PM on Sundays) is a cherished social custom:

- **Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte** — Black Forest cherry cake
- **Apfelstrudel** — Apple strudel (also Austrian)
- **Bienenstich** — "Bee sting cake" (almond cream cake)
- **Käsekuchen** — German cheesecake (often with quark)
- **Streuselkuchen** — Crumble cake

## 7. Austrian Culture

### Vienna (Wien): Imperial Legacy

Vienna was the capital of the Habsburg Empire for centuries, and its cultural legacy is enormous:

- **Wiener Kaffeehauskultur** (Viennese Coffee House Culture): UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. Coffee houses like *Café Central* and *Café Sacher* are institutions where people spend hours reading newspapers, discussing, or simply sitting. You order a *Melange* (similar to cappuccino), a *Großer Brauner* (large coffee with cream), or an *Einspänner* (coffee with whipped cream in a glass).

- **Sachertorte**: The famous Viennese chocolate cake, created at Hotel Sacher in 1832. There was even a legal dispute between Hotel Sacher and Demel bakery over who could call their cake the "original."

- **Wiener Schnitzel**: Breaded veal cutlet, the national dish. By definition, a *Wiener Schnitzel* must be made from veal (*Kalbfleisch*); if made from pork, it must be called *Schnitzel Wiener Art*.

### Austrian Identity

Austrians are proud of their distinct identity and generally do not like being called "German":

- Austria has its own vocabulary variants: *Erdapfel* (not *Kartoffel* for potato), *Topfen* (not *Quark* for curd cheese), *Jänner* (not *Januar* for January), *heuer* (not *dieses Jahr* for this year)
- The "Sound of Music" is an American film phenomenon — most Austrians have not seen it, and it plays little role in Austrian cultural identity
- Austria's musical heritage is enormous: Mozart, Haydn, Schubert, Johann Strauss, Mahler, Bruckner

### Austrian Traditions

| Tradition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Wiener Ball** | Formal ball season in winter, including the famous *Opernball* |
| **Heuriger** | Wine tavern serving new wine and cold dishes, unique to Vienna and Lower Austria |
| **Krampus** | Frightening companion of St. Nicholas (Dec 5), with masks and parades |
| **Lipizzaner** | Famous white horses of the Spanish Riding School in Vienna |

## 8. Swiss Culture

### Multilingualism (Mehrsprachigkeit)

Switzerland has **four official languages**, making it uniquely multilingual:

| Language | % of Population | Region |
|----------|----------------|--------|
| **German** (Schweizerdeutsch) | ~63% | Northern and central Switzerland |
| **French** | ~23% | Western Switzerland (Romandie) |
| **Italian** | ~8% | Ticino and parts of Graubünden |
| **Romansh** | ~0.5% | Parts of Graubünden |

The language border between German and French is called the **Röstigraben** (literally "Rösti ditch"), named after the Swiss potato dish *Rösti* that is more common in the German-speaking part.

### Neutrality (Neutralität)

Switzerland has maintained political and military neutrality since the Congress of Vienna in 1815. This means:
- Switzerland is not a member of NATO or the EU
- It is, however, a member of the UN (since 2002) and hosts many international organizations (Red Cross, WHO, WTO) in Geneva
- The Swiss military is defensive only, and all able-bodied men must serve

### Direct Democracy (Direkte Demokratie)

Switzerland's political system is unique:
- Citizens can call a **Volksinitiative** (popular initiative) to propose changes to the constitution (100,000 signatures required)
- Citizens can call a **Referendum** against any law passed by parliament (50,000 signatures)
- Multiple national votes (*Abstimmungen*) are held throughout the year on specific issues

### Swiss Icons

| Icon | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Schokolade** | Swiss chocolate is world-famous: Lindt, Toblerone, Sprüngli |
| **Uhren** | Swiss watches: Rolex, Omega, Swatch, Patek Philippe |
| **Käse** | Swiss cheese: Emmentaler, Gruyère, Appenzeller, Raclette |
| **Fondue** | Melted cheese dish — the national dish of French-speaking Switzerland |
| **Rösti** | Fried grated potato dish, a staple of German-speaking Switzerland |
| **Alpen** | The Swiss Alps with the Matterhorn as the most iconic peak |

## 9. Regional Dialects

### Hochdeutsch vs. Dialekte

**Hochdeutsch** (Standard German) is the formal, written, and taught form of German. In everyday life, however, millions of German speakers use regional **Dialekte** (dialects) that can differ dramatically from standard German.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|             DIALECT MAP (simplified)                      |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|                                                          |
|  NORTH: Plattdeutsch (Low German)                        |
|    Hamburg, Bremen, Hannover                              |
|    "Moin!" (hello), "Tüdelkram" (nonsense)               |
|                                                          |
|  CENTRAL: Mitteldeutsch (Central German)                 |
|    Berlin, Köln, Frankfurt, Sachsen                      |
|    "Ick" (ich), "Jeld" (Geld) — Berlinerisch             |
|                                                          |
|  SOUTH: Oberdeutsch (Upper German)                       |
|    Bayern, Schwaben, Österreich, Schweiz                 |
|    "Grüß Gott!" (hello), "Semmel" (Brötchen)            |
|                                                          |
+----------------------------------------------------------+
```

### Major Dialect Groups

| Dialect | Region | Example (vs. Hochdeutsch) |
|---------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Plattdeutsch** (Low German) | Northern Germany | *Dat is moi Hus.* (Das ist mein Haus.) |
| **Berlinerisch** | Berlin | *Ick bin ein Berliner.* (Ich bin ein Berliner.) |
| **Kölsch** | Cologne | *Dat es Kölsch.* (Das ist Kölsch.) |
| **Sächsisch** | Saxony | *Nu gloobste's!* (Nun glaubst du es!) |
| **Bayerisch** (Bavarian) | Bavaria | *I mog di.* (Ich mag dich.) |
| **Schwäbisch** (Swabian) | Swabia | *Mir könnet alles. Außer Hochdeutsch.* (We can do everything. Except standard German.) |
| **Wienerisch** (Viennese) | Vienna | *Bist deppert?* (Bist du verrückt?) |
| **Schweizerdeutsch** (Swiss German) | Switzerland | *Grüezi!* (Guten Tag!) / *Merci vilmal!* (Vielen Dank!) |

### Key Differences: Schweizerdeutsch

Swiss German is so different from Hochdeutsch that Germans often struggle to understand it:

| Swiss German | Standard German | English |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| Grüezi | Guten Tag | Hello |
| Uf Widerluege | Auf Wiedersehen | Goodbye |
| Merci vilmal | Vielen Dank | Thank you very much |
| Velo | Fahrrad | Bicycle |
| Natel | Handy | Mobile phone |
| Znüni | Pausenbrot | Morning snack |
| Rüebli | Karotte | Carrot |

> In Switzerland, Schweizerdeutsch is spoken in daily life by everyone, regardless of social class. Hochdeutsch is used for writing, formal speeches, and news broadcasts.

## 11. German Media and Entertainment

### Television (Fernsehen)

**Public Broadcasting**:
- **ARD** (*Arbeitsgemeinschaft der öffentlich-rechtlichen Rundfunkanstalten Deutschlands*): Germany's largest public broadcaster, known as "Das Erste" (Channel One)
- **ZDF** (*Zweites Deutsches Fernsehen*): Second German Television
- **ORF** (*Österreichischer Rundfunk*): Austrian public broadcaster
- **SRF** (*Schweizer Radio und Fernsehen*): Swiss German-language broadcaster

Germans pay a **Rundfunkbeitrag** (broadcasting fee) of 18.36 EUR per month per household to fund public broadcasting.

**Popular Shows**:
- **Tatort**: Germany's most-watched crime series, running since 1970. Each city has its own detective team. Watching the Sunday evening Tatort is a national ritual.
- **Tagesschau**: Germany's main evening news program (ARD), watched by millions at 8 PM.
