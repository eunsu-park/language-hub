# German (Deutsch) — Overview

## About This Topic

This topic provides a comprehensive, progressive curriculum for learning German from scratch to an advanced level. The 25 lessons are organized into 5 stages that build upon each other, moving from foundational concepts to practical real-world application.

German is spoken by approximately 130 million people worldwide and is an official language in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, and Belgium. It is the most widely spoken native language in the European Union and a major language of science, philosophy, engineering, and international business.

## Prerequisites

- No prior German knowledge required (the course begins from zero)
- Basic understanding of grammar concepts in your native language is helpful

## Difficulty

- **Tier**: Beginner (starts from scratch, progresses to advanced)
- **Estimated scope**: A1 through B2/C1 CEFR levels

## Learning Path

```
Stage 1: Foundations (01-05)
  Build the basics: pronunciation, greetings, noun gender, essential verbs, present tense
  ↓
Stage 2: Elementary (06-10)
  Expand: cases (nominative/accusative), adjectives, numbers, modal verbs, daily vocabulary
  ↓
Stage 3: Intermediate Grammar (11-15)
  Master verb system: dative/genitive cases, past tenses, separable verbs, complete verb system
  ↓
Stage 4: Advanced (16-20)
  Refine: Konjunktiv II, adjective declension, passive voice, idioms, thematic vocabulary
  ↓
Stage 5: Practical Application (21-25)
  Apply: reading, writing, DACH culture, business German, exam preparation
```

## How to Use This Course

1. **Follow the order**: Lessons build on each other. Complete each stage before moving to the next.
2. **Do the exercises**: Every lesson includes practice exercises with answers. Work through them.
3. **Review regularly**: Language learning requires repetition. Review previous lessons periodically.
4. **Practice actively**: Don't just read — write out conjugations, say sentences aloud, use new vocabulary.
5. **Reference lessons**: Lessons 15 (verb system), 18 (declension), and 20 (vocabulary) serve as useful references to return to.

## Key Features of German

German has several distinctive features that differ from English and other languages:

- **Three grammatical genders**: masculine (der), feminine (die), neuter (das)
- **Four grammatical cases**: nominative, accusative, dative, genitive
- **Verb-second word order**: The conjugated verb is always in the second position in main clauses
- **Compound nouns**: German freely creates long compound words (e.g., *Handschuh* = hand + shoe = glove)
- **Noun capitalization**: All nouns are capitalized, not just proper nouns
- **Formal/informal address**: The du/Sie distinction is socially important
