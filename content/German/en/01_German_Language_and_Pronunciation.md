## 1. Introduction to German

### 1.1 German in the World

> **German: A Major European Language**
>
> - **Native speakers**: ~100 million
> - **Total speakers**: ~130 million (including L2)
> - **Official in**: 6 countries + EU official language
> Most spoken native language in the EU
>
> - **Language family**: Indo-European → Germanic → West Germanic
> - **Closest relatives**: Dutch, English, Luxembourgish, Yiddish

### 1.2 German-Speaking Countries (DACH + More)

```
Core DACH region:
  D — Deutschland (Germany)         ~84 million
  A — Österreich (Austria)          ~9 million
  CH — die Schweiz (Switzerland)    ~5.4 million (65% of population)

Also official in:
  Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, Belgium (German-speaking community)

Significant minority language in:
  Italy (South Tyrol), Denmark, Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Romania
```

### 1.3 Why Learn German Pronunciation?

German pronunciation is more regular than English, but has sounds that do not exist in English:

- **Mostly phonetic**: Spelling-to-sound correspondence is quite reliable
- **Umlauts (ä, ö, ü)**: Three extra vowel sounds not found in English
- **Eszett (ß)**: A unique letter representing a voiceless "s" after long vowels
- **The ch sound**: Two variants (ich-Laut and ach-Laut) that take practice
- **Consistent rules**: Once you learn the patterns, you can pronounce most new words correctly

## 3. Vowel System

### 3.1 Short vs Long Vowels

German distinguishes between short and long vowels. This distinction changes word meaning and is one of the most important features of German pronunciation.

> **German Vowel Chart (Simplified)**
>
> Front / Central / Back
>
> - **High**: i /iː/ ü /yː/ — u /uː/ (long)
> - **High**: ɪ /ɪ/ ʏ /ʏ/ — ʊ /ʊ/ (short)
>
> - **Mid**: e /eː/ ö /øː/ — o /oː/ (long)
> - **Mid**: ɛ /ɛ/ œ /œ/ ə /ə/ — ɔ /ɔ/ (short)
>
> - **Low**: a /aː/ (long)
> - **Low**: a /a/ (short)
>
> Key rule: Long vowels are tense; short vowels are lax.

### 3.2 How to Tell if a Vowel is Long or Short

```
Long vowel indicators:
  1. Vowel + h (silent h lengthens): Bahn /baːn/, Uhr /uːɐ/, sehen /zeːən/
  2. Double vowel: Haar /haːɐ/, Meer /meːɐ/, Boot /boːt/
  3. ie = long i: Liebe /liːbə/, spielen /ʃpiːlən/
  4. Vowel before single consonant (in open syllable): Name /naːmə/, Ton /toːn/

Short vowel indicators:
  1. Vowel before double consonant: kommen /kɔmən/, Bett /bɛt/
  2. Vowel before ck: Rücken /ʁʏkən/, Brücke /bʁʏkə/
  3. Vowel before multiple consonants: Kind /kɪnt/, Herbst /hɛʁpst/
```

### 3.3 The Five Basic Vowels (Long and Short)

| Vowel | Long IPA | Example | Short IPA | Example |
|-------|----------|---------|-----------|---------|
| a | /aː/ | V**a**ter (father) | /a/ | K**a**tze (cat) |
| e | /eː/ | S**ee** (lake) | /ɛ/ | B**e**tt (bed) |
| i | /iː/ | L**ie**be (love) | /ɪ/ | B**i**tte (please) |
| o | /oː/ | S**o**hn (son) | /ɔ/ | S**o**nne (sun) |
| u | /uː/ | H**u**t (hat) | /ʊ/ | H**u**nd (dog) |

> **The schwa /ə/**: Unstressed **e** in endings is pronounced as a schwa: *hab**e*** /haːbə/, *Lieb**e*** /liːbə/, *komm**e**n* /kɔmən/. This is similar to the "a" in English "sofa."

### 3.4 Umlauts (ä, ö, ü)

Umlauts are fronted versions of a, o, and u. They represent sounds that do not exist in English (except ä).

| Umlaut | Long IPA | Short IPA | How to Produce |
|--------|----------|-----------|----------------|
| ä | /ɛː/ | /ɛ/ | Like "e" in "bed" (long ä is more open) |
| ö | /øː/ | /œ/ | Say /eː/ but round your lips like /oː/ |
| ü | /yː/ | /ʏ/ | Say /iː/ but round your lips like /uː/ |

```
Umlaut examples:

ä (long): Mädchen /mɛːtçən/ (girl), Käse /kɛːzə/ (cheese)
ä (short): Hände /hɛndə/ (hands), Männer /mɛnɐ/ (men)

ö (long): schön /ʃøːn/ (beautiful), Vögel /føːɡl̩/ (birds)
ö (short): können /kœnən/ (can), Wörter /vœʁtɐ/ (words)

ü (long): über /yːbɐ/ (over), Tür /tyːɐ/ (door)
ü (short): fünf /fʏnf/ (five), Stück /ʃtʏk/ (piece)
```

> **Tip for ö**: Position your tongue as if saying "ay" (English) but round your lips as if whistling. Practice: *schön*, *böse* (evil), *hören* (to hear).

> **Tip for ü**: Position your tongue as if saying "ee" (English) but round your lips as if saying "oo." Practice: *grün* (green), *Blüte* (blossom), *müde* (tired).

### 3.5 Diphthongs

German has three main diphthongs:

| Diphthong | Spellings | IPA | Example |
|-----------|-----------|-----|---------|
| ai/ei | ei, ai, ey, ay | /aɪ/ | m**ei**n (my), M**ai** (May), B**ay**ern (Bavaria) |
| au | au | /aʊ/ | H**au**s (house), Br**au**n (brown), Tr**au**m (dream) |
| oi/eu | eu, äu | /ɔʏ/ | h**eu**te (today), H**äu**ser (houses), Tr**äu**me (dreams) |

```
Important: ei and ie are different!

  ei = /aɪ/ (like English "eye")
     Beispiel /baɪʃpiːl/ (example)
     Reis /ʁaɪs/ (rice)
     Wein /vaɪn/ (wine)

  ie = /iː/ (long "ee")
     Liebe /liːbə/ (love)
     Bier /biːɐ/ (beer)
     Spiel /ʃpiːl/ (game)

  Minimal pair: Bein /baɪn/ (leg) vs Biene /biːnə/ (bee)
```

## 4. Consonant System

### 4.1 The CH Sound — *ich-Laut* vs *ach-Laut*

The most distinctive German consonant. It has two variants depending on the preceding vowel:

> **ich-Laut /ç/ — after front vowels (i, e, ä, ö, ü)**
> and after consonants (l, n, r) and in -chen diminutive
>
> ich [ɪç] (I) / Milch [mɪlç] (milk)
> echt [ɛçt] (real) / Mädchen [mɛːtçən] (girl)
> möchte [mœçtə] (would like) / Bücher [byːçɐ] (books)
> Kirche [kɪʁçə] (church) / Chemie [çemiː] (chemistry)
>
> Pronunciation: like a whispered "y" — tongue near palate,
> air flows through a narrow gap. Similar to "h" in English
> "hue" or "human" but more friction.
>
> ---
>
> **ach-Laut /x/ — after back vowels (a, o, u) and diphthong au**
>
> ach [ax] (oh) / Buch [buːx] (book)
> doch [dɔx] (yet) / Bauch [baʊx] (belly)
> Koch [kɔx] (cook) / Tochter [tɔxtɐ] (daughter)
> Nacht [naxt] (night) / Sprache [ʃpʁaːxə] (language)
>
> Pronunciation: like Scottish "loch" — friction at the
> back of the mouth/soft palate. Never like English "k"!

### 4.2 SCH, SP, and ST

```
sch = /ʃ/ (always, like English "sh"):
  Schule [ʃuːlə] (school)      Fisch [fɪʃ] (fish)
  Mensch [mɛnʃ] (person)       Schön [ʃøːn] (beautiful)

sp (word-initial) = /ʃp/:
  Sport [ʃpɔʁt] (sport)        Spaß [ʃpaːs] (fun)
  sprechen [ʃpʁɛçən] (to speak)  Sprache [ʃpʁaːxə] (language)

st (word-initial) = /ʃt/:
  Stadt [ʃtat] (city)           Straße [ʃtʁaːsə] (street)
  Stein [ʃtaɪn] (stone)        Stimme [ʃtɪmə] (voice)

Note: sp and st in the middle or end of a word keep /sp/ and /st/:
  Wespe [vɛspə] (wasp)         Fenster [fɛnstɐ] (window)
  Angst [aŋst] (fear)          Fest [fɛst] (festival)
```

### 4.3 Z and TS

```
z = /ts/ (always, never like English "z"):
  Zeit [tsaɪt] (time)          Zug [tsuːk] (train)
  Zimmer [tsɪmɐ] (room)       Herz [hɛʁts] (heart)
  zusammen [tsuˈzamən] (together)  Platz [plats] (place)

tz = /ts/ (after short vowels):
  Katze [katsə] (cat)          Platz [plats] (place)
  Hitze [hɪtsə] (heat)         sitzen [zɪtsən] (to sit)
```

### 4.4 W, V, and F

```
w = /v/ (like English "v", NEVER like English "w"):
  Wasser [vasɐ] (water)        Wein [vaɪn] (wine)
  Wort [vɔʁt] (word)           Welt [vɛlt] (world)

v = /f/ (in German-origin words) or /v/ (in foreign words):
  Vater [faːtɐ] (father)       viel [fiːl] (much)     → /f/
  Volk [fɔlk] (people)         von [fɔn] (from)       → /f/
  Vase [vaːzə] (vase)          Verb [vɛʁp] (verb)     → /v/
  Visum [viːzʊm] (visa)        Vulkan [vʊlkaːn] (volcano) → /v/

f = /f/ (always):
  Freund [fʁɔʏnt] (friend)     fahren [faːʁən] (to drive)
```

### 4.5 S Sounds

```
s (before a vowel, word-initial or between vowels) = /z/ (voiced):
  Sonne [zɔnə] (sun)           sehen [zeːən] (to see)
  Nase [naːzə] (nose)          lesen [leːzən] (to read)

s (before a consonant or at end of word) = /s/ (voiceless):
  fast [fast] (almost)          Haus [haʊs] (house)
  ist [ɪst] (is)               Glas [ɡlaːs] (glass)

ss = /s/ (voiceless, after short vowel):
  Wasser [vasɐ] (water)        Klasse [klasə] (class)
  essen [ɛsən] (to eat)        wissen [vɪsən] (to know)

ß = /s/ (voiceless, after long vowel or diphthong):
  Straße [ʃtʁaːsə] (street)   Fuß [fuːs] (foot)
  groß [ɡʁoːs] (big)          heißen [haɪsən] (to be called)
```

### 4.6 The German R

German has two main pronunciations of R, depending on dialect and position:

> **Uvular R /ʁ/ — Standard (Hochdeutsch)**
> Produced at the back of the throat (uvula vibrates)
> Used at the beginning of syllables:
> rot [ʁoːt] (red) / Regen [ʁeːɡən] (rain)
> Brücke [bʁʏkə] (bridge) / grün [ɡʁyːn] (green)
>
> **Vocalized R /ɐ/ — Standard in certain positions**
> R becomes a vowel-like sound (similar to British "uh")
> After long vowels: Uhr [uːɐ] (clock), Tür [tyːɐ] (door)
> In unstressed -er: Vater [faːtɐ] (father),
> Kinder [kɪndɐ] (children)
> In prefix er-: erzählen [ɛɐˈtsɛːlən] (to tell)
>
> **Alveolar R /r/ — Regional (Bavaria, Austria, Switzerland)**
> Tongue-tip trill, similar to Spanish or Italian R
> rot [roːt], Brücke [brʏkə]
>
> Both pronunciations are correct and widely understood.

### 4.7 The J Sound

```
j = /j/ (like English "y" in "yes", NEVER like English "j"):
  ja [jaː] (yes)               Jahr [jaːɐ] (year)
  jung [jʊŋ] (young)           jetzt [jɛtst] (now)
  jemand [jeːmant] (someone)   Junge [jʊŋə] (boy)
```

### 4.8 PF — A Distinctly German Sound

```
pf = /pf/ (both sounds pronounced together):
  Pferd [pfeːɐt] (horse)       Pflanze [pflantsə] (plant)
  Apfel [apfl̩] (apple)         Pfanne [pfanə] (pan)
  Kopf [kɔpf] (head)           Dampf [dampf] (steam)

Tip: Start with lips closed for /p/, then release into /f/.
English speakers often drop the /p/ — resist this urge!
```

### 4.9 Final Consonant Devoicing (Auslautverhärtung)

A critical rule: Voiced consonants become voiceless at the end of a syllable or word.

```
b → /p/:  Urlaub [ʊɐlaʊp] (vacation), but: Urlaube [ʊɐlaʊbə]
d → /t/:  Hund [hʊnt] (dog), but: Hunde [hʊndə]
g → /k/:  Tag [taːk] (day), but: Tage [taːɡə]
v → /f/:  brav [bʁaːf] (well-behaved)
s → /s/:  Haus [haʊs] (house), but: Häuser [hɔʏzɐ]

This means: Tag sounds like "Tak", Rad sounds like "Rat",
            but plurals restore the voiced sound.
```

## 5. Syllable Structure

### 5.1 Basic Patterns

```
Common syllable patterns in German:
  CV    → Ta-ge (days), Na-me (name)           Most common
  CVC   → Tag (day), Buch (book)
  V     → A-bend (evening), U-hr (clock)
  VC    → ist (is), alt (old)
  CCV   → Frau (woman), drei (three)
  CCVC  → Stuhl (chair), Staat (state)
  CVCC  → Herbst (autumn), Angst (fear)
  CCCVC → Strom (electricity), Sprung (jump)

German allows complex consonant clusters:
  Word-initial: /ʃpr/ Sprache, /ʃtr/ Straße, /pfl/ Pflanze
  Word-final: /ŋks/ links, /mpf/ Dampf, /ʁpst/ Herbst
```

### 5.2 Syllable Division Rules

```
1. Single consonant between vowels → goes with the following vowel:
   Va-ter (father), le-sen (to read), ha-ben (to have)

2. Double consonants → split between syllables:
   kom-men (to come), Mut-ter (mother), bit-te (please)

3. Digraphs (ch, sch, ck) → stay together, go with the following vowel:
   Bü-cher (books), Fla-sche (bottle)
   Exception: ck splits as k-k: Brü-cke → Brük-ke

4. Multiple consonants → last consonant goes with next vowel:
   Kin-der (children), Fens-ter (window), Herbst-tag (autumn day)
   Exception: consonant clusters that can start a syllable stay together:
   Bru-der (brother — br can start a syllable)

5. Compound words → split at word boundaries:
   Haus-tür (front door), Fuß-ball (football), Bahn-hof (train station)
```

## 6. Stress Rules

### 6.1 Default Stress Patterns

> **Rule 1: Simple German words — stress the FIRST syllable**
>
> 'Arbeit (work) / 'Wasser (water)
> 'Liebe (love) / 'Kinder (children)
> 'Abend (evening) / 'Morgen (morning)
> 'Freiheit (freedom) / 'Zeitung (newspaper)
>
> **Rule 2: Separable verb prefixes — stress the PREFIX**
>
> 'anfangen (to begin) / 'aufstehen (to stand up)
> 'mitkommen (to come along) / 'einkaufen (to shop)
>
> **Rule 3: Inseparable prefixes — stress the STEM (not prefix)**
> Prefixes: be-, emp-, ent-, er-, ge-, miss-, ver-, zer-
>
> be'suchen (to visit) / ver'stehen (to understand)
> er'zählen (to tell) / ent'schuldigen (to excuse)
> ge'fallen (to please) / zer'stören (to destroy)
>
> **Rule 4: Foreign/loan words — often stress the LAST syllable**
>
> Stu'dent (student) / Uni'versität (university)
> Mu'sik (music) / Tele'fon (telephone)
> Compu'ter (computer) / Restau'rant (restaurant)
>
> **Rule 5: Words ending in -tion, -ieren, -ität — stress suffix**
>
> Informa'tion / stu'dieren (to study)
> Nati'on / Universi'tät

### 6.2 Stress in Compound Words

```
Compound words (very common in German) stress the FIRST component:

  'Hausaufgabe (homework)     = Haus + Aufgabe
  'Handschuh (glove)          = Hand + Schuh
  'Bahnhof (train station)    = Bahn + Hof
  'Geburtstag (birthday)      = Geburt + s + Tag
  'Krankenhaus (hospital)     = Kranken + Haus

Secondary stress falls on the second component:
  'Haupt,bahnhof (main station)
  'Bundes,kanzler (federal chancellor)
```

## 7. Intonation Patterns

### 7.1 Declarative Sentences

```
Pitch pattern: rises slightly, then falls at end

  Ich wohne in Berlin.  (I live in Berlin.)
  ───────╱──────────╲
         ↑           ↓

  Er spricht sehr gut Deutsch.  (He speaks very good German.)
  ───╱──────────────────────╲
```

### 7.2 Yes/No Questions (Verb-First)

```
Pitch pattern: rises at the end

  Sprechen Sie Deutsch?  (Do you speak German?)
  ────────────────────╱
                      ↑

  Kommst du morgen?  (Are you coming tomorrow?)
  ────────────────╱
```

### 7.3 W-Questions (Wh-Questions)

```
Pitch pattern: starts high on the question word, falls at end

  Wo wohnen Sie?  (Where do you live?)
  ╱────────╲
  ↑         ↓

  Wie heißen Sie?  (What is your name?)
  ╱──────────╲
```

### 7.4 Exclamations

```
Wide pitch range:

  Das ist fantastisch!  (That is fantastic!)
  ╱╲
  ↑ ↓  (sharp rise and fall)

  Wunderbar!  (Wonderful!)
  ╱──────╲
```

## 8. Common Pronunciation Mistakes for English Speakers

### 8.1 Sounds That Trip Up English Speakers

| Mistake | Correct | Explanation |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Pronouncing w as English /w/ | German w = /v/ | *Wasser* = [vasɐ], not "wasser" |
| English "j" for German j | German j = /j/ (like "y") | *ja* = [jaː], not "dja" |
| English "z" for German z | German z = /ts/ | *Zeit* = [tsaɪt], not "zeet" |
| Skipping the /p/ in pf | Both sounds: /pf/ | *Pferd* = [pfeːɐt], not "ferd" |
| Pronouncing ch as "k" or "sh" | Use /ç/ or /x/ | *ich* = [ɪç], not "ik" or "ish" |
| No distinction long/short vowels | Length matters! | *Staat* /ʃtaːt/ (state) vs *Stadt* /ʃtat/ (city) |
| Ignoring final devoicing | Voiced → voiceless at word end | *Tag* = [taːk], not [taːɡ] |
| Pronouncing umlauts as base vowels | ö ≠ o, ü ≠ u, ä ≠ a | *schön* ≠ "schon" |
| English "th" for German th | German th = /t/ | *Theater* = [teˈaːtɐ], not "thee-ater" |
| Not aspirating initial p/t/k | German aspirates more than English in some positions | But less than English in clusters |

### 8.2 Minimal Pairs to Practice

| Word 1 | Word 2 | Distinction |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| *Staat* /ʃtaːt/ (state) | *Stadt* /ʃtat/ (city) | Long vs short a |
| *Beet* /beːt/ (flower bed) | *Bett* /bɛt/ (bed) | Long vs short e |
| *Ofen* /oːfən/ (oven) | *offen* /ɔfən/ (open) | Long vs short o |
| *Hüte* /hyːtə/ (hats) | *Hütte* /hʏtə/ (hut) | Long vs short ü |
| *Bein* /baɪn/ (leg) | *Biene* /biːnə/ (bee) | ei /aɪ/ vs ie /iː/ |
| *schon* /ʃoːn/ (already) | *schön* /ʃøːn/ (beautiful) | o vs ö |
| *schwul* /ʃvuːl/ (humid) | *Schwüle* /ʃvyːlə/ (mugginess) | u vs ü |
| *Kirche* /kɪʁçə/ (church) | *Kirsche* /kɪʁʃə/ (cherry) | ch /ç/ vs sch /ʃ/ |

## Quick Reference

> **Pronunciation Quick Guide**
>
> Vowels: short vs long — length changes meaning
> ä = /ɛ/ or /ɛː/, ö = /œ/ or /øː/, ü = /ʏ/ or /yː/
> ei = /aɪ/ (like "eye"), ie = /iː/ (like "ee")
> eu/äu = /ɔʏ/ (like "oy"), au = /aʊ/ (like "ow")
>
> ch = /ç/ after front vowels (ich-Laut)
> ch = /x/ after back vowels (ach-Laut)
> sch = /ʃ/ (always "sh")
> sp/st (word-initial) = /ʃp/, /ʃt/
> z = /ts/ (always)
> w = /v/, j = /j/, v = /f/ (usually)
> pf = /pf/ (both sounds!)
> ß = /s/ (after long vowels); ss = /s/ (after short vowels)
> R = /ʁ/ or /ɐ/ (vocalized in endings/after long vowels)
>
> Final consonant devoicing: b→p, d→t, g→k at word end
> Stress: first syllable (native), stem (inseparable prefix),
> prefix (separable), last syllable (many loan words)
