- Gain an introductory understanding of two-way prepositions (Wechselpräpositionen)
- Decline personal pronouns in nominative and accusative
- Apply case rules in sentence construction and comprehension

## 1. What Are Grammatical Cases?

In English, the role a noun plays in a sentence is determined almost entirely by **word order**:

- **The dog** bites **the man**. (The dog is doing the biting.)
- **The man** bites **the dog**. (Now the man is doing the biting.)

In German, word order is more flexible because **grammatical cases** mark the role of each noun. Cases change the form of articles, adjectives, and pronouns to show who is doing what in a sentence.

German has **four cases**:

| Case | German Name | Fall | Function |
|------|-------------|------|----------|
| Nominative | Nominativ | 1. Fall | Subject |
| Accusative | Akkusativ | 4. Fall | Direct object |
| Dative | Dativ | 3. Fall | Indirect object |
| Genitive | Genitiv | 2. Fall | Possession |

In this lesson, we focus on the **nominative** and **accusative** cases. The dative case is covered in Lesson 11.

> **Why does this matter?** Because of cases, the following two German sentences mean the **same thing**:
>
> - **Der Hund** beißt **den Mann**. (The dog bites the man.)
> - **Den Mann** beißt **der Hund**. (The dog bites the man.)
>
> The articles *der* (nominative) and *den* (accusative) tell you who is the subject and who is the object, regardless of word order.

## 2. The Nominative Case (Nominativ — 1. Fall)

The nominative case is used for the **subject** of the sentence — the person or thing performing the action.

### 2.1 Nominative Articles

| Gender | Definite Article | Indefinite Article | English |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Masculine | **der** | **ein** | the / a |
| Feminine | **die** | **eine** | the / a |
| Neuter | **das** | **ein** | the / a |
| Plural | **die** | — (kein**e**) | the / (no) |

### 2.2 Usage: Subject of the Sentence

The nominative answers the question **Wer?** (Who?) or **Was?** (What?):

- **Der Mann** liest ein Buch. — **The man** reads a book.
- **Die Frau** trinkt Kaffee. — **The woman** drinks coffee.
- **Das Kind** spielt im Garten. — **The child** plays in the garden.
- **Ein Hund** läuft durch den Park. — **A dog** runs through the park.

### 2.3 Usage: After sein, werden, bleiben

After the verbs **sein** (to be), **werden** (to become), and **bleiben** (to remain), the noun that follows stays in the **nominative** case. This is because these verbs equate the subject with the predicate, rather than acting on an object.

- Er ist **ein guter Lehrer**. — He is a good teacher.
- Sie wird **eine Ärztin**. — She is becoming a doctor.
- Das bleibt **ein Problem**. — That remains a problem.
- Mein Bruder ist **der Chef**. — My brother is the boss.

> **Key insight**: With *sein*, *werden*, and *bleiben*, both sides of the verb are nominative. Think of it as "Subject = Subject."

## 3. The Accusative Case (Akkusativ — 4. Fall)

The accusative case is used for the **direct object** — the person or thing that directly receives the action of the verb.

### 3.1 Accusative Articles

| Gender | Definite Article | Indefinite Article | English |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|---------|
| Masculine | **den** | **einen** | the / a |
| Feminine | **die** | **eine** | the / a |
| Neuter | **das** | **ein** | the / a |
| Plural | **die** | — (kein**e**) | the / (no) |

### 3.2 The Key Rule: Only Masculine Changes

This is the most important thing to remember about the accusative case:

> **Only the masculine article changes in the accusative.**
> - der → **den**, ein → **einen**
> - Feminine, neuter, and plural articles stay the same.

| Gender | Nominative | Accusative | Change? |
|--------|-----------|------------|---------|
| Masculine | der / ein | **den** / **einen** | **YES** |
| Feminine | die / eine | die / eine | No |
| Neuter | das / ein | das / ein | No |
| Plural | die / keine | die / keine | No |

### 3.3 Usage: Direct Object

The accusative answers the question **Wen?** (Whom?) or **Was?** (What?):

- Ich sehe **den Mann**. — I see the man. (Wen sehe ich? — Den Mann.)
- Sie kauft **einen Computer**. — She buys a computer. (Was kauft sie? — Einen Computer.)
- Wir lesen **das Buch**. — We read the book. (Was lesen wir? — Das Buch.)
- Er trinkt **die Milch**. — He drinks the milk. (Was trinkt er? — Die Milch.)

### 3.4 Nominative vs. Accusative Side by Side

| Sentence | Nominative (Subject) | Accusative (Direct Object) |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Der Mann sieht den Hund. | **der** Mann | **den** Hund |
| Eine Frau kauft einen Tisch. | **eine** Frau | **einen** Tisch |
| Das Kind isst das Brot. | **das** Kind | **das** Brot |
| Die Lehrerin liest die Zeitung. | **die** Lehrerin | **die** Zeitung |

## 4. Articles in Nominative vs. Accusative

### 4.1 Complete Definite Article Table

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|---|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Nominative** | der | die | das | die |
| **Accusative** | **den** | die | das | die |

### 4.2 Complete Indefinite Article Table

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ein | eine | ein |
| **Accusative** | **einen** | eine | ein |

### 4.3 Negative Article (kein)

The negative article *kein* (not a / no) follows the same pattern as *ein*:

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter | Plural |
|---|-----------|----------|--------|--------|
| **Nominative** | kein | keine | kein | keine |
| **Accusative** | **keinen** | keine | kein | keine |

Examples:

- Ich habe **keinen** Bruder. — I have no brother.
- Sie hat **keine** Katze. — She has no cat.
- Wir haben **kein** Auto. — We have no car.
- Er hat **keine** Freunde. — He has no friends.

## 5. Accusative Prepositions

Certain prepositions **always** require the accusative case. Memorize them:

| Preposition | English | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **durch** | through | Wir gehen **durch den Park**. (We walk through the park.) |
| **für** | for | Das Geschenk ist **für meinen Vater**. (The gift is for my father.) |
| **gegen** | against | Er läuft **gegen die Wand**. (He runs against the wall.) |
| **ohne** | without | Sie geht **ohne ihren Mann**. (She goes without her husband.) |
| **um** | around / at (time) | Wir sitzen **um den Tisch**. (We sit around the table.) |
| **bis** | until / to | Ich arbeite **bis Freitag**. (I work until Friday.) |
| **entlang** | along | Er geht **die Straße entlang**. (He walks along the street.) |

> **Memory tip**: You can remember these with the mnemonic **"FUDGE BOW"** — **F**ür, **U**m, **D**urch, **G**egen, **E**ntlang, **B**is, **O**hne, **W**ider (wider is rare but means "against/contrary to").

> **Note on *entlang***: Unlike the other accusative prepositions, *entlang* comes **after** the noun: *die Straße entlang* (along the street).

> **Note on *bis***: *Bis* is often used without an article (bis Montag, bis morgen). When combined with another preposition, the second preposition determines the case: *bis zum Bahnhof* (until/to the train station — *zum* is dative).

## 6. Two-Way Prepositions (Wechselpräpositionen)

These nine prepositions can take **either** the accusative or the dative case, depending on the meaning:

| Preposition | English |
|-------------|---------|
| **in** | in, into |
| **an** | at, on (vertical surface) |
| **auf** | on (horizontal surface) |
| **über** | over, above |
| **unter** | under, below |
| **vor** | in front of, before |
| **hinter** | behind |
| **neben** | next to |
| **zwischen** | between |

### 6.1 The Direction vs. Location Rule

The case depends on whether the sentence describes **motion/direction** or **location/state**:

| Question | Case | Meaning |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Wohin?** (Where to?) | **Accusative** | Direction / movement toward |
| **Wo?** (Where?) | **Dative** | Location / staying in place |

### 6.2 Accusative Examples (Direction — Wohin?)

- Ich gehe **in den Park**. — I go into the park. (movement toward)
- Sie hängt das Bild **an die Wand**. — She hangs the picture on the wall. (placing it)
- Er legt das Buch **auf den Tisch**. — He puts the book on the table. (placing it)
- Die Katze springt **unter das Bett**. — The cat jumps under the bed. (moving to)

### 6.3 Dative Examples (Location — Wo?) — Preview for Lesson 11

- Ich bin **im Park** (= in dem Park). — I am in the park. (already there)
- Das Bild hängt **an der Wand**. — The picture hangs on the wall. (it is there)
- Das Buch liegt **auf dem Tisch**. — The book lies on the table. (it is there)
- Die Katze schläft **unter dem Bett**. — The cat sleeps under the bed. (already there)

> **Tip**: Think of it this way — if there is a **change of position**, use accusative. If the position is **static**, use dative. We will cover the dative forms fully in Lesson 11.

## 7. Personal Pronouns in Nominative and Accusative

Personal pronouns also change form between cases:

| Person | Nominative | Accusative | English (Nom. → Acc.) |
|--------|-----------|------------|----------------------|
| 1st sg. | **ich** | **mich** | I → me |
| 2nd sg. (informal) | **du** | **dich** | you → you |
| 3rd sg. masc. | **er** | **ihn** | he → him |
| 3rd sg. fem. | **sie** | **sie** | she → her |
| 3rd sg. neut. | **es** | **es** | it → it |
| 1st pl. | **wir** | **uns** | we → us |
| 2nd pl. (informal) | **ihr** | **euch** | you (all) → you (all) |
| 3rd pl. | **sie** | **sie** | they → them |
| Formal (sg./pl.) | **Sie** | **Sie** | you (formal) → you (formal) |

### 7.1 Examples with Accusative Pronouns

- Siehst du **mich**? — Do you see **me**?
- Ich liebe **dich**. — I love **you**.
- Wir kennen **ihn**. — We know **him**.
- Ich besuche **sie** morgen. — I visit **her** tomorrow.
- Kannst du **es** finden? — Can you find **it**?
- Er besucht **uns** am Wochenende. — He visits **us** on the weekend.
- Ich lade **euch** ein. — I invite **you all**.
- Ich kenne **Sie** nicht. — I do not know **you** (formal).

> **Note**: The forms for *sie* (she), *sie* (they), and *Sie* (you formal) are identical in the accusative. Context and capitalization distinguish them.

## 8. Common Accusative Verbs

Many common German verbs take a direct object in the accusative case:

| Verb | English | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| haben | to have | Ich habe **einen Hund**. |
| sehen | to see | Er sieht **den Film**. |
| kaufen | to buy | Sie kauft **eine Tasche**. |
| lesen | to read | Wir lesen **das Buch**. |
| essen | to eat | Ich esse **einen Apfel**. |
| trinken | to drink | Er trinkt **den Kaffee**. |
| finden | to find | Findest du **den Schlüssel**? |
| brauchen | to need | Ich brauche **einen Stift**. |
| kennen | to know (person/place) | Kennst du **die Stadt**? |
| suchen | to search for | Sie sucht **ihre Tasche**. |
| besuchen | to visit | Wir besuchen **unsere Großeltern**. |
| mögen | to like | Ich mag **diesen Film**. |

## 9. Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Nominative or Accusative?

Identify whether the underlined noun phrase is in the **nominative** or **accusative** case.

1. **Der Lehrer** erklärt die Grammatik.
2. Ich kaufe **einen Kuchen**.
3. **Das Mädchen** singt ein Lied.
4. Wir sehen **den Mond**.
5. **Die Katze** fängt **die Maus**.
6. Er ist **ein Student**.
7. Sie braucht **keinen Regenschirm**.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **Der Lehrer** — Nominative (subject)
2. **einen Kuchen** — Accusative (direct object of *kaufen*)
3. **Das Mädchen** — Nominative (subject)
4. **den Mond** — Accusative (direct object of *sehen*)
5. **Die Katze** — Nominative (subject); **die Maus** — Accusative (direct object of *fangen*)
6. **ein Student** — Nominative (after *sein*)
7. **keinen Regenschirm** — Accusative (direct object of *brauchen*)

</details>

### Exercise 2: Fill in the Correct Article

Complete each sentence with the correct form of the definite article (der/die/das/den).

1. ________ Frau liest ________ Buch.
2. Ich sehe ________ Mann.
3. ________ Kind trinkt ________ Milch.
4. ________ Hund frisst ________ Knochen. (der Knochen = the bone)
5. ________ Lehrerin fragt ________ Schüler. (der Schüler = the student)

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **Die** Frau liest **das** Buch. (Die = nom. fem.; das = acc. neuter)
2. Ich sehe **den** Mann. (den = acc. masculine)
3. **Das** Kind trinkt **die** Milch. (Das = nom. neuter; die = acc. feminine)
4. **Der** Hund frisst **den** Knochen. (Der = nom. masc.; den = acc. masculine)
5. **Die** Lehrerin fragt **den** Schüler. (Die = nom. fem.; den = acc. masculine)

</details>

### Exercise 3: Accusative Prepositions

Fill in the blank with the correct article form after the accusative preposition.

1. Wir gehen durch ________ Stadt. (die Stadt)
2. Das Geschenk ist für ________ Bruder. (mein Bruder)
3. Er läuft gegen ________ Tür. (die Tür)
4. Sie geht ohne ________ Regenschirm. (ihr Regenschirm)
5. Die Kinder laufen um ________ Haus. (das Haus)

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Wir gehen durch **die** Stadt. (feminine accusative = die)
2. Das Geschenk ist für **meinen** Bruder. (masculine accusative = meinen)
3. Er läuft gegen **die** Tür. (feminine accusative = die)
4. Sie geht ohne **ihren** Regenschirm. (masculine accusative = ihren)
5. Die Kinder laufen um **das** Haus. (neuter accusative = das)

</details>

### Exercise 4: Personal Pronouns

Replace the underlined noun with the correct accusative pronoun.

1. Ich sehe **den Mann**. → Ich sehe ________.
2. Er kauft **das Auto**. → Er kauft ________.
3. Wir besuchen **die Großmutter**. → Wir besuchen ________.
4. Kennst du **den Lehrer und die Lehrerin**? → Kennst du ________?
5. Liebst du **mich**? — Ja, ich liebe ________.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Ich sehe **ihn**. (den Mann → ihn)
2. Er kauft **es**. (das Auto → es)
3. Wir besuchen **sie**. (die Großmutter → sie)
4. Kennst du **sie**? (den Lehrer und die Lehrerin → sie, plural)
5. Ja, ich liebe **dich**. (mich → response uses dich)

</details>

### Exercise 5: Accusative or Dative? (Two-Way Prepositions)

Decide whether the two-way preposition requires **accusative** (direction/motion) or **dative** (location). Write "Akk." or "Dat." for each.

1. Ich gehe in ________ Schule. (I go into the school.)
2. Ich bin in ________ Schule. (I am in the school.)
3. Er stellt die Lampe auf ________ Tisch. (He puts the lamp on the table.)
4. Die Lampe steht auf ________ Tisch. (The lamp stands on the table.)
5. Die Katze springt unter ________ Bett. (The cat jumps under the bed.)

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **Akk.** — Ich gehe in **die** Schule. (direction: wohin?)
2. **Dat.** — Ich bin in **der** Schule. (location: wo?)
3. **Akk.** — Er stellt die Lampe auf **den** Tisch. (direction: wohin?)
4. **Dat.** — Die Lampe steht auf **dem** Tisch. (location: wo?)
5. **Akk.** — Die Katze springt unter **das** Bett. (direction: wohin?)

</details>

### Exercise 6: Translation Practice

Translate into German, paying attention to case.

1. I need a pen. (der Stift)
2. The woman sees the child.
3. He walks through the park.
4. Do you (informal) know him?
5. The gift is for my mother. (die Mutter)

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Ich brauche **einen Stift**.
2. **Die Frau** sieht **das Kind**.
3. Er geht **durch den Park**.
4. Kennst du **ihn**?
5. Das Geschenk ist **für meine Mutter**.

</details>
