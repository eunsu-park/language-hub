## 1. Simple Past Overview

### 1.1 When to Use Simple Past

> **Simple Past = completed actions in the past**
>
> A finished event at a specific (or implied) past time
> 1. Completed actions:
> "I ate lunch."              나는 점심을 먹었다.
> "She went to school."       그녀는 학교에 갔다.
> 2. Past states:
> "I was happy."              나는 행복했다.
> "They were tired."          그들은 피곤했다.
> 3. Past habits (used to):
> "I walked to school every day." 나는 매일 학교에 걸어갔다.
> 4. Sequences of past events:
> "I woke up, ate breakfast, and went to work."
> 나는 일어나서, 아침을 먹고, 출근했다.
> ※ Korean past tense: verb stem + 었/았/였
> 먹다 → 먹었다    가다 → 갔다    하다 → 했다
> English: add -ed (regular) or change form (irregular)

### 1.2 Key Difference from Korean

```
Korean past tense (-었-) is relatively simple:
  All persons use the same form:
  나는 먹었다 / 그는 먹었다 / 우리는 먹었다 (same 먹었다)

English past tense is also the same for all persons (except BE):
  I ate / you ate / he ate / she ate / we ate / they ate
  (same "ate" for everyone — easier than present tense!)

But: English has ~200 irregular past tense forms to memorize!
  go → went (NOT "goed")
  eat → ate (NOT "eated")
  buy → bought (NOT "buyed")
```

## 2. Regular Past Tense: -ed

### 2.1 Spelling Rules

```
Rule 1: Most verbs → add -ed
  walk → walked      play → played      work → worked
  start → started    open → opened      clean → cleaned

Rule 2: Verbs ending in -e → add -d only
  live → lived       like → liked       use → used
  dance → danced     arrive → arrived   close → closed

Rule 3: Verbs ending in consonant + y → change y to -ied
  study → studied    carry → carried    try → tried
  worry → worried    cry → cried        hurry → hurried
  ※ Vowel + y → just add -ed: play → played, enjoy → enjoyed

Rule 4: Short verbs (CVC pattern) → double final consonant + -ed
  stop → stopped     plan → planned     drop → dropped
  shop → shopped     chat → chatted     hug → hugged
  ※ Don't double: w, x, y (snow → snowed, fix → fixed)
  ※ Don't double if unstressed: visit → visited, open → opened
```

## 3. Three Pronunciations of -ed

### 3.1 The Three Sounds

> The -ed ending has THREE pronunciations:
> /t/ — after voiceless consonants: /p/, /k/, /f/, /s/, /ʃ/,
> /tʃ/
> walked /wɔːkt/     cooked /kʊkt/     laughed /læft/
> passed /pæst/      washed /wɒʃt/     watched /wɒtʃt/
> /d/ — after voiced consonants and vowels: /b/, /g/, /v/,
> /z/, /m/, /n/, /l/, vowels
> played /pleɪd/     lived /lɪvd/      opened /ˈoʊpənd/
> called /kɔːld/     rained /reɪnd/    studied /ˈstʌdid/
> /ɪd/ — after /t/ and /d/ only (adds a syllable!)
> wanted /ˈwɒntɪd/   needed /ˈniːdɪd/  started /ˈstɑːrtɪd/
> decided /dɪˈsaɪdɪd/ waited /ˈweɪtɪd/ visited /ˈvɪzɪtɪd/
> ※ Korean speakers often pronounce all -ed as /ɪd/:
> "walked" is ONE syllable /wɔːkt/, NOT "walk-ed" /wɔːkɪd/
> "played" is ONE syllable /pleɪd/, NOT "play-ed" /pleɪɪd/
> Only /ɪd/ after /t/ and /d/ adds a syllable!

### 3.2 Practice Sets

```
/t/ — voiceless final sound (ONE syllable ending):
  asked, cooked, danced, dressed, finished, fixed, helped, hoped,
  jumped, kicked, kissed, knocked, liked, looked, missed, picked,
  pushed, reached, stopped, talked, touched, typed, washed, worked

/d/ — voiced final sound (ONE syllable ending):
  allowed, answered, arrived, believed, belonged, called, changed,
  cleaned, closed, covered, cried, described, earned, enjoyed,
  followed, happened, imagined, learned, listened, loved, moved,
  offered, ordered, planned, played, prepared, pulled, rained,
  remembered, returned, saved, seemed, showed, smiled, stayed,
  studied, tried, turned, used

/ɪd/ — after /t/ or /d/ (ADDS a syllable):
  accepted, added, collected, connected, counted, created, decided,
  ended, expected, fainted, graduated, hated, invented, invited,
  landed, lasted, needed, painted, planted, pointed, printed,
  protected, reported, rested, resulted, started, suggested,
  translated, visited, waited, wanted
```

### 4.4 Memory Tips

> Patterns to help memorize irregular verbs:
> Pattern: -ought/-aught
> buy→bought  bring→brought  think→thought
> catch→caught  teach→taught  fight→fought
> Pattern: vowel → e → o (i/a → e → o)
> speak→spoke→spoken  break→broke→broken
> choose→chose→chosen  freeze→froze→frozen
> steal→stole→stolen  wake→woke→woken
> Pattern: -ew / -own
> blow→blew→blown  grow→grew→grown  know→knew→known
> throw→threw→thrown  fly→flew→flown  draw→drew→drawn
> Pattern: -ang / -ung
> sing→sang→sung  ring→rang→rung  drink→drank→drunk
> swim→swam→swum  begin→began→begun
> Pattern: -ept / -elt / -eft / -ent
> keep→kept  sleep→slept  feel→felt  leave→left
> send→sent  spend→spent  lend→lent  build→built

## 5. was/were — Past of BE

### 5.1 Conjugation

```
Present     Past          Example
I am        I was         I was tired yesterday.
you are     you were      You were late this morning.
he/she/it is he/she/it was  She was happy.
we are      we were       We were at home.
they are    they were     They were students.

Negative:
I was not (wasn't)        I wasn't hungry.
You were not (weren't)    You weren't there.
He was not (wasn't)       He wasn't ready.

Question:
Was I...?                 Was I wrong?
Were you...?              Were you at the party?
Was she...?               Was she your teacher?
```

### 5.2 was/were in Past Expressions

```
"There was / There were" (과거 존재):
  There was a book on the table.      탁자 위에 책이 있었다.
  There were many people at the party. 파티에 사람이 많았다.

"was born" (출생):
  I was born in Seoul.                나는 서울에서 태어났다.
  She was born in 1995.               그녀는 1995년에 태어났다.
  ※ "I was born" (NOT "I born" or "I am born")

"was/were + adjective" (과거 상태):
  It was cold yesterday.              어제 추웠다.
  They were angry.                    그들은 화가 났었다.
```

## 6. Past Tense Questions with Did

### 6.1 Yes/No Questions

```
Formula: Did + subject + base verb?

※ CRITICAL: After "did", use BASE FORM — not past tense!

  She ate lunch.      → Did she eat lunch?     (NOT "Did she ate?")
  He went home.       → Did he go home?        (NOT "Did he went?")
  They played soccer. → Did they play soccer?
  You saw the movie.  → Did you see the movie? (NOT "Did you saw?")

Short answers:
  Did you eat?    → Yes, I did. / No, I didn't.
  Did she come?   → Yes, she did. / No, she didn't.
```

### 6.2 Wh-Questions

```
Formula: Wh-word + did + subject + base verb?

  Where did you go?              어디 갔니?
  What did she eat?              뭘 먹었니?
  When did they arrive?          언제 도착했니?
  Why did he leave?              왜 떠났니?
  How did you come?              어떻게 왔니?
  Who did you meet?              누구를 만났니?

Subject questions (NO did):
  Who came to the party?         누가 파티에 왔니?
  What happened?                 무슨 일이 있었니?
  Which team won?                어느 팀이 이겼니?
```

## 7. Past Tense Negatives

### 7.1 Formation

```
Formula: Subject + did not (didn't) + base verb

  I didn't eat breakfast.         나는 아침을 안 먹었다.
  She didn't go to school.        그녀는 학교에 안 갔다.
  They didn't play soccer.        그들은 축구를 안 했다.
  He didn't know the answer.      그는 답을 몰랐다.

  ※ Same critical rule: base verb after didn't!
    "I didn't went." ✗ → "I didn't go." ✓
    "She didn't ate." ✗ → "She didn't eat." ✓
```

### 7.2 was/were Negatives (No "did")

```
BE verb uses its own negative — NO "did":

  I was not (wasn't) there.       나는 거기 없었다.
  She was not (wasn't) happy.     그녀는 행복하지 않았다.
  They were not (weren't) ready.  그들은 준비되지 않았다.

  ※ "Did she wasn't?" ✗ → "Wasn't she happy?" ✓
```

## 8. Past Time Expressions

### 8.1 Common Time Markers

```
yesterday                어제
last night / week / month / year    지난밤 / 지난주 / 지난달 / 작년
... ago (two days ago, a year ago)  ~전에 (이틀 전, 1년 전)
in 2020 / in January               2020년에 / 1월에
on Monday / on March 5th            월요일에 / 3월 5일에
this morning (if past)              오늘 아침
when I was young                    내가 어렸을 때
the other day                       며칠 전에
once upon a time                    옛날 옛적에
```

### 8.2 Position in Sentence

```
Usually at the END:
  "I went to Seoul yesterday."
  "She graduated two years ago."

Also OK at the BEGINNING (for emphasis):
  "Yesterday, I went to Seoul."
  "Last year, she moved to Busan."

※ "ago" always goes AFTER the time period:
  "two days ago" ✓     "ago two days" ✗
  "a year ago" ✓       "ago a year" ✗
  Korean: 이틀 전 (이틀 + 전)  → English: two days + ago
```

## 9. Common Mistakes by Korean Speakers

### 9.1 Error Chart

> **Error                          Correct**
>
> "I goed to school."            "I went to school."
> (regularizing irregular verb)
> "Did she went home?"           "Did she go home?"
> (past tense after did)
> "She didn't came."             "She didn't come."
> (past tense after didn't)
> "I was go to school."          "I went to school."
> (using was + base verb for past)
> "I am born in Seoul."          "I was born in Seoul."
> (present tense for birth)
> "Yesterday I am happy."        "Yesterday I was happy."
> (present tense with past time)
> "I was walked home."           "I walked home."
> (incorrect passive form)
> "Before 3 years"               "3 years ago" / "3 years before
> (Korean 전 translation error)    that" (in narrative)

## Quick Reference

> **Past Tense Quick Guide**
>
> Regular: base + -ed (walked, played, studied, stopped)
> -ed pronunciation: /t/ after voiceless, /d/ after voiced,
> /ɪd/ after /t/ and /d/ only
> BE past: was (I/he/she/it) / were (you/we/they)
> Questions: Did + S + base verb?  (Did she eat? ✓)
> Negatives: S + didn't + base verb (She didn't eat ✓)
> ※ NEVER use past form after did/didn't!
> Did she went? ✗ → Did she go? ✓
> Top irregular groups:
> Same: cut-cut, put-put, hit-hit, cost-cost
> -ought: buy-bought, bring-brought, think-thought
> -ew/-own: know-knew-known, grow-grew-grown
> -ept/-elt: keep-kept, sleep-slept, feel-felt
> Time markers: yesterday, last week, 2 days ago, in 2020
