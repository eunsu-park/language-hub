## 1. Why Present Perfect is So Hard for Korean Speakers

> **THE HARDEST TENSE FOR KOREAN SPEAKERS**
>
> Korean has NO present perfect tense.
> Korean past tense (-었-) covers both:
> "I ate lunch." (simple past)
> "I have eaten lunch." (present perfect)
> Both = 나는 점심을 먹었다 in Korean!
> But in English, they are DIFFERENT:
> "I ate lunch."          (Simple Past)
> → Focus on PAST event, FINISHED, specific past time
> → 나는 점심을 먹었다 (과거 사건에 초점)
> "I have eaten lunch."   (Present Perfect)
> → Focus on PRESENT result of past action
> → 나는 점심을 먹은 상태이다 (현재 결과에 초점 — 배가 안 고프다)
> The KEY concept: Present perfect CONNECTS past to present.
> The action happened in the past, but the RESULT or
> RELEVANCE is in the PRESENT.

## 2. Present Perfect Formation

### 2.1 Structure

```
Present Perfect = have/has + PAST PARTICIPLE

I          have eaten     (I've eaten)
you        have eaten     (you've eaten)
he/she/it  has eaten      (he's/she's/it's eaten)
we         have eaten     (we've eaten)
they       have eaten     (they've eaten)

Negative:
I have not (haven't) eaten.
She has not (hasn't) eaten.

Question:
Have you eaten?
Has she eaten?
What have you done?

※ Past participle = 3rd column of irregular verb table
  Regular verbs: past participle = same as past tense (-ed)
    walk → walked → walked
    play → played → played
  Irregular verbs: often different
    eat → ate → eaten
    go → went → gone
    see → saw → seen
    do → did → done
    write → wrote → written
```

## 3. Present Perfect Uses

### 3.1 Life Experience (경험)

```
"Have you ever been to Japan?"      일본에 가 본 적 있어?
"I have visited 10 countries."      나는 10개국을 방문한 적이 있다.
"She has never eaten sushi."        그녀는 초밥을 먹어 본 적이 없다.
"I have seen that movie three times." 나는 그 영화를 세 번 봤다.

※ Focus: at any time in your life up to NOW
  The WHEN is not important — the EXPERIENCE matters.
  "I've been to Japan." ✓ (experience exists)
  "I went to Japan in 2019." ✓ (specific past time → simple past)
  "I've been to Japan in 2019." ✗ (specific time → use simple past!)
```

### 3.2 Recent Completion with Present Result (최근 완료 + 현재 결과)

```
"I've finished my homework."        숙제를 끝냈다. (→ 지금 자유다)
"She's lost her keys."              열쇠를 잃어버렸다. (→ 지금 없다)
"He has broken his leg."            다리가 부러졌다. (→ 지금 깁스 중)
"I've eaten too much."              너무 많이 먹었다. (→ 지금 배부르다)
"The taxi has arrived."             택시가 도착했다. (→ 지금 밖에 있다)

※ The KEY: the past action has a VISIBLE RESULT in the present.
  "I lost my keys." = past event, maybe I found them since
  "I've lost my keys." = I STILL don't have them (present result!)
```

### 3.3 Unfinished Time Period (아직 끝나지 않은 기간)

```
"I have eaten three meals TODAY."    오늘 세 끼를 먹었다. (오늘 아직 안 끝남)
"She has written two books THIS YEAR." 올해 책을 두 권 썼다. (올해 아직 안 끝남)
"We haven't had any problems SO FAR." 지금까지 문제없었다.

※ "today", "this week", "this month", "this year" = UNFINISHED
  Use present perfect because the time period is still ongoing.

Compare:
  "I ate three meals today." ✗ (implies today is over)
  "I've eaten three meals today." ✓ (today is still going)

  "She wrote two books last year." ✓ (last year = finished period)
  "She has written two books this year." ✓ (this year = unfinished)
```

### 3.4 Duration Up to Now: since/for (지금까지의 기간)

```
"I have lived in Seoul FOR 5 years."   나는 서울에 5년 동안 살았다. (지금도 산다)
"She has worked here SINCE 2020."      그녀는 2020년부터 여기서 일했다. (지금도 일한다)
"We have been friends SINCE childhood." 우리는 어린 시절부터 친구였다. (지금도 친구)
"He has known her FOR a long time."    그는 그녀를 오래 알았다. (지금도 안다)

※ Present perfect + since/for = started in the past, CONTINUES NOW
  "I lived in Seoul for 5 years." = I don't live there anymore (끝남)
  "I have lived in Seoul for 5 years." = I still live there (계속)
  This distinction does NOT exist in Korean!
```

## 4. Present Perfect vs Simple Past

### 4.1 The Critical Distinction

> **Simple Past                    Present Perfect**
>
> FINISHED past time             CONNECTION to present
> yesterday, last week, in 2020  today, this week, ever, never
> "I ate lunch."                 "I've eaten lunch."
> (when? maybe at noon)          (result: I'm full now)
> "She went to Paris."           "She's been to Paris."
> (specific trip in the past)    (experience: she knows Paris)
> "I lost my keys."              "I've lost my keys."
> (past event, maybe found them) (result: I don't have them NOW)
> "He lived in Seoul for 3 years." "He has lived in Seoul for
> (he doesn't live there now)      3 years." (he STILL lives
> there)
> SPECIFIC TIME → Simple Past    NO SPECIFIC TIME → Pres. Perf.
> yesterday, last week,          ever, never, already, yet,
> in 2020, when I was young      just, so far, recently
> ※ NEVER use present perfect with specific past times:
> "I have eaten lunch yesterday." ✗✗✗
> "I ate lunch yesterday." ✓

### 4.2 Time Marker Rules

```
SIMPLE PAST markers (specific finished time):
  yesterday, last night/week/month/year, ago, in 2020,
  on Monday (past), when I was young, at 3 PM

PRESENT PERFECT markers (unspecified or unfinished time):
  ever, never, already, yet, just, recently, lately,
  so far, today, this week/month/year, since, for,
  before (in life), how long

  "I have been to Japan." ✓ (no specific time — experience)
  "I went to Japan last year." ✓ (specific time — past)
  "I have been to Japan last year." ✗✗✗ (MIXING — never do this!)
```

### 4.3 Conversation Flow: Perfect → Past

```
A natural conversation often starts with present perfect (general)
and then switches to simple past (details):

A: "Have you ever been to Korea?"     (present perfect — experience)
B: "Yes, I have."
A: "When did you go?"                 (simple past — specific time)
B: "I went last summer."              (simple past — specific time)
A: "What did you do there?"           (simple past — specific past events)
B: "I visited Seoul and Busan."       (simple past)

※ First question: general experience → Present Perfect
  Follow-up details: when, where, what → Simple Past
```

## 5. since vs for

### 5.1 The Difference

> **since + POINT IN TIME (시점)     for + DURATION (기간)**
>
> since 2020                       for 4 years
> since Monday                     for 3 days
> since January                    for 6 months
> since 9 AM                       for 5 hours
> since I was a child              for a long time
> since she arrived                for ages
> since last summer                for 2 weeks
> since = starting point (부터)    for = duration length (동안)
> "I've lived here since 2020."    = 2020년부터
> "I've lived here for 4 years."   = 4년 동안
> → Same meaning, different expression!

### 5.2 Common Errors

```
"I live here since 2020." ✗ → "I have lived here since 2020." ✓
(since/for with duration → must use present perfect or continuous!)

"I have lived here since 4 years." ✗ → "...for 4 years." ✓
(4 years = duration → for, not since)

"I have lived here for 2020." ✗ → "...since 2020." ✓
(2020 = point in time → since, not for)
```

## 6. already, yet, just, still

### 6.1 already (이미/벌써)

```
Position: between have and past participle, or at end of sentence

"I've already eaten."                이미 먹었어.
"She has already finished."          그녀는 이미 끝냈어.
"He's already left."                 그는 벌써 떠났어.
"I've done it already."              이미 했어. (end position)

※ "already" = sooner than expected, emphasis on completion
  Used in POSITIVE sentences.
```

### 6.2 yet (아직)

```
Position: at the END of the sentence
Used in: NEGATIVE sentences and QUESTIONS

Negative:
"I haven't eaten yet."              아직 안 먹었어.
"She hasn't finished yet."          아직 안 끝냈어.
"He hasn't called yet."             아직 전화 안 했어.

Question:
"Have you eaten yet?"               벌써 먹었어? / 아직 안 먹었어?
"Has she finished yet?"             벌써 끝났어?

※ "yet" = up to this moment, but expected to happen
  "I didn't eat yet." ✗ (AmE informal OK, but grammatically: present perfect)
  "I haven't eaten yet." ✓
```

### 6.3 just (방금, 막)

```
Position: between have and past participle

"I've just eaten."                   방금 먹었어.
"She has just arrived."              그녀가 방금 도착했어.
"He's just left."                    그가 막 떠났어.
"We've just finished the meeting."   회의가 방금 끝났어.

※ "just" = a very short time ago (방금 전에)
  AmE also allows: "I just ate." (simple past + just)
  BrE strongly prefers: "I've just eaten." (present perfect + just)
```

### 6.4 still (아직도, 여전히)

```
Position: before the auxiliary (before haven't)

"I still haven't finished."         아직도 안 끝냈어.
"She still hasn't called."          아직도 전화 안 했어.
"He still hasn't found a job."      아직도 일자리를 못 찾았어.

※ "still" emphasizes that something expected HASN'T happened
  It shows surprise or frustration.
  "I haven't finished yet." (neutral)
  "I still haven't finished." (it's been too long! frustration)
```

## 7. ever and never

### 7.1 ever (경험 질문 — ~해 본 적 있니?)

```
Position: between have and past participle
Used in: QUESTIONS (and some special patterns)

"Have you ever been to Japan?"       일본에 가 본 적 있어?
"Has she ever eaten Korean food?"    한국 음식을 먹어 본 적 있어?
"Have you ever seen a whale?"        고래를 본 적 있어?
"Is this the best movie you've ever seen?"  지금까지 본 최고의 영화야?

※ "ever" = at any time in your life (살면서 한 번이라도)
  Only in questions and superlative patterns, NOT in regular positives.
  "I have ever been to Japan." ✗
  "I have been to Japan." ✓
```

### 7.2 never (절대 ~한 적 없다)

```
Position: between have and past participle

"I have never been to Africa."       아프리카에 가 본 적 없어.
"She has never eaten sushi."         초밥을 먹어 본 적 없어.
"He has never driven a car."         차를 운전해 본 적 없어.
"We've never met before."           전에 만난 적 없어요.

※ "never" is itself negative — don't add "not":
  "I haven't never been." ✗ (double negative)
  "I have never been." ✓
```

## 8. Past Perfect: had + past participle

### 8.1 Formation

```
Past Perfect = had + past participle (ALL subjects)

I had eaten           (I'd eaten)
she had eaten         (she'd eaten)
they had eaten        (they'd eaten)

Negative: had not (hadn't) + past participle
Question: Had + subject + past participle?
```

### 8.2 When to Use Past Perfect

> **Past Perfect = "the past of the past"**
>
> When you're talking about the past and want to mention
> something that happened EVEN EARLIER.
> Timeline:
> ──────────────X──────────────X──────────── NOW
> past perfect    simple past
> (earlier)       (later)
> "When I arrived, the movie had already started."
> (내가 도착했을 때, 영화는 이미 시작한 상태였다.)
> 1st: movie started (past perfect — earlier)
> 2nd: I arrived (simple past — later)
> "She told me she had been to Korea."
> (그녀는 한국에 가 본 적 있다고 나에게 말했다.)
> 1st: she went to Korea (past perfect — earlier)
> 2nd: she told me (simple past — later)

### 8.3 Past Perfect Examples

```
1. Sequence (순서):
   "I had eaten before she arrived."
   (그녀가 도착하기 전에 이미 먹었다.)

2. Experience up to a past point:
   "By 2020, I had visited 15 countries."
   (2020년까지 15개국을 방문한 상태였다.)

3. Cause of past situation:
   "She was tired because she had worked all day."
   (하루 종일 일했기 때문에 피곤했다.)

4. Reported speech:
   "He said he had finished."
   (그는 끝냈다고 말했다.)

5. Third conditional (if + past perfect → would have):
   "If I had known, I would have helped."
   (알았더라면 도와줬을 텐데.)
```

### 8.4 Past Perfect vs Simple Past

```
When the sequence is CLEAR (using before/after), past perfect
is optional:

  "She left before I arrived."           ✓ (clear sequence)
  "She had left before I arrived."       ✓ (emphasized sequence)

  "After he ate, he watched TV."         ✓ (clear sequence)
  "After he had eaten, he watched TV."   ✓ (emphasized)

When the sequence might be AMBIGUOUS, past perfect helps:

  "When I arrived, she left."
  → She left at the moment I arrived? Or before?

  "When I arrived, she had left."
  → Clear: she left BEFORE I arrived. (내가 도착했을 때 이미 떠난 상태)
```

## 9. Common Mistakes by Korean Speakers

### 9.1 Error Chart

> **Error                          Correct**
>
> "I have been to Japan          "I went to Japan
> last year."                    last year."
> (specific time → simple past)
> "I lost my keys."              "I've lost my keys."
> (when result matters NOW)       (I still don't have them)
> "I live here since 2020."      "I've lived here since 2020."
> (since/for → present perfect)
> "Did you ever eat sushi?"      "Have you ever eaten sushi?"
> (life experience → pres perf)
> "I have eaten lunch             "I ate lunch yesterday."
> yesterday."                    (specific time → simple past)
> ✗✗✗ MOST COMMON ERROR
> "I didn't eat yet."            "I haven't eaten yet."
> (yet → present perfect)         (standard/formal)
> "I have lived in Seoul          "I lived in Seoul
> for 3 years."                   for 3 years."
> (if you DON'T live there now)  (no longer living there)
> ※ If you STILL live there: "I have lived" ✓
> If you NO LONGER live there: "I lived" ✓

## Quick Reference

> **Present Perfect & Past Perfect Quick Guide**
>
> Present Perfect: have/has + past participle
> Uses: experience (ever/never), result (still relevant now),
> unfinished time (today/this year), duration (since/for)
> ※ NEVER with specific past time (yesterday, in 2020, ago)
> ※ Simple past = finished past event at specific time
> ※ Present perfect = connection to present
> since + point:  since 2020, since Monday, since 3 PM
> for + duration: for 5 years, for 3 days, for a long time
> already (이미) → positive: "I've already finished."
> yet (아직) → negative/question: "Haven't finished yet."
> just (방금) → recent: "I've just eaten."
> still (아직도) → frustration: "I still haven't finished."
> ever (한번이라도) → question: "Have you ever been?"
> never (한번도 없다) → negative: "I've never been."
> Past Perfect: had + past participle
> = the EARLIER of two past events
> "When I arrived, she had already left."
