## 1. Introduction to Connectors

### 1.1 What Are Connectors?

Connectors (also called linking words, transition words, or discourse markers) are words and phrases that show the **logical relationship** between ideas.

> Without connectors (choppy):
> "I studied hard. I failed the exam. I was disappointed."
> With connectors (smooth):
> "Although I studied hard, I failed the exam.
> Consequently, I was very disappointed."
> Connectors create COHESION — they make your writing and
> speaking flow logically and smoothly.

### 1.2 Types of Connectors

| Function | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Addition | and, also, moreover, furthermore |
| Contrast | but, however, although, nevertheless |
| Cause | because, since, due to, as |
| Result | so, therefore, consequently, as a result |
| Time | when, while, before, after, then |
| Condition | if, unless, provided that |
| Purpose | to, in order to, so that |
| Example | for example, such as, for instance |
| Summary | in conclusion, to sum up, overall |

### 1.3 Grammatical Categories

```
Conjunctions (join clauses):
  Coordinating: and, but, or, so, yet, for, nor (FANBOYS)
  Subordinating: because, although, when, if, while, since

Adverbs / Adverbial phrases (modify sentences):
  However, Therefore, Moreover, In addition, Nevertheless,
  Consequently, Furthermore, On the other hand

Prepositions (before nouns/gerunds):
  Despite, In spite of, Due to, Because of, Instead of
```

## 2. Addition Connectors

### 2.1 Adding Similar Ideas

> | Connector | Register | Example |
> |---|---|---|
> | and | neutral | "She sings and dances." |
> | also | neutral | "He also speaks French." |
> | too | informal | "I like it too." |
> | as well | informal | "She's coming as well." |
> | in addition | formal | "In addition, the study |
> | found that..." |  |  |
> | moreover | formal | "Moreover, the data |
> | suggests..." |  |  |
> | furthermore | formal | "Furthermore, we must |
> | consider..." |  |  |
> | besides | neutral | "Besides, it's too expensive." |
> | what's more | informal | "What's more, she's smart." |
> | on top of that | informal | "On top of that, he lied." |
> | not only...but also | neutral | "Not only is she smart, |
> | but she's also kind." |  |  |
>
> ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────────────────┤

### 2.2 Punctuation with Adverbial Connectors

```
IMPORTANT: "Moreover," "Furthermore," "In addition," etc.
are ADVERBS, not conjunctions. They cannot join two clauses
with just a comma.

  ✗ "She is smart, moreover she is hardworking."
    (Comma splice — WRONG)

  ✓ "She is smart. Moreover, she is hardworking."
    (Two sentences)

  ✓ "She is smart; moreover, she is hardworking."
    (Semicolon + adverb)

  ✓ "She is smart, and moreover, she is hardworking."
    (Conjunction + adverb)
```

## 3. Contrast and Concession

### 3.1 Showing Contrast

> | Connector | Register | Example |
> |---|---|---|
> | but | neutral | "It's old but reliable." |
> | yet | neutral | "She's young, yet wise." |
> | however | formal | "However, the results |
> | were inconclusive." |  |  |
> | nevertheless | formal | "Nevertheless, we must |
> | proceed." |  |  |
> | nonetheless | formal | "Nonetheless, the study |
> | has limitations." |  |  |
> | on the other hand | neutral | "On the other hand, |
> | some disagree." |  |  |
> | in contrast | formal | "In contrast, Group B |
> | showed no improvement." |  |  |
> | on the contrary | formal | "On the contrary, the |
> | results were positive." |  |  |
> | while / whereas | neutral | "While he prefers tea, |
> | she prefers coffee." |  |  |
> | instead | neutral | "Let's walk instead." |
> | still | neutral | "It was hard. Still, I |
> | managed." |  |  |
> | even so | neutral | "It rained. Even so, we |
> | went out." |  |  |
>
> ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────────────────┤

### 3.2 Concession (Despite / Although)

> CONCESSION: acknowledging a point but stating the opposite
> Conjunctions (+ clause):
> although / even though / though + subject + verb
> "Although it was raining, we went for a walk."
> "Even though she was tired, she kept working."
> "I enjoyed it, though it was long." (informal, end position)
> Prepositions (+ noun/gerund):
> despite / in spite of + noun/-ing
> "Despite the rain, we went for a walk."
> "In spite of being tired, she kept working."
> Common Error:
> ✗ "Despite she was tired..." (NOT followed by a clause)
> ✓ "Despite being tired..." (followed by noun/gerund)
> ✓ "Despite the fact that she was tired..." (workaround)
> ✓ "Although she was tired..." (use conjunction for clause)

## 4. Cause and Reason

### 4.1 Showing Cause

> | Connector | Register | Example |
> |---|---|---|
> | because | neutral | "I stayed home because it |
> | was raining." |  |  |
> | since | neutral/ | "Since you're here, let's |
> | formal | start." |  |
> | as | neutral/ | "As it was late, we left." |
> | formal |  |  |
> | because of | neutral | "Because of the traffic, |
> | I was late." |  |  |
> | due to | formal | "Due to the pandemic, |
> | classes moved online." |  |  |
> | owing to | formal | "Owing to the delay, we |
> | missed our flight." |  |  |
> | on account of | formal | "On account of his age, he |
> | retired." |  |  |
> | thanks to | neutral | "Thanks to her help, I |
> | passed." |  |  |
> | for (conjunction) | formal/ | "We must act quickly, for |
> | literary | time is running out." |  |
> | seeing that / | informal | "Seeing that everyone is |
> | seeing as | here, let's begin." |  |
>
> ├──────────────────────┼───────────┼──────────────────────────────┤

### 4.2 "Because" vs "Because of"

```
because + CLAUSE (subject + verb):
  "I was late because the traffic was heavy."
  "She cried because she was sad."

because of + NOUN / noun phrase / gerund:
  "I was late because of the heavy traffic."
  "She cried because of the sad movie."

Same distinction applies to:
  although / in spite of / despite
  while / during
```

## 6. Time Connectors

### 6.1 Sequencing Events

```
Before an event:     before, prior to, previously, beforehand,
                     until, up to that point

At the same time:    when, while, as, during, meanwhile,
                     at the same time, simultaneously

After an event:      after, afterwards, subsequently, then, next,
                     following that, since then

Beginning:           first, firstly, to begin with, initially,
                     at first, in the beginning

Middle:              then, next, after that, subsequently,
                     following this, meanwhile

End:                 finally, lastly, eventually, in the end,
                     at last, ultimately
```

### 6.2 Examples

```
Sequential:
  "First, preheat the oven. Then, mix the ingredients.
   Next, pour the batter into the pan. Finally, bake
   for 30 minutes."

Simultaneous:
  "While I was cooking, he was setting the table."
  "Meanwhile, the children were playing outside."

Before/After:
  "Before leaving, make sure to lock the door."
  "After the meeting, we went for lunch."
  "I hadn't seen her since 2018."
```

## 7. Condition and Purpose

### 7.1 Condition

```
if                    "If you need help, let me know."
unless                "Unless it rains, we'll go hiking."
provided (that)       "I'll go provided that you come too."
as long as            "You can stay as long as you're quiet."
in case               "Take a jacket in case it gets cold."
otherwise             "Study hard; otherwise, you'll fail."
```

### 7.2 Purpose

```
to + infinitive       "I came here to study English."
in order to           "In order to succeed, you must work hard."
so as to              "She spoke quietly so as not to wake the baby."
so that               "I saved money so that I could travel."
in order that         "In order that everyone may attend, we
                       changed the time." (very formal)
for + noun            "I went to the store for some milk."
for the purpose of    "For the purpose of this study, we
                       focused on..." (very formal)
```

## 8. Formal vs Informal Register

### 8.1 What Is Register?

> **Register = the level of formality in language**
>
> FORMAL:   Academic papers, business emails, official docs
> NEUTRAL:  News articles, general writing, workplace
> INFORMAL: Casual conversation, texting, social media
> Choosing the wrong register can make you sound:
> • Too stiff (formal language in casual chat)
> • Too casual (informal language in a business email)
> • Inappropriate (slang in an academic paper)

### 8.2 Formal vs Informal Vocabulary

| Informal | Neutral | Formal |
|----------|---------|--------|
| get | receive | obtain |
| buy | purchase | acquire |
| ask for | request | solicit |
| help | assist | facilitate |
| need | require | necessitate |
| start | begin | commence |
| end | finish | conclude/terminate |
| think about | consider | contemplate |
| go up | increase | escalate |
| go down | decrease | diminish |
| enough | sufficient | adequate |
| a lot of | many/much | numerous/substantial |
| about | approximately | circa |
| big | large | considerable/substantial |
| good | positive | favorable/beneficial |
| bad | negative | adverse/detrimental |
| show | demonstrate | illustrate/exemplify |
| use | employ | utilize |
| fix | repair | rectify |
| tell | inform | notify/apprise |

### 8.3 Formal vs Informal Connectors

| Informal | Formal |
|----------|--------|
| and | moreover, furthermore, in addition |
| but | however, nevertheless, nonetheless |
| so | therefore, consequently, thus |
| because | due to the fact that, owing to |
| also | additionally, similarly |
| anyway | regardless, in any case |
| plus | additionally |
| like | such as, for example |
| basically | fundamentally, essentially |
| in the end | ultimately, in conclusion |

### 8.4 Formal vs Informal Sentence Structures

```
Informal:
  "I can't come because I'm busy."
  "We need more time, so let's postpone."
  "There's a lot of stuff we haven't covered."

Formal:
  "I regret that I am unable to attend due to prior commitments."
  "Given the time constraints, it would be advisable to postpone."
  "Several topics remain to be addressed."
```

### 8.5 Contractions

```
Informal (contractions):     Formal (no contractions):
  don't, can't, won't         do not, cannot, will not
  I'm, she's, they're         I am, she is, they are
  it's, he'd, we've           it is, he would, we have
  gonna, wanna, gotta         going to, want to, have to
  (very informal)

Rule: Avoid contractions in formal writing (academic papers,
      business reports, official correspondence).
```

## 9. Hedging Language

### 9.1 What Is Hedging?

Hedging means using cautious, tentative language to avoid making absolute claims. It is essential in academic and professional communication.

> Without hedging (too strong):
> "Social media causes depression."
> With hedging (appropriately cautious):
> "Social media may contribute to depression."
> "Research suggests that social media could be linked to
> depression."
> "It appears that excessive social media use is associated
> with higher rates of depression."
> Korean academic writing tends to be more direct.
> English academic writing expects hedging!

### 9.2 Hedging Devices

```
Modal verbs:
  may, might, could, would, can
  "This may indicate a trend."
  "The results could be explained by..."

Adverbs:
  possibly, probably, perhaps, apparently, seemingly,
  arguably, presumably, generally, typically, often,
  sometimes, frequently, occasionally, partly
  "This is probably due to..."
  "The data perhaps suggests..."

Hedging verbs:
  seem, appear, tend, suggest, indicate, imply
  "The results seem to support the hypothesis."
  "The data suggests a correlation."
  "People tend to prefer familiar options."

Hedging phrases:
  it is possible that...     it appears that...
  it seems that...           it is likely that...
  it could be argued that... there is evidence to suggest...
  to some extent...          in most cases...
  on the whole...            to a certain degree...
```

## 10. Academic Discourse Markers

### 10.1 Structuring an Academic Text

```
INTRODUCING A TOPIC:
  "This paper examines..."
  "The aim of this study is to..."
  "This essay will explore..."
  "The purpose of this research is..."

STATING BACKGROUND:
  "It is widely acknowledged that..."
  "Previous research has shown that..."
  "It has been well documented that..."
  "According to Smith (2020),..."

PRESENTING AN ARGUMENT:
  "It can be argued that..."
  "One could contend that..."
  "The evidence suggests that..."
  "A compelling case can be made for..."

GIVING EXAMPLES:
  "For example, / For instance,"
  "...such as..."
  "This is illustrated by..."
  "A case in point is..."
  "To illustrate this point,..."

COMPARING AND CONTRASTING:
  "Similarly, / Likewise, / In the same way,"
  "In contrast, / Conversely, / On the other hand,"
  "Unlike X, Y..."
  "While X..., Y..."

SHOWING CAUSE AND EFFECT:
  "As a result, / Consequently, / Therefore,"
  "This leads to... / This results in..."
  "The consequence of this is..."
  "...thereby + gerund"

SUMMARIZING AND CONCLUDING:
  "In conclusion, / To conclude,"
  "In summary, / To summarize, / To sum up,"
  "Overall, / On the whole,"
  "Taking everything into consideration,"
  "The evidence presented suggests that..."
```

### 10.2 Citing and Referencing

```
Introducing sources:
  "According to Kim (2023),..."
  "As Lee (2022) points out,..."
  "Smith (2021) argues that..."
  "Research by Park (2020) demonstrates that..."
  "As noted by Johnson (2019),..."

Agreeing with a source:
  "As Smith rightly argues,..."
  "This is consistent with findings by..."
  "Supporting this view, Lee (2022) found..."

Disagreeing with a source:
  "However, this view has been challenged by..."
  "Contrary to Smith's assertion,..."
  "While Smith argues X, the evidence suggests Y."
```

## 11. Korean Speaker Notes

### 11.1 Common Issues

> 1. Overusing "and" and "but"
> Korean speakers tend to connect everything with 그리고/하지만
> English has many more varied connectors!
> 2. Formal register confusion
> Korean has clear formal levels (존댓말/반말)
> English formality is more about word choice than grammar
> 3. Missing hedging in academic writing
> Korean academic style can be more direct
> English academic writing requires hedging
> 4. Comma splices
> ✗ "I was tired, however I kept working."
> ✓ "I was tired; however, I kept working."
> ✓ "I was tired. However, I kept working."
> 5. Confusing despite/although
> ✗ "Despite I was tired, I kept working."
> ✓ "Despite being tired, I kept working."
> ✓ "Although I was tired, I kept working."

### 11.2 Korean Connector Equivalents

```
그리고    → and / moreover / furthermore / in addition
하지만    → but / however / nevertheless
그래서    → so / therefore / consequently / as a result
왜냐하면  → because / since / due to the fact that
그러나    → however / yet / nonetheless
그런데    → but / by the way / however
또한     → also / moreover / additionally / furthermore
따라서    → therefore / thus / hence / accordingly
예를 들어 → for example / for instance / such as
결론적으로 → in conclusion / to conclude / to sum up
반면에    → on the other hand / in contrast / whereas
```

### Exercise 3: Fix the Errors

1. Despite she was sick, she went to work.
2. I was tired, therefore I went to bed early.
3. Because of I forgot my wallet, I couldn't pay.
4. Although the rain, we enjoyed the trip.
5. He is smart, moreover he is hardworking.

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

```
1. Despite being sick, she went to work.
   OR: Although she was sick, she went to work.
   ("Despite" needs a noun/gerund, not a clause)

2. I was tired; therefore, I went to bed early.
   OR: I was tired. Therefore, I went to bed early.
   ("Therefore" is an adverb, cannot join clauses with just a comma)

3. Because I forgot my wallet, I couldn't pay.
   OR: Because of forgetting my wallet, I couldn't pay.
   ("Because of" + noun/gerund; "Because" + clause)

4. Despite the rain, we enjoyed the trip.
   OR: Although it rained, we enjoyed the trip.
   ("Although" + clause; "Despite" + noun)

5. He is smart; moreover, he is hardworking.
   OR: He is smart. Moreover, he is hardworking.
   ("Moreover" needs semicolon or new sentence, not comma splice)
```

</details>

## Quick Reference

> **Connectors & Register Quick Guide**
>
> ADDITION:    and, also, moreover, furthermore, in addition
> CONTRAST:    but, however, nevertheless, although, despite
> CAUSE:       because, since, due to, owing to
> RESULT:      so, therefore, consequently, as a result
> TIME:        when, while, before, after, then, finally
> GRAMMAR RULES:
> • Conjunctions join clauses: because, although, while
> • Adverbs modify sentences: however, therefore, moreover
> • Prepositions + noun/gerund: despite, because of, due to
> • No comma splices with adverbs!
> ✗ "I'm tired, however I'll stay."
> ✓ "I'm tired; however, I'll stay."
> REGISTER:
> Formal: moreover, nevertheless, consequently, furthermore
> Informal: also, but, so, and, plus, anyway
> HEDGING: may, might, could, seems, suggests, appears,
> tends to, it is possible that, to some extent
