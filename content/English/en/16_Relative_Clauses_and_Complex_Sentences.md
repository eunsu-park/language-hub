## 1. Introduction to Relative Clauses

### 1.1 What Is a Relative Clause?

A relative clause is a type of subordinate clause that modifies a noun. It gives extra information about the noun and starts with a relative pronoun (who, which, that, whose, where, when).

> Main clause:     "I met the woman."
> Relative clause: "who lives next door"
> Combined:        "I met the woman who lives next door."
> └──────────────────┘
> relative clause
> (modifies "woman")
> The noun modified by the relative clause is called
> the ANTECEDENT.
> Antecedent: "the woman"

### 1.2 Two Types of Relative Clauses

```
Defining (Restrictive):
  Identifies WHICH noun we mean — essential information
  No commas
  "The book that I bought is interesting."
  (Which book? The one I bought.)

Non-Defining (Non-Restrictive):
  Adds EXTRA information — not essential for identification
  Uses commas
  "My sister, who lives in London, is visiting us."
  (I have one sister — the clause adds extra info.)
```

## 2. Relative Pronouns

### 2.1 Overview

| Pronoun | Used For | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **who** | people (subject) | The man **who** called you is here. |
| **whom** | people (object, formal) | The person **whom** I met was kind. |
| **which** | things/animals | The car **which** I bought is red. |
| **that** | people and things | The book **that** I read was great. |
| **whose** | possession (people/things) | The girl **whose** bag was lost cried. |
| **where** | places | The city **where** I was born is small. |
| **when** | times | The day **when** we met was special. |
| **why** | reasons | The reason **why** I called is important. |

### 2.2 Who vs That vs Which

```
For PEOPLE:
  "who" or "that" in defining clauses
  "who" only in non-defining clauses (never "that")

  ✓ The student who passed the exam celebrated.     (defining)
  ✓ The student that passed the exam celebrated.     (defining)
  ✓ My brother, who is a doctor, lives in Seoul.     (non-defining)
  ✗ My brother, that is a doctor, lives in Seoul.    (WRONG)

For THINGS:
  "which" or "that" in defining clauses
  "which" only in non-defining clauses (never "that")

  ✓ The movie which I watched was boring.            (defining)
  ✓ The movie that I watched was boring.             (defining)
  ✓ This book, which I bought yesterday, is great.   (non-defining)
  ✗ This book, that I bought yesterday, is great.    (WRONG)
```

## 3. Defining Relative Clauses

### 3.1 Characteristics

> Defining Relative Clauses:
> • NO commas
> • Essential information — identifies the noun
> • Can use who, which, that, whose, where, when
> • "that" can replace "who" or "which"
> • Relative pronoun can be omitted when it's the OBJECT
> • Removing the clause changes the meaning
> "The man who stole my car was arrested."
> → Which man? The one who stole my car.
> → Without the clause: "The man was arrested."
> (Which man? We don't know!)

### 3.2 Subject vs Object Relative Clauses

```
Subject relative clause (pronoun = subject of relative clause):
  "The woman [who lives next door] is friendly."
   └─antecedent─┘ └──subject──┘
   The woman lives next door → she is friendly

Object relative clause (pronoun = object of relative clause):
  "The book [which I bought] is interesting."
   └antecedent┘       └object┘
   I bought the book → it is interesting
```

### 3.3 Examples

```
Subject:
  "The teacher who teaches math is strict."
  "The dog that bit me was brown."
  "Students who study hard get good grades."

Object:
  "The movie (that) we watched was funny."
  "The cake (which) she made was delicious."
  "The man (who/whom) I met was very tall."

Note: In object relative clauses, the pronoun is often omitted
      in everyday English (shown in parentheses above).
```

## 4. Non-Defining Relative Clauses

### 4.1 Characteristics

> Non-Defining Relative Clauses:
> • USE commas (before and after the clause)
> • Extra information — NOT essential for identification
> • Can use who, which, whose, where, when
> • CANNOT use "that"
> • Relative pronoun CANNOT be omitted
> • Removing the clause does NOT change core meaning
> "My mother, who is 60 years old, still works."
> → I have only one mother — "who is 60" adds info
> → Without: "My mother still works." (Still makes sense)

### 4.2 Common Uses

```
After proper nouns:
  "Seoul, which is the capital of South Korea, has 10 million people."
  "Shakespeare, who was born in 1564, wrote 37 plays."

After unique nouns (my mother, the sun, etc.):
  "My car, which I bought last year, broke down."
  "The moon, which orbits the Earth, has no atmosphere."

Adding comments:
  "He passed the exam, which surprised everyone."
  "She quit her job, which was a brave decision."
```

### 4.3 "Which" Referring to a Whole Clause

In non-defining clauses, **which** can refer to an entire previous statement:

```
"He arrived late, which annoyed the teacher."
 └────────────┘   └─which = the fact that he arrived late─┘

"She speaks five languages, which is very impressive."
 └───────────────────────┘  └─which = speaking five languages─┘

"They cancelled the meeting, which meant I could go home early."
```

## 5. Omitting Relative Pronouns

### 5.1 When Can You Omit?

> You can OMIT the relative pronoun when:
> 1. It is the OBJECT of the relative clause
> 2. It is in a DEFINING clause
> Object (CAN omit):
> "The book (that/which) I read was great."
> "The person (who/whom) she married is Korean."
> Subject (CANNOT omit):
> ✗ "The man ___ lives here is nice."
> ✓ "The man who lives here is nice."
> Non-defining (CANNOT omit):
> ✗ "My sister, ___ is a doctor, called me."
> ✓ "My sister, who is a doctor, called me."

### 5.2 How to Determine Subject vs Object

```
Test: Look at what comes AFTER the relative pronoun.

  If a VERB follows → it's a SUBJECT pronoun (cannot omit)
    "The woman who lives here..." (who + lives = subject)

  If a SUBJECT follows → it's an OBJECT pronoun (can omit)
    "The woman (who) I met..." (who + I = object)

More examples:
  SUBJECT (keep):  "The car that hit me was blue."
                                  └verb follows
  OBJECT (omit):   "The car (that) I hit was blue."
                                    └subject follows
```

## 6. Whose, Where, When, Why

### 6.1 Whose (Possession)

```
"whose" replaces his/her/its/their in relative clauses:

  "I know the man. His car was stolen."
  → "I know the man whose car was stolen."

  "The company whose products are the best won the award."
  "The student whose essay I read deserved an A."
  "This is the house whose roof was damaged by the storm."

Note: "whose" can refer to people OR things:
  People: "The woman whose son won the prize was proud."
  Things: "The building whose windows were broken looked abandoned."
```

### 6.2 Where (Place)

```
"where" = in which / at which

  "This is the city where I was born."
  = "This is the city in which I was born." (formal)

  "The restaurant where we ate was excellent."
  "I remember the park where we used to play."

  Non-defining:
  "Paris, where I spent my honeymoon, is beautiful."
```

### 6.3 When (Time)

```
"when" = in which / on which / at which

  "I'll never forget the day when I graduated."
  = "I'll never forget the day on which I graduated." (formal)

  "The year when she was born was 1995."
  "Do you remember the time when we got lost?"

  Non-defining:
  "In 2020, when the pandemic started, everything changed."
```

### 6.4 Why (Reason)

```
"why" is used after "the reason"

  "The reason why I called is important."
  "That's the reason why she left."

  Often shortened:
  "That's the reason she left." (omitting "why")
  "That's why she left." (omitting "the reason")
```

## 7. Prepositions in Relative Clauses

### 7.1 Informal vs Formal Style

```
Informal (preposition at the end — more natural in conversation):
  "The person who I was talking to is my boss."
  "The hotel which we stayed at was nice."
  "The topic that she was interested in was history."

Formal (preposition before the pronoun — academic writing):
  "The person to whom I was talking is my boss."
  "The hotel at which we stayed was nice."
  "The topic in which she was interested was history."

Rules for formal style:
  • Use "whom" for people (not "who")
  • Use "which" for things (not "that")
  • Never use "that" after a preposition
    ✗ "The hotel at that we stayed..."
    ✓ "The hotel at which we stayed..."
```

### 7.2 Common Verb + Preposition Combinations

```
talk to / about    "The person (who) I talked to..."
listen to          "The music (that) I listen to..."
look at            "The painting (that) she looked at..."
look for           "The keys (that) I was looking for..."
wait for           "The bus (that) we waited for..."
think about        "The problem (that) he was thinking about..."
work with          "The people (who) I work with..."
apply for          "The job (that) I applied for..."
depend on          "The friend (who) I depend on..."
belong to          "The club (that) I belong to..."
```

## 8. Reducing Relative Clauses

### 8.1 Using Present Participles (-ing)

Active meaning relative clauses can be reduced to -ing phrases:

```
Full:     "The man who is standing by the door is my father."
Reduced:  "The man standing by the door is my father."

Full:     "Students who study abroad gain valuable experience."
Reduced:  "Students studying abroad gain valuable experience."

Full:     "The woman who was talking on the phone didn't notice."
Reduced:  "The woman talking on the phone didn't notice."
```

### 8.2 Using Past Participles (-ed / irregular)

Passive meaning relative clauses can be reduced to past participle phrases:

```
Full:     "The book which was written by Tolkien is famous."
Reduced:  "The book written by Tolkien is famous."

Full:     "The houses that were built in the 1990s need repairs."
Reduced:  "The houses built in the 1990s need repairs."

Full:     "Food which is sold on the street is cheap."
Reduced:  "Food sold on the street is cheap."
```

### 8.3 Using To-Infinitive

```
After ordinal numbers (first, second, last) and superlatives:

Full:     "She was the first person who arrived."
Reduced:  "She was the first person to arrive."

Full:     "He was the last one who left the office."
Reduced:  "He was the last one to leave the office."

Full:     "She is the youngest person who has won this award."
Reduced:  "She is the youngest person to have won this award."
```

## 9. Noun Clauses

### 9.1 What Are Noun Clauses?

Noun clauses function as nouns in a sentence (subject, object, or complement).

```
As subject:
  "What she said surprised everyone."
  "Whether he will come is uncertain."
  "How they solved the problem remains a mystery."

As object:
  "I don't know where she lives."
  "She asked what time it was."
  "He believes that honesty is important."

As complement:
  "The question is whether we should go."
  "The truth is that nobody cares."
  "The problem is how we can afford it."
```

### 9.2 Noun Clause Connectors

```
that:       "I know that she is right."
if/whether: "I wonder if/whether it will rain."
what:       "I don't understand what you mean."
who:        "Tell me who did this."
where:      "She asked where the station was."
when:       "I remember when we first met."
how:        "I know how to solve this."
why:        "Explain why you were late."
```

### 9.3 Common Patterns

```
"I think (that)..."        "I believe (that)..."
"I know (that)..."         "I hope (that)..."
"I'm sure (that)..."       "I'm afraid (that)..."
"I wonder if/whether..."   "I don't know if/whether..."

Note: "that" is often omitted in informal speech:
  "I think she's right." = "I think that she's right."
```

## 10. Korean vs English Modification

### 10.1 The Fundamental Difference

> Korean: PRE-MODIFYING (modifier comes BEFORE the noun)
> English: POST-MODIFYING (relative clause comes AFTER noun)
> Korean:  [어제 내가 산] 책이 재미있다.
> [yesterday I bought] book is interesting
> └──modifier──┘ └noun┘
> English: The book [that I bought yesterday] is interesting.
> └noun┘  └───────modifier────────┘
> The modifier and noun positions are REVERSED!

### 10.2 Practical Examples

```
Korean Order → English Order:

  [서울에 사는] 친구        →  a friend who lives in Seoul
  [내가 좋아하는] 음악      →  the music (that) I like
  [빨간 지붕을 가진] 집     →  the house that has a red roof
                              the house with a red roof
  [작년에 지어진] 건물       →  the building (that was) built last year
  [영어를 가르치는] 선생님    →  the teacher who teaches English

  Notice: Korean puts ALL modifiers before the noun,
  even long ones. English uses relative clauses after the noun
  for longer modifications.
```

### 10.3 Common Errors from Korean Speakers

```
✗ "Yesterday I bought book is interesting."
  (Korean word order applied to English)
✓ "The book (that) I bought yesterday is interesting."

✗ "In Seoul living friend is coming."
✓ "My friend who lives in Seoul is coming."

✗ "Red roof has house is beautiful."
✓ "The house that has a red roof is beautiful."

✗ "I know where is the station."
  (Question word order in noun clause)
✓ "I know where the station is."
  (Statement word order in noun clause)
```

## Quick Reference

> **Relative Clauses Quick Guide**
>
> DEFINING (no commas, essential info):
> • who/that (people), which/that (things)
> • Object pronoun can be omitted
> • "that" allowed
> NON-DEFINING (commas, extra info):
> • who (people), which (things)
> • Pronoun CANNOT be omitted
> • "that" NOT allowed
> SPECIAL PRONOUNS:
> whose = possession    where = place
> when = time           why = reason (after "the reason")
> REDUCING:
> Active: -ing phrase    "The man standing..." (=who is)
> Passive: -ed phrase    "The book written..." (=which was)
> Ordinal/superlative: to-infinitive "the first to arrive"
> KOREAN CONTRAST: Korean pre-modifies, English post-modifies
> Korean: [modifier] + noun
> English: noun + [relative clause]
