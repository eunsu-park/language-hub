## 1. Why Articles Are So Hard for Korean Speakers

> **THE #1 HARDEST GRAMMAR POINT FOR KOREAN SPEAKERS**
>
> Korean has NO articles at all:
> "나는 사과를 먹는다" → I eat apple? an apple? the apple?
> In Korean, 사과 (apple) is always just 사과.
> In English, you MUST choose:
> a/an  → "I eat AN apple."    (any apple, first mention)
> the   → "I eat THE apple."   (specific apple, we know which)
> (none)→ "I eat apples."      (apples in general)
> Native English speakers make this choice AUTOMATICALLY
> hundreds of times per day. Korean speakers must LEARN it.
> ※ Even advanced Korean speakers make article errors.
> This lesson is the MOST IMPORTANT lesson for accuracy.

## 2. The Indefinite Articles: a/an

### 2.1 When to Use a/an

```
Use a/an when:

1. First mention — introducing something new:
   "I saw A dog in the park."     (listener doesn't know which dog yet)
   처음 언급할 때 사용

2. One of many — any member of a group:
   "She is A teacher."            (one of many teachers in the world)
   여러 개 중 하나를 말할 때

3. Classification — what kind/type:
   "This is A smartphone."        (classifying the object)
   종류를 말할 때

4. Per/each meaning:
   "I exercise three times A week."  (= per week, 주당)
   "$5 A kilogram"                   (= per kilogram, 킬로당)
```

### 2.2 a vs an

> **a  → before CONSONANT SOUNDS**
>
> an → before VOWEL SOUNDS
> ※ Based on SOUND, not spelling!
> a book        (b = consonant sound)
> a car         (c = consonant sound)
> a dog         (d = consonant sound)
> a university  (u = /juː/ starts with consonant /j/ sound!)
> a European    (E = /jʊ/ starts with consonant /j/ sound!)
> a one-way     (o = /wʌ/ starts with consonant /w/ sound!)
> an apple      (a = vowel sound)
> an egg        (e = vowel sound)
> an hour       (h is silent! /aʊər/ starts with vowel)
> an honest man (h is silent! /ˈɒnɪst/ starts with vowel)
> an MBA        (M = /em/ starts with vowel /e/ sound!)
> an X-ray      (X = /eks/ starts with vowel /e/ sound!)
> ※ Common Korean speaker mistake:
> "a umbrella" ✗ → "an umbrella" ✓
> "an university" ✗ → "a university" ✓

### 2.3 a/an with Jobs and Identity

```
English REQUIRES a/an before singular job titles:
  Korean: 나는 학생이다.        (no article needed)
  English: I am A student.     (article REQUIRED)

  She is A doctor.             ✓
  She is doctor.               ✗ (WRONG — must have "a")
  He is AN engineer.           ✓
  He is engineer.              ✗ (WRONG)

  ※ This is one of the most frequent Korean speaker errors.
    Always use a/an before singular countable nouns after BE verb.
```

## 3. The Definite Article: the

### 3.1 When to Use the

```
Use "the" when both speaker AND listener know WHICH one:

1. Second mention — already introduced:
   "I saw a dog. THE dog was big."
   (두 번째 언급 — 아까 말한 그 개)

2. Only one exists — unique items:
   "The sun is bright."        (태양은 하나뿐)
   "The moon is full tonight." (달도 하나뿐)
   "The President of Korea"    (대통령은 한 명)

3. Shared knowledge — both people know which one:
   "Can you close THE door?"   (this room's door — we both know)
   "Where is THE bathroom?"    (the nearest one)

4. Superlatives and ordinals:
   "She is THE best student."  (최고의 학생)
   "This is THE first lesson." (첫 번째 수업)

5. Specific with of/in/that clause:
   "The capital OF Korea"      (한국의 수도)
   "The book THAT you gave me" (네가 준 그 책)
```

### 3.2 Pronunciation of "the"

```
Before consonant sounds: "the" = /ðə/ (weak, like 더)
  the book /ðə bʊk/
  the car /ðə kɑːr/
  the dog /ðə dɒg/

Before vowel sounds: "the" = /ðiː/ (stronger, like 디)
  the apple /ðiː ˈæpəl/
  the egg /ðiː eg/
  the other /ðiː ˈʌðər/

For emphasis: "the" = /ðiː/ always
  "This is THE /ðiː/ restaurant." (이게 바로 그 식당이야!)
```

### 3.3 the with Geographic and Proper Names

```
USE "the":                          DON'T use "the":
  the United States                   Korea (NOT the Korea)
  the United Kingdom                  Japan
  the Philippines                     France
  the Netherlands                     Seoul
  the Pacific Ocean                   Mount Everest
  the Han River                       Lake Michigan
  the Sahara Desert                   Central Park
  the Alps (mountain ranges)          Broadway
  the New York Times (newspapers)     Harvard University

Rules of thumb:
  Plural country names → the (the Philippines, the Netherlands)
  "Republic/Kingdom/States" → the (the Republic of Korea)
  Oceans, seas, rivers → the
  Individual mountains, lakes → NO the
  Universities → usually NO the (Harvard, Seoul National)
```

## 4. Zero Article (No Article)

### 4.1 When to Use NO Article

```
1. Plural nouns in GENERAL:
   "Dogs are loyal."           (개는 충성스럽다 — dogs in general)
   "Books are expensive."      (책은 비싸다 — books in general)
   ※ NOT "The dogs are loyal" (unless specific dogs)

2. Uncountable nouns in GENERAL:
   "Water is essential."       (물은 필수적이다 — water in general)
   "Music makes me happy."     (음악은 나를 행복하게 한다)
   "Information is power."     (정보는 힘이다)

3. Meals:
   "I eat breakfast at 7."     (아침을 7시에 먹는다)
   "Let's have lunch."         (점심 먹자)
   ※ BUT: "The breakfast was delicious." (specific meal)

4. Languages:
   "I speak Korean."           (나는 한국어를 한다)
   "English is important."     (영어는 중요하다)

5. Sports:
   "I play soccer."            (나는 축구를 한다)
   "She likes tennis."         (그녀는 테니스를 좋아한다)

6. Academic subjects:
   "I study mathematics."      (나는 수학을 공부한다)
   "History is interesting."   (역사는 재미있다)

7. Days, months:
   "I work on Monday."         (나는 월요일에 일한다)
   "It's cold in January."     (1월에는 춥다)
```

## 5. Article Decision Tree

### 5.1 The Master Decision Tree

> **ARTICLE DECISION TREE**
>
> Is the noun COUNTABLE?
> ├── YES (countable)
> │   ├── SINGULAR?
> | ├── Known to listener? ──YES──→ THE book |
> |---|
> | └── Unknown/first mention? ──→ A/AN book |
>
> │   └── PLURAL?
> │       ├── Specific group? ──YES──→ THE books (on the table)
> │       └── General/all? ──→ ∅ books (Books are great.)
> └── NO (uncountable)
> ├── Specific? ──YES──→ THE water (in the glass)
> └── General? ──→ ∅ water (Water is essential.)
> NEVER: a/an + uncountable   (a water ✗, an information ✗)
> NEVER: a/an + plural        (a books ✗, an apples ✗)

### 5.2 Quick Examples

| Situation | Article | Example | Korean |
|-----------|---------|---------|--------|
| First mention, singular | a/an | "I bought **a** book." | 책을 한 권 샀다 |
| Known/specific | the | "I read **the** book." | 그 책을 읽었다 |
| General, plural | (none) | "I like **∅** books." | 나는 책을 좋아한다 |
| General, uncountable | (none) | "I drink **∅** water." | 나는 물을 마신다 |
| Specific, uncountable | the | "**The** water is cold." | 그 물이 차갑다 |
| Job title, singular | a/an | "She is **a** nurse." | 그녀는 간호사이다 |

## 6. Countable vs Uncountable Nouns

### 6.1 The Concept

> Countable nouns (셀 수 있는 명사):
> Can be counted: one book, two books, three books
> Have singular AND plural forms
> Can use a/an: a book, an apple
> Uncountable nouns (셀 수 없는 명사):
> Cannot be counted individually
> Have NO plural form
> Cannot use a/an
> ※ Korean does not have this distinction!
> 물 can be 물 한 잔, 물 두 잔 (using counters 잔)
> English: water (uncountable) — NO "two waters"
> Instead: "two glasses of water"

### 6.2 Common Uncountable Nouns

```
Liquids & Food:     water, milk, coffee, tea, bread, rice, sugar, meat
Materials:          wood, metal, glass, paper, gold, plastic
Abstract concepts:  information, advice, knowledge, news, happiness,
                    love, music, art, work, money, time, weather
Academic:           mathematics, physics, economics, research
Other:              furniture, luggage, equipment, traffic, homework

※ These are ALL singular in English:
  "The news IS good."        (NOT "are")    뉴스는 좋다
  "The information IS useful." (NOT "are")   정보는 유용하다
  "The furniture IS new."    (NOT "are")     가구는 새것이다
```

### 6.3 Counting Uncountable Nouns

```
Use a "counter" phrase (just like Korean counters 개, 잔, 장, etc.):

a glass of water           물 한 잔
a cup of coffee            커피 한 잔
a piece of information     정보 한 가지
a piece of advice          조언 한 가지
a slice of bread           빵 한 조각
a piece of furniture       가구 한 점
a piece of luggage         짐 한 개
a bar of chocolate         초콜릿 한 개
a sheet of paper           종이 한 장

Two glasses of water       물 두 잔
Three pieces of advice     조언 세 가지
```

### 6.4 Nouns That Are Both Countable and Uncountable

```
Some nouns change meaning based on countable/uncountable use:

Uncountable (material/general)    Countable (specific item)
───────────────────────────────   ──────────────────────────
glass (유리)                      a glass (유리잔)
paper (종이)                      a paper (논문/신문)
light (빛)                        a light (조명기구)
room (공간)                       a room (방)
time (시간)                       a time (횟수) "three times"
chicken (닭고기)                   a chicken (닭 한 마리)
coffee (커피 — 물질)               a coffee (커피 한 잔 — informal)
experience (경험 — 추상)           an experience (경험 — 사건)
hair (머리카락 — 전체)              a hair (머리카락 한 올)
```

## 7. Regular Plural Formation

### 7.1 Basic Rules

> **Plural Formation Rules**
>
> Rule 1: Most nouns → add -s
> book → books    cat → cats    dog → dogs    table → tables
> Rule 2: Ends in -s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x, -z → add -es
> bus → buses     class → classes    dish → dishes
> watch → watches  box → boxes       quiz → quizzes
> Rule 3: Ends in consonant + y → change y to -ies
> city → cities   baby → babies    story → stories
> ※ Vowel + y → just add -s: boy → boys, day → days
> Rule 4: Ends in -f/-fe → change to -ves (many exceptions)
> knife → knives   wife → wives    leaf → leaves
> life → lives     wolf → wolves   half → halves
> ※ Exceptions: roof → roofs, chief → chiefs, belief → beliefs
> Rule 5: Ends in -o → add -es (some) or -s (some)
> tomato → tomatoes  potato → potatoes  hero → heroes
> ※ Music/foreign: piano → pianos, photo → photos

### 7.2 Pronunciation of -s/-es Plural

```
The plural ending has THREE pronunciations:

/s/ — after voiceless consonants: /p/, /t/, /k/, /f/, /θ/
  books /bʊks/    cats /kæts/     cups /kʌps/
  roofs /ruːfs/   months /mʌnθs/

/z/ — after voiced consonants and vowels: /b/, /d/, /g/, /v/, /m/, /n/, /l/, etc.
  dogs /dɒgz/     beds /bedz/     rooms /ruːmz/
  days /deɪz/     trees /triːz/   boys /bɔɪz/

/ɪz/ — after sibilants: /s/, /z/, /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /tʃ/, /dʒ/
  buses /ˈbʌsɪz/     boxes /ˈbɒksɪz/     dishes /ˈdɪʃɪz/
  watches /ˈwɒtʃɪz/  judges /ˈdʒʌdʒɪz/   roses /ˈroʊzɪz/

※ Korean speakers often add /ɪ/ to all plurals: "books" ≠ "bookseuh"
  Listen carefully: "cats" is ONE syllable /kæts/, not two!
```

## 8. Irregular Plurals

### 8.1 Common Irregular Plurals

```
Vowel change (모음 변화):
  man → men           woman → women         foot → feet
  tooth → teeth       goose → geese         mouse → mice
  louse → lice

-en ending:
  child → children    ox → oxen

Same singular and plural (단복수 동형):
  sheep → sheep       fish → fish           deer → deer
  aircraft → aircraft series → series       species → species
  means → means

Foreign-origin plurals (외래어 복수):
  criterion → criteria     phenomenon → phenomena
  analysis → analyses      thesis → theses
  crisis → crises          datum → data
  cactus → cacti/cactuses  focus → foci/focuses
  fungus → fungi           alumnus → alumni
  formula → formulae/formulas

-is → -es:
  basis → bases        diagnosis → diagnoses
  oasis → oases        hypothesis → hypotheses
```

### 8.2 Always Plural Nouns (항상 복수형)

```
These nouns are ALWAYS plural (use "are", not "is"):

Paired items (쌍으로 된 물건):
  scissors (가위)      glasses (안경)       pants (바지)
  jeans (청바지)        shorts (반바지)      trousers (바지, BrE)
  binoculars (쌍안경)   headphones (헤드폰)

  "These scissors ARE sharp."  (NOT "is")
  "My pants ARE new."          (NOT "is")

  To count them: "a pair of scissors", "two pairs of pants"

Other always-plural:
  clothes (옷)         stairs (계단)        goods (상품)
  belongings (소지품)   savings (저축)       surroundings (주변환경)
```

## 9. Demonstratives: this/that/these/those

### 9.1 The Four Demonstratives

> **Demonstratives**
>
> Singular         Plural
> Near       this (이것)      these (이것들)
> Far        that (저것)      those (저것들)
> "This" / "These" = near the speaker (가까이)
> "That" / "Those" = far from the speaker (멀리)
> As pronouns:
> "This is my book."           이것은 내 책이다
> "That is your bag."          저것은 네 가방이다
> "These are my friends."      이 사람들은 내 친구들이다
> "Those are expensive."       저것들은 비싸다
> As adjectives (before noun):
> "This book is mine."         이 책은 내 것이다
> "That car is fast."          저 차는 빠르다
> "These shoes are new."       이 신발들은 새것이다
> "Those people are Korean."   저 사람들은 한국인이다

### 9.2 this/that in Time

```
this = current or upcoming:
  "this morning"    (오늘 아침)
  "this week"       (이번 주)
  "this year"       (올해)
  "this Friday"     (이번 금요일)

that = past or distant:
  "that day"        (그 날)
  "that time"       (그때)
  "in those days"   (그 시절에)
```

### 9.3 this/that in Phone Conversations

```
On the phone:
  "Hello, this is Minjun."      (identifying yourself)
  "Is that Sarah?"              (asking who the other person is)

  ※ "This" = me (the speaker)
    "That" = you (the listener)

Introducing in person:
  "This is my friend Sujin."    (person next to you)
  "That is Professor Lee."      (person far away)
```

### 9.4 Comparison with Korean

```
Korean demonstratives:
  이 (this) — near speaker
  그 (that) — near listener or previously mentioned
  저 (that over there) — far from both

English demonstratives:
  this/these — near speaker
  that/those — far from speaker (covers both Korean 그 and 저)

  ※ English has NO equivalent of Korean 그 (near listener).
    "That" covers both 그 and 저.

  Korean 그 책 (the book you mentioned) → English: "that book" or "the book"
  Korean 저 산 (that mountain far away) → English: "that mountain"
```

## Quick Reference

> **Articles & Plurals Quick Guide**
>
> a/an  = first mention, any one, classification
> a + consonant SOUND / an + vowel SOUND
> the   = known to listener, unique, specific, superlative
> ∅     = general plural, general uncountable, meals, sports
> Countable: can count (a book, two books)
> Uncountable: cannot count (water, information, furniture)
> ※ Uncountable nouns: NO a/an, NO plural -s
> Plurals: -s (books), -es (boxes), -ies (cities)
> Irregular: man→men, child→children, tooth→teeth, sheep→sheep
> Demonstratives:
> Near: this (singular) / these (plural)
> Far:  that (singular) / those (plural)
> ※ #1 Korean speaker error: forgetting articles entirely
> Always ask: "Do I need a/an, the, or nothing?"
