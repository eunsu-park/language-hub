## 1. What Are Modal Verbs?

### 1.1 Special Properties

> Modal verbs are SPECIAL auxiliary verbs with unique rules:
> 1. Followed by BASE FORM (no "to"):
> "She can swim." ✓     "She can to swim." ✗
> "He must go." ✓       "He must to go." ✗
> 2. No -s for third person:
> "She can swim." ✓     "She cans swim." ✗
> "He must go." ✓       "He musts go." ✗
> 3. No do-support for questions/negatives:
> "Can you swim?" ✓     "Do you can swim?" ✗
> "I can't swim." ✓     "I don't can swim." ✗
> 4. No infinitive or -ing form:
> "to can" ✗  "canning" ✗  "to must" ✗  "musting" ✗
> The 9 core modals:
> can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would
> Semi-modals (follow some rules):
> have to, need to, ought to, be able to, be going to

### 1.2 Korean Comparison

```
Korean expresses modality through verb endings and helper verbs:
  ~할 수 있다 (can)        ~해야 한다 (must)
  ~할지도 모른다 (might)    ~하면 좋겠다 (should)
  ~할 것이다 (will)         ~하겠다 (would/will)

English uses SEPARATE modal words before the main verb:
  can swim, must go, might rain, should study, will come

※ Korean modal expressions conjugate for tense and politeness.
  English modals do NOT change form — they stay the same!
  "He can swim." = "She can swim." = "They can swim."
```

## 2. can / could — Ability and Permission

### 2.1 can — Present Ability

```
Positive: subject + can + base verb
Negative: subject + cannot (can't) + base verb
Question: Can + subject + base verb?

Ability (능력 — ~할 수 있다):
  "I can swim."                나는 수영할 수 있다.
  "She can speak three languages." 그녀는 3개 국어를 할 수 있다.
  "Can you drive?"             운전할 수 있어?
  "I can't cook."              나는 요리를 못 한다.

Permission (허락 — ~해도 된다):
  "Can I use your phone?"     전화 써도 돼? (informal)
  "You can sit here."         여기 앉아도 돼.
  "Can I go now?"             이제 가도 돼?
  "You can't park here."      여기 주차하면 안 돼.

Possibility (가능성):
  "It can be cold in Seoul in winter."  서울은 겨울에 추울 수 있다.
  "Accidents can happen."      사고는 일어날 수 있다.
```

### 2.2 could — Past Ability, Polite Requests, Possibility

```
Past ability (과거 능력):
  "I could swim when I was five."    나는 5살 때 수영할 수 있었다.
  "She could speak French as a child." 그녀는 어렸을 때 프랑스어를 할 수 있었다.
  "He couldn't read until age 7."    그는 7살까지 글을 읽을 수 없었다.

  ※ For a specific past achievement, use "was able to" or "managed to":
    "I was able to finish the exam." ✓ (specific past achievement)
    "I could finish the exam." △ (sounds more like general ability)

Polite requests (정중한 요청):
  "Could you help me?"         도와주실 수 있으세요? (더 공손)
  "Could I borrow your pen?"  펜 빌려도 될까요?
  "Could you speak more slowly?"  좀 더 천천히 말해주실 수 있나요?

  ※ "could" = MORE polite than "can"
    "Can you help me?" (friendly/neutral)
    "Could you help me?" (polite)
    "Could you possibly help me?" (very polite)

Present possibility (현재 가능성):
  "It could rain later."      나중에 비가 올 수도 있다.
  "She could be at home."     그녀는 집에 있을 수도 있다.
  "This could be a problem."  이것은 문제가 될 수도 있다.
```

### 2.3 can vs be able to

```
can = present only (no infinitive or future form)
be able to = all tenses (more flexible)

Present:  "I can swim." = "I am able to swim."
Past:     "I could swim." ≈ "I was able to swim."
Future:   "I will be able to swim." (NOT "I will can swim" ✗)
Perfect:  "I have been able to swim." (NOT "I have could swim" ✗)
Infinitive: "I want to be able to swim." (NOT "I want to can swim" ✗)

  ※ After another modal or auxiliary, use "be able to":
    "You should be able to do this."    이것을 할 수 있어야 한다.
    "You might be able to come."        올 수 있을지도 모른다.
```

## 3. may / might — Permission and Possibility

### 3.1 may — Permission and Possibility

```
Permission (허락 — formal):
  "May I come in?"             들어가도 됩니까? (공손/격식)
  "May I ask a question?"      질문 해도 됩니까?
  "You may leave."             가도 됩니다.
  "You may not use your phone." 전화기를 사용하면 안 됩니다.

  ※ "may" for permission is MORE FORMAL than "can"
    Can I...? (neutral/informal)
    May I...? (formal/polite)
    Korean: ~해도 될까요? → "May I...?" (formal) / "Can I...?" (casual)

Possibility (가능성 — ~일 수도 있다):
  "It may rain tomorrow."     내일 비가 올 수도 있다.
  "She may be late."          그녀가 늦을 수도 있다.
  "They may not come."        그들은 안 올 수도 있다.

  ※ "may" = about 50% possibility (maybe)
```

### 3.2 might — Weaker Possibility

```
Possibility (더 약한 가능성 — ~일지도 모른다):
  "It might rain."             비가 올지도 모른다. (~30% possibility)
  "She might be sick."         그녀가 아플지도 모른다.
  "I might go to the party."   파티에 갈지도 모른다.
  "He might not come."         그가 안 올지도 모른다.

  ※ "might" = less likely than "may" (more uncertain)
    "It may rain." = maybe (50-50)
    "It might rain." = possibly but unlikely (~30%)
    In practice, many native speakers use them interchangeably.
```

### 3.3 Probability Scale

> **100%  It will rain.           (확실)**
>
> 95%  It must be raining.     (거의 확실한 추론)
> 75%  It should rain.         (예상/기대)
> 50%  It may rain.            (반반 가능성)
> 30%  It might/could rain.    (약한 가능성)
> 0%  It can't/couldn't rain. (불가능)
> It won't rain.          (확실히 아님)

## 4. must / have to — Obligation and Necessity

### 4.1 must — Strong Obligation, Logical Deduction

```
Obligation (의무 — 반드시 ~해야 한다):
  "You must wear a seatbelt."     안전벨트를 착용해야 한다.
  "I must finish this today."     오늘 이것을 끝내야 한다.
  "Students must submit homework by Friday."  금요일까지 제출해야 한다.

Logical deduction (논리적 추론 — ~임에 틀림없다):
  "She must be tired."            그녀는 피곤함에 틀림없다. (확신 95%)
  "He must be at home."           그는 집에 있는 게 틀림없다.
  "This must be the right place." 여기가 맞는 곳임에 틀림없다.

  ※ "must" for deduction = you're very confident based on evidence
    "She's been working for 12 hours. She must be tired."
    (= I'm almost certain she's tired)
```

### 4.2 have to — External Obligation

```
Present: have to / has to + base verb
Past:    had to + base verb
Future:  will have to + base verb

  "I have to go now."            지금 가야 해.
  "She has to work on Saturday." 그녀는 토요일에 일해야 한다.
  "We had to wait for two hours." 두 시간이나 기다려야 했다.
  "You'll have to study harder."  더 열심히 공부해야 할 거야.

  ※ "have to" uses do-support for questions and negatives:
    "Do you have to go?"          가야 해?
    "Does she have to work?"      그녀는 일해야 해?
    "I don't have to go."         나는 갈 필요 없어.
```

### 4.3 must vs have to

> **must = internal obligation (speaker's authority/feeling)**
>
> "I must study harder."  (내가 스스로 그래야 한다고 느낌)
> have to = external obligation (rules, circumstances)
> "I have to wear a uniform." (회사 규정 때문에)
> In practice, they are often interchangeable.
> BUT their NEGATIVES are very different:
> must not (mustn't) = PROHIBITION (금지 — 하면 안 된다!)
> "You mustn't steal."    훔치면 안 된다!
> "You mustn't be late."  늦으면 안 된다!
> don't have to = NO OBLIGATION (불필요 — 안 해도 된다)
> "You don't have to come." 안 와도 돼.
> "She doesn't have to work today." 오늘 일 안 해도 돼.
> ※ mustn't ≠ don't have to !!!
> mustn't = DO NOT DO THIS (forbidden!)
> don't have to = you don't need to (optional)
> This is a CRITICAL distinction!

## 5. should / ought to — Advice and Expectation

### 5.1 should — Advice and Recommendation

```
Advice (조언 — ~하는 게 좋겠다):
  "You should see a doctor."      의사를 만나는 게 좋겠어.
  "She should study more."        더 공부하는 게 좋겠어.
  "You shouldn't eat so much sugar." 설탕을 너무 많이 먹지 않는 게 좋겠어.
  "Should I bring an umbrella?"   우산을 가져가야 할까?

Expectation (기대/예상 — ~할 것이다):
  "The package should arrive tomorrow."  택배가 내일 도착할 거야.
  "She should be home by now."    지금쯤 집에 도착했을 거야.
  "It shouldn't take long."      오래 걸리지 않을 거야.

  ※ "should" is WEAKER than "must":
    "You must see a doctor." (you have no choice — 반드시!)
    "You should see a doctor." (I advise it — 하는 게 좋겠어)
```

### 5.2 ought to — Same as Should (More Formal)

```
"You ought to see a doctor."       의사를 만나는 게 좋겠어.
"She ought to study more."         더 공부하는 게 좋겠어.
"He ought to apologize."           사과하는 게 좋겠어.

  ※ "ought to" = same meaning as "should" but MORE FORMAL
    "should" is far more common in spoken English
    "ought to" is used in formal/written English
    Negative: "ought not to" (rarely used — "shouldn't" is preferred)
```

### 5.3 had better — Stronger Advice/Warning

```
"You'd better hurry."              서둘러야 해. (안 그러면 안 좋을 거야)
"She'd better not be late."        늦으면 안 돼. (경고)
"We'd better leave now."           지금 떠나는 게 낫겠다.

  ※ "had better" = stronger than "should" — implies a bad consequence
    "You should study." (advice)
    "You'd better study." (or else you'll fail! — warning)
    Despite "had", it refers to present/future, NOT past!
```

## 6. will / would — Future and Politeness

### 6.1 will — Future, Promises, Decisions

```
(See Lesson 11 for full "will" coverage)

Future prediction:    "It will rain tomorrow."
Spontaneous decision: "I'll have the pasta."
Promise:              "I'll always love you."
Offer:                "I'll help you."
Threat:               "I'll tell your mother!"
```

### 6.2 would — Politeness, Hypothetical, Past Habit

```
Polite requests (정중한 요청):
  "Would you like some coffee?"     커피 드시겠어요?
  "Would you mind closing the door?" 문 좀 닫아주시겠어요?
  "I would like a glass of water."  물 한 잔 주세요. (I'd like)

  ※ "would" = the most polite form for requests
    "I want coffee." (direct/informal)
    "I'd like coffee." (polite)
    "I would appreciate some coffee." (very polite/formal)

Hypothetical/Conditional (가정 — ~할 텐데):
  "I would go if I had time."       시간이 있으면 갈 텐데.
  "She would help if she could."    할 수 있다면 도와줄 텐데.
  "What would you do?"             너라면 어떻게 하겠어?

Past habit (과거 습관 — ~하곤 했다):
  "When I was young, I would play in the park every day."
  어렸을 때 매일 공원에서 놀곤 했다.
  "She would always sing in the shower."
  그녀는 항상 샤워하면서 노래하곤 했다.

  ※ "would" for past habit ≈ "used to"
    "I used to play in the park." = "I would play in the park."
    BUT: "used to" can describe past STATES; "would" cannot:
    "I used to live in Seoul." ✓
    "I would live in Seoul." ✗ (not for states)
```

## 7. shall, need, dare — Less Common Modals

### 7.1 shall

```
Suggestions (제안 — BrE):
  "Shall I open the window?"       창문 열까요?
  "Shall we go?"                   갈까요?
  "What shall I do?"               뭘 할까요?

  ※ "shall" for suggestions is mainly British English.
    American English uses "should" instead:
    "Should I open the window?" (AmE)
    "Shall I open the window?" (BrE)

Formal/Legal:
  "You shall not pass!"            통과하지 못하리라! (literary)
  "The tenant shall pay rent monthly." (legal/contract)
```

### 7.2 need

```
As a modal (mainly in BrE negatives/questions):
  "Need I say more?"               더 말할 필요가 있겠습니까?
  "You needn't worry."             걱정할 필요 없어.

As a regular verb (more common):
  "You need to study."             공부해야 해.
  "Do you need to go?"             가야 해?
  "She doesn't need to come."      그녀는 올 필요 없어.
  "I needed to rest."              쉬어야 했다.

  ※ "needn't" (modal) = "don't need to" (regular verb) = "don't have to"
    All mean: not necessary (할 필요 없다)
```

### 7.3 dare

```
As a modal (formal/literary):
  "How dare you!"                  감히 어떻게!
  "Dare I ask?"                    감히 물어봐도 될까요?
  "I daren't tell her."            감히 그녀에게 말 못하겠다.

As a regular verb (more common):
  "I don't dare (to) ask."        감히 물어보지 못하겠다.
  "She didn't dare (to) move."    그녀는 감히 움직이지 못했다.

  ※ "dare" is uncommon in everyday English.
    Most often seen in: "How dare you!" (감히 어떻게!)
```

## 8. Modal + have + Past Participle (Past Modals)

### 8.1 Past Modals Overview

> **modal + have + past participle = modal meaning in the PAST**
>
> must have + pp    = 과거 추론 (~했음에 틀림없다)
> may/might have + pp = 과거 가능성 (~했을 수도 있다)
> could have + pp   = 과거 가능성 / 미실현 가능성
> should have + pp  = 과거 후회 (~했어야 했는데)
> would have + pp   = 과거 가정 (~했을 텐데)
> can't have + pp   = 과거 불가능 (~했을 리가 없다)
> needn't have + pp = 불필요한 과거 행동 (~할 필요 없었는데 했다)

### 8.2 must have + past participle (과거 확신 추론)

```
"She must have been tired."        그녀는 피곤했음에 틀림없다.
"He must have left already."       그는 이미 떠났음에 틀림없다.
"They must have missed the bus."   그들은 버스를 놓쳤음에 틀림없다.
"It must have rained last night."  어젯밤에 비가 왔음에 틀림없다.

  ※ = I'm almost sure this happened (based on evidence)
    "The ground is wet. It must have rained." (95% confident)
```

### 8.3 may/might have + past participle (과거 가능성)

```
"She may have left already."       그녀는 이미 떠났을 수도 있다.
"He might have forgotten."         그는 잊었을 수도 있다.
"They may not have received the email."  이메일을 받지 못했을 수도 있다.

  ※ = it's possible this happened (but I'm not sure)
```

### 8.4 should have + past participle (과거 후회/비판)

```
"I should have studied harder."    더 열심히 공부했어야 했는데.
"She should have called."         전화했어야 했는데.
"You shouldn't have said that."   그런 말을 하지 말았어야 했는데.
"We should have left earlier."    더 일찍 출발했어야 했는데.

  ※ = the right action in the past, but it didn't happen
    Expresses REGRET (후회) or CRITICISM (비판)
    "I should have studied harder." = I didn't study hard enough, and I regret it.
```

### 8.5 could have + past participle (미실현 과거 가능성)

```
"I could have helped you."         도와줄 수 있었는데 (안 했다).
"She could have won the race."     경주에서 이길 수 있었는데.
"You could have been killed!"      죽을 뻔했어!
"He could have told me."           나에게 말해줄 수 있었잖아.

  ※ = had the ability/possibility but didn't do it
    Sometimes expresses criticism or frustration.
    "You could have called me!" = Why didn't you call?!
```

### 8.6 would have + past participle (과거 가정)

```
"I would have helped if I had known."  알았더라면 도와줬을 텐데.
"She would have come, but she was sick." 왔을 텐데, 아팠다.
"We would have missed the flight."  비행기를 놓칠 뻔했다.

  ※ = hypothetical past result (3rd conditional)
    If + had + pp, ... would have + pp
    "If I had studied, I would have passed."
```

### 8.7 can't/couldn't have + past participle (과거 불가능 추론)

```
"She can't have said that!"        그녀가 그런 말을 했을 리가 없어!
"He couldn't have known."          그가 알았을 리가 없다.
"They can't have finished already." 벌써 끝냈을 리가 없다.

  ※ = I'm sure this did NOT happen (confident negative deduction)
    Opposite of "must have":
    "must have" = I'm sure it DID happen
    "can't have" = I'm sure it DIDN'T happen
```

## 9. Modal Verbs Summary Table

> **Function    Present/Future          Past**
>
> Ability     can / be able to        could / was able to
> Permission  can / may               could (past context)
> Request     can/could/would you?    —
> Possibility may / might / could     may/might/could have + pp
> Deduction   must (95%)              must have + pp
> Impossibility can't (95% not)       can't/couldn't have + pp
> Obligation  must / have to          had to
> Prohibition mustn't                 —
> No need     don't have to / needn't didn't have to /
> needn't have + pp
> Advice      should / ought to       should have + pp (regret)
> Warning     had better              —
> Offer       shall I? (BrE)          —
> Past habit  —                       would / used to
> Hypothetical would                  would have + pp

## Quick Reference

> **Modal Verbs Quick Guide**
>
> Ability:     can / could / be able to
> Permission:  can (informal) / may (formal) / could (polite)
> Obligation:  must / have to
> Prohibition: mustn't (DON'T!)  ≠  don't have to (no need)
> Advice:      should / ought to / had better (stronger)
> Possibility: may (50%) > might/could (30%)
> Deduction:   must (95% yes) / can't (95% no)
> Polite:      could you / would you / would you mind
> Past modals (modal + have + past participle):
> must have + pp     = ~했음에 틀림없다
> should have + pp   = ~했어야 했는데 (후회)
> could have + pp    = ~할 수 있었는데 (안 했다)
> might have + pp    = ~했을 수도 있다
> would have + pp    = ~했을 텐데 (가정)
> can't have + pp    = ~했을 리가 없다
> Rules: no -s (he can ✓, he cans ✗)
> base form after modal (can swim ✓, can to swim ✗)
> no do-support (can you? ✓, do you can? ✗)
